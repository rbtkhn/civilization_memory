## II. BRITISH EMPIRE / INDIA CONTENT (FROM EXISTING ANGLIA MEMS & SECONDARY SOURCES)

Ferguson frames India as:

- **Jewel in the crown** — the territory whose revenue and strategic weight justified imperial commitment at unprecedented scale (MEM–ANGLIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE–INDIA).
- **Transformative node** — India as the territory that transformed Britain from maritime-commercial power into global administrative empire (MEM–ANGLIA–INDIA, MEM–ANGLIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE–INDIA).
- **Administrative laboratory** — where indirect rule, legal codification, and infrastructure development were tested at scale; procedures developed in India later informed imperial practice elsewhere (MEM–ANGLIA–INDIA).
- **Company-to-Crown trajectory** — transition from East India Company rule to direct Crown administration after 1857; shift from commercial extraction to formal governance, with tension between extraction and governance persisting throughout the Raj (MEM–ANGLIA–INDIA).
- **Imperial method** — Ferguson emphasizes "hardworking bureaucrats and patrician proconsuls," administrative integrity and collaboration, alongside broader imperial expansion (Guardian review; Cambridge *Concise History* framing).

*Empire* is organized across imperial history; India appears in company rule, Crown Raj, and exit phases. Verbatim passages (≥25 words) for India MEMs should be drawn from the full text when available; Wikiquote and open-web access to verbatim *Empire* text were limited at time of this note.

## V. CANDIDATE PASSAGE (VERIFY AGAINST PUBLISHED *EMPIRE*)

The following passage is used in MEM–ANGLIA–INDIA as a **summary** (not yet verified verbatim). It is a strong candidate for **MEM–INDIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE** once checked against the book:

> India functioned as the "jewel in the crown" of the British Empire—the territory that transformed Britain from a maritime-commercial power into a global administrative empire. The transition from East India Company rule to direct Crown administration after 1857 marked a shift from commercial extraction to formal governance, yet the tension between extraction and governance persisted throughout the Raj. India became Britain's administrative laboratory, where indirect rule, legal codification, and infrastructure development were tested at unprecedented scale, even as the costs of maintaining imperial control grew and nationalist resistance mounted.

— Niall Ferguson, *Empire: How Britain Made the Modern World* (London: Allen Lane, 2003) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]. **Status:** Summary-only in MEM–ANGLIA–INDIA; verify wording against published edition before use as verbatim in MEM–INDIA files.
