## IX. SUGGESTED NEW ANGLIA MEMs (ANDERSON-DRIVEN)

Three new MEMs that would draw heavily on Fred Anderson, *Crucible of War*, and fill gaps in the Anglia corpus. No equivalent MEMs exist for these subjects.

| Suggested MEM | Subject | Anderson linkage | Anglia civilizational angle |
|---------------|---------|------------------|----------------------------|
| **MEM–ANGLIA–GEO–OHIO–VALLEY** (or MEM–ANGLIA–OHIO–VALLEY) | Control of the Ohio Valley as origin of the Seven Years' War; French and British confrontation; Washington, Jumonville, Tanaghrisson; Iroquois diplomatic miscalculation; “chaotic competition of two empires.” | Anderson centers the war’s origin in “a single desperate Iroquois chief” and “the interests of the Iroquois Confederacy, the government of New France, the governor of Virginia, and a group of Anglo-American land speculators” converging at the Ohio; Prologue (Jumonville’s Glen) and Introduction. | Imperial competition as **procedure vs geography**: who controls the valley, by what authority; Anglia’s backcountry as legitimacy frontier. |
| **MEM–ANGLIA–CRISIS–STAMP–ACT** (or MEM–ANGLIA–STAMP–ACT–CRISIS) | Stamp Act crisis (1765–66) as postwar imperial governance crisis, not merely “prelude to Revolution”; ministerial reform, revenue, colonial resistance; parallel to Pontiac’s War. | Anderson: “Taking 1766 as its stopping point… detaches the Stamp Act crisis from its usual narrative function as the prologue to the Revolution, and makes manifest the parallels between the Stamp Act riots and Pontiac's War as efforts to defend local autonomy within the empire”; “ministerial efforts to… raise modest revenues… precipitated violent civil disobedience.” | **Procedure vs metropolis**: colonies and metropolis as “postwar era vexed by unanticipated problems”; legitimacy of taxation and representation. |
| **MEM–ANGLIA–WAR–PONTIAC** (or MEM–ANGLIA–PONTIAC–WAR) | Pan-Indian resistance (1763–66), “Pontiac’s Rebellion”; native reaction to changes imposed from above; parallel to Stamp Act; Britain’s resolution of both crises by 1766. | Anderson: “the most successful pan-Indian resistance movement in American history”; “The native peoples of the interior were the first to react negatively to changes imposed from above”; “both the Indians and the colonists… had shown a sudden, unexpected capacity to achieve consensus”; Britain resolved both “in ways that reassured Indians and colonists alike that the new empire would be a tolerable place to live.” | **Imperial governance under strain**: two parallel resistances (Indian and colonial) as efforts to “shape, challenge, [and] resist colonialism” and define empire; Anglia’s procedural response (reassurance, then renewed specification of imperial terms). |

**Cross-links:** All three would connect to MEM–ANGLIA–WAR–SEVEN–YEARS, MEM–ANGLIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE, MEM–ANGLIA–CANADA, MEM–ANGLIA–WAR–AMERICAN–INDEPENDENCE; Ohio Valley to MEM–ANGLIA–WASHINGTON; Stamp Act and Pontiac to MEM–ANGLIA–NATIVE–AMERICANS if present.
