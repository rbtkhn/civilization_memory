# RESEARCH — Indian Military History: Support / Contradict Defection Theory

**Date:** 2026-02-01  
**Scope:** Indian military history episodes testing Mearsheimer structural theory (force at distance vs. fragmentation; defection locus)  
**Anchor:** MEM–INDIA–WAR–ALEXANDER, MEM–INDIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE–CLIVE; CIV–SCHOLAR–INDIA (Mearsheimer lens on WAR pair)  
**Theory under test:** Defection is structurally incentivized; locus varies. **Hyphasis:** force at distance hits limit → invader’s army defects. **Plassey:** fragmentation → defender’s elites defect. Same question (who was exposed when the mechanism failed), opposite answers.

---

## I. THEORY SUMMARY

| Locus | Condition | Defection type | Example |
|-------|-----------|----------------|---------|
| Invader at edge | Force at distance | Invader’s army refuses to advance | Hyphasis (326 BC) |
| Defender | Fragmentation | Defender’s elites defect to invader | Plassey (1757), Panipat (1526), Anglo-Maratha, Anglo-Sikh |
| Invader’s proxy | Dependence on local force | Invader’s local army defects against invader | 1857 Rebellion |

---

## II. SUPPORTING CASES

### A. Defender elite defection / fragmentation (Plassey-type)

**First Battle of Panipat (1526)**  
Ibrahim Lodi’s Afghan nobility was alienated; Daulat Khan Lodi (governor of Punjab) invited Babur to invade and depose Ibrahim. Defender’s mechanism failed before the battle: elite defection and invitation, not mass defeat. **Supports** theory: fragmentation + elite defection enabled Mughal conquest.

**Anglo-Maratha Wars (1775–1819)**  
Maratha princes were “rarely unified”; British exploited lack of coordination and used subsidiary alliances to co-opt elites. British victory through incremental consolidation, not single decisive battle. **Supports** theory: fragmentation enabled Plassey-style exploitation.

**Anglo-Sikh Wars (1845–1849)**  
After Ranjit Singh’s death (1839), Sikh kingdom fell into factional conflict (Sindhanwalias vs. Dogras), army vs. court; rapid succession. British prevailed (e.g. Ferozeshah, Sobraon); instability and defection/splits cited. **Supports** theory: fragmentation and internal defection preceded British military success.

### B. Force at distance / invader limit (Hyphasis-type)

**Ghaznavid return from Somnath (1026)**  
Mahmud’s army suffered “significant hardships” crossing the Sind desert and Sindh-Sagar Doab; “persistent harassment and delays from the local Jat population, protracted withdrawal.” Ghaznavids permanently annexed only Punjab; “broader Indian territories remained difficult to control.” **Supports** theory: force at distance (and depth resistance) limited invader’s hold; not army mutiny but logistical/geographic and local resistance as structural limit.

**Alexander at Hyphasis (326 BC)**  
Already encoded in MEM–INDIA–WAR–ALEXANDER: army refusal at the edge; force at distance as binding constraint.

## III. EXTENDING THE THEORY: THIRD DEFECTION TYPE

**Indian Rebellion of 1857 (Sepoy Mutiny)**  
British rule in India depended structurally on Indian troops: Bengal Army ~159,000, of which ~135,000 Indian (sepoys/sowars), ~24,000 European. When sepoys mutinied (Meerut, May 1857), the **invader’s local proxy force** defected against the invader. Exposure fell on the British mechanism (Company/Crown), not on a defending Indian polity. **Extends** theory: defection can be (1) invader’s army at edge refuses to go further (Hyphasis), (2) defender’s elites defect to invader (Plassey, Panipat), (3) **invader’s local army defects against invader** (1857). Post-1857 British reorganized: greater reliance on “loyal” troops (e.g. Sikhs, Gurkhas) and reduced structural dependence on a single mass sepoy force—recognition of the defection risk.

---

## IV. CONTRADICTIONS / TENSIONS

- **None found that outright contradict.** Cases either support (fragmentation → elite defection; force at distance → invader limit) or extend (1857 = invader’s proxy defection).
- **Tension:** Anglo-Sikh Wars involved hard fighting (e.g. Ferozeshah “close to defeat” for British). So fragmentation enabled entry and reduced cohesion, but battlefield outcome was not purely political—structural incentive for defection and fragmentation operated alongside force. Theory holds if stated as: fragmentation is a **condition** that makes defender vulnerable; it does not require that every victory be bloodless.

---

## V. REVERSE CASE: UNITY DEFEATS FRAGMENTATION

**Mauryan consolidation (post-Alexander)**  
MEM–INDIA–DYNASTY–MAURYA encodes “fragmentation following Achaemenid and Macedonian incursions”; Chandragupta consolidates through military unification, bureaucratic centralization, elite co-option. **Supports** inverse: when defender unifies (and reduces elite defection incentive), the Plassey-type entry is harder. Aligns with theory: fragmentation is the structural condition for defender-elite defection; unity is the condition that resists it.
