## Step 1 — Mercouris Output (Prompt 1)

*Prompt:* Apply Mercouris lens only. Britain loses the Suez Canal in 1956 after Nasser nationalizes it and the US opposes Anglo-French action. Britain withdraws; empire in the Middle East winds down, but the British state survives and remains a key US ally. In 2–3 paragraphs, analyze this outcome. Use Mercouris linguistic markers and analytical requirements (legitimacy, institutional continuity, hedging, non-closure).

**M1 (Mercouris):**

It seems to me that one has to understand Suez 1956 in terms of what the canal represented for British imperial legitimacy, not only as a commercial artery but as a symbol of institutional continuity—the idea that Britain could still project authority and guarantee order along the lifeline to India and beyond. The crucial point, surely, is that when Nasser nationalized the canal, he was attacking not merely an asset but the narrative that Britain had a right to run it. The question, really, is how the British state could withdraw and yet survive as a state. Perhaps one might argue that legitimacy had already been shifting: the empire was becoming harder to justify at home and in the Commonwealth, and the United States’ refusal to back the intervention made it clear that the old Anglo-French claim to manage the region was no longer recognised by the dominant Western power. So what one sees is not simply defeat—it appears that the British state preserved its continuity by accepting the loss of imperial prerogative while retaining its role as a US ally. One has to understand that the survival of the state and the winding down of empire in the Middle East can sit in tension; the institutional continuity of Britain itself was not contingent on holding Suez, whereas the legitimacy of empire was. That tension is never quite resolved—it seems to me that we are left with a picture where the state adapts by shedding one form of legitimacy while retaining another, and the outcome remains open to how one weights legitimacy density in the metropole versus the periphery.

**M2 (Mearsheimer):**

Mercouris is right that the canal functioned as a legitimacy symbol for British imperial authority and that the British state survived by shedding imperial prerogative while retaining its role as a US ally. The structural point, though, is different. The fact is that once the United States refused to support the Anglo-French action—and in practice opposed it—the balance of power in the theatre shifted decisively. Britain and France could not sustain the operation without US financial and diplomatic cover; the incentive structure for London was to end the intervention or face a run on the pound and isolation from Washington. So we have a situation where the distribution of power, not the density of legitimacy, determined the outcome: the state that could afford to withhold support (the United States) and the state that could not afford to lose it (Britain) were in a structural relationship that made withdrawal the only viable move. The structural logic is that Britain lost the canal because it could not project and sustain force in the face of US opposition and Soviet posturing; who could afford to lose what—Britain could not afford to lose the alliance, the United States could afford to let Britain lose the canal—is what drove the result. That is a force-ratio and incentive-structure story. The British state survived because it adapted to the new power reality: it remained useful to the United States as an ally, and so the alliance was preserved even as the imperial asset was given up. So when you contrast the legitimacy story with the power story, they are not the same. The fact is that you need the structural picture to explain why Britain withdrew when it did and why the empire in the Middle East wound down at that moment—the timing and the outcome are driven by who had leverage over whom.
