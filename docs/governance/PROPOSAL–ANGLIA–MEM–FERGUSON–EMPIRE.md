II. FERGUSON *EMPIRE* — TITLE, DATE, SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**Full title:** *Empire: How Britain Made the Modern World*  
**Publication:** 2003 (UK: Allen Lane/Penguin); 2004 (US: Basic Books)  
**Length:** ~400 pages (392 in some editions)  
**Period covered:** c. 1600–1960s (from commercial expansion through decolonization)

**Thesis:** The British Empire acted as a "midwife to modernity," spreading capitalism, telecommunications, the English language, and representative government institutions globally. Ferguson examines both the empire's modernizing force and its costs, arguing for a nuanced understanding of imperial legacy.

**Structure (six main chapters):**

| Chapter | Focus |
|---------|-------|
| **1. WHY BRITAIN?** | Origins, commercial expansion, strategic advantage |
| **2. WHITE PLAGUE** | Emigration, settlement, voluntary and involuntary migration (blacks and whites) |
| **3. THE MISSION** | Missionaries, reformers, moral rhetoric, civilizing ideology |
| **4. HEAVEN'S BREED** | Bureaucrats, proconsuls, administrative officials, colonial governance |
| **5. MAXIM FORCE** | Military power, late 19th-century technology (Maxim gun, etc.), coercive capacity |
| **6. MISSION ACCOMPLISHED?** | Industrial warfare (WWI), competing empires (Japan, Germany, USA), British decline |

**Key Themes:**
- Maritime-financial foundation (naval supremacy, City of London, credit)
- Indirect rule, light governance, cost-efficiency
- Emigration and settler colonies (Canada, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa)
- India as "jewel in the crown" — Company rule, Raj, extraction
- Moral rhetoric alongside strategic interest (missions, "civilizing," abolition)
- Technology and military asymmetry (telegraph, railways, Maxim gun)
- Decolonization and imperial overstretch
