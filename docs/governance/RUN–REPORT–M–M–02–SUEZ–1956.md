## Step 1 — Mearsheimer Output (Prompt 1)

*Prompt:* Apply Mearsheimer lens only. Britain loses the Suez Canal in 1956 after Nasser nationalizes it and the US opposes Anglo-French action. Britain withdraws; empire in the Middle East winds down, but the British state survives and remains a key US ally. In 2–3 paragraphs, analyze this outcome. Use Mearsheimer linguistic markers and analytical requirements (power distribution, incentive structure, force-ratio, state as unit, direct voice).

---

**M2a (Mearsheimer):**

The fact is that Suez 1956 is a story about power distribution and incentive structure, not about who had the better legal or moral claim. Once Nasser nationalized the canal, Britain and France had a choice: use force to reverse it or accept the new reality. The structural picture is that they could not sustain a military operation without American support—and the United States had a different incentive structure. From an American point of view, it made eminently good sense to oppose the Anglo-French intervention: the US wanted to avoid a confrontation with the Soviet Union over the Middle East, to position itself as the champion of decolonisation in the eyes of the Third World, and to ensure that oil and influence in the region flowed through arrangements that Washington could shape. So we have a situation where Britain and France needed US backing to hold the line, and the US had a vested interest in not giving it. The structural logic is that when the dominant power in the alliance withholds support, the junior partners cannot project force effectively—they run out of money, they run out of diplomatic cover, and they withdraw. That is a force-ratio and incentive-structure outcome. The British state survived and remained a key US ally because it adapted: it gave up the imperial asset and aligned with Washington’s preferences. The question is whether Britain could have done otherwise. The answer is no—not given the distribution of power. Who could afford to lose what? Britain could not afford to lose the American alliance; the United States could afford to let Britain lose the canal. So the outcome was determined by that structural relationship.

**M2b (Mercouris):**

Mearsheimer is right that the incentive structure favoured Washington and that Britain could not sustain the operation without American support—and that who could afford to lose what drove the immediate outcome. The crucial point, surely, is that one also has to understand what power analysis leaves out. It seems to me that the canal had accumulated a legitimacy function over decades: for British imperial narrative it was not simply an asset but a symbol of institutional continuity—the idea that Britain could still guarantee order along the lifeline to India and the East. When Nasser nationalized it, he was attacking that narrative as much as the waterway. So the question, really, is how the British state could withdraw and yet preserve its continuity as a state. Perhaps one might argue that legitimacy had already been shifting: the empire was becoming harder to justify at home and in the Commonwealth, and the United States’ refusal to back the intervention made it clear that the old Anglo-French claim to manage the region was no longer recognised by the dominant Western power. That recognition—or withdrawal of recognition—is a legitimacy phenomenon. One has to understand that the survival of the state and the winding down of empire can sit in tension; the institutional continuity of Britain itself was not contingent on holding Suez, whereas the legitimacy of empire was. It appears that what power analysis captures is the mechanism of the withdrawal—who had leverage, who had to back down—but what it misses is the multi-generational horizon: how the British state re-legitimated itself by shedding imperial prerogative while retaining its role as an ally, and how that allowed institutional continuity rather than collapse. So it seems to me that we are left with a picture where structure explains the timing and the leverage, but legitimacy and institutional continuity explain why the state adapted in this way rather than fragmenting or resisting to the point of rupture.
