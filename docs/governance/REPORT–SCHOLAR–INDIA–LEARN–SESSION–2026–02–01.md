## III. NEW LEARNINGS (CHRONOLOGICAL)

### 1. Mearsheimer lens on WAR pair (Hyphasis / Plassey)

- **Force at distance and invader defection:** At Hyphasis, Alexander’s army defects (refuses to advance). Structural condition: force projected to the edge; supply lines long; enemy ahead (Nanda/Magadha) large and uncertain; marginal payoff for rank-and-file negative. Limit = force at distance; defection = invader’s army.
- **Fragmentation and defender defection:** At Plassey, defender’s elites defect to the Company. Structural condition: Mughal decline, regional rivalry, no unified command. British force at distance low; defection of defender’s elites substitutes for mass. Limit = cohesion; defection = defender’s elites.
- **Structural summary:** Same question—who was exposed when the mechanism failed—opposite loci. Alexander: exposure on commander; Clive: exposure on indigenous polity.

### 2. Indian military history evidence (ENTRY 0007)

- **Support (defender elite defection):** First Panipat (1526)—Lodi nobility invited Babur. Anglo-Maratha Wars—Maratha princes rarely unified; British exploited fragmentation. Anglo-Sikh Wars—post–Ranjit Singh factional conflict. All Plassey-type.
- **Support (force at distance / invader limit):** Ghaznavid return from Somnath (1026)—hardship in Sind, Jat harassment, protracted withdrawal; only Punjab held long term. Hyphasis-type (no mutiny, but same structural limit).
- **Extension (third defection type):** 1857 Rebellion—British dependence on Indian troops (~135k sepoys vs ~24k European in Bengal Army); sepoy defection *against* the invader. Invader’s local proxy force defects; exposure on imperial mechanism.
- **Inverse:** Mauryan consolidation post-Alexander—Chandragupta unified fragmented polities; when defender unifies, Plassey-type entry is harder.
- **Contradictions:** None found. Anglo-Sikh had heavy fighting—fragmentation as condition, not guarantee of bloodless victory.

### 3. SYNTHESIS 0003 — Indian military history: three defection types

- **Three types:** (1) Invader at edge — force at distance — Hyphasis, Ghaznavid return. (2) Defender elites — fragmentation — Plassey, Panipat, Anglo-Maratha, Anglo-Sikh. (3) Invader’s proxy — dependence on proxy — 1857.
- **Link:** Extends RLL–INDIA–0002 (two-mode core) with external evidence and third type.
- **Mercouris reframe:** Defection as legitimacy question—encoding as betrayal vs. resistance depends on whose authority and continuity are asserted. Hyphasis = limit of commander’s reach; Plassey = failure of defender’s cohesion; 1857 = resistance (independence narrative) vs. betrayal (imperial narrative). Same structural locus, different civilizational weight.
- **Barnes reframe:** Who was exposed when sepoys mutinied—Company/Crown. Jurisdiction: who had compulsory authority over the sepoy force; after 1857 Crown took over (Company had been on the hook). Who bore the cost; British reorganized to reduce proxy dependence. When invader’s proxy defects, invader’s chain of command is exposed; somebody has to own the liability.

### 4. RLL–INDIA–0002 updates

- **Statement:** Extension added—invader’s local proxy force may defect against the invader (e.g. 1857); exposure then falls on the imperial mechanism. Three defection types (invader at edge, defender elites, invader’s proxy) with pointer to SYNTHESIS 0003.
- **Derivation:** ENTRY 0007, SYNTHESIS 0003 (three defection types; Indian military history evidence); Barnes catalyst (1857: jurisdiction shift, who was on the hook when proxy defected).
