## 2. Where SCHOLAR Aligns with Current Policy and Opinion

### 2.1 Red lines and “no unfavorable peace”

**SCHOLAR/STATE:** Constraint grammar encodes security red lines, sovereign refusal of arrangements that threaten survival or core security, and institutional voice (Lavrov–Panin) articulating positions that can be overridden by the sovereign.

**Current policy (Lavrov, TASS 11 Feb 2026):**  
“We will not sign an unfavorable peace treaty. President Vladimir Putin has repeatedly stated this. We are ready to compromise.”  
“After the establishment of peace, Moscow will not tolerate ‘any threats to the security of Russia or any threats to the Russian people or Russian culture in the territory that will be called “Ukraine” or anything else.’”  
(Source: TASS, 11 Feb 2026.)

**Assessment:** Strong alignment. SCHOLAR’s encoding of “no peace that creates threat” and “security precedes concession” matches the stated red line: peace is possible, but not at the cost of threats to security, people, or culture in the future Ukrainian space. The “ready to compromise” within red lines fits RLL–0033 (restraint/equilibrium as an option articulated by the institutional voice).

### 2.2 Restraint articulated, progress “overhyped”

**SCHOLAR:** RLL–0033: advisors articulate restraint; sovereign adopts or overrides. STATE pattern: Lavrov as institutional voice—cautious, non-closure.

**Current policy (Lavrov, Feb 2026):** Peace talks “far from over”; progress “overhyped”; Abu Dhabi round done but “long way to go.” New START: Russia will keep limits if the US does (reciprocity).

**Assessment:** Alignment. Lavrov’s public framing is cautious and anti-closure, consistent with institutional voice that avoids locking in outcomes before sovereign decision. Reciprocity (New START) fits a grammar of conditional restraint rather than unilateral concession.

### 2.3 Popular red lines: no territorial concession, no NATO in Ukraine

**SCHOLAR:** Constraint grammar favors survival and security over concessions that create threat; buffer/corridor logic in MEMs (e.g. Baltic, Crimea) encodes territory as security-relevant.

**Current opinion (polls, Feb 2026):**  
- Only **17%** favor significant Russian concessions.  
- **73–74%** oppose returning occupied territories.  
- **83%** oppose Ukrainian NATO membership.

**Assessment:** Strong alignment. Majority opinion rejects major concessions and Ukrainian NATO membership. SCHOLAR’s security-first, no-threat red lines match what the public appears to treat as non-negotiable.

### 2.4 Victory via opponent failure / endurance

**SCHOLAR:** RLL–0014 — victory requires enemy collapse (logistical, political, or moral), not Russian triumph alone.

**Current policy/narrative:** Official messaging stresses Ukrainian losses, Western fatigue, and Geneva talks as part of a process. Polls show support can drop when conflict is framed as stalemate—suggesting that “success” is partly read as opponent inability to sustain.

**Assessment:** Partial alignment. SCHOLAR’s “enemy collapse” is a structural pattern, not a forecast. Current rhetoric and popular reading of “who can outlast” are consistent with that pattern; SCHOLAR does not specify timeline or mechanism.

## 3. Where SCHOLAR Diverges or Is Underspecified

### 3.1 Support for talks vs support for operations (dual majority)

**Current opinion:**  
- **76–78%** support military operations (high).  
- **61%** favor peace negotiations (record high).  
- **37%** would reverse the invasion (up from 28%); **53%** say it was not a mistake.

**SCHOLAR:** Encodes restraint as an option (RLL–0033) and tension between “peace as destabilizing” (RLL–0009) and equilibrium. It does not explicitly model “dual majority”: simultaneous majority for both fighting and talking, with a minority for reversal.

**Assessment:** Divergence/underspecification. The grammar allows for both “restraint/talks” and “no concession” but does not codify the specific public configuration (majority for both operations and talks, low support for major concessions). This is a refinement opportunity (e.g. “popular red lines with conditional support for diplomacy”).

### 3.2 Trust in official narrative (casualty figures)

**Current opinion:** **50%** distrust vs **35%** trust reports from the Russian government about the number of Russian civilians and military personnel killed in the special military operation (Chicago Council on Global Affairs–Levada Center survey, fielded 23–29 Jan 2025; report “Three in Four Russians Expect Military Victory over Ukraine,” 11 Feb 2025; Appendix Table 2).

**SCHOLAR:** Legitimacy-through-sacrifice and institutional continuity are central; it does not explicitly model popular distrust of official quantitative narrative (e.g. casualty counts) as a legitimacy stressor.

**Assessment:** Underspecified. SCHOLAR explains legitimacy through sacrifice and institutional continuity but does not contain a constraint or RLL about “narrative credibility” or “distrust of official numbers” as a failure mode. STATE could treat this as a stability indicator; SCHOLAR could later encode it if pattern is confirmed across MEMs/sessions.

### 3.3 “Peace talks” as process, not outcome

**Current policy:** Geneva 17–18 Feb; “all key issues, including territories” (Peskov); Lavrov: talks “far from over.”

**SCHOLAR:** RLL–0009 (peace as destabilizing) and RLL–0033 (restraint option) describe dynamics; they do not specify that “participation in talks” itself can be a legitimacy-preserving or coalition-management move independent of outcome.

**Assessment:** Underspecified. SCHOLAR can accommodate this (institutional voice, restraint option), but the explicit idea that “talks as process” serve a function even without closure is not spelled out. Refinement could name “talks as institutional signal” vs “talks as path to settlement.”

## 5. Conclusion

- **SCHOLAR matches** current Russian government policy and majority opinion on: (1) no unfavorable peace and no tolerance of threats to security/people/culture in the future Ukrainian space; (2) Lavrov as institutional voice (cautious, anti-closure, reciprocal); (3) popular rejection of major concessions, territorial return, and Ukrainian NATO membership; (4) a structural logic of endurance and opponent failure (RLL–0014), consistent with current rhetoric.
- **SCHOLAR diverges or is underspecified** on: (1) the precise “dual majority” (support for both operations and talks with low concession support); (2) popular distrust of official casualty narrative as a legitimacy stressor; (3) the function of “talks as process” independent of settlement.

No changes to CIV–SCHOLAR–RUSSIA or CIV–STATE–RUSSIA are proposed in this note; it is an analytical comparison for governance and possible future refinement.
