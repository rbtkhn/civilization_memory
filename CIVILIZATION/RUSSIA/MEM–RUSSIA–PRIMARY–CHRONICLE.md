I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the *Primary Chronicle* as the **foundational
civilizational memory text** of early Rus’: the narrative source that
defines origins, legitimacy, moral order, and historical causation
for Russian statehood.

The Chronicle functions not as neutral history, but as a **legitimacy
compiler**—encoding authority, faith, and dynastic logic into narrative
form.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGIN NARRATIVE & AUTHORITY LOGIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Chronicle frames Rus’ origins through **invited rulership**.

Core claim:
> “Our land is great and rich, but there is no order in it. Come rule us.”

Key implications:
• Authority precedes consent
• Order is externalized before institutionalized
• Rule is justified by necessity, not popular will

This origin story normalizes coercive stabilization as legitimate.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. DYNASTIC CONTINUITY AS HISTORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
History is structured genealogically.

Features:
• Princes listed through lineage
• Events anchored to rulers, not institutions
• Political legitimacy equals dynastic continuity

The Chronicle treats dynasty as the **spine of time itself**.

Breaks in lineage = historical catastrophe.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ORTHODOXY AS CIVILIZATIONAL FILTER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Christianization reorders causality.

Narrative logic:
• Pagan disorder → Christian order
• Victory = divine favor
• Defeat = punishment or trial

The Chronicle moralizes power outcomes,
binding sovereignty to faith.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. VIOLENCE, BETRAYAL, AND MORAL EXEMPLARS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Violence is recorded without sanitization.

Patterns:
• Fratricide among princes
• Betrayal and vengeance
• Martyrdom (Boris and Gleb)

Moral coding:
• Obedience sanctified
• Resistance framed ambiguously
• Suffering integrated into legitimacy

Political morality favors endurance over rebellion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MEMORY OF FRAGMENTATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Chronicle accepts fragmentation as tragic but normal.

Features:
• Appanage rivalries normalized
• Unity presented as ideal, not constant
• Collapse anticipated through moral failure

Disunity is explained morally, not structurally.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CHRONICLE AS OPERATING SYSTEM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Primary Chronicle supplies enduring assumptions:
• Authority must be personal
• Unity requires coercion
• Faith stabilizes power
• Dynasty legitimizes rule
• Disorder invites external domination

Later Russian rulers inherit these premises implicitly.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Russian history begins as moral narrative, not civic record.
• Authority is justified by necessity before law.
• Dynasty substitutes for constitutional continuity.
• Orthodoxy provides causal explanation for power outcomes.
• Fragmentation is tolerated until catastrophe occurs.

Tension preserved without verdict.
