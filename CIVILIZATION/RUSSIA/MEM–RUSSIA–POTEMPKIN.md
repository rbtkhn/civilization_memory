────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Grigory Potemkin as the **imperial frontier architect**
of Catherine’s Russia: the strategist who translated court power into
territorial absorption, demographic engineering, and durable southern
imperial infrastructure.

Potemkin represents **empire built through administration and settlement**,
not conquest alone.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. COURT POWER & IMPERIAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Potemkin’s authority derives from proximity to Catherine.

Key features:
• Personal trust over formal office
• Broad delegated authority
• Fusion of military, civil, and economic roles

He operates as an imperial executor rather than a theorist.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. SOUTHERN STRATEGY & NEW RUSSIA (НОВОРОССИЯ)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Potemkin designs Russia’s southern expansion.

Core actions:
• Integration of steppe territories
• Founding of cities (Kherson, Sevastopol, Ekaterinoslav)
• Naval base construction in the Black Sea
• Population transfer and settlement programs

Empire is stabilized through **people, ports, and permanence**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CRIMEA & BLACK SEA IMPERIALIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Potemkin oversees the annexation of Crimea (1783).

Dynamics:
• Diplomatic containment of Ottoman response
• Military deterrence without immediate war
• Administrative absorption of Muslim populations

Crimea becomes a strategic hinge:
→ Warm-water access
→ Naval projection
→ Southern security depth

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. WAR ADMINISTRATION & IMPERIAL LOGISTICS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
During Russo–Turkish conflicts, Potemkin coordinates scale.

Characteristics:
• Emphasis on logistics and supply
• Integration of allied commanders (Suvorov)
• Tolerance for delay to ensure durability

War is treated as **system management**, not heroic spectacle.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. THE “POTEMKIN VILLAGE” MYTH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Later caricature frames Potemkin as a deceiver.

Counter-memory:
• Demonstrative displays existed
• Infrastructure and settlement were real
• Myth reflects outsider misunderstanding of imperial staging

Symbolism functions as legitimacy theater, not fabrication.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. ADMINISTRATIVE STYLE & LIMITATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Potemkin’s governance blends vision and excess.

Strengths:
• Strategic imagination
• Long-horizon planning
• Ability to mobilize resources

Weaknesses:
• Financial overextension
• Dependence on personal authority
• Uneven execution across regions

Imperial ambition strains fiscal discipline.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Empire consolidates through settlement as much as war.
• Personal authority can substitute for institutional clarity.
• Geography becomes power when populated and fortified.
• Southern expansion reorients Russia’s strategic axis.
• Potemkin operationalizes Catherine’s imperial vision.

Tension preserved without verdict.
