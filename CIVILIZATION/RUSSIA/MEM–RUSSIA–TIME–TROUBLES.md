I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Time of Troubles as the **system-failure case**
of Russian statehood: a period when dynastic extinction, legitimacy
collapse, foreign intervention, and social breakdown converged to
nearly dissolve the Muscovite state.

For Russian civilizational memory, the Time of Troubles represents
**collapse without conquest**—a warning case where internal coherence
failed before external domination succeeded.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. DYNASTIC EXTINCTION & LEGITIMACY VACUUM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The crisis begins with the end of the Rurikid line.

Key triggers:
• Death of Tsar Fyodor I (1598)
• No uncontested dynastic successor
• Erosion of sacral authority

Autocracy without lineage loses metaphysical grounding.

Legitimacy becomes improvisational.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. STATE FRAGILITY & SOCIAL SHOCK
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Structural stresses accelerate collapse.

Contributing factors:
• Economic dislocation
• Serfdom tightening
• Administrative exhaustion after Ivan IV
• Severe famine (1601–1603)

Material scarcity undermines obedience.

The state can no longer enforce order reliably.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PRETENDERS & FRACTURED SOVEREIGNTY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Authority fragments across claimants.

Key phenomena:
• False Dmitries
• Competing boyar factions
• Mercenary and Cossack forces
• Shifting loyalties

Sovereignty becomes negotiable and transactional.

The idea of a single ruler dissolves.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. FOREIGN INTERVENTION & OCCUPATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
External powers exploit internal collapse.

Key actors:
• Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth
• Swedish intervention

Moscow itself is occupied.

Foreign rule is possible not by conquest,
but by invitation and exhaustion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ORTHODOXY, POPULAR MOBILIZATION, & RECOVERY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Recovery emerges from below, not above.

Key dynamics:
• Orthodox Church as legitimacy anchor
• Popular militias (Minin and Pozharsky)
• Expulsion of foreign forces (1612)

Collective defense precedes dynastic restoration.

Faith substitutes for absent sovereignty.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. ROMANOV ACCESSION & RESTORATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The election of Mikhail Romanov (1613) ends the crisis.

Significance:
• Dynastic reset
• Restoration of sacral monarchy
• Preference for stability over reform

The lesson internalized:
→ Order precedes ambition.

Trauma conditions future governance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Dynastic legitimacy is structurally indispensable.
• Autocracy without coherence collapses inward.
• Foreign domination follows internal fracture.
• Orthodoxy can substitute for state authority temporarily.
• Russian political culture internalizes fear of chaos.
• Stability becomes a supreme political value.

Tension preserved without verdict.
