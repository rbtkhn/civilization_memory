────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Vladimir the Great as the **civilizational re-coder
of Rus’**: the ruler whose conversion decision and coercive enforcement
of Christianization fused dynastic rule to an Orthodox-sacral legitimacy
framework that outlived his personal reign.

Vladimir represents **legitimacy through faith as infrastructure**:
a shift from warlord/dynastic coercion (Rurik, Oleg) toward a
trans-personal justification for obedience that can survive ruler
quality and dynastic turbulence.

This file does NOT resolve:
• sincerity vs statecraft in Vladimir’s conversion
• competing baptism-location traditions
• whether Christianization reduced coercion or re-labeled it

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. VIOLENCE FIRST: ACCESSION BY FORCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vladimir’s political entry condition is violent consolidation. In the
Rus’ formation sequence, “moral re-coding” does not replace coercion; it
arrives after coercion has already secured the throne.

Analytical constraint:
• Coercion is the gate; ideology is the stabilizer.

Academic Reference engagement (registrational, non-doctrinal):
• Karamzin’s dynastic narrative tradition treats Vladimir as a founding
  moral sovereign; this should be read as memory construction rather
  than proof of moral discontinuity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE CONVERSION DECISION AS GOVERNANCE CHOICE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The conversion is remembered not merely as private faith, but as a
public, state-forming selection that binds Rus’ into a Christian
diplomatic and legitimacy system.

The Primary Chronicle tradition embeds the “choice of faith” logic as a
civilizational selection mechanism: legitimacy through alignment with a
prestige civilizational center (Byzantium), rather than legitimacy
through law (Yaroslav) or expansion alone (Oleg).

Academic Reference engagement:
• Solovyov’s longue-durée framing emphasizes this moment as a hinge from
  corridor-tribal power toward civilizational membership and continuity
  under a shared sacred grammar.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. IDOLS OVERTHROWN: CONVERSION ENFORCED, NOT MERELY ANNOUNCED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vladimir’s conversion is operationalized through visible demolition of
the prior sacral order. The act is coercive, theatrical, and designed
to make reversal socially costly.

Primary source anchor (embedded; ≥25 words):
> “When the Prince arrived at his capital, he directed that the idols
> should be overthrown, and that some should be cut to pieces and others
> burned with fire. He thus ordered that Perun should be bound to a
> horse’s tail and dragged down Borichev to the stream… After they had
> thus dragged the idol along, they cast it into the Dnipro. But Vladimir
> had given this injunction ‘If it halts anywhere, then push it out from
> the bank, until it goes over the falls. Then let it loose.’”
> — Primary Chronicle (PVL), Christianization narrative (compiled early 12th c.)

Analytical implication:
• Sacral sovereignty begins with coercive desecration of the old sacral
  system, not with persuasion.

Academic Reference engagement:
• Klyuchevsky’s state-formation lens is useful here: the state does not
  become moral; it becomes *legible* under a new sacred code.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MASS BAPTISM AS LEGITIMACY REGISTRATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Christianization is imposed as a public registration act: a mass ritual
that makes the polity’s identity visible, countable, and difficult to
disavow.

Primary source anchor (embedded; ≥25 words):
> “Thereafter Vladimir sent heralds throughout the whole city to proclaim
> that if any inhabitants, rich or poor, did not betake himself to the
> river, he would risk the Prince’s displeasure. When the people heard
> these words, they wept for joy, and exclaimed in their enthusiasm,
> ‘If this were not good, the Prince and his boyars would not have
> accepted it.’ On the morrow… a countless multitude assembled.”
> — Primary Chronicle (PVL), Christianization narrative (compiled early 12th c.)

Analytical implication:
• Faith becomes governance infrastructure precisely because it is
  enforced as collective participation, not optional creed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. SACRAL SOVEREIGNTY: TRANS-PERSONAL AUTHORITY IS BORN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The ruler’s legitimacy is reframed as participation in a sacred order.
This creates a trans-personal legitimacy layer that can outlive
individual competence and can be invoked during crisis.

Primary source anchor (embedded; ≥25 words):
> “Vladimir, rejoicing that he and his subjects now knew God himself,
> looked up to heaven and said, ‘Oh God, who has created heaven and
> earth, look down, I beseech thee, on this thy new people, and grant
> them, oh Lord, to know thee as the true God… Confirm in them the true
> and inalterable faith, and aid me, oh Lord, against the hostile
> adversary… that, hoping in thee and in thy might, I may overcome his
> malice.’”
> — Primary Chronicle (PVL), Christianization narrative (compiled early 12th c.)

Academic Reference engagement:
• Lieven’s empire/legitimacy framing (applied retroactively as a
  comparative lens) helps clarify why this matters: sacred legitimacy can
  stabilize authority across territorial scale and elite contestation
  even when institutional enforcement remains thin.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. MORAL REORDERING AS STATE-CAPACITY SUBSTITUTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Post-conversion memory emphasizes charity, church-building, and moral
norms. In structural terms, this functions as a substitute for absent
bureaucratic enforcement: moral obligation and clerical networks extend
rule beyond the prince’s immediate retinue.

Constraint:
• Moral codes can widen compliance margins, but they do not create
  automatic succession stability.

Academic Reference engagement:
• Solovyov and Klyuchevsky both treat Christianization as a continuity
  technology: it standardizes identity and binds elites to a shared
  symbolic order, without necessarily creating modern institutions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. SUCCESSION AFTER VLADIMIR: SACRALITY DOES NOT SOLVE FRAGMENTATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vladimir’s sacral re-coding does not eliminate dynastic conflict. The
civilizational pivot is real, but it does not automatically generate
succession mechanisms.

Failure mode:
• sacred authority outlives dynastic competence
• fragmentation returns because enforcement remains personal

This sets the stage for later tension:
• Kievan pluralism vs Muscovite consolidation
• sacred legitimacy vs administrative coercion

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. COMPARATIVE CIVILIZATIONAL ROLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vladimir’s function resembles other “civilizational re-coders” who bind
authority to a prestige sacred system:

• Constantine (Rome) — imperial legitimacy fused to Christianity
• Charlemagne (Germania) — political order fused to Latin Christianity
• Qin Shi Huang (China) — unification fused to a standardized state code
  (non-sacral, but similarly trans-personal)

Structural divergence:
• Rus’ gains sacred grammar before robust administrative enforcement,
  creating a long-lived legitimacy layer that can mask institutional
  thinness.
