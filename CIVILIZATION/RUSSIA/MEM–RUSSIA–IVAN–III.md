I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Ivan III as the **founder of sovereign Muscovy**:
the ruler who ended Mongol suzerainty, annihilated rival Rus’ centers,
and transformed a tributary principality into an autonomous,
centralizing state.

For Russian civilizational memory, Ivan III represents **sovereignty
achieved through consolidation**, not rebellion or liberation myth.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. INHERITING THE MONGOL SYSTEM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ivan III does not overthrow Horde logic; he absorbs it.

Context:
• Muscovy as tribute collector for the Golden Horde
• Familiarity with census, taxation, coercion
• Political culture trained in delegated authority

Transition logic:
→ Stop paying tribute, keep the machinery.

Sovereignty emerges through institutional takeover.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. DESTRUCTION OF RUS’ PLURALISM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ivan III dismantles competing centers of authority.

Key actions:
• Annexation of Novgorod (1478)
• Subjugation of Tver (1485)
• Suppression of boyar autonomy

Plural Rus’ is replaced by singular Muscovy.

Unity is enforced, not negotiated.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. THE END OF THE TATAR YOKE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Stand on the Ugra River (1480) marks symbolic rupture.

Characteristics:
• No decisive battle
• Mutual withdrawal
• End of tribute obligations

Liberation memory emphasizes:
• Patience
• Attrition
• Quiet assertion of independence

Muscovy does not revolt—it **outlasts**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. AUTOCRATIC DOCTRINE FORMATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ivan III hardens princely authority into doctrine.

Mechanisms:
• Reduction of aristocratic privilege
• Expansion of service landholding
• Centralization of law and court

The ruler becomes supreme arbiter.

Autocracy crystallizes as survival logic.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. SACRAL & IMPERIAL SYMBOLISM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ivan III reframes Muscovy’s identity.

Key symbols:
• Marriage to Sophia Palaiologina
• Adoption of Byzantine double-headed eagle
• Emergence of Moscow-as-Third-Rome logic

Imperial imagination precedes empire.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LEGAL & INSTITUTIONAL FOUNDATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
State authority becomes codified.

Key developments:
• Sudebnik of 1497
• Curtailment of peasant mobility
• Formalized judicial hierarchy

Law serves central power.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Sovereignty is claimed by absorbing, not destroying, prior systems.
• Russian unity emerges through annihilation of alternatives.
• Autocracy forms before empire.
• Patience and endurance substitute for rebellion.
• Ivan III creates the Muscovite template all successors inherit.

Tension preserved without verdict.
