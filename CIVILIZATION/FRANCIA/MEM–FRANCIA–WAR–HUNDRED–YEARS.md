────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Hundred Years’ War as Francia’s prolonged
legitimacy crisis and subsequent reconstitution: a civilizational
near-failure in which dynastic authority collapsed, territorial integrity
fractured, and survival required a re-forging of moral, sacral, and
collective legitimacy.

For Francia, the war is not remembered as continuity, but as recovery
through reinvention.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT & ORIGIN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war originates in overlapping failures of legitimacy.

Context:
• Capetian extinction and Valois succession insecurity
• Competing claims to the French crown
• Weak central authority
• Fragmented territorial loyalties

Dynastic legality, not national identity, defines the opening phase.

Francia enters the conflict internally fragile.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. SYSTEMIC DISINTEGRATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Early phases expose state weakness.

Conditions:
• Defeats at Crécy and Poitiers
• Capture of the king
• Noble fragmentation
• Mercenary devastation of countryside

Authority collapses downward.

The crown survives symbolically, not functionally.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. OCCUPATION, FRACTURE, AND HUMILIATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Anglian presence becomes normalized.

Dynamics:
• Territorial loss in the north and west
• Burgundian alignment against the crown
• Treaty-based disinheritance narratives

Francia exists as a contested legitimacy space, not a sovereign unit.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. SACRAL–POPULAR LEGITIMACY TURN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Recovery does not begin administratively.

Catalysts:
• Joan of Arc’s sacral intervention
• Re-coronation symbolism at Reims
• Fusion of crown, faith, and people

Legitimacy is re-moralized, not procedurally repaired.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CENTRALIZATION THROUGH WAR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Late-phase recovery hardens institutions.

Outcomes:
• Standing army emergence
• Tax extraction normalized
• Noble autonomy curtailed

War becomes the instrument of state formation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. TERMINATION & RECONSTITUTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war ends without treaty closure.

Result:
• Anglia expelled from continental core
• Valois legitimacy stabilized
• Territorial integrity largely restored

Victory is existential, not imperial.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. LONG-TERM CIVILIZATIONAL CONSEQUENCES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Enduring effects:
• Francia internalizes the lesson of near-collapse
• Centralized authority justified by survival
• Universalism later grafted onto recovered sovereignty

Francia learns to reset regimes to preserve moral authority.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Legitimacy collapse precedes territorial collapse.
• Francia survives by reinventing authority, not conserving form.
• Sacral legitimacy can reconstitute failed dynastic systems.
• Centralization is justified retrospectively by survival.
• The Hundred Years’ War is Francia’s first legitimacy resurrection.

Tension preserved without verdict.
