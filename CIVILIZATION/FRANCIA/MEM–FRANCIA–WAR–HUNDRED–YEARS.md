I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Hundred Years’ War as **Francia’s prolonged
legitimacy collapse and subsequent reconstitution**: a civilizational
near-failure in which dynastic authority fractured, territorial integrity
disintegrated, and survival required the reinvention of moral, sacral,
and collective legitimacy.

For Francia, the war is not continuity but **recovery through rupture**.
It is the first sustained episode in which Francia learns that authority
may fail catastrophically and yet be rebuilt through symbolic and moral
reassertion rather than procedural continuity.

This file records how legitimacy was lost and re-forged, not whether the
conflict was avoidable or just.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT & DYNASTIC ORIGINS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war originates in overlapping failures of dynastic legitimacy.

Structural conditions:
• Extinction of the senior Capetian line
• Valois succession insecurity
• Competing Anglian claims to the French crown
• Weak fiscal and administrative centralization
• Fragmented noble loyalties

At the outset, dynastic legality—not national identity—defines the
conflict’s grammar.

As Johannes Fried notes, Francia enters the war as a **juridical
construct without consolidated sovereignty**, vulnerable to external
claims precisely because legitimacy is still personal rather than
institutional (*The Middle Ages*).

Francia enters the war internally fragile.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. SYSTEMIC DISINTEGRATION & EARLY COLLAPSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Early phases expose the crown’s incapacity.

Indicators of collapse:
• Catastrophic defeats at Crécy (1346) and Poitiers (1356)
• Capture of King John II
• Fiscal breakdown and urban revolt
• Mercenary devastation (*écorcheurs*)
• Loss of elite confidence

Jean Froissart records the psychological collapse of authority:

> “The realm of France was so discomfited that there was neither order,
> counsel, nor governance, and every man did what seemed good in his
> own eyes.”
> — Froissart, *Chroniques*

Authority persists symbolically but disintegrates operationally.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. OCCUPATION, FRACTURE, & LEGITIMACY HUMILIATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Anglian power becomes normalized within French space.

Dynamics include:
• Territorial loss in the north and west
• Burgundian alignment against the Valois crown
• Treaty of Troyes (1420) disinheriting the Dauphin
• Dual-monarchy legitimacy narratives

Peter Wilson emphasizes that by this stage Francia functions less as a
state than as a **contested legitimacy arena**, where authority exists
only where it can be ritually and militarily defended (*Europe’s
Tragedy*).

Francia survives as a name, not a system.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. SACRAL–POPULAR LEGITIMACY TURN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Recovery does not begin administratively or militarily.

Catalysts:
• Joan of Arc’s sacral intervention
• Re-coronation at Reims (1429)
• Fusion of crown, faith, and popular belief
• Moral reversal preceding material recovery

Joan frames legitimacy as divinely sanctioned restoration:

> “I was sent to bring the Dauphin to his coronation, and to restore
> the kingdom to its rightful lord.”
> — Joan of Arc, trial testimony

Justine Firnhaber-Baker notes that this moment represents not nationalism
but **emergency moral mobilization**, where belief compensates for
institutional absence (*French History: A Very Short Introduction*).

Legitimacy is re-moralized before it is re-institutionalized.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CENTRALIZATION THROUGH WAR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Late-phase recovery hardens institutions through necessity.

Structural shifts:
• Emergence of a standing royal army
• Regularized taxation (taille)
• Decline of private noble warfare
• Expansion of crown-directed administration

War becomes the instrument of state formation rather than aristocratic
competition.

Will Durant observes that Francia learns here a durable lesson: survival
requires **central authority justified by collective memory of
near-destruction** (*The Story of Civilization*, Vol. VII).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. TERMINATION WITHOUT CLOSURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war ends without comprehensive treaty settlement.

Outcomes:
• Expulsion of Anglia from the continental core (1453)
• Valois legitimacy stabilized
• Territorial integrity largely restored
• Institutional recovery incomplete but irreversible

Victory is existential, not imperial.

The crown survives because it has become necessary, not because it is
uncontested.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. LONG-TERM CIVILIZATIONAL CONSEQUENCES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Enduring effects include:
• Internalization of near-collapse as formative memory
• Justification of centralization through survival
• Fusion of sacral symbolism with political authority
• Precedent for legitimacy reset after catastrophic failure

Henry Kissinger identifies this period as the foundation of France’s
later insistence that **authority must never again be allowed to
fragment**, even at the cost of pluralism (*Diplomacy*).

Universalism is later grafted onto recovered sovereignty.

X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Legitimacy collapse precedes territorial collapse.
• Moral authority can resurrect failed dynastic systems.
• Sacral symbolism can precede institutional recovery.
• Centralization is justified retrospectively by survival.
• The Hundred Years’ War is Francia’s first legitimacy resurrection.

No verdict imposed.
