────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Hundred Years’ War as Anglia’s continental
overreach and corrective withdrawal: a long conflict that demonstrated
the limits of dynastic conquest abroad and accelerated Anglia’s shift
toward insular consolidation and maritime orientation.

For Anglia, the war represents learning through loss, not collapse.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT & ENTRY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Anglia enters the war with dynastic ambition.

Context:
• Plantagenet continental inheritance
• Claim to the French crown
• Military confidence
• Feudal mobilization capacity

The war begins as a dynastic expansion project.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. EARLY OPERATIONAL SUCCESS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Initial phases favor Anglia.

Conditions:
• Tactical superiority
• Decisive battlefield victories
• Naval mobility
• Professionalized forces

Success reinforces continental aspiration.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL LIMITS EMERGE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Victory does not convert into control.

Constraints:
• Distance from core legitimacy base
• Fiscal strain
• Reliance on mercenary systems
• Inability to absorb Francia culturally or administratively

Territory proves expensive to hold.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. SHIFT FROM CONQUEST TO MANAGEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Anglia adapts incrementally.

Responses:
• Acceptance of intermittent stalemate
• Focus on defensible holdings
• Increased domestic political bargaining

The war becomes managed persistence, not decisive conquest.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. WITHDRAWAL WITHOUT SYSTEMIC FAILURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Loss accelerates reorientation.

Outcomes:
• Gradual abandonment of continental claims
• No dynastic extinction
• Institutional continuity preserved

Defeat does not rupture Anglia’s core systems.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. INSULAR CONSOLIDATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Post-war effects redirect Anglia.

Shifts:
• Reduced continental entanglement
• Parliamentary finance evolution
• Naval orientation strengthened

Failure produces strategic clarity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. LONG-TERM CIVILIZATIONAL CONSEQUENCES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Enduring effects:
• Anglia abandons continental empire logic
• Maritime future prioritized
• Replacement without rupture normalized

The war teaches Anglia what not to pursue.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Dynastic conquest abroad strains procedural systems.
• Loss can strengthen civilizations that replace ambition early.
• Withdrawal preserves institutional continuity.
• Maritime orientation emerges from continental failure.
• The Hundred Years’ War is Anglia’s first strategic exit.

Tension preserved without verdict.
