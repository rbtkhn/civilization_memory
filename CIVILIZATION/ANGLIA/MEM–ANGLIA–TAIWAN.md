────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Taiwan as an Anglia-facing geopolitical interface:
not a civilizational core, but a forward-positioned strategic node used
to shape, delay, and condition outcomes in the Western Pacific.

From the Anglia perspective, Taiwan is not primarily a nation-state or
identity project. It is a geopolitical instrument—an externalized
pressure point applied against the China civilization core through
deterrence, alliance signaling, and legitimacy framing.

This memory runs in direct parallel to MEM–CHINA–TAIWAN, but is written
from Anglia’s maritime, alliance-centric, narrative-driven strategic
logic under late-system constraints.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. POSTWAR ORIGIN: TAIWAN AS STRATEGIC RESIDUE (1945–1950)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After 1945, Taiwan enters Anglia planning space as unresolved residue of
the Chinese Civil War rather than as a designed objective.

Key characteristics:
• Transfer from Japan creates unresolved sovereignty ambiguity
• Kuomintang retreat converts Taiwan into an anti-Communist redoubt
• Early Anglia assessments treat Taiwan as expendable absent wider war

The Korean War produces a decisive reclassification:
→ Taiwan shifts from peripheral residue to forward defensive asset

This shift is strategic, not moral.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. COLD WAR FUNCTION: FIXED FORWARD PLATFORM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
During the Cold War, Anglia logic conceptualizes Taiwan as:

• A fixed platform for regional power projection
• A barrier node within the First Island Chain
• A credibility anchor for containment architecture
• A pressure lever on Beijing without direct mainland engagement

Core logic:
→ Taiwan is defended not for itself, but for what its loss would signal
  about Anglia resolve, alliance durability, and deterrence reliability

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. LEGITIMACY MANAGEMENT & STRATEGIC AMBIGUITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Anglia sustains Taiwan through calibrated ambiguity rather than
formalization.

Key mechanisms:
• Deliberate non-clarification of sovereignty status
• Security guarantees without treaty finality
• Arms provision without recognition escalation
• Narrative framing emphasizing “status quo” preservation

Function:
→ Delay resolution while preserving maximum maneuver space

Ambiguity is not indecision.
It is a governance tool.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. POST–COLD WAR REFRAMING (1991–2016)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
With the Soviet collapse, Taiwan’s function subtly shifts.

New overlays:
• Democracy-symbol framing replaces pure containment logic
• Taiwan becomes a values proxy within Anglia narrative systems
• Economic integration with China coexists with security hedging

Contradiction:
→ Taiwan is rhetorically elevated while strategically frozen

This phase introduces latent instability between narrative expansion
and strategic restraint.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LATE-SYSTEM ESCALATION DYNAMICS (2016–PRESENT)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In the late-system phase, Taiwan becomes a high-voltage signaling node.

Features:
• Increased political signaling without formal commitment
• Arms transfers optimized for denial, not victory
• Alliance discipline messaging directed at regional partners
• Media–narrative amplification exceeding military posture

Structural constraint:
→ Anglia seeks deterrence without entrapment
→ China seeks reunification without premature war

Taiwan sits inside this unresolved tension.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. STRATEGIC LIMITS & FAILURE MODES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
From the Anglia perspective, Taiwan presents hard limits:

• Geographic proximity favors China escalation dominance
• Alliance participation is uncertain under kinetic stress
• Narrative escalation risks commitment traps
• Time favors the continental power, not the maritime one

Thus:
→ Taiwan is a delay mechanism, not a permanent solution

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CORE DOCTRINAL EXTRACTIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Strategic Interface Doctrine
   Taiwan functions as a pressure interface, not a sovereign endpoint.

2. Ambiguity as Deterrence
   Unresolved status preserves flexibility and delays forced choice.

3. Credibility Signaling Law
   Taiwan’s value lies in what its defense signals to others, not in
   intrinsic territorial importance.

4. Late-System Escalation Risk
   Narrative inflation outpaces material commitment capacity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. SYSTEMIC ROLE VIS-À-VIS CHINA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For Anglia:
→ Taiwan is leverage, delay, and signal

For China:
→ Taiwan is unfinished civilizational integration

Mismatch:
→ Anglia seeks indefinite postponement
→ China seeks eventual closure

This asymmetry defines the risk structure.
