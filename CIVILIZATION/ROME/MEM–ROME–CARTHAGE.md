────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Carthage as **Rome’s lost mirror**: a sophisticated,
commercial, maritime civilization whose destruction defined Rome’s path
to uncontested Mediterranean supremacy.

Carthage represents **a viable alternative imperial model**—
one Rome could not absorb, only erase.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. PHOENICIAN ORIGINS & MARITIME LOGIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Carthage emerges from Phoenician colonization.

Core traits:
• Trade-network expansion
• Naval dominance
• Coastal city-state replication
• Economic extraction over territorial rule

Power flows from routes, not land.

This creates resilience—
but weakens interior integration.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. POLITICAL–MILITARY ARCHITECTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Carthage governs through oligarchic-commercial elites.

Characteristics:
• Merchant aristocracy
• Mercenary armies
• Limited civic militarization
• Wealth substituting for mass loyalty

This system excels in commerce,
but strains under existential war.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. HANNIBAL AS SYSTEMIC OUTLIER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Hannibal Barca transcends Carthaginian norms.

Traits:
• Strategic genius
• Personal loyalty networks
• Long-range expeditionary warfare

Hannibal’s success exposes a paradox:
Carthage can produce brilliance,
but cannot structurally support it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL MISALIGNMENT WITH TOTAL WAR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Carthage is unsuited to annihilatory conflict.

Limitations:
• Mercenary unreliability
• Political hesitation
• Inadequate mass mobilization
• Inability to absorb prolonged loss

Against Rome’s citizen-army model,
Carthage fights asymmetrically—but incompletely.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. DESTRUCTION & HISTORICAL SILENCING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Carthage’s fall (146 BCAD) is absolute.

Consequences:
• City razed
• Population killed or enslaved
• Language and archives erased
• Narrative monopolized by Rome

Carthage survives largely through hostile sources.

Its absence is part of Rome’s story.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. AFTERLIFE & COUNTERFACTUAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Carthage endures as possibility, not legacy.

Questions it leaves behind:
• Could commerce rival conquest?
• Could empire exist without annihilation?
• Was Rome’s path inevitable?

Rome answers by erasure.
History remains uncertain.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Carthage represents the road not taken.
• Trade empires lack tools for total war.
• Mercenary systems fracture under pressure.
• Rome defines itself by destroying its mirror.
• The victor controls memory as well as territory.

Tension preserved without verdict.
