I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Corsica as **Rome’s difficult island**: a western
Mediterranean possession whose strategic position is clear but whose
terrain, social structure, and resistance limit full integration.

For Rome, Corsica is a **buffer without depth**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC ACQUISITION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome acquires Corsica following the First Punic War.

Purpose:
• Secure Tyrrhenian Sea lanes
• Shield Italy’s western approaches
• Deny rival naval usage

Corsica’s acquisition is preventive, not aspirational.

Rome wants denial, not development.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. TERRAIN & GOVERNANCE RESISTANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Corsica resists Roman ordering.

Challenges:
• Mountainous interior
• Fragmented tribal structures
• Limited urbanization
• Persistent unrest

Roman authority is coastal and episodic.

The interior remains opaque.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ROMAN ADMINISTRATIVE APPROACH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Governance emphasizes:
• Coastal control
• Limited extraction
• Punitive expeditions over assimilation
• Law applied unevenly

Corsica is ruled **from the edges inward**, never fully absorbed.

Rome tolerates incompleteness.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. STRATEGIC FUNCTION OVER SOCIAL TRANSFORMATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Despite difficulty, Corsica remains valuable.

Roles:
• Naval buffer
• Anti-piracy staging
• Western Mediterranean surveillance

Corsica protects Italy
without becoming Italian.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LATE EMPIRE & SUCCESSIVE CONTROL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Corsica persists as a possession through imperial decline.

Patterns:
• Retained by necessity
• Administratively light
• Strategically continuous

The island’s value endures
even as its governance remains shallow.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CIVILIZATIONAL SIGNIFICANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Corsica illustrates Rome’s limits.

Lessons:
• Not all spaces reward integration.
• Terrain can nullify administrative ambition.
• Empire can function with incomplete control.
• Strategic buffers need not be civilized.

Corsica is Rome’s lesson in restraint.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Control does not equal transformation.
• Peripheral resistance can be tolerated.
• Buffers stabilize cores.
• Rome governs outcomes, not interiors.
• Empire survives by accepting imperfection.

Tension preserved without verdict.
