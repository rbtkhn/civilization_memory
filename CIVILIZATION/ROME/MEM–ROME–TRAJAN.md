────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Trajan as **Rome’s apex expansionary emperor**:
the ruler who extended Roman territory to its maximum extent and embodied
the classical ideal of conquest-based legitimacy.

Trajan represents **success at the edge of sustainability**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT: CONFIDENCE AFTER STABILITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trajan inherits a secure empire.

Preconditions:
• Stable succession
• Disciplined legions
• Consolidated finances
• High elite confidence

Expansion appears safe.
The system has not yet felt strain.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. DACIAN WARS & RESOURCE LOGIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trajan’s Dacian campaigns exemplify rational conquest.

Outcomes:
• Decisive victory
• Territorial annexation
• Massive gold inflows
• Frontier straightening

Conquest funds governance.
Victory reinforces legitimacy.

This is **expansion that pays for itself**—temporarily.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PARTHIAN CAMPAIGNS & OVERREACH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Eastern expansion reveals limits.

Challenges:
• Long supply lines
• Unstable local legitimacy
• Revolts in rear areas
• Environmental unfamiliarity

Although victorious on paper,
the gains prove fragile.

Trajan captures territory he cannot integrate.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CONQUEST AS LEGITIMACY ENGINE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trajan’s authority rests on action.

Traits:
• Military charisma
• Public benefaction
• Monumental commemoration (Trajan’s Column)

The emperor embodies Roman virtue through victory.

But legitimacy becomes **performance-dependent**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. SUCCESSION & IMPLICIT CRITIQUE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Hadrian’s reversal is revealing.

Immediate actions:
• Withdrawal from Mesopotamia
• Frontier consolidation
• Strategic pause

This is not rejection of Trajan—
but recognition of structural cost.

Expansion was brilliant.
It was not repeatable.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CIVILIZATIONAL ROLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trajan becomes the Roman benchmark.

Later memory:
• “Best of emperors”
• Idealized conqueror
• Moral exemplar

Yet Rome quietly learns:
permanent expansion destabilizes permanence.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Expansion magnifies replacement burden.
• Resource windfalls mask structural cost.
• Legitimacy tied to conquest is volatile.
• Success can exceed sustainability.
• Peak moments define limits as much as glory.

Tension preserved without verdict.
