────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Sardinia as **Rome’s western stabilizer**: an island
that combines buffer-zone function with extractive utility, occupying a
middle position between Sicily’s centrality and Corsica’s resistance.

For Rome, Sardinia is **useful, not beloved**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ACQUISITION & STRATEGIC ROLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome acquires Sardinia after the First Punic War.

Strategic aims:
• Secure western Mediterranean lanes
• Protect Italy’s maritime flank
• Prevent Punic resurgence

Sardinia and Corsica are administratively paired,
but strategically unequal.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. RESOURCE EXTRACTION & ECONOMIC ROLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Unlike Corsica, Sardinia yields material value.

Resources:
• Grain
• Minerals
• Timber

Sardinia contributes to Roman logistics,
though never at Sicily’s scale.

Extraction coexists with unrest.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. GOVERNANCE & CONTROL PATTERNS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Roman authority is uneven.

Characteristics:
• Coastal cities integrated
• Interior resistance persistent
• Periodic revolts suppressed
• Heavy taxation breeds instability

Sardinia is governed firmly,
but never comfortably.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. STRATEGIC ENDURANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Despite unrest, Sardinia remains indispensable.

Functions:
• Western maritime buffer
• Secondary supply node
• Anti-piracy platform

Rome accepts cost
to preserve geometry.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LATE EMPIRE & CONTINUITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sardinia endures through imperial transitions.

Patterns:
• Continued strategic relevance
• Administrative simplification
• Retention by successor powers

Geography outlasts governance complexity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CIVILIZATIONAL SIGNIFICANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sardinia exemplifies **managed difficulty**.

Lessons:
• Some provinces cost more than they give.
• Strategic geometry can justify extraction.
• Resistance does not negate value.
• Empire balances gain against friction.

Sardinia is Rome’s reluctant asset.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Utility can outweigh harmony.
• Resource islands stabilize maritime systems.
• Control persists despite unrest.
• Sardinia bridges Sicily and Corsica in function.
• Empire survives by tolerating strain.

Tension preserved without verdict.
