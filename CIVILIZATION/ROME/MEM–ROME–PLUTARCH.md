────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Plutarch as **Rome’s moral comparator**: the Greek
philosopher-biographer who interpreted Roman power through ethical
parallelism, pairing Roman lives with Greek exemplars to test whether
imperial success corresponded to moral greatness.

Plutarch does not ask why Rome conquered.
He asks whether Rome *deserved* to.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. POSITION AS CIVILIZATIONAL INTERMEDIARY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plutarch writes as neither Roman official nor subject in revolt.

Context:
• Greek intellectual under Roman imperial peace
• Priest at Delphi
• Educated in Platonic philosophy
• Beneficiary of Roman order without political dependence

He inhabits a **civilizational middle space**:
• Greek moral tradition
• Roman political reality

This allows judgment without rebellion
and admiration without submission.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PARALLEL LIVES AS ANALYTICAL METHOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plutarch’s defining innovation is *comparison*.

Method:
• Pair one Greek life with one Roman life
• Emphasize character, decision, and virtue
• De-emphasize chronology and institutional detail
• Conclude with explicit moral comparison (*synkrisis*)

History becomes **ethical laboratory**, not causal science.

Power is evaluated through character under pressure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. MORAL CAUSATION OVER STRUCTURAL CAUSATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plutarch privileges ethical agency.

He states explicitly:
> “I am not writing histories, but lives; and the most glorious exploits
> do not always furnish us with the clearest discoveries of virtue or
> vice.”  
> — *Life of Alexander*

This contrasts with:
• **MEM–ROME–POLYBIUS** (institutional mechanics)
• **MEM–ROME–GIBBON** (structural causality)
• **MEM–ROME–TACITUS** (experiential compression)

For Plutarch:
Virtue precedes structure.
Character precedes outcome.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ROMAN FIGURES AS MORAL TEST CASES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plutarch’s Roman subjects include:
• Caesar
• Pompey
• Brutus
• Cicero
• Antony

These lives are not treated as inevitabilities.

Instead:
• Caesar is brilliance without restraint
• Brutus is virtue without efficacy
• Antony is passion without discipline
• Cicero is eloquence without power

This aligns directly with:
• **MEM–ROME–CAESAR**
• **MEM–ROME–CICERO**
• **MEM–ROME–ANTONY** (implicit)
• **MEM–ROME–AUGUSTUS** (by contrast)

Rome’s crisis is rendered as *ethical misalignment*.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. EMPIRE AS MORAL STRESS TEST
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plutarch does not condemn empire outright.

Instead, he treats empire as:
• Amplifier of virtue
• Accelerator of vice
• Revealer of limits

Absolute power does not corrupt equally.
It **exposes**.

This anticipates:
• **MEM–ROME–SUETONIUS** (personality leakage)
• **Doctrine: Decline as Loss of Speakability** (frozen)

Plutarch shows *who fails before silence sets in*.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LIMITS OF PLUTARCHAN ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What Plutarch omits is deliberate.

Absent:
• Logistics
• Economics
• Administrative capacity
• Military replacement systems

Thus:
• He cannot explain Rome’s rise
• He cannot explain Rome’s endurance
• He can explain Rome’s *moral memory*

Plutarch explains **how Rome is judged**, not how it functions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plutarch encodes Rome into the **Greek ethical canon**.

Functions:
• Makes Roman power legible to Greek philosophy
• Preserves Greek moral authority under Roman rule
• Converts conquest into comparative evaluation

Rome wins politically.
Greece judges morally.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Comparison is a civilizational weapon.
• Empire invites moral auditing.
• Character determines how power is remembered.
• Structural success does not equal ethical success.
• Rome inherits Greek judgment even as it conquers Greece.

Tension preserved without verdict.
