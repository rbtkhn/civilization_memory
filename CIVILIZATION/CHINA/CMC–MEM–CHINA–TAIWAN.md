────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Taiwan as a **maritime frontier–island interface** of
Chinese civilization: a space where **distance, ocean barriers, migration,
and regime discontinuity** have repeatedly prevented stable political
closure while intensifying symbolic and strategic significance.

Taiwan is not a civilizational core, but a **liminal node**—defined by
contestation, projection, and unresolved sovereignty rather than continuous
administration.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC & MARITIME MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Taiwan is separated from the mainland by the Taiwan Strait (台湾海峡),
a shallow but weather-volatile maritime corridor.

Core geographic features:
• Island terrain with mountainous interior  
• Typhoon exposure and seismic instability  
• Natural maritime barrier to sustained control  
• Proximity without continuity  

Strategic axiom:
> “隔海而治，难以久控。”
(Governance across the sea is difficult to sustain.)

Water imposes discontinuity even at short distance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PRE-MODERN INTERFACE & MIGRATION MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Before modern state incorporation, Taiwan functioned as a **frontier
migration zone**, not an integrated province.

Key features:
• Indigenous Austronesian populations  
• Seasonal and permanent Han settlement from Fujian/Guangdong  
• Weak imperial penetration  
• Localized, clan-based governance  

Qing assessment was cautious:
> “台湾者，海外荒服也。”
(Taiwan is an overseas, lightly governed land.)

Incorporation followed migration, not policy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. IMPERIAL INCORPORATION & LIMITS (QING)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Qing annexed Taiwan in 1683 after defeating Zheng regime remnants.

Characteristics of Qing rule:
• Reluctant incorporation  
• Limited administrative investment  
• Persistent rebellion and unrest  
• Perception as peripheral liability  

Imperial maxim:
> “得之不喜，失之不忧。”
(Gain brings no joy; loss brings no sorrow.)

Taiwan was governed defensively, not embraced.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. COLONIAL DISCONTINUITY (JAPAN)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Japanese rule (1895–1945) marked the **first sustained modern state
penetration** of Taiwan.

Key transformations:
• Infrastructure construction  
• Centralized policing  
• Economic integration  
• Cultural reorientation  

Institutional imprint endured beyond regime change:
• Modern transport and public health systems  
• Bureaucratic rationalization  
• Developmental governance templates  

Colonial logic was extractive but effective:
> “殖民以治。”
(Colonialism governs through administration.)

This period introduced **non-Chinese modernity** into Taiwan’s institutional
memory.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. POST-1945 REGIME TRANSFER & FRACTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After 1945, Taiwan underwent abrupt regime replacement rather than gradual
reintegration.

Key dynamics:
• ROC relocation after 1949  
• Martial law and security state  
• Demographic and elite replacement  
• Cold War entrenchment  

Political rupture hardened identity divergence:
> “政权未定，认同先裂。”
(When sovereignty is unsettled, identity fractures first.)

Continuity was administrative, not civilizational.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. MODERN STRATEGIC & POLITICAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In the contemporary era, Taiwan functions as:
• Maritime forward edge of the Chinese civilizational sphere  
• Strategic node in great-power competition  
• Symbolic legitimacy stressor  

Modern governance emphasizes:
• De facto autonomy  
• External security reliance  
• Identity differentiation  

Time functions as strategic asset:
> “问题不在一时解决，而在持续管控。”
(The issue is not immediate resolution, but continuous management.)

Prolonged ambiguity preserves equilibrium when balances hold.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. EXTERNAL INTERFACE & POWER PROJECTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Taiwan occupies a critical position in the **first island chain**.

Strategic roles:
• Barrier to continental maritime projection  
• Platform for surveillance and containment  
• Springboard for external power projection into coastal approaches  

Dual geostrategic function:
• Denies uncontested breakout
• Enables reciprocal pressure on the littoral

Geostrategic maxim:
> “控岛即控海。”
(Who controls the island controls the sea.)

Taiwan’s value exceeds its material scale.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Maritime distance produces persistent political ambiguity.
• Taiwan is governed as a symbol more than a territory.
• External guarantees convert sovereignty disputes into credibility tests.
• Islands magnify strategic meaning beyond intrinsic value.
• Unresolved status can remain stable longer than forced resolution.

Tension is managed, not eliminated.
