────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Mao Zedong as the revolutionary reconstitutor of Chinese
state power: the figure who destroyed the old order, rebuilt the state through
mass mobilization, and permanently altered the grammar of authority in modern
China.

Mao represents foundational rupture—not continuity—yet his actions produced
the institutional conditions that later enabled durable centralization.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGINS & REVOLUTIONARY FORMATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mao emerged from Hunan peasant society, not the urban elite or military
academies.

Key formative traits:
• Rural social base  
• Anti-elitist ideology  
• Suspicion of bureaucratic hierarchy  
• Belief in perpetual struggle  

Revolutionary premise:
> “枪杆子里面出政权。”
(Political power grows out of the barrel of a gun.)

Legitimacy derived from victory and mobilization, not institutions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. REVOLUTIONARY STRATEGY & POWER SEIZURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mao’s path to power diverged from orthodox Marxism.

Strategic innovations:
• Peasant-centered revolution  
• Protracted people’s war  
• Rural encirclement of cities  
• Fusion of ideology and coercion  

Operational logic:
> “农村包围城市。”
(The countryside surrounds the cities.)

This strategy resolved China’s warlord–fragmentation problem by force.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STATE RECONSTITUTION & MOBILIZATIONAL RULE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After 1949, Mao rebuilt the Chinese state through mass campaigns rather than
bureaucratic consolidation.

Core mechanisms:
• Party supremacy over all institutions  
• Continuous political campaigns  
• Class struggle as governance tool  
• Fusion of ideology and administration  

Governance axiom:
> “不断革命。”
(Continuous revolution.)

The state penetrated society through mobilization, not stability.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. IDEOLOGY, LEGITIMACY, & MASS POLITICS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Maoist legitimacy rested on ideological purity and revolutionary authenticity.

Key features:
• Cultivation of personal authority  
• Mass participation as proof of legitimacy  
• Moral absolutism  
• Suppression of dissent as counter-revolution  

Symbolic formulation:
> “人民，只有人民，才是创造世界历史的动力。”
(The people, and the people alone, are the makers of world history.)

Legitimacy required constant reaffirmation through struggle.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CATASTROPHE AS GOVERNANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Maoist rule produced systemic shocks.

Major episodes:
• Great Leap Forward (1958–61)  
• Mass famine  
• Cultural Revolution (1966–76)  

Governance logic remained ideological:
> “破字当头。”
(Destruction comes first.)

Catastrophe functioned as both failure and internalized lesson for successors.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. MILITARY, SECURITY, & SOVEREIGNTY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mao ensured absolute Party control over coercive force.

Key outcomes:
• PLA loyalty to Party, not state  
• Suppression of regional militarism  
• Restoration of territorial integrity  

Security maxim:
> “党指挥枪。”
(The Party commands the gun.)

This principle remains foundational.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. INTERNATIONAL POSITION & STRATEGIC POSTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mao repositioned China externally through defiance and autonomy.

Key dynamics:
• Break with Soviet Union  
• Revolutionary export rhetoric  
• Strategic isolation followed by opening  

Strategic realism emerged late:
> “以斗争求生存。”
(Survival through struggle.)

External pressure reinforced internal cohesion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. FAILURE, DEATH, & ABSORPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mao’s death ended the era of permanent mobilization.

Absorption dynamics:
• Ideology partially abandoned  
• Party supremacy preserved  
• Catastrophe internalized as cautionary memory  

Retrospective judgment was controlled:
> “功过七三。”
(Achievements seventy percent, errors thirty.)

The system survived by learning from excess.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Revolutionary regimes consolidate power before they stabilize it.  
• Mobilization can build states but destroys societies if prolonged.  
• Personal authority accelerates rupture but resists institutionalization.  
• Mao solved fragmentation but created volatility as legacy.  
• Later durability depended on selective reversal without repudiation.

Tension preserved without verdict.
