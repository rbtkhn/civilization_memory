────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Jin dynasty as a **successor frontier empire** that
**adapted and intensified the dual-administration model** pioneered by the
Liao, while progressively deepening engagement with Chinese institutions.

The Jin represents **partial fusion under pressure**: a regime that conquered
northern China, ruled it directly, and increasingly depended on Chinese
bureaucratic capacity—without fully resolving legitimacy tensions.

Contradictions are retained without forced synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. DYNASTIC OVERVIEW (MEMORY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Founded by the Jurchen (女真) under Wanyan Aguda (完颜阿骨打), the Jin overthrew
the Liao and then defeated the Northern Song, seizing control of North China.

Core characteristics:
• Non-Han conquering elite  
• Direct rule over agrarian Chinese heartland  
• Militarized aristocracy  
• Accelerating Sinicization under constraint  

Jin self-understanding combined conquest with pragmatism:
> “因地制宜，以治为先。”
(Govern according to conditions, placing order first.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ADMINISTRATIVE & INSTITUTIONAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Jin inherited and modified the **dual-administration logic**, but with
greater emphasis on centralized Chinese-style governance.

Core features:
• Jurchen military elite dominance  
• Restoration and expansion of civil bureaucracy  
• Use of Chinese law codes and fiscal systems  
• Gradual revival of examinations (科举)  

Administrative tension was persistent:
> “兵权在上，政务在下。”
(Military authority above, civil administration below.)

Fusion increased capacity but diluted elite cohesion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ECONOMIC & FISCAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jin economic legitimacy depended on control of North China’s agrarian base.

Key elements:
• Direct land taxation  
• Grain requisition for military support  
• Urban centers inherited from Song territory  
• Heavy fiscal burden during prolonged wars  

Fiscal stress was openly acknowledged:
> “赋重则民困。”
(When taxes are heavy, the people suffer.)

Extraction sustained the state but weakened consent.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LEGITIMACY & MANDATE MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jin legitimacy rested on:
• Military conquest  
• Performance governance  
• Selective Confucian framing  

Unlike the Liao, the Jin increasingly claimed orthodox rulership:
> “得中原者，承天命。”
(He who gains the Central Plain receives Heaven’s mandate.)

This claim intensified legitimacy expectations—and scrutiny.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MILITARY POWER & STRATEGIC OVERREACH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jin military power initially rested on elite Jurchen cavalry.

Characteristics:
• Highly effective early campaigns  
• Dependence on ethnic military aristocracy  
• Difficulty sustaining manpower depth  

Strategic imbalance emerged:
> “兵强而源竭。”
(The army was strong, but its sources were exhausted.)

Prolonged conflict with the Southern Song and Mongols proved decisive.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CULTURAL & CIVILIZATIONAL POSITION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jin culture moved steadily toward Chinese norms.

Key features:
• Adoption of Chinese court rituals  
• Confucian education for officials  
• Decline of Jurchen language and customs among elites  

Cultural anxiety accompanied adaptation:
> “俗变而本弱。”
(Customs changed, and the foundations weakened.)

Identity erosion paralleled political vulnerability.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. EXTERNAL PRESSURES & SHOCKS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The decisive external pressure was the rise of the Mongols (蒙古).

Compounding stresses:
• Multi-front warfare  
• Fiscal exhaustion  
• Elite fragmentation  

Late Jin observers noted inevitability:
> “内耗未止，外患已至。”
(Internal exhaustion had not ended when external threats arrived.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. FAILURE, FALL, & ABSORPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Jin collapsed in 1234 under Mongol assault.

Absorption dynamics:
• Mongols inherited Jin administrative experience  
• Northern Chinese institutions passed into Yuan governance  
• Jin served as an intermediate bridge between Song and Yuan  

Retrospective judgment:
> “金亡而北地尽入胡元。”
(The Jin fell, and the northern lands fully entered Mongol rule.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Partial Sinicization increases capacity but raises legitimacy costs.
• Frontier elites lose cohesion as bureaucracy expands.
• Conquest states ruling core agrarian zones face accelerated pressures.
• Jin bridges Liao duality and Yuan imperial integration.

Tension is preserved without verdict.
