────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Mengzi as the **moral escalator of Confucian statecraft**:
the thinker who transformed Confucianism from a doctrine of ritual repair
into a **normative theory of conditional sovereignty, popular welfare, and
ethical limits on power**.

Mengzi does not dissolve hierarchy or advocate egalitarian rule.
He **raises the moral price of authority** while preserving the civilizational
architecture of elite governance.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT & POSITIONING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mengzi wrote amid intensified interstate competition, mass warfare, and
state extraction during the Warring States period.

Structural conditions:
• Expanding conscription and taxation  
• Decline of ritual restraint alone  
• Large-scale civilian suffering  
• Rising administrative coercion  

Mengzi accepts power politics as unavoidable but insists:
• Moral legitimacy is not ornamental  
• Popular survival is a condition of rule  
• Tyranny voids authority  

Self-conscious stance:
> “吾善养吾浩然之气。”
(I am good at nurturing my vast, unyielding moral force.)

This is **ethical assertiveness**, not pacifism.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CORE LEGITIMACY AXIOM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mengzi redefines sovereignty as **conditional upon performance**.

Foundational hierarchy:
> “民为贵，社稷次之，君为轻。”
(The people are most important; the state comes next; the ruler is least.)

Implications:
• Rulers exist for the people, not the reverse  
• Legitimacy derives from welfare and moral care  
• Prolonged suffering signals mandate failure  

This constitutes the **strongest moral constraint** in classical Chinese
political thought.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. RIGHT OF OVERTHROW (REDEFINED)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mengzi explicitly legitimizes regime removal under tyranny, but with care.

Canonical formulation:
> “闻诛一夫纣矣，未闻弑君也。”
(I have heard of executing the villain Zhou, not of murdering a ruler.)

Key features:
• Kingship is de-sacralized  
• Rebellion is moralized, not anarchic  
• Civilizational continuity is preserved  

Important limitation:
This principle functions **primarily ex post**—as moral justification after
victory—rather than as a procedural mechanism for orderly regime change.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. HUMAN NATURE & GOVERNANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mengzi’s anthropology is optimistic but demanding.

Core claim:
> “人皆有不忍人之心。”
(All people possess a heart that cannot bear others’ suffering.)

Governance implications:
• Benevolence produces more durable loyalty than fear  
• Moral cultivation strengthens stability  
• Excessive coercion corrodes allegiance  

This is **strategic optimism**, not naïve idealism.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ECONOMICS, WELFARE, & STATE CAPACITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mengzi links legitimacy directly to material conditions.

Key assertions:
• Stable livelihoods precede moral order  
• Excess extraction destroys loyalty  
• Famine indicts rulers, not Heaven  

Operational logic:
> “无恒产而有恒心者，惟士为能。”
(Only scholars can remain virtuous without secure livelihood.)

Structural tension:
In fiscally exhausted or war-ridden states, attempts to apply Mengzian
welfare ethics without administrative capacity often accelerated collapse
(e.g., late Han, late Ming reform efforts).

Mengzi supplies **legitimacy criteria**, not fiscal solutions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. COMPARATIVE POSITIONING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Within Chinese traditions:
• Against Legalism: rejects fear as durable glue  
• Beyond Kongzi: adds explicit political conditionality  
• Against Daoism: insists on active benevolence  

Comparative contrasts:
• Unlike Locke/Rousseau: no individual consent or social contract  
• Unlike Hobbes: rejects fear as primary bond, yet recognizes chaos risk  
• Parallel to natural-law resistance traditions, without individualism  

Mengzi is **Confucianism’s conscience**, not its administrator.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. PRACTICAL UTILITY & FAILURE MODES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mengzian doctrine is structurally double-edged.

Patterns of use:
• Most potent during legitimacy crises and transitions  
• Muted during consolidation phases  
• Invoked after collapse to justify moral judgment  

Risks:
• Premature moral delegitimation accelerates disorder  
• Ethical absolutism destabilizes absent successor structures  
• Welfare rhetoric without capacity breeds disillusion  

Mengzi offers **judgment without institutions**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CIVILIZATIONAL ABSORPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Later regimes absorbed Mengzi selectively.

Dynamics:
• Cited to justify rebellion after success  
• Suppressed during high-confidence consolidation  
• Revived during erosion of authority  

Historical echoes:
• Qin–Han transition  
• Ming founding rhetoric  
• Taiping moral language  

Mengzi endures as a **latent moral ceiling** on power.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Authority collapses when survival is neglected.  
• Moral legitimacy accelerates collapse when power is already hollow.  
• Welfare is a strategic, not charitable, imperative.  
• Ethical resistance doctrines lack stabilizing machinery.  
• States rediscover Mengzi when confidence erodes.  
• Legitimacy contests increasingly center on material delivery claims.

Tension preserved without verdict.
