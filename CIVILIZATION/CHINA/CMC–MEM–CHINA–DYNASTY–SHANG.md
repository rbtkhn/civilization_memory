────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Shang dynasty as the **sacral–ritual foundation layer**
of Chinese civilization: the era in which **kingship, divination, ancestor
worship, and bronze-based power** were fused into an early state system.

Shang China establishes the **theological grammar of authority** that Zhou
later moralized and systematized.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. DYNASTIC OVERVIEW (MEMORY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Shang dynasty ruled from a series of capitals centered in the Yellow River
region, culminating at Yin (殷). Power rested on **ritual legitimacy, kinship,
and military force**, not moral abstraction.

Core characteristics:
• Sacral kingship  
• Ancestor mediation  
• Bronze-based military economy  
• Divination-driven governance  

Royal authority was framed as spiritual mediation:
> “王事唯卜。”
(The king’s affairs rely upon divination.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. LEGITIMACY & SACRAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shang legitimacy derived from ancestral favor rather than Heaven as an
abstract moral force.

Key features:
• Ancestors as active political agents  
• King as chief diviner  
• No concept of conditional mandate  

Oracle bone inscriptions reveal the logic:
> “贞：祖乙其受年？”
(Divined: Will Ancestor Yi grant a good harvest?)

Legitimacy was transactional, not ethical.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. POLITICAL & INSTITUTIONAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shang governance was personal and kin-based.

Key elements:
• Royal clan dominance  
• Peripheral vassals bound by ritual and force  
• No impersonal bureaucracy  

Authority was centralized in the person of the king:
> “令出于王。”
(Commands issue from the king.)

Institutional continuity depended on lineage strength.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ECONOMIC & MATERIAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Shang economy supported elite ritual and warfare.

Key features:
• Bronze casting as strategic core  
• Tribute extraction  
• Agricultural surplus management  
• Forced labor and warfare captives  

Material power was inseparable from ritual:
> “以鼎示威。”
(Using ritual vessels to display authority.)

Control of bronze production equaled military dominance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MILITARY & SECURITY MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shang military power was closely tied to ritual authority.

Characteristics:
• Chariot warfare  
• Elite warrior aristocracy  
• Campaigns framed as ancestral mandate  

Military success was sacralized:
> “伐方受命。”
(The campaign proceeds by received command.)

Defeat implied spiritual withdrawal.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CULTURAL & INTELLECTUAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shang culture was dominated by ritual practice rather than philosophy.

Key features:
• Oracle bone script (甲骨文)  
• Sacrificial rites  
• Limited abstraction  

Written language served divination:
> “卜辞刻于骨。”
(Divination charges carved on bone.)

Later literati retroactively interpreted Shang through Zhou lenses.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. INTERNAL STRESS & COLLAPSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shang decline reflected erosion of ritual authority and military defeat.

Key pressures:
• Over-extension  
• Vassal defection  
• Zhou rebellion  

Zhou justification emphasized Shang excess:
> “商罪贯盈。”
(The crimes of Shang were filled to the brim.)

Collapse was explained morally after the fact.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. FAILURE, ABSORPTION, & LEGACY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shang collapse did not erase its civilizational imprint.

Absorption dynamics:
• Zhou retained ancestor worship  
• Ritual vocabulary persisted  
• Bronze culture informed early Zhou authority  

Later synthesis:
> “殷礼未亡。”
(The rites of Yin did not perish.)

Sacral authority was transformed, not eliminated.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Authority initially rests on sacral mediation, not morality.  
• Ritual precedes law and administration.  
• Shang theology sets preconditions for Mandate of Heaven.  
• Later dynasties inherit ritual logic even as they moralize it.

Tensions preserved without verdict.
