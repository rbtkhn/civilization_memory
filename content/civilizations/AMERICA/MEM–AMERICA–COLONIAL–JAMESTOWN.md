I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Jamestown as **America's encoding of the first
permanent English settlement in North America**: Virginia Company
foundation (1607), survival through the Starving Time, tobacco as
export staple, and the House of Burgesses (1619)—first representative
assembly in Anglian America—establishing precedent for colonial
self-governance within imperial frame.

Jamestown does not found the republic.
It **founds the template for colonial assembly and commercial
settlement** that later feeds into procedural secession.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FOUNDATION & SURVIVAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Virginia Company of London; three ships; landing May 1607 on James
River; fort and settlement. Early years: disease, conflict with
Powhatan polity, supply failure. Starving Time (1609–1610)—near
collapse; reinforcement and order under Lord De La Warr. John Rolfe's
tobacco (c. 1614)—export staple; economic viability and expansion.
First Africans brought (1619); indentured and later enslaved labour
embedded in colonial economy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. HOUSE OF BURGESSES (1619)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
July 1619: first meeting of the General Assembly—governor, council,
and elected burgesses from each settlement. First representative
legislative body in English North America. Precedent: **colonial
consent and local voice within imperial authority**; not independence
but institutionalisation of grievance and law-making that later
supports "no taxation without representation" and Continental
Congress logic.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jamestown encodes **permanent Anglian foothold and assembly
precedent**—commercial settlement, representative institution,
and the continuity of colonial Virginia into the Revolution
(Washington, Jefferson, Virginia Declaration of Rights).

Lessons: Colonial assembly as precedent for procedural legitimacy;
tobacco and staple economy as driver of expansion; Virginia as
core of later Southern and founding political culture.
