I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Richard Nixon as **America's realpolitik architect
and procedural rupture figure**: the president who opened to China,
pursued détente with the Soviet Union, and extricated the U.S. from
Vietnam ("peace with honor")—and who then violated procedure through
Watergate, prompting resignation and succession under constraint.

Nixon does not abandon the republic.
He **extends** its diplomacy (opening, détente) and **tests** its
procedure (covert action, cover-up, impeachment path). Procedure
reasserts through resignation and oversight; the office survives
the man.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGIN & STYLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nixon assumes office (1969) amid Vietnam and domestic fracture.

Context:
• Vice President under Eisenhower; narrow loss to Kennedy (1960);
  comeback in 1968
• "Silent Majority"—legitimacy claim through law-and-order and
  middle-American resentment of elites and protest
• Realpolitik as governing frame: balance of power, triangular
  diplomacy, interest over ideology

Authority is framed as **strategic competence and majority mandate**—
the president who could manage great-power relations and speak for
"forgotten" Americans. Procedure is instrumental; when it blocks
political survival, it is circumvented.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. OPENING TO CHINA & DÉTENTE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nixon reorients Cold War geometry.

Sequence:
• Secret diplomacy (Kissinger trip, 1971); Nixon visit to Beijing
  (1972)—normalisation with PRC while maintaining Taiwan ties
• Strategic logic: triangulation against USSR; China as counterweight;
  extra-hemispheric balance as tool of hegemony maintenance
• Détente with Soviet Union—SALT I, summits; nuclear and economic
  engagement alongside continued competition

The opening is **realpolitik applied**: ideology subordinated to
balance; procedure (summit, treaty, recognition) used to reshape
alliance and threat. Hemisphere perimeter (Monroe) is not abandoned
but supplemented by great-power management beyond the hemisphere.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. VIETNAM: EXIT & "PEACE WITH HONOR"
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nixon inherits Kennedy–Johnson war and seeks exit.

Elements:
• Vietnamisation—transfer of combat burden to South Vietnamese;
  U.S. withdrawal while maintaining support
• Bombing escalation (Cambodia, Linebacker); force as lever for
  negotiation
• Paris Accords (1973)—ceasefire, U.S. withdrawal; South Vietnam
  left to eventual collapse (1975)
• "Peace with honor"—legitimacy framed as dignified exit, not
  victory; procedure (treaty, withdrawal) used to end direct
  commitment

Settlement is **procedural exit**: agreement, timetable, handover.
The outcome (later fall of Saigon) is encoded separately; Nixon's
memory is the mechanism of exit—negotiation and withdrawal—not
the long-term survival of the ally.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. WATERGATE & PROCEDURAL RUPTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Break-in, cover-up, and resignation define Nixon's domestic legacy.

Sequence:
• Watergate break-in (1972)—covert action against political opponents;
  authority in persons and clandestine operation, not process
• Cover-up—obstruction, denial; executive privilege asserted against
  Congress and courts
• Impeachment process; tapes; Supreme Court orders compliance;
  Nixon resigns (August 1974) before full impeachment vote
• Gerald Ford succeeds; pardon follows; procedure holds—succession
  without coup or refusal; oversight norms reinforced

Watergate encodes **procedure violated then reasserted**: authority
exercised outside process; investigation and judicial constraint;
resignation as institutional outcome. The republic absorbs the
rupture; the only presidential resignation to date becomes precedent
for constraint on "imperial presidency."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nixon encodes **realpolitik extension and procedural rupture with
corrective reassertion**.

Lessons:
• Extra-hemispheric balance (opening to China, détente) can serve
  hegemony maintenance; ideology can be subordinated to triangular
  diplomacy when the structural incentive is clear.
• Exit from prolonged war can be framed as "peace with honor"—treaty,
  withdrawal, transfer—even when the eventual outcome is ally defeat;
  procedure (agreement) bounds the commitment.
• Procedure violated (covert action, cover-up) triggers investigation,
  judicial order, and resignation; legitimacy reasserts through
  oversight and succession, not through preservation of the incumbent.
• Resignation is precedent: the office continues; the person exits;
  constraint on executive abuse is reinforced by institutional
  response.

Nixon is America's **realpolitik president and resignation precedent**:
diplomatic extension (China, USSR) and domestic rupture (Watergate),
with procedure surviving both.
