I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Plymouth as **America's encoding of covenant
and compact as founding precedent**: Pilgrim settlement (1620),
Mayflower Compact—consent and self-government among settlers
before landing—and the narrative of survival, Wampanoag encounter,
and Thanksgiving, embedding **compact as legitimacy** in
civilizational memory.

Plymouth does not found the republic.
It **founds the covenant template**—people bound by agreement—
that later echoes in colonial and federal constitutional thought.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FOUNDATION & COMPACT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mayflower (1620); Pilgrims and others; landing at Plymouth (Massachusetts).
Before disembarking, adult males sign the Mayflower Compact: agreement
to form a civil body politic and submit to laws for the general good.
First written framework for self-government in English North America
based on **consent and compact**, not royal charter alone. First
winter: hardship and death; Wampanoag assistance; harvest and
commemoration (Thanksgiving narrative).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PLYMOUTH COLONY & ABSORPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plymouth Colony remains smaller and distinct from Massachusetts Bay
(Boston, 1630). Eventually absorbed into Massachusetts (1691). Memory
outlasts polity: **compact as precedent** for voluntary political
association; covenant theology and civil covenant in New England
thought; Pilgrim narrative as American origin story alongside
Jamestown.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plymouth encodes **compact and consent as legitimacy**—people
constitute polity by agreement; precedent for later constitutional
and federal compact theory (including contested use in secession
arguments).

Lessons: Covenant and compact as American political grammar;
Pilgrim and Thanksgiving as enduring narrative; New England and
Massachusetts as second pole of colonial founding (with Virginia).
