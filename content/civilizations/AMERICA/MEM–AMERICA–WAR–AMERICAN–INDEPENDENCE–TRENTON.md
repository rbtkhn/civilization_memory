I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Trenton as America's encoding of the
morale-reversing strike after the loss of New York: Washington
crossing of the Delaware (Christmas night 1776), surprise attack
on Hessian garrison at Trenton (Dec 26), and follow-up at Princeton;
the ten crucial days that restore Continental Army credibility and
public hope. Trenton does not win the war; it preserves the
possibility of winning.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT AND SEQUENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After British capture of New York and retreat across New Jersey,
Continental Army depleted; enlistments expiring. Washington gambles
on counterstroke: cross Delaware Dec 25, attack Trenton dawn Dec 26;
Hessians surprised and captured. Follow-up Princeton Jan 3 1777;
British reaction blunted. Army withdraws to winter quarters; morale
and recruitment improve.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trenton encodes symbolic victory as strategic necessity; force
preservation and morale inseparable; Washington willingness to
risk a strike; precedent for asymmetric war and endurance.
