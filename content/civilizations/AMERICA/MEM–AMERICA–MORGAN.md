I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves J. Pierpont Morgan as **America's archetype
of private financial authority**: the figure who reorganised
railroads, assembled U.S. Steel (1901), and acted as **lender of
last resort** in the Panic of 1907—encoding concentration of
capital and the tension between private stabilisation and
public procedure (Federal Reserve follows as institutional
response).

Morgan does not break the republic.
He **stabilises** it in crisis (1907) and **concentrates** it in
peacetime (U.S. Steel, Northern Securities); procedure responds
with trust-busting and then the Fed.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGIN & STYLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Morgan rises through banking and rail reorganisation.

Context:
• House of Morgan; investment banking; rail reorganisations—
  restructuring bankrupt or overbuilt lines; authority from
  **capital and coordination**
• No elected office; legitimacy from **creditor and syndicate
  power**—the private actor who can impose order on chaotic
  markets
• Personal authority—direct, imperious; "Morganisation" as
  restructuring and concentration

Authority is framed as **financial sovereign**: the banker who
can rescue or restructure; the counterpart to Roosevelt's
public assertion (TR trust-busts Northern Securities—a Morgan
creation).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. U.S. STEEL, NORTHERN SECURITIES, CONCENTRATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Morgan assembles and is then constrained by procedure.

Elements:
• U.S. Steel (1901)—purchase of Carnegie Steel; creation of
  first billion-dollar corporation; vertical integration and
  scale
• Northern Securities—rail holding company; TR's trust-busting
  target; Supreme Court orders dissolution (1904)
• Concentration of capital in **one man's coordination**—Morgan
  as dealmaker and stabiliser; procedure (antitrust) then
  constrains the structure

The tycoon who **builds** (U.S. Steel) is the same who **is
broken** (Northern Securities); procedure absorbs and corrects.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PANIC OF 1907 & LENDER OF LAST RESORT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Morgan acts as private central bank.

Elements:
• Panic of 1907—bank runs, liquidity crisis; no Federal Reserve
  (created 1913, after Morgan's death)
• Morgan convenes bankers; commits capital; orchestrates
  private rescue; **private authority** substitutes for
  missing public institution
• Consequence: impetus for Federal Reserve (1913)—procedure
  **institutionalises** what Morgan did ad hoc; the republic
  creates the Fed so it need not depend on one man

Morgan encodes **private stabilisation then public replacement**—
the crisis that proves the need for a central bank.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Morgan encodes **financial concentration, private stabilisation,
and procedural response**.

Lessons:
• Private financial authority can stabilise crisis (1907) when
  no public institution exists; then procedure creates the
  institution (Fed) to replace the man.
• Concentration (U.S. Steel, Northern Securities) can be
  assembled by one actor and then broken or constrained by
  antitrust; procedure follows concentration.
• The tycoon as **lender of last resort** is both hero and
  problem—legitimacy of private power vs. legitimacy of
  public procedure.

Morgan is America's **financial sovereign**: U.S. Steel, 1907,
and the Fed's precursor.
