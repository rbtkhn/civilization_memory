I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Lyndon B. Johnson as **America's legislative
president and succession steward**: the figure who assumed power
after Kennedy's assassination (1963), secured passage of civil
rights and Great Society legislation through mastery of Congress,
and escalated the Vietnam War—encoding **procedure holding under
shock** (succession) and **ambition counteracting ambition** (his
use of the presidency to move a reluctant Congress) while also
encoding **war as domestic and strategic failure** that would
consume his legacy.

Johnson does not found a new order.
He **extends** the federal role (civil rights, Medicare, War on
Poverty) through legislative skill and political pressure, and
**inherits and deepens** the Vietnam commitment—procedure holds
for succession and for lawmaking; procedure is strained by war
and division.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. SUCCESSION & PASSAGE OF POWER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Johnson becomes president on the death of John F. Kennedy
(November 1963). The transition is **immediate and constitutional**—
oath on the plane at Love Field; "Let us continue." No rupture;
the office survives the man. Robert Caro, in *The Passage of Power*
(2012), on what power does to the man who holds it:

SOURCE — Caro, *The Passage of Power* (ARC-T-MODERN):
> "But although the cliche says that power always corrupts, what is seldom said ... is that power always reveals. When a man is climbing, trying to persuade others to give him power, concealment is necessary. ... But as a man obtains more power, camouflage becomes less necessary."
> — Robert A. Caro, *The Passage of Power* (The Years of Lyndon Johnson, vol. 4) (New York: Knopf, 2012) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

Johnson had been **powerless** as vice president—Caro documents his
humiliation in the Kennedy administration. With the presidency,
power **reveals**: the same man who had mastered the Senate now
uses the Oval Office and the memory of Kennedy to demand action.
Caro, citing the columnist James Reston, on the contrast between
the two presidents:

SOURCE — Caro, *The Passage of Power* (ARC-T-MODERN):
> "President Kennedy's eloquence was designed to make men think; President Johnson's hammer blows are designed to make men act."
> — Robert A. Caro, *The Passage of Power* (New York: Knopf, 2012), citing James Reston (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

**Procedure holds**: succession is seamless; the republic absorbs
leader loss again. Johnson's legitimacy is **derivative** (continuation
of Kennedy's mandate) and **instrumental** (use of that mandate to
break congressional deadlock).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. GREAT SOCIETY & CIVIL RIGHTS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Johnson drives through Congress the legislation that Kennedy had
proposed but not passed: Civil Rights Act (1964), Voting Rights Act
(1965), Medicare, Medicaid, War on Poverty, education. He treats
the presidency as a **lever**—"What the hell's the presidency for?"
when advised not to waste capital on civil rights. Caro on Johnson's
place in the statute books:

SOURCE — Caro, *The Passage of Power* (ARC-T-MODERN):
> "He was to become the lawmaker for the poor and the downtrodden and the oppressed. He was to be the bearer of at least a measure of social justice to those whom social justice had so long been denied. ... By the time Lyndon Johnson left office he had done a lot of writing in those books, had become, above all presidents save Lincoln, the codifier of compassion, the president who wrote mercy and justice in the statute books by which America was governed."
> — Robert A. Caro, *The Passage of Power* (New York: Knopf, 2012) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

**Ambition counteracting ambition** operates in reverse here: Johnson
uses **presidential** pressure to overcome **congressional** resistance—
Southern Democrats (e.g. Richard Russell) had blocked civil rights;
Johnson, who knew the Senate and its folkways, could not be beaten
the way Kennedy could. Russell is quoted by Caro: "We could have
beaten John Kennedy on civil rights, but not Lyndon Johnson."
Legitimacy of the **federal role** and of **equality before the law**
is written into statute; the president is the catalyst.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. VIETNAM — ESCALATION & LEGACY COST
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Johnson inherits the Kennedy commitment in Vietnam and **escalates**—
troop levels rise; bombing intensifies; the war becomes "Johnson's
war." Structural incentive: to avoid being seen as losing South
Vietnam; to preserve credibility (domino logic); to satisfy military
and Cold War consensus. The **domestic cost**—protest, division,
trust in government eroded—consumes his second term. He does not
run again (1968); procedure (election, succession) continues without
him. Nixon later inherits the war and seeks exit; Johnson is encoded
as the president who **deepened** the commitment, not the one who
ended it.

VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Johnson encodes **succession under shock**, **legislative presidency**,
and **war as legacy cost**.

Lessons:
• Procedure holds: vice president becomes president without rupture;
  oath, continuity, "Let us continue."
• The presidency can be used as **lever** to move Congress—Johnson's
  mastery of Senate folkways and willingness to spend political
  capital break deadlock on civil rights and social legislation.
• Power **reveals** (Caro): the same man, powerless as VP, deploys
  power fully as president—ambition counteracting ambition can
  be harnessed by the executive to overcome legislative resistance.
• War escalation can **consume** domestic achievement; Vietnam
  overshadows Great Society in memory; Johnson is contested—codifier
  of compassion and the president who deepened Vietnam.
