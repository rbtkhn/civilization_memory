I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Nathaniel Hawthorne as **America's chronicler
of the Puritan legacy**: the novelist and tale-teller who turned
New England's past—sin, guilt, the scarlet letter, the custom-house—
into the **moral and psychological** grammar of American fiction.
He encodes the weight of the past on the present and the
indelibility of moral transgression in a civilizational frame
that Emerson and Whitman often bypass.

Hawthorne does not celebrate the self or the republic.
He **burdens** both with the past—Puritan judgment, secret sin,
and the impossibility of full escape. His legitimacy is **moral
and historical**: the republic and the individual carry what
they have done and what their ancestors did.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. THE SCARLET LETTER AND THE CUSTOM-HOUSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
*The Scarlet Letter* (1850) is introduced by "The Custom-House,"
where Hawthorne frames himself as **editor** of a found tale—the
manuscript of Hester Prynne's story. The device ties the novel
to the author's own tenure in the Salem custom-house and to
the **authenticity** of the colonial past as recoverable but
mediated.

SOURCE — Hawthorne, "The Custom-House" (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD):
> "It is a little remarkable, that—though disinclined to talk overmuch of myself and my affairs at the fireside, and to my personal friends—an autobiographical impulse should twice in my life have taken possession of me, in addressing the public."
> — Nathaniel Hawthorne, "The Custom-House," introductory to *The Scarlet Letter* (Boston: Ticknor, Reed, and Fields, 1850) (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-PRIMARY].

The novel itself encodes **sin, shame, and endurance**: Hester's
letter, Dimmesdale's hidden guilt, Chillingworth's revenge. The
**Puritan** frame—public judgment, inner torment, no clean
escape—becomes American civilizational memory. Legitimacy is
**moral**: the past judges the present; the individual cannot
shed the consequences of act and inheritance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. YOUNG GOODMAN BROWN AND THE FALL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
"Young Goodman Brown" and other tales extend the theme: the
discovery of universal sin, the loss of innocence, the forest
as site of temptation and revelation. Hawthorne's America is
not Whitman's multitudes or Emerson's soul; it is **fallen**
and **historical**—the Puritan settlement as origin of a
moral grammar that persists. The **structural** fact: New
England's Calvinist past supplies the vocabulary of guilt and
judgment that transcendentalism and democratic celebration
qualify but do not erase.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CONCORD AND TENSION WITH EMERSON
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Hawthorne lived in Concord (Old Manse); he knew Emerson and
Thoreau. His fiction, however, **contradicts** Emersonian
optimism: there is no transparent soul in *The Scarlet Letter*;
there is concealment, punishment, and the long shadow of the
past. The tension between Hawthorne's guilt and Emerson's
self-reliance is **preserved** in American letters—two
readings of the individual and the past.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Hawthorne encodes **the Puritan legacy and the burden of the past**:
sin, guilt, judgment, and the impossibility of full moral escape.
He supplies the **moral and historical** counterweight to
transcendental and democratic affirmation—the republic and the
self as carriers of inherited and committed wrong.

Lessons:
• American literature carries a Puritan strand—sin, guilt,
  public and private judgment; Hawthorne is its chief 19th-c. voice.
• The past weighs on the present; the custom-house and the
  letter are symbols of mediation and indelibility.
• Contrast with Emerson (soul), Whitman (celebration), Poe
  (psychological gothic); Hawthorne is moral and theological
  in a distinct key.
