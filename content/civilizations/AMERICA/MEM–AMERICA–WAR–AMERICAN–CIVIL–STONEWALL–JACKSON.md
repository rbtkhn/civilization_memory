I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Thomas Jonathan "Stonewall" Jackson as **the
Confederacy's most effective tactical instrument**: the general whose
Valley Campaign (1862), Second Bull Run, Antietam, Fredericksburg,
and Chancellorsville exemplified rapid movement and shock—and whose
death (May 1863) removed Lee's principal strike arm and foreshadowed
Confederate inability to replace such leadership.

Jackson does not define Confederate strategy.
He **executes it to the limit of possibility**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ROLE & ORIGIN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jackson becomes the archetype of Confederate tactical brilliance and
irreplaceable loss.

Context:
• Virginia; West Point; Mexican–American War; VMI (Virginia Military
  Institute) instructor; devout, demanding, secretive
• **First Bull Run (July 1861)** — Brigadier Bee's sobriquet "Stonewall";
  brigade holds; name encodes immovable defence and later offensive
  shock
• **Army of Northern Virginia** — Lee's right arm; corps commander;
  Shenandoah and eastern theatre

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. VALLEY CAMPAIGN, SECOND BULL RUN, CHANCELLORSVILLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jackson's campaigns encode movement, concentration, and shock.

Elements:
• **Shenandoah Valley (1862)** — Outnumbered; rapid marches; defeat
  of Union forces in detail; diversion of Union strength from
  Richmond; template for "foot cavalry" and operational tempo
• **Second Bull Run (1862)** — March to Pope's rear; decisive
  Confederate victory; coordination with Longstreet under Lee
• **Antietam (1862)** — Defence of Confederate left; heavy cost
• **Fredericksburg (1862)** — Assault on Union right; tactical success
• **Chancellorsville (May 1863)** — Flank march and attack; crushing
  Union defeat; **friendly fire**—Jackson mortally wounded by
  Confederate pickets; death May 10, 1863

His loss deprives Lee of the commander best able to execute
high-risk flank and movement operations; Gettysburg (July 1863)
occurs without him.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jackson encodes **tactical excellence and irreplaceability**—Confederate
capacity for operational brilliance and the structural limit: one
death removes a capability the system could not regenerate.

Lessons:
• Confederate army could achieve local superiority through speed and
  shock; Valley and Chancellorsville as proof of concept.
• Loss of key commanders (Jackson 1863) compounds strategic
  exhaustion; no institutional depth to replace such figures.
• "Stonewall" as enduring symbol: steadfastness and aggression in
  one figure; memory outlives the Confederacy.
