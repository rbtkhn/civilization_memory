I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Andrew Jackson as **Anglia’s populist rupture
figure**: the leader who reintroduced mass emotion, personal authority,
and direct majoritarian pressure into an Anglian-derived procedural
system—testing how much institutional restraint could absorb before
distorting.

Jackson does not abandon Anglian structure.
He **pressurizes it from below**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGIN CONDITIONS & SOCIAL BASE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jackson emerges from frontier society.

Context:
• Weak institutional density
• High violence tolerance
• Personal honor culture
• Suspicion of distant elites
• Expanding suffrage among white males

Authority shifts from deference to identification.

Legitimacy becomes emotional and personal.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. POPULISM AS LEGITIMACY CLAIM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jackson reframes authority as embodiment.

Core posture:
• “The people” as moral source
• Direct electoral mandate emphasized
• Elites portrayed as corrupt or detached

Procedure is tolerated only when aligned with popular will.

Jefferson, in a letter to William C. Jarvis (1820), supplies the
ideological precursor—the people as sole safe depository of power—that
Jackson later weaponizes as populist legitimacy:

SOURCE — Jefferson, to William C. Jarvis (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD):
> "I know no safe depository of the ultimate powers of the society, but the people themselves: and if we think them not enlightened enough to exercise their control with a wholesome discretion, the remedy is, not to take it from them, but to inform their discretion by education. This is the true corrective of abuses of constitutional power."
> — Thomas Jefferson to William C. Jarvis, 28 Sept. 1820, in *The Papers of Thomas Jefferson*, Retirement Series (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-PRIMARY].

This departs from Anglian elite-mediated balance.

Tocqueville, visiting during Jackson's presidency (1831–1832), described
the democratic pressure that Jackson embodied—and the new kind of risk
it posed:

SOURCE — Tocqueville, *Democracy in America*, Vol. II (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD):
> "I think that the species of oppression by which democratic nations will be threatened will resemble nothing that has preceded it. I seek in vain for an expression that exactly reproduces the idea; the old words despotism and tyranny will not suit. It is a new thing."
> — Alexis de Tocqueville, *Democracy in America*, Vol. II (1840), ed. Mansfield and Winthrop (Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2000) or equivalent (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL].

V. THE BANK WAR & INSTITUTIONAL HOSTILITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jackson targets abstract authority.

Bank of the United States:
• Viewed as elite concentration
• Financial abstraction framed as tyranny
• Destroyed through executive action

Outcome:
• Short-term popular legitimacy
• Long-term financial instability
• Precedent for politicized economic control

Anglian financial restraint is weakened.

Mark Twain, in his Autobiography ("The Tennessee Land," written c. 1870),
on his father's ruin in the financial crash of 1834—the human cost of
the Bank War's aftershock:

SOURCE — Twain, Autobiography (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD):
> "Four years after the purchase came the great financial crash of '34, and in that storm my father's fortunes were wrecked. From being honored and envied as the most opulent citizen of Fentress County—for outside of his great landed possessions he was considered to be worth not less than three thousand five hundred dollars—he suddenly woke up and found himself reduced to less than one-fourth of that amount. He was a proud man, a silent, austere man, and not a person likely to abide among the scenes of his vanished grandeur and be the target for public commiseration."
> — Mark Twain, *The Autobiography of Mark Twain*, "The Tennessee Land" (written c. 1870), in *Chapters from My Autobiography* or standard ed. (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-PRIMARY].

Arthur M. Schlesinger Jr., in *The Age of Jackson* (1945), frames the
recurring cycle linking Jacksonian democracy to later liberal reform:

SOURCE — Schlesinger Jr., *The Age of Jackson* (ARC-T-MODERN):
> "In the past, when liberalism has resolved the [economic] crisis and restored tranquility, conservatism has recovered power by the laws of political gravity; then it makes a new botch of things, and liberalism again must take over in the name of the nation. But the object of liberalism has never been to destroy capitalism, as conservatism invariably claims — only to keep capitalists from destroying it."
> — Arthur M. Schlesinger Jr., *The Age of Jackson* (Boston: Little, Brown, 1945) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

V.A RETURN OF CENTRAL BANKING: FEDERAL RESERVE (1913)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jackson's destruction of the Second Bank did not end central banking in the United States. The **Federal Reserve** (1913) reintroduced a national monetary authority—under a new design, after a secret 1910 meeting on Jekyll Island, Georgia, at which bankers and Treasury officials drafted the framework that became the Federal Reserve Act.

G. Edward Griffin, in *The Creature from Jekyll Island: A Second Look at the Federal Reserve* (1994), frames the Fed as the **return** of the elite financial concentration Jackson had attacked—a "creature" born in private conference, not in open congressional debate. Griffin's thesis is interpretive and polemical; mainstream historiography emphasizes the Aldrich Commission and congressional passage. Either way, the structural point holds: central banking returned. Jackson won the Bank War; he did not win the long war over who controls the money supply.

SUMMARY — Griffin, *The Creature from Jekyll Island* (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-CRITICAL]:
> The men who met on Jekyll Island in 1910 represented the same kind of concentrated financial power that Jackson had denounced when he destroyed the Bank of the United States. They designed the Federal Reserve to serve private interests while appearing to serve the public. Jackson would have recognized the pattern—and would have opposed it.
> — G. Edward Griffin, *The Creature from Jekyll Island: A Second Look at the Federal Reserve* (Westlake Village, CA: American Media, 1994) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-CRITICAL]. Summary-only note: Open-web access to a verbatim text was not available at time of update.

Populist victory over the Bank did not prevent the later return of central-bank logic.
