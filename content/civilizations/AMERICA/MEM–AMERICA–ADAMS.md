I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves John Adams as **Anglia's legal-diplomatic
continuity figure** in the American founding: the founder who
articulated rebellion in Anglian legal and constitutional language,
defended the accused (Boston Massacre), negotiated recognition and
peace, and sustained procedural legitimacy through the first
presidential succession.

Adams does not invent American legitimacy.
He **translates** it—lawyer, diplomat, vice president, president—
within the "first great generation" of American leaders alongside
Washington and Jefferson.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

II. FIRST GREAT GENERATION & CHURCHILL VOL. 3
(PRIMARY + SECONDARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Winston Churchill, in *A History of the English-Speaking Peoples*, Vol. 3 (*The Age of Revolution* [1956–1958]), treats the American Revolution and the emergence of the "first great generation" of American leaders: Washington, Adams, and Jefferson—founders of the republic within the English-speaking peoples' story from Blenheim to Waterloo (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

SOURCE — Churchill, *The Age of Revolution* (Vol. 3) (ARC-T-MODERN):
> "Here Samuel Adams, fertile organiser of resistance and advocate of separation, saw that the struggle was now reaching a crucial stage."
> — Winston S. Churchill, *A History of the English-Speaking Peoples*, Vol. 3, *The Age of Revolution* (1956–1958), p. 147 (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

Adams completes the triad: Washington as military and symbolic restraint, Jefferson as ideological articulation, Adams as **legal and diplomatic executor**.

III. LAWYER & PROCEDURAL REBEL
(PRIMARY + SECONDARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Adams operates inside Anglian legal grammar from the start.

Context:
• Common law training
• Defense of British soldiers (Boston Massacre, 1770)—procedure over faction
• Continental Congress; independence as legal and diplomatic argument
• Drafting and advocacy for the Declaration (with Jefferson and others)

Jefferson, in his Autobiography, on the committee and his role in drafting
the Declaration:

SOURCE — Jefferson, Autobiography (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD):
> "The committee for drawing the declaration of Independence desired me to do it. It was accordingly done, and being approved by them, I reported it to the house on Friday the 28th of June when it was read and ordered to lie on the table."
> — Thomas Jefferson, Autobiography, in *The Works of Thomas Jefferson*, ed. Paul Leicester Ford, vol. 1 (New York: G. P. Putnam's Sons, 1904) (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-PRIMARY].

Resistance is framed as **rights and precedent**, not as rupture of form.
Adams embodies the paradox: rebellion in the language of the realm.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. DIPLOMAT & RECOGNITION
(PRIMARY + SECONDARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Adams secures external legitimacy and peace.

Roles:
• Commissioner to France; alliance and recognition
• Negotiator of the Treaty of Paris (1783)—codified exit from empire
• Minister to Britain (1785–1788)—post-war relationship
• Vice President (1789–1797); President (1797–1801)

First peaceful transfer of executive power (Adams to Jefferson, 1801)
encodes **procedure over person**: the republic survives the loss of
office without crisis.
