I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves George Washington as **Anglia's externalized
continuity figure**: the leader who carried Anglian procedural,
legal, and restraint-based civilizational grammar across the Atlantic
while severing imperial sovereignty.

Washington is rupture in allegiance, continuity in form.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGIN CONDITIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Washington emerges from Anglian colonial society.

Context:
• Common law tradition
• Representative assemblies
• Protestant moral order
• Imperial military experience

The American rebellion is fought by Anglian institutions
against Anglian authority.

Winston Churchill, in *A History of the English-Speaking Peoples*, Vol. 3 (*The Age of Revolution* [1956–1958]), treats Washington as part of the "first great generation" of American leaders—Washington, Adams, and Jefferson—who emerge from the American Revolution as founders of the republic within the English-speaking peoples' story (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

SOURCE — Churchill, *The Age of Revolution* (Vol. 3) (ARC-T-MODERN):
> "In this campaign a young Virginian officer named George Washington learnt his first military lessons."
> — Winston S. Churchill, *A History of the English-Speaking Peoples*, Vol. 3, *The Age of Revolution* (1956–1958), p. 118 (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. WAR LEADERSHIP AS RESTRAINT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Washington's command style mirrors Anglian logic.

Characteristics:
• Avoidance of decisive annihilation battles
• Preservation of force over glory
• Endurance prioritized over speed
• Deference to civilian authority

Victory is achieved by survival, not domination.

Ron Chernow, in *Washington: A Life* (2010), frames Washington's military
role as endurance and cohesion rather than battle-winning:

SOURCE — Chernow, *Washington: A Life* (ARC-T-MODERN):
> "His military triumphs had been neither frequent nor epic in scale. He had lost more battles than he had won, had botched several through strategic blunders, and had won at Yorktown only with the indispensable aid of the French Army and fleet. But he was a different kind of general fighting a different kind of war, and his military prowess cannot be judged by the usual scorecard of battles won and lost. His fortitude in keeping the impoverished Continental Army intact was a major historic accomplishment."
> — Ron Chernow, *Washington: A Life* (New York: Penguin, 2010) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. VOLUNTARY POWER SURRENDER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Washington's defining act is renunciation.

Actions:
• Resignation of military command
• Refusal of monarchy
• Acceptance of term limits

Authority is legitimized by relinquishment.

Jefferson, in a letter to Walter Jones (1814), on Washington's singular
destiny—war, council, and scrupulous obedience to law:

SOURCE — Jefferson, to Walter Jones (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD):
> "For his was the singular destiny and merit of leading the armies of his country successfully through an arduous war for the establishment of its independence, of conducting its councils through the birth of a government, new in its forms and principles, until it had settled down into a quiet and orderly train, and of scrupulously obeying the laws through the whole of his career, civil and military, of which the history of the world furnishes no other example."
> — Thomas Jefferson to Walter Jones, 2 Jan. 1814, in *The Papers of Thomas Jefferson*, Retirement Series, vol. 7, ed. J. Jefferson Looney (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2010) (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-PRIMARY].

Chernow on Washington's surrender of power:

SOURCE — Chernow, *Washington: A Life* (ARC-T-MODERN):
> "Again and again, the American people had entrusted him with power, secure in the knowledge that he would exercise it fairly and ably and surrender it when his term of office was up. He had shown that the president and commander-in-chief of a republic could possess a grandeur surpassing that of all the crowned heads of Europe."
> — Ron Chernow, *Washington: A Life* (New York: Penguin, 2010) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

This act transmits Anglia's deepest norm:
procedure over person.

Tocqueville, a half-century later, described the compound republic that
Washington's restraint had helped secure—sovereignty divided, results
visible in the states:

SOURCE — Tocqueville, *Democracy in America*, Vol. I (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD):
> "The sovereignty of the Union is an abstract thing which is connected with but few external objects. The sovereignty of the States is perceived by the senses, is understood without effort; every day it produces results which fall under every eye."
> — Alexis de Tocqueville, *Democracy in America*, Vol. I (1835), ed. Mansfield and Winthrop (Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2000) or equivalent (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-PRIMARY].

Franklin, at the same Convention, gave the same frame his consent
despite doubt:

SOURCE — Franklin, Constitutional Convention (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD):
> "I consent, Sir, to this Constitution because I expect no better, and because I am not sure, that it is not the best."
> — Benjamin Franklin, speech at the Constitutional Convention, 17 Sept 1787 (read by James Wilson), in *Records of the Federal Convention*, ed. Max Farrand (1911) (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-PRIMARY].
