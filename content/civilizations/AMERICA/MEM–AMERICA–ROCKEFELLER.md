I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves John D. Rockefeller as **America's archetype
of industrial trust**: the figure who built Standard Oil through
vertical integration, ruthless consolidation, and trust structure—
encoding **concentration of a strategic commodity (oil)** in
private hands—and who faced antitrust (Sherman Act, breakup 1911)
and later reframed legacy through philanthropy.

Rockefeller does not break the republic.
He **tests** it: procedure (antitrust, dissolution) responds to
concentration; legitimacy of capital vs. legitimacy of competition
and public interest.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGIN & STYLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rockefeller rises from Cleveland oil and refining.

Context:
• Refining and pipeline; elimination of rivals (price, rebate,
  acquisition); Standard Oil Trust (1882)—legal form for
  concentration
• Vertical integration—extraction, transport, refining, distribution;
  horizontal—dominance of U.S. refining
• No elected office; legitimacy from **efficiency and scale**—
  the trust as "natural" outcome of competition; critics frame
  it as monopoly

Authority is framed as **control of a strategic commodity**: oil
as industrial and later geopolitical necessity; private control
precedes state regulation and antitrust.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. STANDARD OIL & ANTITRUST
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The trust becomes the target of procedural response.

Elements:
• Standard Oil as dominant refiner; trust structure; state and
  federal suits
• Sherman Antitrust Act (1890); Northern Securities (TR); Standard
  Oil breakup (1911)—Supreme Court orders dissolution into
  separate companies
• Procedure **reasserts**: concentration is not illegal per se
  but "unreasonable" restraint of trade; breakup as remedy

Rockefeller encodes **trust and dissolution**—private empire
then procedural correction; the republic absorbs the tycoon
and then constrains the structure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PHILANTHROPY & LEGACY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rockefeller reframes legitimacy through giving.

Elements:
• Rockefeller Foundation; University of Chicago; medical research;
  education; global philanthropy
• Legacy as **benefactor** alongside **monopolist**—tension
  preserved; procedure (antitrust) and philanthropy (foundation)
  as separate tracks

The tycoon who was broken up is also the tycoon who endowed
institutions; civilizational memory holds both.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rockefeller encodes **industrial trust, antitrust response, and
philanthropic reframing**.

Lessons:
• Strategic commodities (oil) can be concentrated in private
  hands until procedure (antitrust) responds; dissolution
  as remedy, not confiscation.
• Trust structure tests the limits of permissible concentration;
  Supreme Court and Congress define the boundary.
• Philanthropy can coexist with antitrust; legitimacy reframed
  after the fact.

Rockefeller is America's **trust archetype**: Standard Oil,
breakup, and foundation.
