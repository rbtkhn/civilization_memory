I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Second Schleswig War as **Bismarck's first
orchestrated war**: the conflict that established the operational
template—diplomatic isolation, limited objectives, rapid termination—
that would be refined in 1866 and perfected in 1870.

The Dano-Prussian War is not a minor border conflict.
It is the **proof-of-concept** for Bismarckian warfare: demonstrating
that wars fought for limited, achievable objectives under diplomatic
cover could reshape European power without triggering coalition response.

For Germania, this war encodes the lesson that **alliance with a future
rival (Austria) can serve as temporary scaffolding for larger objectives**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. THE SCHLESWIG-HOLSTEIN QUESTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The duchies of Schleswig and Holstein constituted one of 19th-century
Europe's most complex dynastic-national tangles.

Structural conditions:
• Holstein: German Confederation member, majority German population
• Schleswig: outside Confederation, mixed Danish-German population
• Both duchies: ruled by Danish crown under personal union
• Legal status: disputed succession laws, conflicting claims

Lord Palmerston's famous quip captured the confusion:

> "Only three people have ever really understood the Schleswig-Holstein
> business—the Prince Consort, who is dead—a German professor, who has
> gone mad—and I, who have forgotten all about it."
> — Lord Palmerston (attributed)

The question was deliberately insoluble—which made it useful to Bismarck.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. DANISH PROVOCATION AND GERMAN RESPONSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Denmark triggered the crisis by attempting to integrate Schleswig
into the Danish constitutional system (March 1863 Constitution).

This violated the 1852 London Protocol, which had guaranteed the
duchies' special status. German nationalist opinion demanded intervention.

Bismarck's calculation:

> "The question is not whether the Danes are wrong—they manifestly are—
> but how their wrongdoing can be made to serve Prussian interests."
> — Otto von Bismarck, correspondence, 1863

Bismarck transformed a German national cause into a Prussian state
interest by insisting on joint action with Austria.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. AUSTRO-PRUSSIAN ALLIANCE: TEMPORARY SCAFFOLDING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The alliance with Austria was Bismarck's masterstroke of tactical cynicism.

Functions:
• Legitimized intervention as German Confederation action
• Prevented Austrian opposition to Prussian expansion
• Created future pretext for conflict (disputed spoils)
• Isolated Denmark diplomatically (no great power support)

Peter Wilson notes the strategic calculation:

> "Bismarck understood that Austria's partnership was a means, not an end.
> The joint victory would create the very dispute he needed to justify
> the next war."
> — Peter Wilson, *Heart of Europe* (2016)

Austria entered as ally; Austria would exit as victim.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MILITARY CAMPAIGN (FEBRUARY–OCTOBER 1864)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The campaign demonstrated Prussian military modernization.

Key operations:
• Storming of Düppel fortifications (April 18, 1864)
• Naval operations in the Baltic
• Occupation of Jutland peninsula
• Danish isolation without British or Russian support

Prussian advantages:
• Breech-loading needle gun (first major use)
• Superior artillery
• Railroad mobilization
• Professional officer corps

Denmark's defeat was swift and complete. An armistice led to the
Treaty of Vienna (October 30, 1864).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. TREATY OF VIENNA AND DISPUTED SPOILS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The peace settlement created the conditions for the next war.

Terms:
• Denmark ceded Schleswig, Holstein, and Lauenburg
• Joint Austro-Prussian administration established
• No clear division of authority

Bismarck had deliberately created an unworkable arrangement:

> "The duchies must be administered jointly because no permanent
> settlement is possible. What cannot be settled must eventually be
> fought over—and we shall fight when we are ready."
> — Otto von Bismarck (paraphrased from diplomatic correspondence)

The Convention of Gastein (1865) temporarily partitioned administration:
Prussia took Schleswig; Austria took Holstein. The arrangement was
inherently unstable—exactly as Bismarck intended.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. TEMPLATE ESTABLISHED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war established Bismarck's operational template:

Elements:
• Diplomatic isolation of target (Denmark had no allies)
• Legal pretext (Danish violation of 1852 Protocol)
• Limited military objective (duchies only)
• Alliance with future rival (Austria as temporary partner)
• Rapid campaign termination
• Settlement creating future casus belli

Henry Kissinger identifies the pattern:

> "The greatest diplomatic and political achievement by any leader in
> the last two centuries."
> — Henry Kissinger, *Diplomacy* (1994)

The Danish War was the first movement in this achievement.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For Germania, the Dano-Prussian War encodes critical lessons:

• Small wars can establish operational patterns for larger conflicts
• Temporary alliances with rivals can serve strategic purposes
• Unworkable settlements create predictable future crises
• National causes can be subordinated to state interests
• Diplomatic isolation precedes military action

The war demonstrates that **sequential statecraft requires patience**:
the Danish conflict was step one in a three-war sequence (1864, 1866, 1870).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. MEARSHEIMER STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

IX.A. POWER DISTRIBUTION
The war marginally increased Prussian power while creating shared
administration that guaranteed future conflict with Austria.

IX.B. ALLIANCE DYNAMICS
The Austro-Prussian alliance demonstrates that states can ally with
future adversaries when immediate interests align. Alliances are
instrumental, not permanent.

IX.C. BUCK-PASSING
Britain and Russia passed the buck on Danish defense. Neither wanted
war over Schleswig-Holstein. Diplomatic isolation enabled Prussian
action.

IX.D. LIMITED WAR
The campaign exemplifies limited war: specific territorial objective,
rapid termination, no ideological escalation. Denmark was defeated,
not destroyed.
