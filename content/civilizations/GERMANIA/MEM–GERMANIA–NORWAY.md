I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Norway** as Germania's northern periphery:
a maritime-mountain space adjacent to German systems yet resistant
to direct absorption, functioning historically as buffer, transit
zone, and strategic depth rather than core territory.

Norway is near Germania.
It is not Germanizable.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC SEPARATION & STRUCTURAL DISTANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Norway's geography enforces separation.

Features:
• Mountain barriers (Scandinavian range)
• Fjord-fragmented coastline
• Sparse agrarian base
• Maritime orientation toward the North Atlantic

Distance is enforced by terrain, not diplomacy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. MEDIEVAL UNION WITHOUT INTEGRATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Norway enters Germanic political orbit through dynastic union,
not administrative fusion.

The Union of Kalmar (1397) articulates shared crown without
shared system:

> "That the three realms shall henceforth be united under one king,
> yet each kingdom shall retain its own laws, rights, and customs,
> without compulsion or alteration."
> — Union of Kalmar Charter (1397)

Union preserves sovereignty grammars.
Integration is explicitly denied.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. DENMARK AS INTERMEDIARY FILTER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Germania encounters Norway indirectly.

Pattern:
• Denmark mediates access
• Administration flows southward
• Norway remains peripheral to German systems

German systems stop at Copenhagen.
Norway remains beyond effective reach.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MARITIME VALUE WITHOUT ADMINISTRATIVE DEPTH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Norway offers resources, not governance leverage.

Assets:
• Timber for shipbuilding
• Naval manpower
• Atlantic access

Limit:
• No institutional lattice to absorb
• Population too dispersed for administration

Utility does not equal incorporability.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MODERN SEPARATION & STRATEGIC QUIET
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Norway's modern independence (1905) confirms distance.

> "The Kingdom of Norway is a free, independent, indivisible,
> and inalienable realm. Its form of government is a limited
> and hereditary monarchy."
> — Norwegian Constitution (1814)

Germania registers autonomy, not rivalry.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. WWII OCCUPATION & LIMITS OF FORCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
German occupation (1940–1945) demonstrates administrative limits.

Patterns:
• Resistance despite military occupation
• Terrain impedes control
• Resource extraction never reaches planned levels
• Occupation costly relative to benefits

Even at maximum force, Germania cannot fully absorb Norway.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Norway encodes **proximity without absorptive potential**.

For Germania, this demonstrates:
• Geography enforces civilizational limits
• Union does not imply integration
• Maritime spaces resist administrative capture
• Germania's northern edge stabilizes through distance
• Some territories are useful but not governable

Norway defines where German systems stop.
