I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Second Crusade as **Germania's first
fully imperial crusading enterprise**—the expedition where
a reigning emperor personally led German forces eastward,
and where crusading ideology was formally extended to justify
war against pagan Slavs in the Baltic.

The Second Crusade is not remembered as success.
It is remembered as **catastrophic failure redeemed by
ideological expansion**. The Syrian campaign ended in
disaster at Damascus; the Wendish Crusade against Slavs
achieved little militarily but established the precedent
that crusading could operate on multiple fronts, including
German ones.

For Germania, the Second Crusade matters because it
demonstrated both imperial commitment to crusading and the
limits of German military capacity when operating far from
home. The Wendish Crusade mattered more: it authorized
the Baltic crusading that would create the Teutonic Order
State.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Second Crusade emerged from the fall of Edessa:

FALL OF EDESSA (1144):
Zengi, atabeg of Mosul and Aleppo, captured the County of
Edessa—the first crusader state to fall. The shock prompted
calls for a new crusade.

PAPAL CALL (DECEMBER 1145):
Pope Eugene III issued *Quantum praedecessores*, calling for
a new crusade. Bernard of Clairvaux became the crusade's
chief preacher.

IMPERIAL PARTICIPATION:
Unlike the First Crusade, the Second saw reigning monarchs
participate. King Louis VII of France and King Conrad III
of Germany both took the cross.

> "The Second Crusade was the first in which crowned heads
> led armies. Conrad III's participation signified that
> crusading was not merely aristocratic adventure but
> imperial obligation. The emperor himself marched."
> — Peter H. Wilson, *Heart of Europe* (2016), p. 156
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. BERNARD OF CLAIRVAUX AND GERMAN RECRUITMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Bernard's preaching transformed German participation:

DIET OF SPEYER (CHRISTMAS 1146):
Bernard preached before Conrad III and the German nobility.
His eloquence overcame Conrad's initial reluctance; the
king took the cross.

> "Bernard's preaching at Speyer was theatrical and effective.
> He challenged Conrad personally: would the emperor refuse
> when Christ called? Conrad wept and submitted. The German
> army was born in that moment of public pressure."
> — Christopher Tyerman, *God's War* (2006), p. 281
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

ANTI-JEWISH VIOLENCE:
Bernard explicitly condemned anti-Jewish violence, traveling
to the Rhineland to suppress a monk named Radulf who was
inciting attacks. Bernard's intervention limited but did
not prevent violence.

RECRUITMENT SCALE:
Conrad's army was substantial—perhaps 20,000 combatants,
one of the largest German expeditions of the medieval period.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. THE SYRIAN CAMPAIGN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The German campaign in the East ended in disaster:

MARCH THROUGH ANATOLIA (1147):
Conrad's army crossed Byzantine territory and entered
Seljuk Anatolia. Supply difficulties and Turkish harassment
depleted the force.

BATTLE OF DORYLAEUM (OCTOBER 1147):
The Germans suffered a major defeat near Dorylaeum. Conrad
was wounded; the army fragmented. Survivors retreated to
Nicaea and eventually reached the Levant by sea.

> "Dorylaeum was not annihilation, but it was comprehensive
> defeat. The great German army that had marched from Speyer
> arrived in the Holy Land as a remnant. Conrad's crusade
> was effectively finished before it reached Syria."
> — Christopher Tyerman, *God's War* (2006), p. 312
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

SIEGE OF DAMASCUS (JULY 1148):
The combined crusader forces—German, French, and local—
attacked Damascus. The siege collapsed within days due to
poor coordination, local politics, and alleged treachery.
The crusade ended in failure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE WENDISH CRUSADE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Second Crusade's German theater proved more consequential:

PAPAL AUTHORIZATION (APRIL 1147):
Eugene III authorized crusading against the pagan Slavs
(Wends) east of the Elbe. Crusaders in this theater received
the same indulgences as those marching to Jerusalem.

> "The Wendish Crusade was the Second Crusade's lasting
> achievement for Germany. The Syrian expedition failed;
> the Baltic front was just beginning. Papal authorization
> made war against Slavic pagans equivalent to war for
> Jerusalem."
> — Eric Christiansen, *The Northern Crusades* (1997), p. 53
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

PARTICIPANTS:
Saxon nobles—including Henry the Lion and Albert the Bear—
led the expedition. The targets were the Slavic tribes:
Abodrites, Liutizi, and others who had resisted Christian
expansion.

MILITARY RESULTS:
The Wendish Crusade achieved limited immediate success.
Some tribes submitted; conversions were superficial;
resistance continued. But the precedent was established.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. IDEOLOGICAL SIGNIFICANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Wendish Crusade's importance was doctrinal:

MULTI-FRONT CRUSADING:
For the first time, a crusade officially operated on
multiple fronts simultaneously. Crusading was not limited
to the Holy Land; it could be redirected to pagan frontiers
wherever they existed.

GERMAN CRUSADE:
The Wendish theater was specifically German. Unlike the
Syrian expedition, which mixed nationalities, the Baltic
front was a German enterprise under German lords against
Germany's eastern neighbors.

> "The Wendish Crusade created a German crusading tradition
> distinct from the French-dominated eastern expeditions.
> Germans could crusade in their own backyard, against their
> own enemies, and receive the same spiritual rewards. This
> precedent enabled the Northern Crusades."
> — Peter H. Wilson, *Heart of Europe* (2016), p. 159
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

PERMANENT AUTHORIZATION:
Eugene III's bull was renewed and extended. The Baltic
crusade became perpetual—a standing authorization that
enabled the Livonian and Prussian Crusades of the
following century.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LONG-TERM CONSEQUENCES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Second Crusade produced lasting effects:

NORTHERN CRUSADES:
The Wendish Crusade precedent enabled the full Northern
Crusade apparatus: the conquest of Livonia (1198–1290),
Prussia (1226–1283), and the attempt on Novgorod (1240–1242).

TEUTONIC ORDER:
Though founded during the Third Crusade (1190), the Teutonic
Order operated within the framework the Second Crusade
established. Northern crusading was their mission.

> "The Teutonic Order was the Second Crusade's institutional
> heir. The Order applied crusading to the Baltic with
> systematic efficiency. Eugene III's authorization made
> possible the Ordensstaat."
> — Eric Christiansen, *The Northern Crusades* (1997), p. 78
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

GERMAN EASTERN EXPANSION:
The crusade legitimized German expansion eastward. Ostsiedlung
(eastern settlement) proceeded under the ideological cover
that crusading had provided. Conquest became Christianization;
colonization became civilization.

CRUSADING FAILURE:
The Syrian disaster demonstrated the limits of German military
capacity when operating far from supply bases. Barbarossa's
Third Crusade would repeat this lesson with the emperor's
own death.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. DOCTRINAL IMPLICATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Second Crusade instantiates the MULTI-FRONT CRUSADE pattern:

PATTERN: Imperial crusading to the Holy Land fails due to
logistical overextension; German crusading in the Baltic
succeeds because it operates within sustainable range.
The crusade that mattered was the one fought at home.

APPLICABLE DOCTRINES:
• DOCTRINE 06: Corridor Depth Over Dispersed Reach — Syrian
  campaign overextended; Wendish campaign operated in depth
• DOCTRINE 02: Replacement Before Expansion — Baltic crusading
  enabled the population replacement that Syrian crusading
  could never achieve

  correlates with proximity; Baltic crusading succeeded where
  Syrian crusading failed
• RLL–GERMANIA–0002 (Replacement Before Expansion Law): Baltic
  crusading enabled colonization; Syrian crusading did not
