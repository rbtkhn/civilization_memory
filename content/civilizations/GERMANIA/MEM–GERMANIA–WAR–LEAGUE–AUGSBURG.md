I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the War of the League of Augsburg as **Germania's
first sustained coalition resistance to French hegemonic pressure**:
the conflict in which the Holy Roman Empire, for all its structural
weakness, demonstrated that defensive coalition could check Louis XIV's
western expansion—at the cost of becoming the primary devastation zone.

For Germania, this war is not about the Palatinate succession.
It is about **survival through coalition rather than integration**.

Peter Wilson captures the paradox:

> "The Empire proved itself capable of organizing collective defense,
> but only by accepting that coordination, not command, would remain
> its operational ceiling."
> — Peter H. Wilson, *Heart of Europe* (2016)
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT: LOUIS XIV'S WESTERN PRESSURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
French policy under Louis XIV treats the Rhineland as expansion space.

Conditions:
• Reunions policy (1679–1684) annexes imperial territory incrementally
• Revocation of Edict of Nantes (1685) generates Protestant alarm
• French claims on Palatinate succession threaten further encroachment
• Imperial Diet cannot counter without external coalition

The Truce of Ratisbon (1684) paused but did not resolve French gains.
By 1688, German princes perceive existential pressure on the western
frontier.

Louis XIV's logic is structural: Germania's fragmentation invites
piecemeal absorption. The question is whether fragmentation also
permits coalition response.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. COALITION FORMATION: THE LEAGUE OF AUGSBURG
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The League forms in 1686, initially as defensive alignment.

Members:
• Holy Roman Emperor (Leopold I)
• Spain
• Sweden (as imperial estate holder)
• Bavaria, Saxony, Palatinate (German princes)
• Later joined by England, Dutch Republic (after 1688)

The League represents Germanic capacity for **reactive coordination**
rather than proactive assertion. No member commands; all negotiate.

Martyn Rady notes the Habsburg position:

> "Leopold I understood that the Empire could only function through
> persuasion and subsidy, not through command—a reality that made
> coalition warfare the natural Habsburg method."
> — Martyn Rady, *The Habsburgs* (2020)
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

Coalition substitutes for integration.
Coordination substitutes for command.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. THE PALATINATE DEVASTATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
French forces systematically destroy the Palatinate in 1688–1689.

Method:
• Towns burned (Heidelberg, Mannheim, Speyer, Worms)
• Agricultural infrastructure eliminated
• Population displacement
• Denial strategy: territory rendered unusable

This is not incidental damage but deliberate policy. Louis XIV's
generals implement scorched earth to create a buffer zone.

Contemporary outrage is recorded:

> "The French have made a desert of the Palatinate, leaving neither
> house nor harvest; what the sword spared, the torch consumed."
> — Imperial protest to the Diet, 1689
> [ERC: PRIMARY]

Germania experiences the war as devastation absorbed, not victory achieved.
The Rhineland becomes **terrain** rather than actor.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. WILLIAM III AND THE COALITION EXPANSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Glorious Revolution (1688) transforms the coalition.

Effects:
• William III brings England into anti-French alignment
• Dutch resources committed against Louis XIV
• Maritime powers provide subsidies and naval pressure
• War becomes genuinely European rather than merely German

For Germania, this is double-edged:
• External resources enable sustained resistance
• War aims diverge from purely German interests
• Decision-making migrates to London and The Hague

Gordon Craig observes the pattern:

> "German princes discovered that coalition warfare meant accepting
> external priorities; survival came at the cost of strategic autonomy."
> — Gordon A. Craig, *The Germans* (1982)
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

Germania survives by delegation.
Germania does not direct.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. IMPERIAL MILITARY ORGANIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war reveals both capability and limitation.

Achievements:
• Reichsarmee mobilized (albeit slowly)
• Kreise (imperial circles) coordinate regional defense
• Austrian forces provide backbone
• Margrave Ludwig Wilhelm of Baden emerges as effective commander

Limitations:
• Funding disputes delay operations
• Command fragmentation persists
• Logistics depend on external subsidy
• No lasting institutional reform achieved

The Empire demonstrates that it can wage coalition warfare.
It does not demonstrate that it can wage efficient warfare.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. THEATER OPERATIONS AND STRATEGIC STALEMATE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war settles into attritional pattern.

Rhine theater:
• French hold left bank gains
• Imperial forces contest but cannot expel
• Fortress warfare dominates
• Neither side achieves decisive breakthrough

Secondary theaters:
• Spanish Netherlands (Anglo-Dutch focus)
• Italy (Austrian interest)
• Ireland (English priority)
• Colonial conflicts (maritime powers)

Germania's war becomes one front among many.
German interests are subsumed in coalition priorities.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. PEACE OF RYSWICK (1697)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The settlement restores most French gains from the Reunions.

Terms:
• France returns Lorraine, Freiburg, Breisach, Philippsburg
• Strasbourg remains French (critical exception)
• Palatinate restored but devastated
• Status quo ante bellum with permanent French salient

For Germania, the outcome is ambiguous:
• Coalition succeeded in checking expansion
• Territory recovered but ruined
• Strasbourg lost permanently
• No structural reform achieved

The war ends without collapse.
The war ends without correction.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war encodes **coalition survival without integration** into
Germanic strategic memory.

Lessons:
• Fragmented systems can resist hegemony through coalition
• Coalition warfare converts German space into terrain
• External allies impose external priorities
• Survival does not equal reform
• The Rhineland absorbs devastation as structural condition

A. J. P. Taylor notes the persistent pattern:

> "Germany's central position made her the natural battleground of
> Europe; coalition wars were fought over Germany more often than
> by Germany."
> — A. J. P. Taylor, *The Course of German History* (1945)
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

The war confirms what Germania is: a coordination space, not a command
system. This will persist until Prussia demonstrates an alternative.

XI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Coalition can check hegemony without achieving integration.
• German space becomes terrain in coalition wars.
• External allies bring resources but impose priorities.
• Devastation concentrates in buffer zones.
• Survival without reform defers structural correction.
• The Empire coordinates; it does not command.
