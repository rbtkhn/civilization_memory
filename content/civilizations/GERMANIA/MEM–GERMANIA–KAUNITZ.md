I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Wenzel Anton von Kaunitz as **Austria's systemic
diplomat**: the statesman who engineered the Diplomatic Revolution of
1756 and restructured European alliance architecture for a generation.

Kaunitz is not remembered for battlefield victories.
He is remembered for **making coalitions possible**.

For Germania, Kaunitz represents the Habsburg capacity for patient
strategic reconfiguration—the ability to reverse centuries of enmity
(Austria-France) in pursuit of existential objectives (Silesia recovery).

This file records diplomatic architecture, not moral judgment.
Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRUCTURAL CONTEXT: HABSBURG CRISIS AFTER 1740
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Kaunitz rises in the aftermath of catastrophic Austrian failure.

Conditions inherited:
• Loss of Silesia to Prussia (1740–1742)
• Pragmatic Sanction contested despite guarantees
• Traditional alliance system (Britain-Austria) inadequate
• Prussia now a permanent structural rival

The War of the Austrian Succession exposed a fatal weakness:
Austria's alliances could not deliver Silesian recovery.

Kaunitz diagnosed the problem as **systemic, not tactical**.
Recovery required new partners, not better execution.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE DIPLOMATIC REVOLUTION (1756)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Kaunitz engineered the most dramatic alliance reversal of the
eighteenth century.

Core transformation:
• Austria abandons Britain as primary ally
• Austria allies with France (centuries-long rival)
• Russia joins the anti-Prussian coalition
• Prussia isolated with only Britain remaining

Fred Anderson emphasizes the magnitude of this shift:

> "The alliance of France and Austria, enemies for two and a half
> centuries, was the diplomatic equivalent of an earthquake—a
> realignment so profound that contemporaries could scarcely believe it."
> — Fred Anderson, *Crucible of War* (2000)

Kaunitz's logic was structural:
→ Britain would not fight a continental war for Silesia.
→ France would, if properly incentivized.
→ Coalition depth was the only path to Prussian containment.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRATEGIC METHOD: PATIENCE AS WEAPON
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Kaunitz operated on diplomatic timescales most statesmen could not
sustain.

Characteristics:
• Multi-year preparation before execution
• Tolerance for temporary humiliation
• Information discipline
• Exploitation of dynastic vanity (Madame de Pompadour)

Martyn Rady observes:

> "Kaunitz understood that diplomacy was not about winning arguments
> but about creating conditions in which opponents found agreement
> preferable to resistance."
> — Martyn Rady, *The Habsburgs* (2020)

Patience substituted for military initiative.
Time became a strategic asset.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE SEVEN YEARS' WAR: COALITION WITHOUT VICTORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Kaunitz's coalition was formidable but ultimately insufficient.

Coalition advantages:
• Three-front pressure on Prussia
• Superior combined resources
• Diplomatic isolation of Frederick

Coalition failures:
• Coordination problems across theaters
• Russian unreliability (Peter III's withdrawal, 1762)
• French distraction in colonial theaters
• Prussian discipline and replacement depth

Anderson notes the structural lesson:

> "The coalition that Kaunitz assembled was the most powerful
> combination any European state had ever faced. That Prussia
> survived it at all testified to the fragility of alliances held
> together by hatred rather than common purpose."
> — Fred Anderson, *Crucible of War* (2000)

Silesia remained Prussian.
The coalition dissolved.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. POST-WAR RECALIBRATION (1763–1792)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Kaunitz remained State Chancellor for nearly three more decades.

Post-war adjustments:
• Acceptance of Prussian permanence
• Focus on internal Austrian reform
• Participation in Polish partitions (1772, 1793)
• Maintenance of French alliance

The failure to recover Silesia did not discredit Kaunitz's method.
It demonstrated the **limits of coalition without commitment**.

Peter Wilson observes:

> "Kaunitz's true legacy was not the recovery of Silesia but the
> demonstration that Austria could reshape European diplomacy when
> it chose patience over provocation."
> — Peter Wilson, *Heart of Europe* (2016)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. MEARSHEIMER STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

VII.A. POWER DISTRIBUTION LOGIC
Kaunitz recognized that Austria alone could never defeat Prussia.
Coalition was structurally necessary, not merely advantageous.

VII.B. ALLIANCE ARCHITECTURE
The Diplomatic Revolution demonstrated that ancient enmities could
be reversed when structural incentives aligned. Ideology yielded to
interest.

VII.C. COALITION FRAGILITY
Multi-power coalitions suffer from coordination costs, defection
risk, and divergent war aims. Kaunitz's coalition had all three.

VII.D. REPLACEMENT DEPTH ASYMMETRY
Prussia's survival validated Anderson's thesis: administrative-
military integration enabled endurance that coalition numbers
could not overcome through attrition alone.

IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Coalitions can be constructed but not commanded.
• Ancient enmities dissolve when structural incentives align.
• Patience is a strategic weapon when opponents expect haste.
• Coalition depth cannot substitute for coalition cohesion.
• Administrative states survive what coalition numbers should destroy.
• Diplomatic revolution requires willingness to abandon identity.
