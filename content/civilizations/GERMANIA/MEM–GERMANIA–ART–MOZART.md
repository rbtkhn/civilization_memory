────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart as **Germania's musical
apotheosis**: the composer who achieved a synthesis of Italian lyricism,
German counterpoint, and Enlightenment clarity that remains the standard
against which all subsequent music is measured.

Mozart is not merely a genius.
He is the **proof** that Germanic musical culture could produce
perfection—effortless, inexhaustible, absolute.

In Mozart, Vienna becomes the musical center of Europe.
After Mozart, that centrality is permanent.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRUCTURAL CONTEXT: SALZBURG TO VIENNA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mozart's life traced the geography of Germanic musical culture.

Origins:
• Born Salzburg (1756): prince-archbishopric, ecclesiastical territory
• Child prodigy tours (1762–1773): courts of Europe
• Break with Salzburg (1781): dismissed by Archbishop Colloredo
• Vienna (1781–1791): freelance career, death at 35

Mozart described his liberation:

> "The heart ennobles the man; and though I am no count, I have
> perhaps more honor in me than many a count. Lackey or count,
> whoever insults me is a scoundrel."
> — Mozart, letter to his father, 1781

The break with Salzburg was also a break with servitude.
Mozart attempted what few composers before him had: independence.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. MUSICAL SYNTHESIS: THREE TRADITIONS UNIFIED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mozart absorbed and unified disparate traditions.

Italian influence:
• Opera seria and buffa
• Lyrical melody
• Vocal writing

German influence:
• Contrapuntal complexity (Bach, Handel)
• Structural rigor
• Instrumental weight

Enlightenment influence:
• Clarity and balance
• Formal perfection
• Rational organization of emotion

Mozart stated his method:

> "I pay no attention whatever to anybody's praise or blame. I simply
> follow my own feelings."
> — Mozart, letter to his father, 1781

The synthesis was personal—but its result was universal.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. OPERATIC ACHIEVEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mozart transformed opera into psychological drama.

Major works:
• *Le nozze di Figaro* (1786): class tension, forgiveness
• *Don Giovanni* (1787): desire, damnation, ambiguity
• *Così fan tutte* (1790): irony, human frailty
• *Die Zauberflöte* (1791): Enlightenment allegory, Masonic symbolism

Mozart on character:

> "In an opera, poetry must be altogether the obedient daughter of
> music. Why do Italian comic operas please everywhere? Because
> the music reigns supreme."
> — Mozart, letter to his father, 1781

Opera was not text with accompaniment.
Opera was music that absorbed text, character, and drama.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. INSTRUMENTAL MASTERY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mozart's instrumental works established permanent standards.

Achievements:
• Piano concertos: 27 works defining the genre
• Symphonies: culminating in 39, 40, 41 (the "Jupiter")
• String quartets: "Haydn Quartets" dedicated to his mentor
• Chamber music across all combinations

Haydn told Leopold Mozart:

> "Before God and as an honest man I tell you that your son is the
> greatest composer known to me either in person or by name. He has
> taste, and what is more, the most profound knowledge of composition."
> — Joseph Haydn to Leopold Mozart, 1785

This was not flattery.
Haydn—himself a master—recognized something unprecedented.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ECONOMY & PATRONAGE CRISIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mozart's freelance career was economically precarious.

Conditions:
• No permanent court position after Salzburg
• Dependent on commissions, teaching, concerts
• Chronic debt despite substantial income
• Death in relative poverty (1791)

Mozart wrote to a patron:

> "I am obliged to give lessons in order to earn money, and I cannot
> compose because I have no time."
> — Mozart, letter to Michael Puchberg, 1789

The transition from patronage to market was incomplete.
Mozart arrived too early for the concert economy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. DEATH & MYTHOLOGIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mozart died at 35, leaving the *Requiem* unfinished.

Facts:
• Death December 5, 1791
• Cause: likely rheumatic fever (disputed)
• Buried in common grave (standard Viennese practice)
• *Requiem* completed by Süssmayr

The mystery generated mythology:
• Poisoning rumors (Salieri)
• Masonic conspiracy theories
• Romantic transfiguration of poverty

Mozart's early death became part of the narrative:
genius too pure for the world.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. MEARSHEIMER STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

VIII.A. VIENNA AS CULTURAL CAPITAL
Mozart's migration to Vienna reflected power concentration. The
Habsburg capital attracted talent because it controlled resources.
Cultural prestige followed political weight.

VIII.B. PATRONAGE TRANSITION
The shift from court patronage to market economy was underway but
incomplete. Mozart's struggles reflect structural transformation,
not personal failure.

VIII.C. GERMAN-ITALIAN SYNTHESIS
Mozart absorbed Italian opera and transformed it through German
structure. This was cultural technology transfer: importing forms,
adding depth, re-exporting the synthesis.

VIII.D. STANDARD-SETTING
Mozart's achievement established norms that constrained successors.
Beethoven, Brahms, and all later composers worked in Mozart's shadow.
Setting the standard is a form of power.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mozart encodes **synthesis as perfection**.

Lessons:
• Genius can absorb multiple traditions and unify them.
• Formal perfection does not exclude emotional depth.
• The transition from patronage to market is perilous.
• Early death generates mythology that obscures reality.
• Vienna's centrality in music began with Haydn and Mozart.
• Mozart is the standard against which all music is measured.

Mozart proves what Germanic musical culture could achieve—
at its apex, before Beethoven's rupture.
