I. STRATEGIC PURPOSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Amur River as a **terminal expansion
frontier and Sino-Russian boundary**: a river that marks the
eastern limit of Russian continental reach while defining the
border with China.

The fact is that the Amur is not an interior artery. It is
a **boundary river** that determines where Russian power ends
and Chinese power begins.

States that control the Amur access the Pacific.
The Amur also defines the limit of continental expansion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. RIVER GEOMETRY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Amur's strategic character derives from its length, position,
and boundary function.

Physical structure:
• ~2,824 km length (tenth longest in world)
• Forms Russia–China border for ~1,600 km
• Terminates in Pacific Ocean (Sea of Okhotsk)
• Tributaries extend deep into both territories
• Extreme distance from Russian core (~6,000 km from Moscow)

Strategic implications:
• Long: major boundary definition
• Border river: separates two great powers
• Pacific terminus: enables maritime access
• Remote: extreme logistical strain

The river geometry means that the Amur creates **terminal
boundary conditions** at the edge of Russian reach.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. SINO-RUSSIAN BOUNDARY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Amur defines the border between Russia and China.

Boundary logic:
• River forms natural demarcation line
• Both banks matter for control
• Crossing points are strategic assets
• Boundary requires bilateral management

Strategic implications:
• Amur control is shared between powers
• Neither side dominates river alone
• Boundary definition reduces friction
• Cooperation or conflict determined by river relationship

The Amur is not a unilateral asset.
It is a **shared boundary** requiring management.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PACIFIC ACCESS FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Amur provides Russian access to the Pacific.

Access logic:
• River flows to Sea of Okhotsk
• Enables navigation from interior to ocean
• Vladivostok nearby (but not on Amur)
• Pacific Fleet operations supportable

Strategic implications:
• Amur control enables Pacific presence
• Interior resources accessible for export
• Maritime projection possible from Far East
• Alternative to Trans-Siberian rail

The Amur converts continental depth into **Pacific reach**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. DISTANCE AS CONSTRAINT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Amur's extreme distance from the Russian core creates
structural limitations.

Distance effects:
• ~6,000 km from Moscow
• Sparse population and infrastructure
• Extreme logistical strain
• Slow reinforcement capability

Strategic implications:
• Presence requires disproportionate investment
• Local defense capacity limited
• Dependent on Trans-Siberian rail
• Vulnerable to interdiction

Distance converts presence into **structural fragility**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ASYMMETRIC POPULATION PRESSURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Population asymmetry creates structural imbalance.

Asymmetry logic:
• Russian Far East: ~6 million people
• Adjacent Chinese provinces: ~100+ million people
• 15:1 or greater population ratio
• Economic activity heavily skewed

Strategic implications:
• Russia cannot match Chinese demographic weight
• Economic integration favors China
• Resource extraction is primary Russian activity
• Population pressure creates long-term vulnerability

The Amur boundary marks **asymmetric power distribution**
favoring China in demographic and economic terms.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. POWER DISTRIBUTION IMPLICATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In the international system, geography constrains state behavior.
The Amur constrains both Russia and China.

For Russia:
• Amur enables Pacific access
• Boundary with China is defined and stable
• Distance creates logistical strain
• Asymmetric population pressure is long-term risk

For China:
• Amur boundary is accepted but not preferred
• Resource access via Russian territory available
• Economic leverage through population and trade
• Boundary stability serves current interests

For Pacific dynamics:
• Russian Far East is strategic depth
• Amur access enables Pacific operations
• Sino-Russian cooperation affects regional balance
• Boundary stability reduces friction

The structural reality is that the Amur creates a **managed
boundary** between two great powers with asymmetric positions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. TERRAIN AS PERMANENT CONSTRAINT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Geography does not change. Political control changes.
The Amur's strategic logic remains constant across all regimes.

Permanent patterns:
• Terminal rivers define expansion limits
• Boundary rivers require bilateral management
• Distance converts presence into fragility
• Population asymmetry creates long-term pressure

This applied to:
• Qing China, Imperial Russia, Soviet Union, PRC, RF

The terrain is permanent. The advantage shifts based on
relative power, but the boundary function persists.
