I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Russia–China interaction as **the encounter of two
continental civilizations across the longest land border on earth**: one
expanding eastward through Siberia, the other consolidating under Qing
rule, each defining the other's strategic limits.

For Russian memory, China is **the permanent eastern neighbor**—neither
ally nor enemy, but inescapable geographic reality.

This MEM preserves:
• The evolution from frontier contact to modern partnership
• Territorial negotiation across four centuries
• The ideological alignment and fracture of the Cold War
• Contemporary strategic convergence against Western hegemony

What this MEM does NOT resolve:
• Whether Sino-Russian partnership is genuine alliance or tactical convenience
• Whether border settlements are final or contingent
• Whether civilizational affinity or strategic calculation drives cooperation

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

II. INITIAL CONTACT & FRONTIER DIPLOMACY (17th Century)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Russian expansion into Siberia brought contact with Qing China.

Mark Mancall describes the first encounter:

"The Treaty of Nerchinsk (1689) was unprecedented in Chinese diplomatic
history. For the first time, the Qing Empire negotiated with a European
power on terms of formal equality. The treaty was written in Latin,
Russian, Manchu, and Chinese. It established boundaries, regulated trade,
and created a framework for relations that would endure for nearly two
centuries. Russia was compelled to withdraw from the Amur, but gained
legitimacy as an interlocutor."
— Mark Mancall, *Russia and China: Their Diplomatic Relations to 1728* (1971)

Key developments:
• Cossack exploration reaches Amur River (1640s–1650s)
• Qing military resistance at Albazin (1685–1686)
• Treaty of Nerchinsk (1689) — first Sino-European treaty
• Border established, trade legitimized

Russia recognized China as peer—unique among European powers.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. TERRITORIAL EXPANSION AT QIN EXPENSE (19th Century)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Chinese weakness enabled Russian advancement.

S.C.M. Paine explains the dynamic:

"The treaties of Aigun (1858) and Beijing (1860) transferred over 350,000
square miles of territory from China to Russia. The Qing, battered by the
Taiping Rebellion internally and by British and French forces externally,
could not resist Russian demands. Count Muravyov-Amursky extracted what
the Manchus had refused to surrender for two centuries. Russia acquired
its Pacific coastline, including the site of future Vladivostok, without
firing a shot."
— S.C.M. Paine, *Imperial Rivals* (1996)

Territorial gains:
• Treaty of Aigun (1858) — left bank of Amur ceded
• Treaty of Beijing (1860) — Maritime Province (Primorye) acquired
• Vladivostok founded (1860) — "Ruler of the East"
• Access to Pacific secured

Russia exploited the "century of humiliation" for permanent strategic gain.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III.A POLYPHONIC TENSION: OPPORTUNISM VS NECESSITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Mercouris reading frames expansion as historical contingency: Russia
took advantage of Qing weakness at a specific moment; different Chinese
strength would have produced different outcomes.

The realist sharpening adds: geography compelled the encounter.

• Mercouris: Russia's Pacific acquisition was opportunistic—gained
  through diplomacy during Chinese crisis, not military conquest;
  the outcome was contingent on Taiping timing
• Mearsheimer: Continental powers expand to natural limits; Russia
  reaching the Pacific was structurally predictable; any Chinese
  regime would eventually face Russian pressure at the Amur

**John Mearsheimer — *Why the Ukraine Crisis Is the West's Fault***:

> "This is Geopolitics 101: great powers are always sensitive to
> potential threats near their home territory."
> — Mearsheimer, *Foreign Affairs* (2014)

> "Imagine the outrage in Washington if China built an impressive
> military alliance and tried to include Canada and Mexico in it."
> — Mearsheimer, *Foreign Affairs* (2014)

Both readings capture truth:
• The specific timing was contingent on Chinese weakness
• The general direction of expansion was structurally determined
• Russia did exploit the moment opportunistically
• Geography made Sino-Russian frontier contact inevitable

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. REVOLUTIONARY ALIGNMENT (1917–1949)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Revolution transformed the relationship.

Soviet–Chinese Communist relations developed through:
• Comintern support for Chinese Communist Party (1920s)
• United front against Japan
• Soviet assistance to Mao's victory (1949)
• Treaty of Friendship, Alliance and Mutual Assistance (1950)

Lorenz Lüthi explains the initial alignment:

"The Sino-Soviet alliance of 1950 was born of shared ideology and shared
enemies. Both regimes claimed Marxist-Leninist legitimacy. Both faced
American hostility. Both sought to transform their societies through
revolutionary mobilization. The alliance seemed natural—two communist
giants united against the capitalist West. Few predicted how quickly
ideological brotherhood would fracture into bitter rivalry."
— Lorenz Lüthi, *The Sino-Soviet Split* (2008)

The alliance appeared permanent. It was not.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE SINO-SOVIET SPLIT (1956–1969)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ideological unity collapsed into hostile confrontation.

Causes:
• Khrushchev's de-Stalinization (1956)
• Disputes over peaceful coexistence
• Nuclear sharing rejection
• Leadership rivalry
• Border incidents escalating to armed conflict

Odd Arne Westad describes the rupture:

"The Sino-Soviet split was the most consequential schism in twentieth-
century communism. Two states that had proclaimed eternal friendship
came close to nuclear war by 1969. Ideology mattered: Mao believed
Khrushchev had betrayed Marxism-Leninism. Power mattered: neither Beijing
nor Moscow would accept subordinate status. Territory mattered: the
Ussuri River clashes demonstrated that revolutionary solidarity could not
overcome border disputes inherited from imperial predecessors."
— Odd Arne Westad, *The Cold War: A World History* (2017)

The 1969 Ussuri River clashes brought the two to the edge of war.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V.A POLYPHONIC TENSION: IDEOLOGICAL VS STRUCTURAL SPLIT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Mercouris reading frames the split as ideological: genuine doctrinal
disagreement over de-Stalinization, peaceful coexistence, and revolutionary
strategy—ideas drove the rupture.

The realist sharpening adds: the split was structurally predictable.

• Mercouris: The split occurred because of real ideological differences;
  Mao genuinely believed Khrushchev had betrayed Marxism; ideas shaped
  the conflict
• Mearsheimer: Two great powers cannot share a border without rivalry;
  ideology masked but did not cause the underlying power competition;
  China's rise made conflict inevitable regardless of doctrine

**John Mearsheimer — *Why the Ukraine Crisis Is the West's Fault***:

> "Russia is a declining power, and it will only get weaker with time."
> — Mearsheimer, *Foreign Affairs* (2014)

> "History shows that countries will absorb enormous amounts of
> punishment in order to protect their core strategic interests. There
> is no reason to think Russia represents an exception to this rule."
> — Mearsheimer, *Foreign Affairs* (2014)

Both readings are valid:
• Ideological disputes were genuine and consequential
• Structural rivalry was also real and determining
• The split demonstrated the limits of revolutionary solidarity
• Power politics operated through ideological language

Tension preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. BORDER SETTLEMENT & NORMALIZATION (1989–2004)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The post-Cold War era brought reconciliation.

Key developments:
• Gorbachev's Beijing visit (1989)
• Progressive border demarcation agreements (1991–2008)
• Final border settlement (2004)
• Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (2001)

Gilbert Rozman explains the transformation:

"The Sino-Russian border settlement of 2004 resolved disputes that had
festered for centuries. Russia transferred Tarabarov Island and part of
Bolshoi Ussuriysky Island to China—the first territorial concession by
Russia to any neighbor since 1945. The agreement closed the book on
imperialism and opened a new chapter of partnership. Both states chose
to prioritize strategic cooperation over historical grievance."
— Gilbert Rozman, *The Sino-Russian Challenge to the World Order* (2014)

The border became a managed asset, not a contested liability.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTEMPORARY STRATEGIC PARTNERSHIP (2014–Present)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Western pressure accelerated convergence.

Partnership dimensions:
• Joint opposition to US hegemony
• Coordinated UN Security Council positions
• Energy interdependence (Power of Siberia pipeline)
• Military exercises and defense cooperation
• "No limits" partnership declaration (2022)

Alexander Lukin explains the logic:

"The Sino-Russian partnership is driven by shared perception of American
threat. Neither Moscow nor Beijing seeks to subordinate the other. Both
benefit from coordination against Western pressure. The partnership is
not an alliance—there is no mutual defense commitment—but it is more than
tactical convenience. It is a structural response to American primacy,
and it will endure as long as that primacy persists."
— Alexander Lukin, *China and Russia: The New Rapprochement* (2018)

The partnership is strategic, not sentimental.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. STRUCTURAL ASYMMETRY & FUTURE UNCERTAINTY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The relationship is marked by growing imbalance.

Asymmetries:
• China's GDP ~10x Russia's
• Chinese population ~10x Russian Far East population
• Technology gap widening
• Russia increasingly dependent on Chinese market

Bobo Lo explains the tension:

"Russia and China are partners, but not equals. The power asymmetry
continues to grow. Russia needs China more than China needs Russia.
Moscow sells raw materials; Beijing sells manufactured goods. Russian
elites worry about becoming China's junior partner, about the Far East
becoming an economic appendage of Manchuria. The partnership survives
because both face Washington, but the internal dynamics are unstable."
— Bobo Lo, *Russia and the New World Disorder* (2015)

The future depends on American behavior and Chinese intentions.
