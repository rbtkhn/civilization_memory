I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Great Turkish War as **Russia’s first structured
recognition that Ottoman decline could be exploited—but not yet
mastered**: a coalition conflict in which Russia participates without
possessing the control mechanisms needed to convert opportunity into
dominance.

For Russian memory, the war is a **lesson in premature entry**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT — OTTOMAN HIGH-WATER MARK
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war begins after the failed Ottoman siege of Vienna (1683).

Coalition powers:
• Habsburg Monarchy
• Poland-Lithuania
• Venice
• Russia (late entrant)

Ottoman power is stressed—but not yet broken.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. RUSSIA’S MOTIVATION — STEPPE SECURITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Russia’s entry is driven by:
• Crimean Tatar raids
• Southern vulnerability
• Desire to eliminate proxy threats
• Limited interest in European balance politics

Russia fights for **relief**, not dominance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. AZOV CAMPAIGNS — PARTIAL SUCCESS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Peter I captures Azov (1696):

• Symbolic maritime access achieved
• Fortified foothold established
• No straits access
• No fleet sustainability

Azov provides recognition—but not leverage.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. STRUCTURAL LIMITATION — NO TERMINAL CONTROL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Despite victory:
• Ottoman naval power intact
• Black Sea remains closed
• Crimea remains hostile
• Russian fleet constrained

The war exposes the difference between **possession and command**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. COALITION MISALIGNMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
European allies seek:
• Ottoman rollback in Balkans
• Balance-of-power stabilization

Russia seeks:
• Southern security
• Steppe neutralization

Objectives diverge.
Outcomes fragment.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. MEMORY FUNCTION — PRECURSOR, NOT FOUNDATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Great Turkish War is remembered as:
• Proof of Ottoman vulnerability
• Evidence of Russian unreadiness
• Catalyst for Petrine reform
• Warning against symbolic gains

Russia learns that **entry without infrastructure invites reversal**.

IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Coalitions reveal weakness but do not create control.
• Early access can delay deeper reform.
• Maritime power requires terminals, not victories.
• Recognition precedes domination.
• Premature success can mislead strategy.

Tension preserved without verdict.
