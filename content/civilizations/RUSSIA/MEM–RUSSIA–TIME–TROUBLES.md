I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Time of Troubles as the **system-failure case**
of Russian statehood: the period in which dynastic extinction,
succession ambiguity, legitimacy collapse, foreign intervention, and
social disintegration converged to nearly dissolve the Muscovite state
without decisive external conquest.

For Russian civilizational memory, the Time of Troubles represents
**collapse without replacement**—a phase in which sovereignty ceased
to function while civilizational identity endured.

This file explicitly does NOT resolve:
• whether the collapse was avoidable
• whether Romanov restoration was the optimal outcome
• whether autocracy itself caused or merely amplified failure

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. DYNASTIC EXTINCTION & LEGITIMACY VACUUM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The crisis begins with the extinction of the Rurikid dynasty in 1598.

Trigger conditions:
• Death of Tsar Fyodor I without heir
• Absence of an uncontested dynastic successor
• Loss of genealogical grounding for autocracy
• Erosion of metaphysical certainty regarding sovereignty

Autocracy in Muscovy is not merely institutional.
It is **dynastic-sacral**.

When lineage ends, authority becomes structurally indeterminate.

Political axiom (RLL-0005 precursor):
→ Autocracy without dynasty loses operational legitimacy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. SACRAL ENDURANCE WITHOUT SOVEREIGN FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Orthodox sacral authority remains intact throughout the crisis.

Continuities:
• No mass apostasy
• No schism or rival sacral system
• Church institutions persist
• Orthodoxy retains moral authority

Yet governance collapses.

Structural distinction:
• Sacral legitimacy preserves **identity**
• Dynastic legitimacy enables **rule**

The Church can sanctify authority,
but it cannot select, enforce, or transfer sovereignty.

Sacral endurance stabilizes civilization,
but cannot operate the state.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STATE FRAGILITY & MATERIAL SHOCK
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Structural fragility accelerates collapse.

Contributing stresses:
• Administrative exhaustion after Ivan IV
• Elite depletion and distrust
• Tightened serfdom
• Fiscal strain
• Famine of 1601–1603

Material scarcity undermines obedience.

Coercive capacity degrades faster than legitimacy can be repaired.

The state ceases to enforce order consistently.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. PRETENDERS & TRANSACTIONAL SOVEREIGNTY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Authority fragments across competing claimants.

Key phenomena:
• False Dmitries
• Boyar factionalism
• Mercenary and Cossack forces
• Rapid loyalty switching

Pretenders are not anomalies.
They are **market responses** to legitimacy scarcity.

Structural insight (RLL-0005):
• Dynastic logic remains culturally authoritative
• Verification mechanisms are weak
• Plausible continuity is preferred to uncertainty

Sovereignty becomes negotiable and transactional.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. FOREIGN INTERVENTION AS SECONDARY EFFECT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
External powers exploit internal collapse rather than cause it.

Key actors:
• Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth
• Swedish intervention

Moscow itself is occupied.

Foreign rule becomes possible not through conquest,
but through invitation, exhaustion, and elite fragmentation.

External domination follows internal fracture.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. POPULAR MOBILIZATION & SACRAL REACTIVATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Recovery begins **outside formal sovereignty**.

Key dynamics:
• Orthodox Church as legitimacy anchor
• Popular militias (Minin and Pozharsky)
• Collective defense framed as civilizational survival
• Expulsion of foreign forces (1612)

Faith substitutes temporarily for absent sovereignty.

However:
• Military recovery does not equal state restoration
• Identity is defended before authority is rebuilt

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. DYNASTIC RESET & ROMANOV RESTORATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The election of Mikhail Romanov (1613) ends the interregnum.

Significance:
• Dynastic continuity restored
• Sacral monarchy re-anchored
• Sovereign function reactivated
• Preference for stability over reform codified

Critical rule (RLL-0005 formalized):
→ Sacral legitimacy preserves civilization through collapse.
→ Dynastic legitimacy restores sovereignty after collapse.

Order precedes ambition.
Continuity precedes innovation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. STRUCTURAL LESSONS INTERNALIZED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Time of Troubles imprints durable constraints on Russian governance:

• Extreme aversion to succession ambiguity
• Fear of elite pluralism
• Preference for strong central authority
• Suspicion of improvisational legitimacy
• Elevation of stability as supreme political value

Later autocracy is shaped by **memory of near-extinction**, not ideology.
