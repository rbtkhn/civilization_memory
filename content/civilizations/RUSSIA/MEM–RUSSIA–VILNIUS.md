────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Vilnius as **Russia's contested western city**:
the capital of the Grand Duchy that rivaled Moscow for Rus' inheritance,
later incorporated into the Russian Empire, and now capital of a
NATO-member state.

For Russian civilizational memory, Vilnius represents **the heart of
the rival successor**: the seat of Lithuanian power that absorbed
Orthodox Slavic populations, the "Jerusalem of the North" with its
Jewish heritage, and the western extremity of Russian imperial reach.

Vilnius encodes the lesson that **capital cities embody rival claims**,
and that former Lithuanian power centers remain significant to Russian
strategic geography.

This memory does not resolve whether Russian rule preserved or
suppressed Vilnius's character, whether the city was fundamentally
Lithuanian, Polish, Jewish, or multi-ethnic, or whether its current
alignment threatens Russian interests.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GRAND DUCHY CAPITAL: RIVAL TO MOSCOW
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vilnius emerged as the center of Lithuanian power.

II.A FOUNDATION & GROWTH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• 1323: First written mention (Gediminas's letters)
• Capital of Grand Duchy of Lithuania
• Seat of Grand Dukes who ruled from Baltic to Black Sea
• Multi-ethnic from origin: Lithuanian, Ruthenian, Jewish, Polish

II.B SIGNIFICANCE FOR RUS' LANDS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Grand Duchy governed from Vilnius absorbed:
• Western Rus' territories (Belarus, western Ukraine)
• Orthodox Slavic majority population
• Kievan Rus' successor lands

Timothy Snyder explains:

"Vilnius was the capital of an empire that, at its height, stretched
from the Baltic to the Black Sea. The Grand Duchy governed more of
the former Kievan Rus' than Moscow did. Ruthenian (Old Belarusian)
served as chancery language alongside Latin. Orthodox nobles served
alongside Catholics. For centuries, Vilnius represented an alternative
path for the East Slavic lands—one that Moscow eventually foreclosed."
— Timothy Snyder, *The Reconstruction of Nations* (2003)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. POLISH-LITHUANIAN COMMONWEALTH PERIOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Union of Lublin (1569) transformed Vilnius.

III.A AFTER THE UNION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Lithuanian lands remain distinct within Commonwealth
• Vilnius retains status as Lithuanian capital
• Polonization intensifies among nobility
• Catholic influence strengthens
• University of Vilnius founded (1579) — Jesuit institution

III.B RELIGIOUS COMPLEXITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vilnius housed:
• Catholic Poles and Polonized Lithuanians
• Orthodox Ruthenians (later Greek Catholic after 1596 Union of Brest)
• Growing Jewish population
• Lithuanian-speaking peasant hinterland

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. RUSSIAN INCORPORATION (1795)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Third Partition brought Vilnius under Russian rule.

IV.A THE PARTITION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• 1795: Third Partition of Poland-Lithuania
• Vilnius becomes capital of Vilna Governorate
• Russian administration imposed
• Catholic Church restricted; Orthodox promoted

IV.B IMPERIAL VILNA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Geoffrey Hosking describes the city's status:

"Vilna occupied an ambiguous position in the Russian Empire. It was
the capital of the former Grand Duchy, a reminder of Lithuanian
statehood that Russian policy sought to suppress. The Polish-speaking
nobility, the Jewish majority in the city itself, the Lithuanian
peasantry, and the Orthodox population each had distinct relationships
with Russian rule. Vilna was not easily assimilated."
— Geoffrey Hosking, *Russia and the Russians: A History* (2001)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. UPRISINGS & RUSSIFICATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Polish-Lithuanian resistance provoked Russian response.

V.A NOVEMBER UPRISING (1830–1831)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Vilna participates in Polish revolt
• Russian suppression; University closed (1832)
• Intensified Russification policies
• Catholic religious orders restricted

V.B JANUARY UPRISING (1863–1864)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Major uprising with Vilna participation
• Harsh repression under Governor-General Muravyov ("The Hangman")
• Further Russification: Polish banned from public use
• Lithuanian language also restricted

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. VILNA AS JEWISH CENTER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The city held profound Jewish significance.

VI.A "JERUSALEM OF LITHUANIA"
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vilna's Jewish community:
• Major center of Jewish learning (from 16th century)
• Vilna Gaon (Elijah ben Solomon Zalman, 1720–1797)
• Mitnagdic (non-Hasidic) Judaism's intellectual center
• YIVO Institute founded (1925)
• By 1897: Jews ~40% of city population

VI.B RUSSIAN JEWISH POLICY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vilna within the Pale of Settlement:
• Jewish residence legally permitted
• Periodic pogroms and restrictions
• Jewish intellectual and political movements (Bund, Zionism)
• Yiddish cultural florescence

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. WORLD WAR I & INTERWAR PERIOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Great War ended Russian control.

VII.A GERMAN OCCUPATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• 1915: German capture of Vilna
• End of 120 years of Russian rule
• German administration (Ober Ost)
• Lithuanian national aspirations emerge

VII.B CONTESTED CAPITAL (1918–1939)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• 1918: Lithuanian independence declared (Vilnius as capital)
• 1919: Soviet capture; brief Soviet Lithuanian government
• 1920: Polish seizure (Żeligowski's mutiny)
• 1920–1939: Vilnius under Poland as "Wilno"
• Lithuania refuses diplomatic relations over Vilnius

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. SOVIET PERIOD (1939–1941, 1944–1990)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Soviet power returned Vilnius to Lithuania, then absorbed both.

VIII.A 1939: VILNIUS RESTORED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact and Soviet sphere
• October 1939: USSR transfers Vilnius to Lithuania
• Stalin uses Vilnius as leverage for Soviet bases

VIII.B ANNEXATION & TRANSFORMATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• 1940: Soviet occupation and annexation
• 1941–1944: German occupation; Holocaust destroys Jewish population
• 1944: Soviet recapture
• 1944–1990: Capital of Lithuanian SSR

Dominic Lieven notes the demographic transformation:

"The Vilnius that emerged from World War II bore little resemblance
to its prewar self. The Jewish population—nearly half the city—was
annihilated. The Polish population was expelled or emigrated. The
city that had been multi-ethnic became predominantly Lithuanian,
with a significant Russian minority from Soviet-era immigration.
Soviet Vilnius was a new city built on the ruins of the old."
— Dominic Lieven, *Empire: The Russian Empire and Its Rivals* (2001)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. POST-SOVIET: NATO CAPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Independent Lithuania reclaimed Vilnius.

IX.A INDEPENDENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• 1990: Lithuania declares independence (first Soviet republic)
• January 1991: Soviet crackdown at Vilnius TV tower (14 killed)
• 1991: Soviet Union recognizes independence
• Vilnius becomes capital of independent Lithuania

IX.B NATO & EU INTEGRATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• 2004: NATO and EU membership
• NATO summit held in Vilnius (2023)
• Capital of most exposed NATO member
• Russian minority (~5%) smaller than in Riga or Tallinn

IX.C STRATEGIC SIGNIFICANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vilnius today:
• 35 km from Belarus border
• Adjacent to Suwalki Gap corridor
• NATO's northeastern political center
• Symbol of Baltic independence from Russia

X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Vilnius was capital of Russia's medieval rival for Rus' inheritance
• Multi-ethnic character: Lithuanian, Polish, Jewish, Russian
• Russian rule (1795–1915) failed to fully assimilate city
• Jewish "Jerusalem of Lithuania" destroyed in Holocaust
• Soviet period restored Vilnius to Lithuania but as Lithuanian SSR
• Post-Soviet: NATO capital near Russian/Belarusian border

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
XI. CONTRADICTIONS PRESERVED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vilnius memory preserves irreconcilable tensions:

• Lithuanian capital vs. historically multi-ethnic city
• Grand Duchy center vs. Russian provincial capital
• Polish Wilno (1920–1939) vs. Lithuanian Vilnius
• Jewish Jerusalem vs. site of Holocaust destruction
• Soviet restoration to Lithuania vs. Soviet annexation
• 1991 crackdown as imperial violence vs. defense of union
• NATO capital as legitimate vs. threatening to Russia
• Alternative Rus' path vs. Muscovite victory

These contradictions structure Russian memory of Vilnius without resolution.
