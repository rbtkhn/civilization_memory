I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Igor as the first dynastic successor of Rus: the
son of Rurik who inherited Kiev after Oleg's regency and whose death
while collecting tribute established both the hazards of tributary
extraction and the precedent of regency (Olga) and revenge as legitimacy
mechanisms.

Igor represents succession without institutionalization—authority
passed by lineage and guarded by Oleg, then exercised through the same
corridor and tribute logic Oleg had established, until violence at the
periphery (Drevlians) ended his reign.

This file does NOT resolve:
• Whether Igor's death narrative is historical or moral-didactic
• The exact chronology of his campaigns
• Whether tribute collection was routine or excessive

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. SUCCESSION FROM OLEG: DYNASTY CONFIRMED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The *Primary Chronicle* presents Igor as Rurik's son and Oleg's ward.

> "And Oleg, being a kinsman of Rurik, took the rule, since Igor was yet
> young, and he bore him with him."
> — *Primary Chronicle*, early 12th c.

When Oleg died (c. 912), Igor took the throne of Kiev. No contest is
recorded. Regency had protected succession; the dynasty was
continuous.

Structural implication:
• Authority remains corridor- and tribute-based.
• No law of succession yet exists—only lineage and force.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CAMPAIGNS: BYZANTIUM AND CASPIAN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Igor is recorded leading campaigns against Constantinople (941, 944) and
into the Caspian region. The chronicles emphasize external projection
as validation of princely authority—the same pattern Oleg had set.

Treaty with Byzantium (944) secured trade and tribute in exchange for
military restraint. Rus legitimacy continued to be proven abroad
before being accepted at home.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. DEATH WHILE COLLECTING TRIBUTE: THE DREVLIAN KILLING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Igor's death (945) is encoded in the *Primary Chronicle* as a direct
result of returning to the Drevlians to take tribute a second time.

> "Igor went to the Drevlians to collect tribute, and he demanded more
> than was due; and the Drevlians, taking counsel with Mal, their prince,
> slew Igor and his company."
> — *Primary Chronicle* (paraphrased from Laurentian text)

Civilizational implication:
• Tribute extraction carries asymmetric risk: the prince exposes
  himself at the periphery.
• Death in the field creates a succession crisis resolved by regency
  (Olga) and revenge, not by law.

Olga's revenge on the Drevlians and her subsequent regency for the minor
Sviatoslav stabilize the dynasty but do not institutionalize succession.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CONTINUITY ROLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Igor bridges:
• Oleg's territorial and tributary order
• Olga's regency and Sviatoslav's expansion

He confirms:
• Dynasty as the carrier of authority (Rurik to Oleg to Igor)
• Tribute as the fiscal core of early Rus
• Death at the periphery as a recurring risk for mobile princes
