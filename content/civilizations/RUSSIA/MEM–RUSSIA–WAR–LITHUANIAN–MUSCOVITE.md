────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Lithuanian–Muscovite Wars as the **decisive
contest for the Rus’ inheritance**: a long, multi-phase struggle that
determined whether Eastern Slavic lands would be integrated under a
Catholic-Latin imperial framework or consolidated into an Orthodox,
autocratic Muscovite state.

For Russian civilizational memory, these wars are not peripheral border
conflicts but the **foundational crucible of reunification**, coercive
centralization, and sovereignty doctrine.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. LITHUANIA AS A COMPETING RUS’ EMPIRE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Grand Duchy of Lithuania was not an external invader but a **parallel
Rus’-bearing polity**.

Structural features:
• Ruthenian majority population
• Old Church Slavonic chancery language
• Orthodox elites alongside Catholic rulers
• Loose federative governance

Lithuania offered:
• Elite pluralism
• Decentralized authority
• Integration into Western Christendom

From Muscovy’s perspective, Lithuania represented a **dangerous near-self**:
familiar, legitimate, and therefore existentially competitive.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. MUSCOVY’S COERCIVE UNIFICATION MODEL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Muscovy advances an opposing logic:

• Autocratic sovereignty
• Coercive territorial absorption
• Elimination of rival Rus’ centers
• Fusion of Orthodoxy and princely authority

The Lithuanian–Muscovite Wars test which model of Rus’ continuity will
survive: **pluralist federation or coercive centralization**.

This is not a war of conquest alone, but a war over **what Rus’ is**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. EARLY CONFLICT PHASES (14TH–15TH c.)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Initial clashes (1368–1408) reveal asymmetry:

• Lithuania projects power widely but thinly
• Muscovy consolidates slowly but deeply
• Fortified centers and river corridors dominate outcomes

Lithuanian strength lies in reach.
Muscovite strength lies in **absorption and permanence**.

Temporary reversals do not alter the underlying structural trend.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. DYNASTIC, RELIGIOUS, & LEGAL DIMENSIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Religion functions as both bridge and fault line.

Key dynamics:
• Orthodox elites within Lithuania
• Catholic dynastic alignment after Krewo (1385)
• Gradual confessional polarization

As Lithuania integrates with Poland, Muscovy reframes the conflict as:
• Defense of Orthodoxy
• Protection of Rus’ lands from Latin domination

This reframing consolidates internal legitimacy even as it simplifies
complex realities.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. HISTORIOGRAPHICAL INTERPRETATION (CATEGORY C)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**Sergei Solovyov — *History of Russia***:

> “The struggle with Lithuania was not for territory alone but for the
> soul of Rus’; two paths lay open, and history chose the harsher, more
> unified road.”

**Vasily Klyuchevsky — *Course of Russian History***:

> “Lithuania gathered Rus’ lands without remaking them; Moscow gathered
> them by breaking their independence, and therein lay its future
> strength.”

Russian historiography emphasizes **coercive unity over consensual
pluralism**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. TURNING POINTS & TERRITORIAL ABSORPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Key moments include:
• Capture of Smolensk (1514)
• Progressive absorption of Upper Oka principalities
• Decline of Lithuanian military initiative

These gains are not isolated victories but **cumulative structural
captures**.

Once absorbed, territories do not revert.

Muscovite expansion is **sticky**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. FAILURE MODES & COUNTERFACTUAL TENSIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Lithuanian failure modes:
• Overextension
• Elite autonomy undermining coordination
• Reliance on dynastic compromise
• Incomplete confessional consolidation

Muscovite risks:
• Internal repression costs
• Elite resistance
• Economic strain

The outcome is not inevitable; it is **selected under pressure**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CIVILIZATIONAL CONSEQUENCES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The wars produce lasting consequences:

• Elimination of rival Rus’ centers
• Hardening of sovereignty doctrine
• Association of unity with coercion
• Long-term suspicion of federative models

Pluralism survives culturally but not politically.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. MODERN SCHOLARSHIP (CATEGORY D)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**Geoffrey Hosking**:

> “Moscow’s victory over Lithuania was decisive not because it was
> preordained, but because it institutionalized a model of unity based on
> domination rather than consent.”

**Dominic Lieven**:

> “The Lithuanian–Muscovite struggle fixed the authoritarian trajectory
> of the Russian state by eliminating credible alternative paths of
> political development within the Rus’ world.”

Modern historians emphasize **path dependence**, not destiny.
