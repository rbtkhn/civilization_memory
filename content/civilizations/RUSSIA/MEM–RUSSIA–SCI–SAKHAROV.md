I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Andrei Sakharov as **Russia's scientist as
restraint voice**: the physicist who co-developed the Soviet hydrogen
bomb and then turned to dissent, human rights, and opposition to
nuclear recklessness—articulating a **Panin-type** option (restraint,
conscience, openness) that the sovereign rejected until the Gorbachev
era.

Sakharov delivered the weapon; then he spoke against its misuse.

For Russian civilizational memory, Sakharov encodes **RLL–RUSSIA–0033**
(Sovereign Restraint vs Expansion): an advisor (here, the scientist as
moral voice) articulates restraint; the sovereign may adopt or override.
Sakharov articulated restraint—test bans, human rights, glasnost—and
was punished (exile to Gorky 1980–1986); Gorbachev later released him
and allowed his return to politics. The MEM does not resolve whether
his turn was "too late" or "just in time"; it preserves the pattern:
**restraint articulated, overridden, then partially vindicated**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FORMATIVE CONDITIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sakharov emerged from Soviet physics in the postwar period.

Context:
• Moscow State University; physics during the war
• FIAN (Lebedev Physical Institute); theoretical physics
• Joined the thermonuclear program (Tamm group); key contributions to
  Soviet H-bomb (1953, 1955)
• Moral and political turn from the late 1950s; test-ban advocacy;
  *Progress, Coexistence, and Intellectual Freedom* (1968)
• Human rights activism; Nobel Peace Prize (1975); internal exile
  (1980–1986); released by Gorbachev (1986); deputy in Congress (1989);
  died December 1989

He was **inside** the weapon project before he was **against** its
unchecked use and against the system that had built it. His authority
as a critic rested on his prior role as a creator.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. RESTRAINT ARTICULATED (MERCOURIS FRAME)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
From a legitimacy and institutional-continuity perspective, Sakharov
embodies the **scientist as conscience**—the figure who uses the
authority conferred by technical achievement to argue for limits. It
seems to me that the crucial point is that he did not merely defect
from the project; he **reframed** it. Test bans, coexistence, and
intellectual freedom were not anti-Soviet in his formulation; they
were conditions for survival. The legitimacy he claimed was that of the
expert who had seen the weapon and therefore had the right to speak
about its use. The state's response—harassment, then exile—was the
**override**: the sovereign (Brezhnev, Andropov) chose to silence the
restraint voice. Gorbachev's release of Sakharov was a partial
**adoption**: the new sovereign allowed the voice back into the
political space, though the system was already unravelling. Institutional
continuity here is ambiguous: the scientific establishment had produced
both the weapon and the critic; the state had punished the critic and
then, under a new leader, unbanned him. Tension preserved.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL FRAME (MEARSHEIMER COGNITION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Structurally, Sakharov's position was determined by **after** the
balance-of-power imperative had been met. The Soviet Union had the
bomb and the H-bomb; parity was achieved. The marginal value of
further tests and secrecy was declining; the marginal cost (moral,
political, environmental) was rising. Sakharov's restraint argument—
test bans, reduced confrontation—was structurally rational from a
state-interest view: avoid unnecessary escalation, preserve resources.
The sovereign, however, was optimising for **regime survival** and
ideological control, not only for strategic efficiency. So the
structural reading: Sakharov articulated a restraint option that was
**aligned with long-run state interest** but **misaligned with
short-run regime interest**. The sovereign overrode; the outcome
(exhaustion, collapse, then Gorbachev) came later. RLL–0033 holds:
restraint articulated, overridden; exhaustion followed; partial
vindication under a new sovereign.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LIABILITY AND MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: Sakharov had no formal political authority until 1989;
his authority was **moral and expert**. He spoke as a scientist and
citizen; the state had jurisdiction over his person (exile, deprivation
of honours).

Liability: Who was on the hook for the weapon? Sakharov accepted
moral responsibility by turning against its unchecked use; he did not
deny his role in creating it. The **sovereign** bore responsibility for
the decision to build and to test; the **scientist** bore responsibility
for the design—and, in Sakharov's case, for the later advocacy of
restraint. When the state exiled him, it was assigning **blame** to him
(for dissent), not accepting blame for the weapon. Irreversibility: the
nuclear knowledge and arsenal could not be undone; Sakharov's turn
did not reverse the bomb, it only added a voice for limits. The MEM
records the liability split: sovereign decides; scientist delivers,
then may dissent; the state may punish the dissenter.

VI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Scientist as restraint voice (Panin-type): articulate limits;
  sovereign may adopt or override; override does not erase the
  articulation.
• RLL–0033: Sakharov is the clearest Russian case of restraint
  articulated by a non-diplomat, overridden, then partially vindicated
  under a new sovereign (Gorbachev).
• Contrast with Kurchatov: Kurchatov delivered and stayed inside the
  system; Sakharov delivered and then spoke—and was punished.
• Cross-civ: Planck (Germania) chose quiet stewardship under catastrophe;
  Sakharov chose open dissent. Both faced sovereigns who overrode
  restraint; responses differed.
