I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Sergei Prokofiev as **Russia's returning prodigal**:
the composer who left revolutionary Russia, achieved international
success in exile, then voluntarily returned to Stalin's Soviet Union—
a decision that defined his late career and tragic end.

Prokofiev represents **the paradox of chosen captivity**.

For Russian civilizational memory, Prokofiev demonstrates that even
genius cannot escape the structural constraints of Russian geography
and power—the gravitational pull of the motherland.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. EARLY BRILLIANCE AND DEPARTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Prokofiev emerged as enfant terrible of Russian music.

Early characteristics:
• Conservatory prodigy (Saint Petersburg)
• "Barbaric" modernism (*Scythian Suite*, 1915)
• Percussive piano style
• Sardonic humor and grotesquerie

Departure (1918):
• Left Russia during Civil War
• Official permission obtained
• International career: USA, Paris, Germany
• Major works abroad: operas, ballets, symphonies

Orlando Figes notes the departure logic:

"Prokofiev left Russia not as a political refugee but as an
ambitious artist seeking larger stages. He did not burn bridges
but maintained contacts, returning for visits throughout the
1920s. Unlike Stravinsky, who severed ties, Prokofiev always
imagined himself coming home."
— Orlando Figes, *Natasha's Dance: A Cultural History of Russia* (2002)

Departure without rupture.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE FATAL RETURN (1936)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Prokofiev permanently returned to the Soviet Union in 1936—
the year of the *Pravda* attack on Shostakovich.

Return motivations:
• Soviet cultural prestige projects (commissions, resources)
• Homesickness and Russian identity
• Declining Western career (Depression-era economics)
• Soviet propaganda encouraging return

The timing was catastrophic:
• Terror at its height
• Artistic constraints tightening
• Escape now impossible

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III.A REALIST SHARPENING: THE TRAP
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Mercouris reading frames the return as cultural homecoming—
Prokofiev drawn back by Russian identity and artistic community.

The realist sharpening adds: the return was a trap.

Power distribution logic:
• Soviet regime actively recruited émigré talent
• Return demonstrated regime legitimacy to world
• Once inside, departure became impossible
• Prokofiev's international prestige was captured

The regime gained:
• World-class composer producing Soviet content
• Propaganda victory (genius "chooses" Soviet system)
• Hostage whose family ensured compliance

Prokofiev's first wife Lina was arrested (1948) and sent to Gulag.
The trap was complete.

The structural insight: Prokofiev's "choice" operated within a
coercive field he did not understand until too late.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. SOVIET WORKS: CONSTRAINT AND ACHIEVEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Prokofiev's Soviet-era works navigated ideological constraint.

Major works:
• *Romeo and Juliet* ballet (1936) — universally beloved
• *Alexander Nevsky* cantata (1939) — film collaboration with Eisenstein
• *War and Peace* opera (1943–1952) — epic patriotic work
• Symphony No. 5 (1944) — wartime triumph
• *Peter and the Wolf* (1936) — children's classic

These works achieved genuine artistic success while fulfilling
propaganda functions. The tension was productive.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. 1948: ZHDANOV DECREE AND HUMILIATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The 1948 Zhdanov decree condemned Prokofiev, Shostakovich, and
others for "formalism."

Consequences:
• Public humiliation and forced confession
• Works banned or restricted
• Income severely reduced
• Health deteriorating

Prokofiev's "confession" (February 1948):

"I am grateful to the Central Committee for the clear directives
which help me find the correct path..."
— Prokofiev, public statement, 1948

The confession was survival, not belief.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. DEATH ON STALIN'S DAY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Prokofiev died March 5, 1953—the same day as Stalin.

The coincidence was erasure:
• Prokofiev's death overshadowed entirely
• No flowers available (all for Stalin)
• Minimal attendance at funeral
• State attention focused on Stalin's mourning

The symbolic logic:
→ The artist disappears into the leader's shadow.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Prokofiev encodes durable Russian lessons:

• The pull of homeland can override rational calculation
• Return to Russia is easier than departure from Russia
• Constraint can produce great art but destroys the artist
• Regime prestige projects capture individual genius
• Timing determines fate (1936 vs 1918)
• Even the prodigal son cannot escape structural power

Prokofiev is **Russia's lesson in captive genius**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Voluntary return becomes involuntary captivity.
• Regimes recruit prestige as legitimacy resource.
• Constraint can coexist with genuine artistic achievement.
• Timing is structural: 1936 was not 1918.
• Family hostages ensure compliance.
• Death is absorbed into greater state narratives.
