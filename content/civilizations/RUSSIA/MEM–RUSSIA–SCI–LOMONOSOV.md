I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Mikhail Lomonosov as **Russia's institutionaliser
of science after rupture**: the polymath who founded Moscow University
(1755), advanced chemistry, physics, and rhetoric, and embodied the
conversion of Petrine coercive modernisation into durable institutional
and intellectual capital.

Lomonosov builds order in the wake of Peter's rupture.

For Russian civilizational memory, Lomonosov represents **institutions
that outlast the sovereign**—state-sponsored science and higher
education as the legacy of rupture rather than its repudiation. Cross-
civilizational contrast: Germania's Planck discovers rupture while
attempting conservation; Lomonosov builds institutions after rupture
has already been imposed (MEM–GERMANY–SCI–PLANCK).

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FORMATIVE CONDITIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Lomonosov emerged from the margins of the Petrine and post-Petrine state.

Context:
• Peasant origin; Arkhangelsk region (White Sea)
• Walked to Moscow (1730) to study; Slavonic-Greek-Latin Academy
• Marburg and Freiberg (1736–1741); training in European science
• Return to St Petersburg Academy of Sciences (1741)

He was trained in the very Western systems that Peter had forced open;
his career is the **institutional harvest** of that opening. The state
had created the Academy (1724) and the need for native scholars;
Lomonosov filled the role.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. INSTITUTIONAL FOUNDATION (MERCOURIS FRAME)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
From a legitimacy and institutional-continuity perspective, Lomonosov
embodies the **stabilisation of science as state project**. Moscow
University (1755), established with his advocacy and under Elizabeth,
became the first Russian university and a lasting vehicle for elite
formation. It seems to me that the crucial point is that this was not
rupture for its own sake—it was **order built on top of rupture**.
Peter had broken the old equilibrium; Lomonosov helped build the
institutions that would make the new one durable. Chemistry, grammar,
history, rhetoric: all were harnessed to the idea of a Russian
Enlightenment that could stand alongside European centres without
merely imitating them. The Academy and the University outlasted their
founders; they became the infrastructure of Russian intellectual life.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL FRAME (MEARSHEIMER COGNITION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Structurally, Lomonosov's position was determined by the **state's
need for native scientific capacity**. Peter had imported experts and
institutions; the successor state needed domestically trained cadres to
reduce dependence and to legitimise imperial claims. The incentive
structure was clear: the Academy and the University served the state's
interest in competence and prestige. Lomonosov's polymathic output—
from atmospheric electricity to mosaic art to grammar—was the supply
side of that demand. No sovereign had to choose between "restraint" and
"expansion" in the Panin sense; the choice was already made (Peter's
rupture). Lomonosov's role was to **consolidate** the gains: to make
Russian science and letters institutionally sustainable. That is the
structural reading: post-rupture consolidation, not restraint at the
threshold.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LIABILITY AND MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: Lomonosov operated under sovereign and Academy authority;
his foundation of Moscow University required imperial approval
(Elizabeth). He was not an independent actor—he was a state-sponsored
builder.

Liability: The "who is on the hook" question applies to institutional
legacy. If the institutions he helped build later served repression or
imperial overreach, the architect is not thereby liable for every use—
but the state that funded and directed him bears the continuity of
authority. Lomonosov himself faced internal Academy rivalries and
political constraints; his success was a function of alignment with
state priorities. Irreversibility: once Moscow University existed, it
could not be un-invented; the institutional fact outlasted the man. The
MEM records the pattern without assigning moral blame for later uses.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Science and higher education in Russia were institutionalised in the
  **aftermath** of coercive modernisation, not as its alternative.
• Lomonosov encodes "build order after rupture"—contrast to Planck's
  "discover rupture while conserving" (Germania).
• Moscow University and the Academy became durable infrastructure;
  they outlasted Lomonosov and the sovereigns of his day.
• Cross-civ: Germania (Planck) and Russia (Lomonosov) both produce
  institutional continuity, but from opposite temporal positions—
  Planck at the threshold of rupture, Lomonosov after rupture.
