I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Sviatoslav I as **the last pagan expansionist of
Kievan Rus'**: the prince who campaigned against the Khazars, Bulgars,
and Byzantium, refused Christianization, and died at the hands of
Pechenegs at the Dnieper rapids—encoding **expansion without sacral
integration** and the vulnerability of the prince when absent from the
centre.

Sviatoslav represents **mobile, martial legitimacy**—authority
validated by campaign and conquest rather than by ritual, law, or
capital. His reign precedes Vladimir I's baptism; Rus' under Sviatoslav
remains religiously and institutionally unconsolidated.

This file does NOT resolve:
• The moral character of his campaigns
• Whether his refusal of baptism was ideological or tactical
• The exact sequence of his Balkan and steppe operations

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ACCESSION: OLGA'S REGENCY AND SVIATOSLAV'S MAJORITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sviatoslav was the son of Igor and Olga. After Igor's death (945), Olga
ruled as regent and avenged the Drevlians. When Sviatoslav came of age
(c. 962), he took full military authority.

The *Primary Chronicle* and Byzantine sources depict him as **perpetually
on campaign**, leaving Kiev and its administration to others. Authority
is exercised through movement and battle, not through fixed capital
governance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CAMPAIGNS: KHAZARS, VOLGA BULGARS, DANUBE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sviatoslav's recorded campaigns include:
• Destruction or severe weakening of the Khazar Khaganate (960s)
• Campaigns against the Volga Bulgars
• Invasion of Danube Bulgaria and conflict with Byzantium (968–971)

These operations extended Rus' reach but did not create durable
administrative or sacral integration. Conquest supplied **prestige and
plunder**, not institutional depth.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PECHENEG SIEGE OF KIEV (968) AND THE COST OF ABSENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
While Sviatoslav was on the Danube, the Pechenegs besieged Kiev. Olga
and Sviatoslav's sons held the city. The prince was forced to return to
relieve the siege.

This episode encodes a structural lesson:
**Imperial ambition abroad creates vulnerability at the centre.**

Rus' had no standing defensive apparatus independent of the prince's
retinue. When the prince was away, the capital was exposed. (See
MEM–RUSSIA–WAR–PECHENEGS.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. DEATH AT THE DNIEPER RAPIDS (972)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Returning from the Balkans, Sviatoslav was ambushed and killed by
Pechenegs at the Dnieper rapids (portage zone). The chronicle tradition
reports that the Pecheneg khan had a cup made from Sviatoslav's skull.

Structural implication:
• The prince's **mobility is his vulnerability**.
• Death at a chokepoint (rapids, portage) becomes a recurring motif in
  Rus' memory—the ruler exposed outside the capital.

Sviatoslav's sons (Yaropolk, Oleg, Vladimir) then contested the
succession; Vladimir prevailed and later undertook Christianization.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONTINUITY ROLE: PRE-SACRAL EXPANSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sviatoslav bridges:
• Igor's tribute-based order and Olga's regency
• Vladimir I's sacral consolidation

He embodies:
• Expansion as legitimacy without sacral or legal coding
• The limits of purely martial authority—no trans-personal legitimacy
• The geographic and strategic risks of overextension
