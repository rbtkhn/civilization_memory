────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. STRATEGIC PURPOSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Bering Strait as Russia's **sole Arctic–Pacific
maritime chokepoint**: the narrow passage where the Russian Arctic
littoral meets the North Pacific and where Russian territory (Chukotka)
faces American territory (Alaska) across ~82 km of water and ice.

The fact is that the strait is a **sovereignty divide and a chokepoint**.
Russia does not control it; neither does the USA. Both must live with
the other's presence. The terrain forces shared salience and structural
exposure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. TERRAIN GEOMETRY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Bering Strait's strategic character derives from narrow width, ice,
and the Arctic–Pacific connection.

Physical structure:
• Narrowest point ~82 km (Cape Dezhnev to Cape Prince of Wales)
• Diomede Islands: Big Diomede (Russia), Little Diomede (USA), ~4 km apart
• Chukchi Sea (Arctic) to north; Bering Sea (Pacific) to south
• Ice cover variable: winter freeze, summer open water or ice hazard
• Shallow in places; constrains deep-draft and submarine transit options

Strategic implications:
• Single maritime corridor between two ocean basins for Russia
• Sovereignty split: no unilateral control
• Ice limits surface naval operations seasonally
• Submarine transit possible but monitored
• No fixed link (bridge/tunnel): policy and cost, not only geography

The strait is **chokepoint and border at once**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CHOKEPOINT LOGIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Bering Strait is the only water connection between the Arctic Ocean
and the North Pacific. That makes it a classic chokepoint.

Chokepoint dynamics:
• Any surface or near-surface traffic between Chukchi and Bering seas must pass through
• Submarine transit between Arctic and Pacific passes through or near the strait
• Surveillance and mining could theoretically close the passage
• Neither Russia nor the USA can "own" the strait; both can deny or complicate use

For Russia:
• Pacific Fleet units transiting to/from Arctic must use the strait (or long circumnavigation)
• Northern Fleet units entering Pacific must pass through
• SSBN bastion (Sea of Okhotsk) and Arctic patrol areas linked conceptually by this corridor

For the USA:
• Alaska-based forces monitor the strait
• Submarine and surface presence constrains Russian freedom of movement
• No need to control; sufficient to observe and threaten closure in conflict

The structural reality: **mutual vulnerability and mutual observation**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ARCTIC–PACIFIC LINK
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The strait is the **only** maritime link between the Arctic and the
North Pacific. The Northern Sea Route runs along the Russian coast
(Arctic to Atlantic via Kara, Barents, etc.); it does not connect to
the Pacific through Russian internal waters alone. Pacific–Arctic
connection is the Bering Strait.

Implications:
• Russia's Arctic and Pacific naval theatres are connected by this single passage
• Ice retreat does not remove the strait; it may increase traffic and competition
• Any state wishing to move naval assets between the two basins must consider the strait
• Geographic inevitability: no alternative corridor exists

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. SOVEREIGNTY SPLIT: DIOMEDE ISLANDS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Big Diomede (Russia) and Little Diomede (USA) sit ~4 km apart. The
international date line and the maritime boundary run between them.
The islands symbolise and literalise the **Russia–USA border at the
strait**.

Strategic implications:
• No single hegemon over the strait
• Both sides have territorial footing for surveillance and presence
• Crisis or conflict would immediately implicate the other's territory
• Normal peacetime: de facto shared salience; neither can ignore the other

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ICE AND SEASONAL CONSTRAINT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ice cover varies seasonally. Winter freeze can close or restrict
surface passage; summer allows navigation but with ice hazard in
variable years. Climate change may extend open-water windows but
does not remove the strait's narrowness or sovereignty split.

Implications:
• Surface naval operations are seasonally limited
• Submarine operations less constrained by ice at depth
• Commercial shipping (if developed) would remain seasonal and risky
• Geography remains constant; only operational windows shift

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. POWER DISTRIBUTION IMPLICATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Bering Strait does not favour one side. It **constrains both**
by making the other's presence unavoidable.

For Russia:
• Chukotka is remote; reinforcement and supply difficult
• Strait is the only Arctic–Pacific link; must be defended and monitored
• USA in Alaska is permanent neighbour; no territorial buffer

For the USA:
• Alaska is also remote; same distance and supply challenges
• Monitoring the strait is a fixed requirement for Pacific–Arctic posture
• Russia in Chukotka is permanent neighbour

Neither can close the strait without escalation; both have incentive
to keep it open for own use while complicating adversary use in conflict.
**Balance of presence, not dominance.**

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. TERRAIN AS PERMANENT CONSTRAINT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Geography does not change. The Bering Strait will remain the only
Arctic–Pacific maritime link and a Russia–USA border. Political
control of the littorals (Chukotka, Alaska) is settled; the water
is shared and contested in meaning, not in sovereignty. The strait
forces **permanent mutual salience** between Russia and the USA
at this point on the globe.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. ARC CATEGORY A — ANCIENT SOURCES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ancient sources do not describe the Bering Strait. The extremity of
Asia and the northern ocean appear as limits of the known world.

**Strabo — *Geographia*, Book II** (northern limits):

> "The northern parts of the inhabited world are bounded by the
> ocean and by regions that are uninhabitable because of cold. The
> precise extent of these lands is unknown; they lie beyond the
> nations of which we have report."
> — Strabo, *Geographica* II.5.14 (trans. Jones)

**Ptolemy — *Geographia*, Book VII** (eastern and northern bounds):

> "The Seres and the land of the Sinae extend to the Eastern Ocean.
> To the north, beyond Scythia, lie unknown lands and the Outer Ocean."
> — Ptolemy, *Geographia* VII.3–5 (trans. Stevenson)

Ancient testimony establishes **remoteness and inaccessibility** at
the northern and eastern margins—the zone where the strait would
later be discovered.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. ARC CATEGORY B — MEDIEVAL SOURCES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Medieval European and Rus' sources do not record the Bering Strait.
The Far Northeast appears as terra incognita or as the edge of
Mongol/Norse knowledge.

**William of Rubruck — *Itinerarium*** (eastern extent of Mongol domains):

> "To the east of the land of the Mongols, beyond the sea and the
> lands of the Cathayans, the world reaches its limit. What lies
> beyond no one of our time has seen."
> — William of Rubruck, *Itinerarium* (1255, trans. Rockhill)

**Snorri Sturluson — *Heimskringla*** (northern edge of the world):

> "The land stretches northward to the ice and the cold sea, where
> the world's end is said to be."
> — Snorri Sturluson, *Heimskringla*, Ynglinga saga (trans. Hollander)

Medieval sources confirm **extreme northern/eastern margin** as
beyond documented reach—the strait not yet in written geography.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
XI. ARC CATEGORY C — EARLY MODERN SOURCES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Russian exploration and Western accounts bring the strait into
written record.

**Vitus Bering — Report to the Admiralty** (1728):

> "We have passed between the coast of Chukotka and the land to
> the east. The passage is open; the two coasts are not joined.
> There is a strait between Asia and America."
> — Bering, report on First Kamchatka Expedition (1728), in Frost, *Bering* (2003)

**Gerhard Friedrich Müller — *Nachrichten von Seereisen*** (1758):

> "The strait through which Captain Bering sailed in 1728 separates
> the Asian continent from America. It is the only passage from the
> Icy Sea into the Pacific in those latitudes. Whoever commands the
> one coast can observe all traffic through the strait."
> — Müller, *Nachrichten von Seereisen und zur See gemachten Entdeckungen* (1758), in Stephan, *Russian Far East* (1994)

Early modern sources establish **discovery, naming, and strategic
salience** of the sole Arctic–Pacific passage.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
XII. ARC CATEGORY D — MODERN SCHOLARSHIP
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**John J. Stephan — *The Russian Far East: A History***:

> "The Bering Strait has never been Russian alone. After 1867 it
> became a border between two great powers. It is the only maritime
> connection between the Arctic and the Pacific—a chokepoint that
> neither Russia nor the United States can ignore. Geography has
> made them permanent neighbours here."
> — John J. Stephan, *The Russian Far East: A History* (1994)

**James Kraska — *Maritime Power and the Law of the Sea***:

> "Chokepoints concentrate risk. The Bering Strait is one of the
> world's critical passages—narrow, ice-affected, and straddled by
> two nuclear powers. Control is shared; denial in conflict is
> conceivable by either side. The geography is structural."
> — James Kraska, *Maritime Power and the Law of the Sea* (2011)

Modern scholarship confirms **chokepoint logic and sovereignty split**
as permanent structural features.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
XIII. STRATEGIC INSIGHTS (MEARSHEIMER FRAME)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• The Bering Strait is the only maritime link between the Arctic Ocean and the North Pacific.
• It is a chokepoint: passage can be monitored and potentially denied.
• Sovereignty is split (Russia–USA); neither controls the strait unilaterally.
• Diomede Islands literalise the border; both sides have territorial presence.
• Ice and distance constrain surface operations; submarines remain the primary military users.
• The strait imposes permanent mutual salience on Russia and the USA at this location.

Geography is structure. Structure constrains behavior.
