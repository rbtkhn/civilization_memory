────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Siberia as a **civilizational process**, not merely
a space: the long arc by which Russia conquered, administered, settled,
extracted, exiled into, mythologized, and industrialized an immense
territory—thereby reshaping Russian governance, coercion, and identity.

Siberia is remembered as **imperial practice in motion**: a laboratory
where distance reduced resistance, scarcity disciplined power, and
scale normalized coercive administration.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ENTRY & CONQUEST (COSSACK PHASE)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Russian entry into Siberia begins as opportunistic penetration.

Key dynamics:
• Cossack-led expeditions (e.g., Yermak)
• Riverine advance and fort placement
• Defeat and incorporation of khanates
• Minimal settlement, maximal reach

Conquest is fast because resistance is dispersed.

Imperial axiom:
→ Control precedes integration.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. TRIBUTE, EXTRACTION, & THIN RULE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Early Siberia is governed through **extraction, not assimilation**.

Mechanisms:
• Yasak (fur tribute) imposed on indigenous peoples
• Forts as administrative anchors
• Small garrisons enforcing vast claims

The state values **output over presence**.

This produces durable habits of:
• Fiscal coercion
• Delegated violence
• Peripheral neglect

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. INDIGENOUS PEOPLES & IMPERIAL ASYMMETRY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Indigenous societies encounter overwhelming asymmetry.

Patterns:
• Tribute without representation
• Cultural displacement
• Episodic violence
• Administrative invisibility

Memory emphasizes:
• Survival without incorporation
• Loss without annihilation
• Empire without intimacy

Siberia trains Russia to rule populations it does not fully know.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. EXILE, PUNISHMENT, & SOCIAL ENGINEERING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Siberia becomes a **penal geography**.

Functions:
• Political exile
• Criminal punishment
• Forced labor
• Social isolation

Exile logic:
→ Distance substitutes for execution.

This normalizes:
• Administrative cruelty
• Bureaucratic indifference
• Moral externalization

The state exports disorder to preserve core stability.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. SETTLEMENT, MYTH, & NATIONAL PSYCHOLOGY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Over time, Siberia acquires symbolic weight.

Myths:
• Frontier hardship
• Moral purification through suffering
• Renewal through endurance
• Space as destiny

Settlement is framed as:
• Sacrifice
• Service
• National expansion

Siberia becomes both **punishment and promise**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. INDUSTRIALIZATION & TOTAL MOBILIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Soviet era intensifies Siberia’s function.

Key shifts:
• Gulag system expansion
• Resource megaprojects
• Wartime industrial relocation
• Strategic redundancy

Siberia becomes:
• Industrial reserve
• Population sink
• Strategic fallback

Coercion scales to match territory.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. POST-SOVIET CONTINUITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After 1991, patterns persist.

Continuities:
• Resource extraction dominance
• Sparse governance
• Infrastructure over society
• Peripheral depopulation

Siberia remains essential yet distant.

Empire recedes, but habits endure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CIVILIZATIONAL EFFECTS ON RUSSIA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Siberia reshapes Russian statecraft.

Enduring effects:
• Acceptance of thin control
• Comfort with coercive distance
• Reliance on space as solution
• Externalization of suffering

Russia learns to govern **through removal**, not inclusion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Empire can expand faster than it integrates.
• Distance reduces moral constraint.
• Extraction-first governance creates durable coercive habits.
• Exile stabilizes the core at the cost of the periphery.
• Siberia becomes both shield and shadow of Russian power.

Tension preserved without verdict.
