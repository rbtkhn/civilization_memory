I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Bolshevik Revolution as **the rupture that
redefined Russian civilization**: the seizure of power that ended
tsarist continuity, established communist rule, and created the
Soviet experiment that would dominate the twentieth century.

The Revolution represents **regime discontinuity within civilizational
continuity**.

For Russian civilizational memory, October 1917 is the hinge—the
moment when one legitimacy system ended and another began, yet
the underlying patterns of Russian civilization persisted beneath
the ideological transformation.

This MEM preserves:
• The collapse of tsarist authority (February–October 1917)
• The Bolshevik seizure of power
• The immediate consolidation of communist rule
• The revolution's meaning for Russian civilizational continuity

What this MEM does NOT resolve:
• Whether the revolution was liberation or catastrophe
• Whether continuity or rupture predominates
• Whether alternative outcomes were possible

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT: THE COLLAPSE OF TSARISM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Bolshevik Revolution followed the February Revolution's destruction
of imperial authority.

February 1917:
• Bread riots in Petrograd
• Military mutiny
• Nicholas II's abdication
• Provisional Government established

Orlando Figes describes the collapse:

"The Russian Empire did not fall to revolution—it collapsed from
exhaustion. Three years of war had broken the army, bankrupted the
treasury, and shattered the tsar's authority. When the Petrograd
garrison refused to fire on demonstrators, the regime was finished.
Nicholas abdicated not because revolutionaries defeated him but
because no one would defend him. The Provisional Government inherited
a state that had already ceased to function."
— Orlando Figes, *A People's Tragedy* (1996)

The Bolsheviks inherited **not a functioning state but a vacuum**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE SEIZURE OF POWER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The October Revolution was a coup executed against a failing government.

October 25–26, 1917:
• Bolshevik Red Guards seize key points in Petrograd
• Winter Palace stormed (minimal resistance)
• Provisional Government arrested
• Lenin announces Soviet power at Congress of Soviets

Alexander Rabinowitch describes the event:

"The October Revolution was neither the spontaneous mass uprising of
Bolshevik mythology nor the conspiratorial putsch of anti-communist
historiography. It was a carefully planned seizure of power executed
by a disciplined party that had built genuine support among workers
and soldiers. The Bolsheviks won because they promised peace, land,
and bread—and because their opponents could promise nothing except
continued war and deferred reform."
— Alexander Rabinowitch, *The Bolsheviks Come to Power* (1976)

The revolution was **planned but also contingent on context**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III.A POLYPHONIC TENSION: REVOLUTION VS COUP
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Mercouris reading frames October as genuine revolution: mass
support, popular legitimacy, the culmination of decades of revolutionary
struggle against autocracy.

The realist sharpening adds: the seizure was a power grab in a collapsing
state.

• Mercouris: The Bolsheviks had real support—workers, soldiers, and
  peasants wanted what Lenin promised; the revolution expressed
  popular will, however imperfectly; legitimacy was earned, not
  merely claimed
• Mearsheimer: Power abhors a vacuum; the Provisional Government was
  failing; the Bolsheviks were the most organized faction willing to
  act; any disciplined group could have seized power in October 1917

Both readings capture truth:
• The Bolsheviks did have genuine support in key constituencies
• The seizure was also opportunistic exploitation of state collapse
• Popular legitimacy and strategic opportunism coexisted
• The revolution was both expression of mass politics and vanguard action

Tension preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CONSOLIDATION: FROM POWER TO RULE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Seizing power was easier than holding it.

Consolidation measures:
• Peace with Germany (Brest-Litovsk, March 1918)
• Land decree (redistribution to peasants)
• Nationalization of banks and industry
• Cheka established (secret police)
• Constituent Assembly dissolved (January 1918)

Richard Pipes describes the logic:

"The Bolsheviks consolidated power through a combination of popular
measures and ruthless coercion. They gave peasants land they had
already seized; they ended a war the army had already lost. But they
also suppressed opposition, closed non-Bolshevik newspapers, and
dissolved the elected Constituent Assembly when it refused to ratify
their rule. Within months, the 'dictatorship of the proletariat'
became dictatorship of the party—and soon, dictatorship of one man."
— Richard Pipes, *The Russian Revolution* (1990)

The revolution's democratic promise died in its first winter.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVIL WAR: SURVIVAL THROUGH TERROR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The revolution's survival required war against multiple enemies.

Civil War (1918–1922):
• White armies (monarchist, liberal, socialist opposition)
• Allied intervention (Britain, France, US, Japan)
• National separatist movements
• Peasant uprisings against requisitioning

Evan Mawdsley describes Bolshevik victory:

"The Bolsheviks won the Civil War not because they were stronger than
all their enemies combined but because their enemies never combined.
The Whites were geographically dispersed, ideologically divided, and
politically unimaginative. The Bolsheviks held the center, controlled
the railways, and maintained unified command. They were also willing
to use terror systematically—against class enemies, against wavering
allies, against their own population when necessary."
— Evan Mawdsley, *The Russian Civil War* (1987)

The revolution survived because **alternatives were worse organized**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL CONTINUITY BENEATH RUPTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The revolution changed the regime but not the civilization.

Continuities preserved:
• Centralized autocratic authority
• State dominance over society
• Suspicion of Western models
• Imperial territorial ambitions
• Messianic ideological claims

Sheila Fitzpatrick explains the pattern:

"The Soviet system that emerged from the revolution bore striking
resemblance to the tsarist system it replaced. The party replaced the
autocracy; the Politburo replaced the tsar's council; the Cheka
replaced the Okhrana. The rhetoric was new—class struggle instead of
divine right—but the structures of power were familiar. Russia
remained what it had been: an authoritarian state ruling a vast
territory through centralized bureaucracy and systematic coercion."
— Sheila Fitzpatrick, *The Russian Revolution* (2017)

The revolution was **rupture in continuity**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI.A POLYPHONIC TENSION: TRANSFORMATION VS RECURRENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Mercouris reading frames the revolution as genuine transformation:
new ideology, new social structures, new possibilities—even if outcomes
disappointed, the break was real.

The realist sharpening adds: structural patterns reasserted themselves.

• Mercouris: The revolution genuinely transformed Russia—industrialization,
  literacy, social mobility, changed gender roles; the Soviet system
  was not tsarism in red clothing; ideology mattered
• Mearsheimer: Geography and power imperatives remained constant; any
  regime governing Russia's territory would face similar pressures;
  centralization, militarization, and territorial control are structural
  requirements, not ideological choices

Both readings are valid:
• The revolution did produce genuine social transformation
• Structural continuities persisted beneath ideological change
• Ideology shaped choices within structural constraints
• Russia remained recognizably Russian under Soviet rule

Tension preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LEGITIMACY TRANSFORMATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The revolution replaced one legitimacy system with another.

Tsarist legitimacy:
• Divine right
• Dynastic succession
• Orthodox Christianity
• Traditional authority

Soviet legitimacy:
• Scientific socialism
• Party vanguard
• Historical necessity
• Revolutionary authority

Stephen Kotkin describes the new system:

"The Bolsheviks built a new legitimacy on the claim that they
understood history's direction. Marxism-Leninism was not merely
ideology but science—the party knew the laws of historical development
and therefore had the right to guide society toward its inevitable
destination. This claim justified unlimited power: opposition was not
merely political disagreement but resistance to history itself."
— Stephen Kotkin, *Stalin: Volume I* (2014)

The revolution replaced one absolute claim with another.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Bolshevik Revolution occupies a contested place in Russian memory.

Encoded meanings (Soviet/nostalgic):
• Liberation from oppression
• Beginning of a new era
• Foundation of Soviet achievement
• Proof of Russian revolutionary capacity

Encoded meanings (critical/post-Soviet):
• Catastrophe and violence
• Beginning of totalitarian terror
• Destruction of traditional Russia
• Warning against utopian politics

The revolution is remembered as **origin and catastrophe**—the
event that made the Soviet century possible and inevitable.

IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Revolutions occur when regimes lose the capacity to repress.
• Organized minorities can seize power from disorganized majorities.
• Revolutionary promises are easier to make than to keep.
• Structural patterns persist beneath ideological transformation.
• Legitimacy systems can be replaced without changing power structures.
• Civil wars are won by the side that maintains unity.

POLYPHONIC TENSIONS (v2.8):
• MERCOURIS: Revolution as popular uprising → genuine mass support
• MEARSHEIMER: Revolution as power vacuum exploitation → organized faction wins
• MERCOURIS: Transformation as real → new society created
• MEARSHEIMER: Recurrence as dominant → structural patterns reassert
• MERCOURIS: Ideology as causal → Marxism shaped outcomes
• MEARSHEIMER: Ideology as superstructure → power imperatives determine
• Both: The revolution was simultaneously rupture and continuity
