I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Ukraine as the **most charged internal–external
borderland** in Russian civilizational memory: simultaneously origin
space, cultural kin zone, frontier buffer, and contested sovereignty
arena.

Ukraine is remembered not as a neutral neighbor, but as a **foundational
inheritance whose political orientation determines Russian security and
identity outcomes**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGIN CORE: KIEVAN RUS’
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In Russian memory, Ukrainian lands—especially Kiev—form the **cradle of
statehood**.

Key associations:
• Baptism of Rus’
• Early princely authority
• Orthodox civilizational genesis

Memory axiom:
→ Political separation does not erase civilizational origin.

Loss of Kiev is remembered as historical fracture, not civilizational
divorce.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. BORDERLAND CONDITION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ukraine historically functions as a borderland between:
• Steppe and forest
• Orthodox and Catholic worlds
• Empire and republic models

Consequences:
• Recurrent external intervention
• Elite fragmentation
• Shifting loyalties

Borderlands absorb pressure rather than generate stability.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. COSSACK AUTONOMY & AMBIGUITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cossack polities embody duality.

Features:
• Military self-rule
• Negotiated allegiance
• Frontier defense function

In memory:
• Autonomy is tolerated when aligned
• Rebellion is framed as betrayal when reoriented

Ukraine’s autonomy oscillates between asset and liability.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. IMPERIAL INTEGRATION LOGIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Russian imperial policy frames Ukraine as:
• Strategic depth
• Agricultural heartland
• Manpower reservoir
• Cultural kin territory

Integration is justified as:
→ Restoration of historical unity and security.

Resistance is interpreted through external manipulation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. SOVIET RECODING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Soviet period:
• Formalizes Ukrainian republichood
• Centralizes power
• Rewrites historical narrative
• Industrializes eastern regions

Unity is administrative, not consensual.

Contradictions are deferred, not resolved.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. POST-SOVIET RUPTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After 1991:
• Borders harden
• Identity narratives diverge
• External alignments shift

In Russian memory:
• Ukraine’s westward alignment = direct security threat
• Separation becomes strategic vulnerability

Ukraine is no longer buffer but exposure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For Russia, Ukraine functions as:
• Identity hinge
• Security glacis
• Legitimacy amplifier or negator

Ukraine’s orientation is never neutral in Russian strategic cognition.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Tension preserved without verdict.
