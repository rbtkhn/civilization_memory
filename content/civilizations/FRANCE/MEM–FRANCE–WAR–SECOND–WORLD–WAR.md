I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Second World War as **Francia's catastrophic
defeat and contested restoration**: the conflict that began with
declaration in 1939, collapsed in six weeks in 1940, and produced
occupation, Vichy, resistance, and Liberation—a trauma that fractured
national legitimacy and left post-war identity dependent on Allied
victory and republican renewal.

For Francia, the war encodes **defeat, division, and deferred
vindication**—1940 broke the army that had held at Verdun; Vichy
and Resistance split the nation; Liberation restored the Republic
but not the myth of self-sufficient sovereignty.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. 1939: DECLARATION WITHOUT DELIVERY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
France declared war on Germany (3 September 1939) in fulfilment of
its guarantee to Poland. Poland fell in weeks. The Phoney War
(September 1939–May 1940) saw no major offensive—the Maginot Line
held the German border; Belgium remained neutral. France had learned
from 1914: avoid premature attack; let the enemy bleed on fortifications.

The lesson was wrong. Blitzkrieg bypassed the Maginot through the
Ardennes.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. MAY–JUNE 1940: COLLAPSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
10 May 1940: Germany attacked Belgium and the Netherlands. French
and British forces moved north. The German main thrust came through
the Ardennes—a terrain the French high command judged impassable to
armour. Sedan fell; the Meuse was crossed. The Allied left was cut
off; Dunkirk evacuated British forces. Paris fell 14 June. The Third
Republic asked for an armistice.

Winston Churchill recorded the shock:

"The French Army had been fought to a standstill and was breaking up.
The French government was divided and bowed beneath the weight of
defeat."
— Winston Churchill, *The Second World War* (1948)

Six weeks. The army that had held Verdun for ten months collapsed
in six weeks.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. VICHY: THE REGIME OF REPUDIATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
22 June 1940: Armistice at Compiègne—in the same railway carriage
where Germany had surrendered in 1918. France was partitioned:
northern and western zones occupied; a "free zone" under Pétain's
authority. The Third Republic voted itself out of existence; Vichy
claimed "National Revolution"—collaboration, order, anti-republicanism.

Pétain, the hero of Verdun, became the head of the collaborationist
state. The paradox was intolerable: the man who had embodied "Ils
ne passeront pas" now signed away French sovereignty.

Mers-el-Kébir (3 July 1940): the British attacked the French fleet
at Oran to prevent it falling to Germany. French sailors died at
British hands. The memory poisoned Vichy–London relations; it also
encoded the harsh truth—France could not control its own destiny.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. RESISTANCE AND LIBERATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
From 1940, a minority resisted. De Gaulle's appeal of 18 June from
London; the internal Resistance—networks, maquis, sabotage. By 1944,
the Resistance had grown; the Allies landed in Normandy (6 June)
and Provence (15 August). Paris rose (19–25 August 1944); de Gaulle
paraded down the Champs-Élysées. Liberation restored the Republic.

The price: dependence on Anglo-American power. France had not freed
itself; it had been freed. The myth of 1918—France among the victors—
could not be repeated. Post-1945 legitimacy would rest on Resistance
memory, European integration, and nuclear independence—compensations
for the loss of autonomous victory.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Second World War encodes **defeat, division, and restored
sovereignty under constraint**.

• 1940 collapse shattered the Maginot logic; Blitzkrieg revealed
  the limits of defensive rigidity
• Vichy fractured legitimacy—collaboration vs resistance; neither
  could be erased
• Liberation restored the Republic but not self-sufficient victory
• Post-war France: alliance dependence, European integration,
  rupture-and-reconstitution (1958) as renewal mechanism

DOCTRINE v0.4 (Post-Hegemonic Strategic Anxiety) finds reinforcement
here: 1940 proved that territorial integrity alone could not prevent
defeat; alliance and nuclear autonomy became imperatives.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: Whose authority governed France 1940–1944? Vichy
claimed continuity; de Gaulle claimed legitimacy in exile. Post-war
purges and trials (Pétain, Laval) resolved the question retroactively—
Vichy was "null and void"; the Republic had never ceased.

Personal liability: Pétain, Laval, Darlan—collaborators bore exposure.
De Gaulle, as head of Free France, claimed republican continuity.
Defection incentives: Armistice faction vs. continuation faction;
soldiers and officials chose Vichy or London. Institutional survival:
The Republic was suspended, not abolished—de Gaulle's narrative. The
army dissolved, reformed in North Africa and Normandy. Irreversibility:
Occupation, Vichy, Holocaust complicity (Vel' d'Hiv, 1942), Liberation—
the civil war within the war could not be undone. Post-1945 France
inherited a divided memory.
