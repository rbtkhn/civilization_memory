I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the First World War as **Francia's victory of
exhaustion**: the conflict that avenged 1870, reclaimed Alsace-Lorraine,
and proved France would not break—but at a cost that exhausted the
nation and shaped the defensive rigidity that 1940 would exploit.

For Francia, the Great War encodes **survival through sacrifice**—
Marne, Verdun, the trenches, 1.4 million dead—and the paradox that
victory produced trauma sufficient to distort the next war's strategy.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. 1870 AVENGED: REVANCHE FULFILLED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war was framed as completion of unfinished business.

Franco-Prussian humiliation (1870–1871) had left Alsace-Lorraine in
German hands and revanche psychology embedded. When war came in 1914,
France entered as defender—but also as claimant. Victory in 1918
restored the lost provinces and vindicated forty-eight years of
grievance.

Niall Ferguson contextualizes:

"The Franco-Prussian War was the hinge. It created the German Empire,
humiliated France, and left a revanche psychology that would drive
French policy for two generations. The war of 1870 made the war of
1914 thinkable—and for many on both sides, desirable."
— Niall Ferguson, *The Pity of War* (1998)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. MARNE: THE MIRACLE THAT SAVED FRANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
September 1914: Paris threatened, government in Bordeaux, army in
retreat. The Marne reversed catastrophe. Joffre's counter-attack,
von Kluck's exposed flank, the taxi cabs of Paris—France survived
when collapse seemed certain.

The Marne proved France would not repeat 1870. It also transformed
the war from movement to attrition. The trenches were born.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. VERDUN: SACRED GROUND
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Verdun (February–December 1916) became Francia's defining memory—
"Ils ne passeront pas," Pétain's noria, 70 of 96 divisions rotated
through. The battle designed to bleed France bled Germany equally.
France held; the front returned to February positions. Ten months,
~700,000 casualties, no territorial change—but France had proven
it would sacrifice everything for its soil.

Verdun taught that attack costs everything. Defense must be absolute.
The Maginot Line is Verdun rendered in concrete.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE SOMME AND INDUSTRIAL SLAUGHTER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Somme (July–November 1916) ran in parallel—primarily British
but with French participation. Industrial warfare consumed armies
without producing decision. Attrition became strategy when breakthrough
proved impossible. The Western Front had its own logic, immune to
generalship.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. 1918: VICTORY AND EXHAUSTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Foch's coordinated offensives, American reinforcement, German
exhaustion—the Armistice of 11 November 1918 ended the war. France
won. Alsace-Lorraine returned. Germany was defeated.

But France was exhausted. 1.4 million dead, 4.2 million wounded,
northern departments devastated. The victory was real; the cost
was civilizational. The Republic had survived and prevailed—but
the trauma would shape the interwar period and the defensive
mentality that contributed to 1940.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The First World War encodes **victory through endurance and its cost**.

• 1870 avenged; Alsace-Lorraine reclaimed
• Marne proved France would not break
• Verdun became sacred ground; "Ils ne passeront pas"
• Victory exhausted the nation
• Trauma produced Maginot logic—avoid repetition at any cost
• 1940 would test whether defensive rigidity could survive Blitzkrieg

DOCTRINE v0.4 (Post-Hegemonic Strategic Anxiety) finds origin here:
the 1916 bloodletting reinforced defensive orientation; the refusal
to repeat 1916 casualties created the strategic rigidity that 1940
exploited.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: Whose authority triggered the war? The Franco-Russian
alliance and German mobilization created competing claims to legitimate
response. Belgium's neutrality—violated by the Schlieffen Plan—posed
jurisdictional defiance: whose law governed the Low Countries?

Personal liability: Joffre, Foch, Pétain, and Clemenceau bore operational
and political exposure. Post-war, the Republic's legitimacy rested on
victory; defeat would have collapsed the Third Republic. Defection
incentives: 1917 mutinies after Nivelle—soldiers refused to attack;
Pétain restored order through limited offensives and improved conditions.
Institutional survival: The Republic survived; the army held. Irreversibility:
1.4 million dead; Alsace-Lorraine reclaimed; Versailles imposed. The
settlement could not be undone without another war.
