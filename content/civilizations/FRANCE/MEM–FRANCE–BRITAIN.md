I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Francia–Britain interface as **the maritime
rival France could not defeat and the ally France could not do
without**: Great Britain (from 1707) as naval hegemon, colonial
competitor, Napoleonic enemy, and—after 1904—partner against Germany.

For Francia, Britain encodes **sea power that closed the continental
option**—Trafalgar, the Continental System's failure, and the
structural truth that France was a land power facing a maritime one.
Alliance in 1914 and 1939 did not erase that memory.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. THE EIGHTEENTH CENTURY: COLONIAL LOSS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Seven Years' War (1756–1763) cost France Canada and French India.
Choiseul: "We have lost America not in the forests of Canada, but on
the seas, where we were never strong enough to contest England's
mastery." Britain's naval and financial superiority decided the
outcome. France intervened in the American War (1778–1783) to wound
Britain—and succeeded; Yorktown humiliated Britain. But the deeper
pattern held: France could not sustain naval competition.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. NAPOLEON AND TRAFALGAR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Napoleon's invasion plan required naval superiority in the Channel.
Trafalgar (1805) destroyed it. John Keegan: "Trafalgar did not defeat
Napoleon; it determined the form his defeat would take." The
Continental System—economic warfare against Britain—followed. It
failed: British credit, empire, and sea control sustained the
coalition. Portugal, Spain, Russia defected or resisted. France
could not close the continent to British trade.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ENTENTE AND ALLIANCE (1904–1945)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Entente Cordiale (1904) realigned France and Britain against
Germany. Two world wars made them allies. 1940: Britain fought on
after France fell; Mers-el-Kébir demonstrated that Britain would
neutralize the French fleet rather than risk it falling to Germany.
Liberation (1944) depended on Anglo-American power. France had not
freed itself—it had been freed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Britain encodes **maritime rival and necessary ally**.

• Naval inferiority structural; Trafalgar confirmed it
• Continental strategy (Napoleon) could not defeat sea power
• 20th c.: alliance against Germany; dependence on Anglo-American
  power for liberation
• Post-1945: European integration, NATO—Britain sometimes partner,
  sometimes apart (Brexit)
