I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Treaty of Versailles as **Francia's vindication
of victory**: the settlement that reclaimed Alsace-Lorraine, imposed
reparations, and inscribed German war guilt—but whose enforcement
depended on allies France could not control.

For Francia, Versailles encodes **justice declared and security
unfulfilled**—revanche completed on paper, yet the structure required
Anglo-American commitment that evaporated. The treaty became both
legitimacy achievement and strategic liability.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. REVANCHE FULFILLED: ALSACE-LORRAINE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The treaty's cardinal achievement for France was territorial.

• Alsace-Lorraine restored to France (Articles 51–79)
• Saar placed under League administration (15 years)
• Rhine demilitarized
• Treaty of Frankfurt (1871) superseded

Forty-eight years of grievance reversed. The Hall of Mirrors—where
the German Empire had been proclaimed in 1871—hosted the signing.
Symbolic symmetry: humiliation avenged, provinces reclaimed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. REPARATIONS AND WAR GUILT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Article 231 assigned Germany sole responsibility for the war.

Reparations were to compensate Allied losses; the total was deferred
(1921: 132 billion gold marks). Clemenceau insisted on maximal
extraction—France's northern departments were devastated; Germany
must pay. Wilson and Lloyd George tempered but could not eliminate
French demands.

For Francia, reparations were **justice and reconstruction**—not
punishment alone but material redress. The failure to collect fully
encoded Allied fissure, not French excess.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. SECURITY AND ENFORCEMENT WEAKNESS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
France sought guarantees Germany could not rearm or invade again.

• Rhineland demilitarized
• German army limited to 100,000
• Anglo-American security treaties promised

The US Senate rejected the treaty and the security guarantee (1920).
Britain withdrew its guarantee when the US did. France was left to
enforce Versailles alone—exhausted, with Belgium as sole reliable
ally on the continent. The Maginot Line logic follows: France could
not rely on treaties; it must rely on concrete.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Versailles encodes **declaration without durable enforcement**.

• Justice was declared (territory, guilt, reparations)
• Enforcement depended on allies who defected
• The settlement was narratable as honorable for France—revenge
  fulfilled, security sought—but structurally fragile
• Interwar French policy oscillated between harshness and
  accommodation; neither secured lasting security

For Francia, Versailles is the treaty France won but could not
sustain—the vindication that required Anglo-American partnership
France did not receive.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: Who had authority to impose terms? The victors—
France, Britain, US, Italy—negotiated; Germany was excluded.
No Talleyrand at the table. The defeated had no voice in the
settlement structure; they could only sign or face renewed invasion.

Personal liability: Clemenceau bore exposure for French demands;
Wilson for the League and the failed ratification. German signatories
("November criminals") bore domestic liability—they had accepted
the Diktat. Defection incentives: US defected first (non-ratification);
Britain drifted toward accommodation. France alone had incentive to
enforce; France alone lacked capacity. Institutional survival: The
Third Republic's legitimacy was tied to victory and Versailles;
revision threatened that narrative. Irreversibility: The treaty was
signed; territorial transfers occurred. But enforcement was reversible—
reparations were scaled back (Dawes, Young); Rhineland was remilitarized
(1936). The settlement frayed faster than France could repair it.
