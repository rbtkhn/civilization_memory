I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Poland** as Francia's **eastern conscience**: a space
France has repeatedly declared support for, aligned with as a strategic
counterweight, and failed to protect when continental structure turned
against it.

For Francia, Poland encodes **solidarity without delivery**—the gap between
universalist principle (Polish liberty as liberty for all) and structural
powerlessness to enforce it.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. EARLY MODERN ALLIANCE & DYNASTIC LINK
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
France and Poland share early dynastic entanglement.

Marie Leszczyńska (1703–1768), daughter of Stanisław Leszczyński, deposed
King of Poland, became Queen of France as wife of Louis XV (1725). The
marriage briefly aligned French diplomacy with Polish restoration hopes.
Stanisław regained the Polish throne briefly (1733–1736) with French
support, then lost it again. The episode establishes a pattern: **French
declaration of support precedes French capacity to sustain it.**

Will Durant frames the dynastic and diplomatic context:

> "Poland had been partitioned out of existence by her neighbors; France
> had been too far away and too absorbed in her own revolution to prevent
> it. The Polish cause became a cause célèbre in Paris—a symbol of liberty
> crushed by despotism."
> — Will Durant, *Rousseau and Revolution* (1967) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Legitimacy attaches to the cause; capacity does not follow.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. NAPOLEONIC DUCHY OF WARSAW & REVERSIBLE RESTORATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Napoleon creates the Duchy of Warsaw (1807) from Prussian-conquered
Polish territory—a French-sponsored Polish polity.

The Duchy functions as:
• Eastern buffer against Russia and residual Prussia
• Source of Polish troops for Napoleonic campaigns
• Symbolic restoration of Polish statehood

David Chandler notes the strategic logic:

> "The creation of the Duchy of Warsaw was a masterstroke of Napoleonic
> statecraft—it secured Polish loyalty, extended French influence deep
> into Eastern Europe, and posed a permanent threat to the Russian and
> Prussian flanks."
> — David Chandler, *The Campaigns of Napoleon* (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Napoleon's defeat dissolves the Duchy. The Congress of Vienna partitions
Polish lands among Russia, Prussia, and Austria. **French-sponsored
restoration proves contingent on French victory.** When Francia falls,
Poland falls with it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. NINETEENTH-CENTURY EXILES & PARIS AS REFUGE
────────────────────────────────────────────
Poland disappears from the map; Polish exiles flock to Paris.

Paris becomes:
• Haven for leaders of the November Uprising (1830–31)
• Base for émigré intellectual and military activity
• Symbolic capital of the "Poland of the spirit"

The Great Emigration (Wielka Emigracja) transforms Polish nationalism
in exile. Adam Mickiewicz, Frédéric Chopin, and others shape a romantic
Polish identity centered on loss and eventual restoration. France offers
**hospitality and moral solidarity**—not armies or diplomacy capable of
reversing partition.

Henry Kissinger observes the structural constraint:

> "France could sympathize with Poland's plight and provide asylum to
> its exiles; she could not challenge the territorial settlement imposed
> by Russia, Prussia, and Austria. Moral support and strategic impotence
> coexisted."
> — Henry Kissinger, *Diplomacy* (1994) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Declaration and capacity diverge.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. POST-WWI RESTORATION & FRENCH GUARANTEE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Poland is restored in 1918. France becomes Poland's principal ally.

Dynamics:
• Military mission and loan support
• Alliance against German revisionism
• Poland as eastern counterweight to Germany and Soviet Russia

Francia treats Polish independence as **legitimate and necessary**—part
of the Versailles order France helped design. Support is real but
conditional on French capacity to project eastward.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. 1939: DECLARATION WITHOUT DELIVERY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
France declares war on Germany (3 September 1939) in fulfilment of its
guarantee to Poland.

The declaration is **honored in law, empty in effect**. France does not
launch a meaningful offensive to relieve Poland; the Wehrmacht overruns
Poland in weeks. When France falls in 1940, Poland is already occupied.

Niall Ferguson captures the credibility collapse:

> "The Anglo-French guarantee to Poland had been meant to deter Hitler.
> When it failed to do so, the Western powers discovered they had
> committed themselves to a war they could not win in time to save
> their ally. Poland was the first victim of a guarantee that was
> declarative but not executable."
> — Niall Ferguson, *The War of the World* (2006) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Solidarity is asserted. Delivery fails. Poland encodes **the limit of
Francia's eastern reach.**

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. POST-1945: POLAND BEHIND THE IRON CURTAIN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After 1945, Poland falls under Soviet domination. France—exhausted,
reconstructing, pivoting to European integration—cannot contest the
outcome.

Poland becomes:
• Part of the Soviet bloc
• Lost to the Western sphere
• A wound in French strategic memory

Francia's eastern conscience remains unresolved. Support for Polish
liberation (Solidarity, 1980s) is **moral and rhetorical**—France
cannot project power into the Warsaw Pact. 1989 restores Polish
independence; France welcomes it. The EU enlarges to include Poland
(2004). **Inclusion replaces guarantee.** Multilateralism absorbs
bilateral alliance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Poland encodes **Francia's eastern solidarity deficit**.

For Francia, this demonstrates:
• Universalist principle (Polish liberty) can be declared without
  capacity to enforce
• Alliance commitments can outrun deliverable power
• Moral support and strategic impotence can coexist
• Eastern counterweight logic requires western strength—when Francia
  is weak, Poland is lost

Poland is the ally Francia has repeatedly embraced and repeatedly
failed to protect.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Declaration precedes delivery; often delivery never comes.
• Dynastic link (Leszczyńska) established pattern of support without
  sustainability.
• Duchy of Warsaw: restoration contingent on French victory.
• Paris as refuge: solidarity through asylum, not intervention.
• 1939: guarantee declared, Poland overrun before France could act.
• Post-1945: Poland behind Iron Curtain; Francia powerless.
• Poland encodes the eastern limit of French strategic reach.

Tension preserved without verdict.
