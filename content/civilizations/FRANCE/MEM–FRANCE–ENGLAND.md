────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Francia–England interface as **the medieval
and early-modern rivalry that defined French territorial and dynastic
identity**: the kingdom across the Channel whose kings held French
lands, claimed the French crown, and fought Francia for two centuries
before being expelled in 1453.

For Francia, England (pre-1707) encodes **the vassal who became
usurper**—Plantagenet holdings in Normandy, Anjou, Aquitaine; the
Hundred Years' War; Agincourt and the Treaty of Troyes; eventual
expulsion and the closing of the continental door.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. THE PLANTAGENET INHERITANCE (1152–1204)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Henry II of England held more of France than the French king.

Eleanor of Aquitaine's marriage to Henry Plantagenet (1152) created
the Angevin empire: England, Normandy, Anjou, Aquitaine. The king of
France faced a vassal who was also a king—whose Continental domains
surpassed the royal domain. Philip Augustus spent thirty years
chipping away: Normandy lost (1204), Anjou recovered, Aquitaine
reduced. By 1224, the balance had shifted toward the Capetians.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE HUNDRED YEARS' WAR (1337–1453)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
England revived its claim through Edward III (1328 succession dispute)
and fought for the French crown. Crécy (1346), Poitiers (1356),
Agincourt (1415)—English tactics and French disunity produced
catastrophic defeats. The Treaty of Troyes (1420) made Henry V
heir to France; Charles VI disinherited the dauphin. Joan of Arc
(1429) reversed momentum; by 1453 England retained only Calais.

Juliet Barker frames the stakes:

"The France that Henry V invaded in 1415 was a kingdom at war with
itself. The Armagnac and Burgundian factions had been fighting since
1407... Henry V understood this perfectly. His chevauchée was designed
to force battle against a divided enemy."
— Juliet Barker, *Agincourt: Henry V and the Battle That Made England* (2005)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. EXPULSION AND CONSOLIDATION (1453–1707)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Calais fell (1558); England lost its last Continental foothold. The
Tudors and Stuarts shifted attention to sea and empire. France
consolidated; England receded from French soil. The Reformation
and Wars of Religion altered alignments—but the medieval pattern
held: England had been the rival who held French territory and
claimed the crown; by 1707 (Act of Union with Scotland), England
became Britain, and the interface shifted to maritime competition.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
England encodes **territorial and dynastic rivalry resolved by
expulsion**.

• Plantagenet holdings challenged Capetian legitimacy
• Hundred Years' War: chivalric collapse (Agincourt), recovery
  (Joan of Arc), eventual French victory
• 1453: England expelled from France; continental rivalry closed
• Post-1707: Britain succeeds England; see MEM–FRANCE–BRITAIN
