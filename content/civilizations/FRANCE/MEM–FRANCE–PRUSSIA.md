────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Prussia as **Francia's disciplined northern rival**:
the polity that embodied institutional depth over symbolic authority,
that Napoleon annihilated at Jena and that—reformed, enlarged—returned
to humiliate France at Sedan and terminate French continental primacy.

For Francia, Prussia is not remembered as Germania's survival
laboratory. It is remembered as **the adversary that exposed the gap
between prestige and capacity**—defeated decisively in 1806, then
victor in 1870–1871. Prussia encodes the structural lesson: bureaucracy,
staff systems, and procedural discipline can overturn inherited
confidence. Austria was the Habsburg eastern wing; Prussia became the
northern engine of a unified Germany that France could not contain.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. PRUSSIA IN THE PRE-NAPOLEONIC FRAME
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Before 1806, Prussia occupies a secondary place in French strategic
calculation. Austria and Spain dominate the Habsburg encirclement;
Prussia is a Protestant power that Richelieu and later French
diplomacy cultivated as a counterweight to Vienna. Frederick II's
survival through the Seven Years' War impresses but does not yet
threaten French primacy—Prussia remains one among several German
states, fractious and useful.

The structural shift begins when Prussia fails to align decisively
with Napoleonic France. Neutrality exhausts; isolation follows.
Prussia enters the 1806 campaign without allies and without
institutional adaptation to the new military system.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. JENA: NAPOLEONIC ANNIHILATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jena-Auerstädt (14 October 1806) encodes Prussia as **defeated in a
single day**. Napoleon's operational brilliance—combined arms,
initiative, extraction-system effectiveness—destroys Prussian military
power. Prussia is eliminated as an independent actor for seven years.

> "The greatest demonstration of Napoleonic operational art:
> a Great Power annihilated in one campaign."
> — David Chandler, *The Campaigns of Napoleon*
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

For Francia, Jena validates declarative authority through decisive
victory. Prussia becomes the object lesson in what happens when
traditional military prestige meets modern operational integration.
The Prussian army—venerated, rigid—collapses. The French system
triumphs.

Prussia survives as a polity. It does not disappear. But it is
broken—and from that break emerges the Reform Movement that will
later produce the staff systems and discipline that France will
underestimate in 1870.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CONTINENTAL SYSTEM: PRUSSIAN SELECTIVE COMPLIANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Under Napoleonic hegemony, Prussia is a reluctant participant in the
Continental System. Occupied, truncated, financially drained—Prussia
complies minimally while preserving institutional core. Survival
logic prevails over loyalty to the bloc.

The structural lesson: Prussia absorbs humiliation and adapts. It
does not rebel openly; it does not embrace the system. It endures,
extracts what it can from reform (military, administrative), and
waits. Francia's declarative economic warfare cannot fully subordinate
a state that privileges procedure over declaration.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. 1815–1866: PRUSSIA AS REVIVED RIVAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After the Congress of Vienna, Prussia gains territory—Rhineland,
Saxony's northern portion—that shifts its center of gravity westward.
Prussia becomes a German power with Rhine frontage, not only an
eastern marcher state. French diplomacy watches Prussian consolidation
but does not yet treat Prussia as the principal threat; Austria
remains the Habsburg counterweight, and German unification seems
distant.

Bismarck's rise and the Austro-Prussian War (1866) change the
calculus. Prussia excludes Austria from Germany. Kleindeutsch
triumphs. The North German Confederation forms. Francia faces a
unified northern Germany led by Prussia—institutional depth,
mobilization capacity, staff coordination—before France has fully
adapted.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. 1870–1871: PRUSSIAN VICTORY AND FRENCH COLLAPSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Franco-Prussian War inverts Jena. Prussia—now the core of
mobilizing Germany—defeats France decisively. Sedan captures the
Emperor. Paris falls. Alsace-Lorraine is lost. French continental
primacy terminates.

Henry Kissinger frames the misreading: France assumed status
guaranteed outcomes; structure had shifted. Prussian discipline,
staff systems, and institutional preparedness proved superior to
French prestige and centralized rhetoric (*Diplomacy*).

> "The Franco-Prussian War was the hinge. It created the German
> Empire, humiliated France, and left a revanche psychology that
> would drive French policy for two generations."
> — Niall Ferguson, *The Pity of War* (1998)
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

Prussia encodes the adversary that **learned from defeat**. Jena
taught Prussia to reform; Sedan taught France that reform had
occurred elsewhere. The same structural logic—institutional depth
over symbolic authority—that Francia had once demonstrated against
Prussia was now turned against Francia.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LIABILITY AND MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**Jurisdiction:** Prussia's authority rested on dynastic claim and
administrative competence. When Napoleon dissolved the Holy Roman
Empire (1806), Prussia lost the imperial framework within which it
had operated—but retained its own crown and bureaucratic spine.
Liability for Jena rested with Prussian command and rigidity;
France bore no obligation to spare Prussia. The reversal at Sedan
shifted liability: France's regime chose war; France's institutional
deficits produced defeat.

**Irreversibility:** Jena broke Prussian military prestige but did
not dissolve the state. Reform reversed the military outcome within
a generation. Sedan, by contrast, was irreversible for France:
Alsace-Lorraine, imperial collapse, regime change. Prussia's victory
created the German Empire; France could not undo it by declaration.

**Institutional survival:** Prussia endured defeat (1806), occupation,
and territorial truncation. Its bureaucracy and officer corps
preserved core capacity. The lesson for Francia: procedural depth
outlasts battlefield humiliation. Prussia survived to win; France
lost and could not recover primacy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. DOCTRINAL IMPLICATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Prussia relationship instantiates the PRESTIGE vs CAPACITY pattern:

PATTERN: Prussia as disciplined rival; Jena = Francia demonstrates
operational superiority; Continental System = Prussia survives through
selective compliance; 1870–1871 = Prussia (as Germany) inverts the
outcome, terminates French primacy.

APPLICABLE DOCTRINES (CIV–DOCTRINE–FRANCE):
• Status vs structural capacity
• Institutional depth over symbolic authority
• Adversary learning from defeat
