The session closed two gaps: France had lacked an account of the Ottoman Empire and the Holy Roman Empire to set beside Germany's. The analysis surfaced the contrast. On the Ottomans, France weighs the strategic partner that broke the Habsburg ring—raison d'état born in that alliance—while Germany weighs the adversary that shaped Habsburg primacy. On the Empire, France weighs the eastern obstacle Richelieu fractured and Napoleon dissolved; Germany weighs a governance experiment sustained by fragmentation. The same past, opposite readings. The axis between them turns on that tension. When you take up France again, or when someone inherits this work, the record preserves what was offered and why it mattered.
