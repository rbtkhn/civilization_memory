I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Austria as **Francia's Habsburg rival and eastern
wing of the encirclement**: the territorial and dynastic core through
which Habsburg power projected across Central Europe, contested French
continental hegemony, and was defeated by Napoleon before becoming the
base for Metternich's Concert and, eventually, the buffer-component
of Austria-Hungary.

For Francia, Austria is not remembered as imperial institutionalization.
It is remembered as **the eastern face of the Habsburg ring**—the
archduchy that held the imperial title, defended Vienna against the
Ottomans, and stood between France and Central European dominance.
Richelieu fractured it; Louis XIV pressed it; Napoleon broke it at
Austerlitz and Wagram. After 1815, Austria became Metternich's
instrument—stabilizer and rival at once.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. HABSBURG RIVAL AND THE EASTERN WING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
By the sixteenth century, Francia faces the Habsburg geometry:

• Spain to the south and west
• The Holy Roman Empire to the east, with Austria as its dynastic core
• Habsburg control of Burgundy, Italy, the Low Countries

> "The Emperor Charles V held a position of unprecedented
> territorial concentration. France was caught between Spanish
> dominions in the south and the Holy Roman Empire in the east.
> Survival required breaking the ring."
> — Geoffrey Parker, *The Grand Strategy of Philip II* (1998), p. 42
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

Austria is the **eastern anchor** of that ring. The Habsburg archduchy
provides the territorial base from which the imperial title is exercised;
Vienna is the administrative and military center. Francia's Franco-Ottoman
alliance (1536) targets this geometry—Ottoman pressure on Hungary and
Vienna diverts Austrian resources, fractures Habsburg eastern cohesion.
Austria absorbs the Ottoman front while France maneuvers in Italy and
the Rhineland.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. RICHELIEU AND LOUIS XIV: CONTESTING AUSTRIA
───────────────────────────────────────────────
Richelieu's intervention in the Thirty Years' War fractures the
Habsburg-imperial nexus. Austria—as imperial core—is the object:
subsidize Protestant princes, prevent Habsburg consolidation,
embed French guarantors at Westphalia. Austria survives but
emerges weakened, its authority constrained.

Louis XIV's wars press Austria repeatedly:
• War of Devolution, Dutch War, Nine Years' War, War of Spanish
  Succession—each tests Austrian resilience
• Strasbourg annexed (1681); Alsace secured
• Austria holds but cannot dominate; the Rhine remains contested

> "French policy transformed the war from a religious struggle
> into a contest over the structure of European power, permanently
> altering the meaning of legitimacy in interstate conflict."
> — Peter Wilson, *Europe's Tragedy* (2009), p. 814
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

Austria endures. It does not collapse. But Francia learns that
Austria can be **pressed**—that Habsburg administration, for all
its resilience, cannot project power without coalition. The
structural logic favors fracture over conquest.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. NAPOLEON AND AUSTRIAN DEFEAT
────────────────────────────────────────────
Napoleon breaks Austria decisively.

Austerlitz (1805): The Third Coalition collapses. Austrian and
Russian forces are routed. The Peace of Pressburg strips Austria
of Venice, Istria, Dalmatia. Austria exits the war humiliated.

Wagram (1809): Austria re-enters; Napoleon defeats it again.
The Peace of Schönbrunn further reduces Austrian territory.
Francis II had already dissolved the Holy Roman Empire (1806);
Austria survives as a reduced state, no longer imperial core.

> "A masterful plan, an appreciation of terrain, superb timing…
> had given Napoleon the greatest victory of his career."
> — Andrew Roberts, *Napoleon: A Life* (2014), on Austerlitz
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

For Francia, Austerlitz and Wagram encode **Austria as defeated
opponent**—the Habsburg core broken, the eastern wing of the ring
shattered. Napoleon completes what Richelieu began: the structural
denial of Habsburg continental hegemony. Austria remains—weakened,
adaptable—but it no longer threatens French survival.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. METTERNICH AND THE CONCERT
────────────────────────────────────────────
After 1815, Austria's role shifts. Metternich makes Vienna the
hub of the Concert of Europe—conservative equilibrium, legitimacy
through restoration, suppression of revolution. Francia's relation
to Austria becomes ambivalent: Austria restrains both German and
Russian excess; Austria also blocks French liberal projection.

> "The first and greatest task of states is not to perfect mankind,
> but to prevent its destruction; Europe exists by balance, not by
> uniformity, and the Austrian state stands precisely to uphold
> that equilibrium."
> — Metternich, *Memoirs*, Vol. II
> [ERC: CONTEXTUAL]

Austria as **stabilizer**—useful when it absorbs pressure, frustrating
when it blocks French influence. The Congress of Vienna restores
Austrian position; Talleyrand negotiates from weakness but preserves
France as a power. Austria and France are neither allies nor permanent
enemies; they are **structural counterweights** in the Concert.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. FROM RIVAL TO BUFFER: AUSTRIA-HUNGARY
────────────────────────────────────────────
The Ausgleich (1867) transforms Austria into Austria-Hungary. Francia
reads this as **system salvage**—institutionalizing contradiction to
preserve continuity. Austria remains the administrative core; Hungary
gains parity. The dual monarchy absorbs pressure that would otherwise
radiate westward. Its weakness stabilizes Europe—temporarily.

World War I collapses the buffer. Austria-Hungary dissolves. The
center of Europe ceases to absorb violence; pressure rushes unchecked.
For Francia, Austria's end removes a layer of continental insulation—
the Habsburg core that had contested, then stabilized, then buffered,
is gone.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LIABILITY AND MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────
**Jurisdiction:** Austria's authority rested on dynastic claim and
imperial title. When Napoleon dissolved the Holy Roman Empire (1806),
Francis II renounced the imperial mantle to prevent its capture. The
jurisdictional rupture was irreversible—Austria could not reclaim
the Empire. Liability for the dissolution rested with Napoleon; Austria
preserved the archduchy by surrendering the universal claim.

**Irreversibility:** Austerlitz and Wagram broke Austrian military
prestige. The Congress of Vienna restored territory but could not
restore the pre-1805 position. Austria's exclusion from German
unification (1866, 1871) was the structural consequence—Bismarck
replaced Habsburg primacy with Prussian hegemony. Austria survived
as a reduced power; the reversal could not be undone.

**Institutional survival:** The Habsburg state endured until 1918.
Administration outlasted military defeat; process outlasted prestige.
The empire ended when administration lost authority—nationalism,
war, dissolution. Austria encodes endurance through procedure, and
the limits of that endurance.

VIII. DOCTRINAL IMPLICATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────
The Austria relationship instantiates the ENCIRCLEMENT → FRACTURE →
BUFFER pattern:

PATTERN: Austria as eastern wing of Habsburg encirclement; fracture
through coalition warfare (Richelieu, Louis XIV) and decisive defeat
(Napoleon); repurposing as stabilizer (Metternich, Austria-Hungary);
final collapse removing continental insulation.

APPLICABLE DOCTRINES (CIV–DOCTRINE–FRANCE):
• Balance-of-power and encirclement fracture
• Structural denial of hegemonic concentration
• Utility of buffers—and their eventual failure
