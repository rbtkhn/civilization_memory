I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the South African (Boer) War as **Anglia’s closing
imperial stress test in Churchill’s *The Great Democracies***: the
conflict with which Vol. 4 concludes—Briton versus Boer, empire
versus settler republic, and the moment when imperial legitimacy is
tested by guerrilla resistance, concentration camps, and global
scrutiny.

The Boer War does not end the empire.
It **encodes its moral cost**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CHURCHILL VOL. 4 — CLOSING FRAME
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Winston Churchill, in *A History of the English-Speaking Peoples*,
Vol. 4 (*The Great Democracies* [1956–1958]), concludes the volume
with the South African War (Boer War): Lord Salisbury’s governments,
the conflict with the Boer republics, and the consolidation of
British authority in southern Africa at the price of lasting moral
and political controversy (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

III. STRATEGIC CONTEXT & TRIGGER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
War erupts from imperial and mineral logic.

Context:
• British Cape and Natal; Boer republics (Transvaal, Orange Free State)
• Gold and diamonds; imperial expansion vs. Boer sovereignty
• Franchise and rights disputes
• Ultimatum and invasion (1899)

Anglia frames the conflict as order and empire; Boers frame it as
republican survival.

Both claim legitimacy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CONVENTIONAL PHASE & GUERRILLA TURN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Initial British setbacks give way to conventional victory—then
protracted guerrilla war.

Characteristics:
• Sieges (Ladysmith, Mafeking, Kimberley)
• Field force superiority and eventual Boer defeat in set-piece warfare
• Boer commando resistance; British countermeasures (blockhouses, sweeps)
• Concentration camps; civilian mortality and global outrage

Victory is achieved at high moral and reputational cost.

Procedure is stretched by counter-insurgency.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LEGITIMACY STRESS & IMPERIAL RECKONING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war exposes Anglia’s imperial contradictions.

Effects:
• Liberal and international criticism
• Concentration-camp scandal
• Dominion loyalty (Australia, Canada, New Zealand contribute troops)
• Reconciliation and Union of South Africa (1910) as delayed outcome

Empire wins the war; legitimacy bears the scar.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Boer War encodes **empire at the edge of moral consensus**.

Lessons:
• Imperial expansion can outrun legitimacy.
• Counter-insurgency and civilian control blur procedure.
• The volume of English-speaking history closes on a war that foreshadows twentieth-century decolonization stress.

Churchill’s *The Great Democracies* ends here—not in triumph alone, but in the full weight of the Victorian legacy.
