I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Henry VI as **Anglia’s weak-king sovereign**:
the ruler who inherited Henry V’s French claims as an infant, lost
France, and presided over the collapse of Lancastrian stability—triggering
the Wars of the Roses and his own deposition (1461) and brief
restoration (1470–1471).

Henry VI does not invent civil war.
He **triggers** it: weak kingship, noble factionalism, and the loss of
continental possession combine to make legitimacy litigated by arms.

This file does not moralize Henry’s piety or mental incapacity. It
preserves *weak kingship as structural failure*: the king who cannot
hold the realm cannot hold the crown. Shakespeare’s three *Henry VI*
plays encode the collapse—civil war, rose imagery, Cade’s rebellion—as
Anglian memory of how legitimacy fractures when capacity fails.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

II. LOSS OF FRANCE & COLLAPSE OF HENRY V’S LEGACY (PRIMARY + SECONDARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Henry VI inherits the dual monarchy (England and France) as an infant.
Regency and factionalism replace unity. By 1453, English continental
possession is effectively lost; the Hundred Years’ War ends in
withdrawal. The structural consequence is **legitimacy drain**: the
realm that could not hold France turns inward, and noble rivalries
(Lancaster vs York) escalate into open civil war.

Henry VI’s personal piety and later mental instability are not the
cause of collapse—they are **amplifiers**. The system required a king
who could coordinate elites and project authority; Henry VI could not.
Shakespeare encodes the cost: “So many hours must I take my rest” (*3H6*)
and the repeated imagery of civil war and broken succession.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. WARS OF THE ROSES TRIGGER (PRIMARY + SECONDARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Wars of the Roses (1455–1485) begin under Henry VI. Lancaster
(Henry’s line) and York (claimants with stronger bloodline arguments)
fight for the crown. Henry is deposed by Edward IV (1461), briefly
restored (1470–1471), then murdered (1471). The structural point is that
**weak kingship** does not cause civil war alone—but it removes the
coordinating centre that might have contained noble rivalry.

Shakespeare’s *Henry VI* plays encode the conflict: rose imagery,
Cade’s rebellion (*2H6*), and the slow unravelling of Lancastrian
authority. The king becomes a figure to be claimed or discarded, not a
stabilizing force.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Henry VI encodes **weak kingship as structural failure** and **civil
war as legitimacy litigation**. The king who cannot hold the realm
cannot hold the crown; the realm fractures into Lancaster and York.

Lessons:
• Henry V’s legacy (dual monarchy, martial unity) cannot survive a weak successor.
• Loss of France drains legitimacy and turns conflict inward.
• Civil war is legitimacy litigated by arms—rose against rose.
• Weak kingship removes the coordinating centre; nobles fill the vacuum.

Henry VI is Anglia’s weak-king sovereign—the figure who made the Wars
of the Roses possible by failing to hold the centre.
