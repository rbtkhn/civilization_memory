I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves John Stuart Mill as **Anglia's philosopher of
liberal liberty**: the thinker who combines utilitarianism with a
robust defence of individual freedom, empiricism with reform, and
who articulates the harm principle and the case for representative
government within Anglia's procedural tradition.

Mill does not abandon utility.
He **limits its reach to protect liberty**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT & FORMATIVE CONDITIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mill emerges from Benthamite reform and Victorian stability.

Context:
• Post-Napoleonic settlement
• Parliamentary reform (1832, 1867)
• Empire and industrial expansion
• Liberal and radical movements

Mill bridges philosophical radicalism and liberal restraint.
He defends reform without revolution.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. UTILITY AND LIBERTY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mill accepts the greatest-happiness principle but constrains it.

Core claims:
• The only end of conduct is happiness (utility)
• But the individual is sovereign over self-regarding conduct
• Harm to others is the only warrant for interference

This is **liberal utilitarianism**.

Liberty is not opposed to utility;
it is its condition in a free society.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. EMPIRICISM AND REFORM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mill's epistemology is empiricist; his politics are reformist.

Key ideas:
• Knowledge from experience; induction as logic
• Representative government as best practicable form
• Liberty of thought and discussion as condition of progress

He does not romanticize tradition.
He **reforms it by principle**.

V. PRIMARY SOURCE ANCHORS (VERBATIM)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PRIMARY SOURCE ANCHOR A — *ON LIBERTY*

Source:
• John Stuart Mill, *On Liberty* (1859)

Verbatim Quote:
"The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others."

Context Function:
This anchors the harm principle: liberty is default; interference requires justification.

Provenance Confidence: HIGH

VII. RELATION TO ANGLIAN CIVILIZATIONAL LOGIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mill formalizes Anglia's liberal proceduralism.

Parallels:
• Liberty as default; interference as exception
• Reform by principle, not rupture
• Representative institutions and free discussion

Mill is Anglia's philosopher of **reform without revolution**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mill encodes **liberty within utility**.

Lessons:
• Happiness is the end; liberty is the means.
• Harm to others is the only warrant for coercion.
• Progress depends on free thought and discussion.
