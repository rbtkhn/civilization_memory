I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the House of Plantagenet as the **imperial-expansion
and legal-foundation dynasty** of Anglia: a ruling house that simultaneously
constructed England’s enduring legal architecture and overextended its
continental ambitions.

The Plantagenets embody **law-built strength paired with imperial excess**.
Their memory is defined by expansion followed by structural recoil.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. DYNASTIC ORIGINS & FORMATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Plantagenets originate in the Angevin domains of western Francia.

Key features:
• Franco-Norman ruling elite
• Cross-Channel landholdings
• Feudal-military legitimacy
• Dynastic marriage as empire-building tool

Foundational figure:
• Henry II (r. 1154–1189)

Dynastic premise:
→ England is a base; empire is the objective.

This embedded continental entanglement into Anglia’s core.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ANGEVIN EMPIRE & IMPERIAL LOGIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At peak extent, the Angevin Empire stretched from Scotland’s border
to the Pyrenees.

Key characteristics:
• Personal-rule empire, not institutional federation
• Fragmented loyalties across domains
• Permanent military expenditure
• Chronic succession instability

Strategic flaw:
→ Empire without unified administration.

Territorial scale exceeded governance coherence.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. LEGAL & INSTITUTIONAL LEGACY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Plantagenets’ most durable contribution was **law, not land**.

Key achievements:
• Common Law development
• Royal courts and itinerant justices
• Jury system expansion
• Limitation of baronial autonomy through procedure

Enduring axiom:
→ Law substitutes for presence.

This legal infrastructure outlasted all territorial losses.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CONSTRAINT & RESISTANAD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Imperial pressure generated internal resistance.

Key moments:
• Magna Carta (1215)
• Baronial revolts
• Parliamentary assertion of consent norms

Structural shift:
→ Taxation requires negotiation.

The dynasty unintentionally seeded constitutional constraint.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. WAR, FINANCE, & SYSTEM STRAIN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sustained warfare defined Plantagenet governance.

Key pressures:
• Continental wars with Capetian Francia
• Scottish containment campaigns
• Welsh conquest (Edward I)
• Rising fiscal extraction

Failure mode:
→ Military ambition exceeded fiscal tolerance.

The system strained faster than legitimacy regenerated.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. TERMINAL FRACTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The late Plantagenet period saw systemic erosion.

Key failures:
• Loss of French holdings
• Dynastic infighting
• Weak monarchs (e.g., Richard II)
• Elite coalition collapse

Terminal event:
• Deposition of Richard II (1399)

Dynasty ended not by invasion, but by **elite withdrawal of consent**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Legal institutions can survive imperial collapse.
• Cross-border dynasties amplify overreach risk.
• Empire without administrative unity accelerates legitimacy decay.
• Parliamentary constraint emerges from fiscal necessity.
• Anglia learned durability by abandoning continental empire.

Tension preserved without verdict.
