I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Palmerston as **Anglia’s mid-Victorian foreign-policy
instrument**: the statesman who translated parliamentary legitimacy and
naval reach into liberal intervention, balance-of-power management, and
assertive diplomacy without transforming Britain into a continental
military power.

Palmerston does not conquer.
He **projects**—and in projecting, encodes Anglia’s habit of moral
rhetoric alongside strategic restraint.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

III. FOREIGN POLICY AS PARLIAMENTARY INSTRUMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Palmerston operates **through Parliament and press**, not above them.

Core roles:
• Liberal and constitutional causes abroad (Belgium, Italy, Greece)
• Balance-of-power management (Crimean War, Eastern Question)
• Naval and diplomatic pressure; limited land commitment
• Public rhetoric as tool of policy

Authority flows through visibility and assertiveness—not through
continental conquest.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. RESTRAINT WITHIN ASSERTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Palmerston’s intervention is selective.

Characteristics:
• No permanent continental entanglements
• Naval and financial leverage over mass armies
• Alliance with Francia when necessary (Crimea)
• Moral language without ideological crusade

He encodes **liberal intervention as system**, not revolution.

V. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Palmerston encodes **projection without occupation**.

Lessons:
• Foreign policy can be assertive without conquest.
• Moral rhetoric and strategic interest coexist.
• Parliament and press sustain legitimacy; the crown does not command.
• Recovery and Reform (Vol. 4) places Palmerston between Crimean War and the Victorian Age—mid-century consolidation.
