I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Castlereagh as **Anglia's architect of restraint**:
the Foreign Secretary who shaped the post-Napoleonic order through
balance-of-power management, diplomatic restraint, and legitimacy
through system stability rather than revolutionary transformation.

Castlereagh does not seek territorial expansion.
He **manages order**—and in managing, encodes Anglia's preference for
system stability over continental interventionism.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. KISSINGER *A WORLD RESTORED* — PRIMARY SOURCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Henry Kissinger, in *A World Restored: Metternich, Castlereagh and the
Problems of Peace, 1812–1822* (1957), centers his analysis on
Castlereagh as the architect of British restraint in the post-Napoleonic
settlement, contrasting Castlereagh's approach with Metternich's
continental interventionism (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

SUMMARY — Kissinger, *A World Restored* (ARC-T-MODERN):
> Castlereagh represented Britain's preference for restraint over
> interventionism. Unlike Metternich, who sought to maintain order through
> continental intervention and suppression of revolutionary movements,
> Castlereagh shaped the post-Napoleonic order through balance-of-power
> management and diplomatic restraint. Britain's approach emphasized
> legitimacy through system stability rather than territorial expansion or
> ideological crusades.
> — Henry Kissinger, *A World Restored: Metternich, Castlereagh and the Problems of Peace, 1812–1822* (Boston: Houghton Mifflin, 1957) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]. Summary-only note: Open-web access to a verbatim text was not available at time of update.

III. CONGRESS OF VIENNA & POST-NAPOLEONIC ORDER
(PRIMARY + SECONDARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Castlereagh functions as the architect of the Vienna settlement.

Key roles:
• Balance-of-power management
• Legitimacy through restraint
• Maritime-financial primacy over territorial expansion
• Contrast with Metternich's continental interventionism

The Congress of Vienna (1814–1815) establishes a post-Napoleonic order
in which Britain, through Castlereagh, plays a crucial role as the
architect of restraint rather than continental dominance.

SUMMARY — Kissinger, *A World Restored* (ARC-T-MODERN):
> The Congress of Vienna established a post-Napoleonic order in which
> Britain, through Castlereagh, played a crucial role as the architect of
> restraint. Unlike Metternich's continental interventionism, Britain's
> approach emphasized balance-of-power management, legitimacy through
> restraint, and maritime-financial primacy rather than territorial
> expansion. The settlement reflected Britain's preference for system
> stability over revolutionary transformation.

IV. RESTRAINT VS. METTERNICH'S INTERVENTIONISM
(SECONDARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Castlereagh's method contrasts sharply with Metternich's approach.

Divergences:
• Castlereagh: Balance-of-power management, diplomatic restraint
• Metternich: Continental intervention, suppression of revolution
• Castlereagh: System stability through legitimacy
• Metternich: Order through repression and intervention

Britain shapes the settlement not through territorial expansion but through
balance-of-power management and diplomatic restraint—a method that
contrasts sharply with Metternich's continental interventionism and
reflects Britain's institutional preference for system stability over
revolutionary transformation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LEGITIMACY THROUGH SYSTEM STABILITY
(SECONDARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Castlereagh's approach emphasizes legitimacy through restraint.

Characteristics:
• Maritime-financial primacy over territorial expansion
• Balance-of-power management as system maintenance
• Diplomatic restraint as strategic strength
• System stability over revolutionary transformation

The Vienna settlement reflected Britain's restraint under Hanoverian
constitutional monarchy. Through Castlereagh, Britain shaped the
post-Napoleonic order not through territorial expansion but through
balance-of-power management and diplomatic restraint.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LONG-TERM CIVILIZATIONAL CONSEQUENCES

Enduring effects:

Castlereagh encodes **restraint as system**, not weakness.
