I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the House of York as the **counter-legitimacy
dynasty** that escalated dynastic conflict into open civil war.

York represents **resolution attempted through force**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CLAIM & CONFLICT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yorkist claims emphasize:
• Stronger bloodline legitimacy
• Noble coalition backing
• Military enforcement

Legitimacy is litigated on the battlefield.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. RULE THROUGH VIOLENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Edward IV stabilizes rule temporarily.

Characteristics:
• Strong personal authority
• Noble patronage
• Fragile peace

Institutional weakness persists.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. TERMINAL FRACTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Richard III accelerates collapse.

Key failures:
• Elite alienation
• Moral legitimacy deficit
• Battlefield defeat (Bosworth, 1485)

York fails to close the legitimacy cycle.

Shakespeare encodes Yorkist rise and fall: “Now is the winter of our
discontent / Made glorious summer by this sun of York” (*Richard III*,
1.1) and Richard’s desperate “A horse, a horse! My kingdom for a
horse!” (5.4).
