I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Seven Years’ War as **Anglia’s decisive systems
victory**: the conflict in which Anglia conclusively demonstrated that
maritime supremacy, public credit, and coalition finance could decide
global outcomes more reliably than continental prestige warfare.

For Anglia, the war is not a crisis to be survived.
It is **imperial confirmation**.

The Seven Years’ War marks the transition from contender to
system-setter: Anglia proves it can manage global war as a *portfolio*
rather than a singular battlefield.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT & ENTRY CONDITIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Anglia enters the war with unusual strategic coherence.

Context:
• Escalating colonial rivalry with Francia
• Mature naval infrastructure and global basing
• Deep, liquid public credit markets
• Parliamentary legitimacy for long wars
• Acceptance of limits in continental Europe

Strategic premise:
→ **The decisive theater is global, not European.**

Anglia does not seek to dominate Germany.
It seeks to **deny rivals access to the world system**.

Winston Churchill, in *A History of the English-Speaking Peoples*, Vol. 3 (*The Age of Revolution* [1956–1958]), treats the Seven Years’ War as part of Britain’s rise to world leadership—the volume traces the Spanish and Austrian Successions, Utrecht, the Seven Years’ War, and the American and French revolutions as stages in that rise (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

SOURCE — Churchill, *The Age of Revolution* (Vol. 3) (ARC-T-MODERN):
> "In this campaign a young Virginian officer named George Washington learnt his first military lessons."
> — Winston S. Churchill, *A History of the English-Speaking Peoples*, Vol. 3, *The Age of Revolution* (1956–1958), p. 118 (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. GLOBAL WAR AS DESIGN, NOT ACCIDENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Unlike Francia, Anglia treats multi-theater war as intentional design.

Integrated theaters:
• North America (Canada / Ohio Valley)
• Caribbean sugar islands
• India (Company territories)
• Atlantic and Indian Ocean sea lanes

Command logic:
• Navy-first prioritization
• Peripheral strikes over symbolic capitals
• Economic strangulation over dynastic prestige

Dispersion is not weakness.
It is leverage.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MARITIME SUPREMACY AS DECISION MECHANISM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Naval dominance determines outcomes across theaters.

Key effects:
• Sustained blockade of French ports
• Isolation of overseas garrisons
• Protection of Anglian commerce
• Rapid force redeployment

Concrete results:
• Fall of Canada
• Collapse of French power in India
• Capture of key Caribbean holdings

Victory is decided at sea,
then ratified on land.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. FINANCE AS WARFARE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Anglia’s fiscal system outperforms all rivals.

Mechanisms:
• Long-term public debt at manageable interest
• Broad, reliable taxation base
• Parliamentary legitimacy for borrowing
• Ability to subsidize allies continuously

War does not destabilize the regime.
It **validates it**.

Money becomes a force multiplier, not a vulnerability.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTINENTAL RESTRAINT & PROXY WAR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Anglia deliberately limits continental exposure.

Dynamics:
• Financial and diplomatic support of Prussia
• Acceptance of stalemate in Germany
• Refusal to seek decisive continental battles

Anglia trades territory for time,
and time for global dominance.

Europe is managed.
The world is taken.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. SYSTEMIC ASYMMETRY WITH FRANCIA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war exposes civilizational divergence.

Contrasts:
• Anglia — naval, financial, parliamentary
• Francia — court-centered, army-heavy, prestige-driven

Anglia absorbs loss without panic.
Francia absorbs loss as humiliation.

The same conflict yields opposite memories and opposite futures.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. POST-WAR ORDER & IMPERIAL TAKEOFF
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Treaty of Paris (1763) confirms Anglian ascendancy.

Outcomes:
• Colonial primacy in North America
• Maritime routes secured
• Commercial empire expanded
• Strategic confidence institutionalized

Anglia becomes the first civilization capable of **global system
management**, not merely overseas possession.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. LONG-TERM CIVILIZATIONAL CONSEQUENCES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Seven Years’ War locks in trajectories.

Enduring effects:
• Global war becomes Anglia’s specialty
• Naval dominance normalized
• Credit legitimacy entrenched
• Imperial confidence institutionalized

But victory carries deferred costs:
• Fiscal expansion
• Colonial governance strain
• American rupture incubated

Anglia wins the system—
and inherits its contradictions.

SUMMARY — Kissinger, *Diplomacy* (ARC-T-MODERN):
> Victory creates obligations faster than institutions can absorb them. The Seven Years' War demonstrated this pattern: Britain's triumph created an empire of unprecedented scale, but the administrative, financial, and governance challenges that followed exceeded the capacity of existing institutions to manage effectively, ultimately contributing to the American rupture.
> — Henry Kissinger, *Diplomacy* (New York: Simon & Schuster, 1994) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]. Summary-only note: Open-web access to a verbatim text was not available at time of update.
