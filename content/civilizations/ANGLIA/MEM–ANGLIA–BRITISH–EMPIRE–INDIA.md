I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **British Empire in India** as Anglia's central
imperial node: the relationship that transformed Anglia from maritime–
commercial power into administrative empire and tested the limits of
indirect rule, revenue extraction, and procedural withdrawal.

India is not one possession among many.
It is **the structural pivot** of empire—revenue base, governance
laboratory, and legitimacy stress-test.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. EMPIRE NODE VS TERRITORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This MEM foregrounds the *imperial relationship*—how the British
Empire operates in and through India—rather than India as abstract
territory.

Focus:
• Empire method applied to India
• Revenue, administration, and governance
• Company-to-Crown trajectory
• India's role within the global imperial system

India supplies the empire with scale, revenue, and administrative
precedent. The empire supplies India with law, infrastructure, and
externally imposed order. Neither fully assimilates the other.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ENTRY: COMMERCE TO TERRITORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The East India Company enters as trader; exits as sovereign-like actor.

Phases:
• Chartered trading posts (1600s–1740s)
• Military-diplomatic expansion (1740s–1760s) — Clive era
• Revenue extraction and territorial control (1760s–1858)
• Crown administration (1858–1947)

The transition from Company to Crown (1857 Rebellion) marks a shift
from commercial extraction to formal governance—yet the tension
between extraction and governance persists throughout.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. REVENUE AS IMPERIAL LOGIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
India funds empire more than empire funds India.

Mechanisms:
• Diwani (revenue rights) over Bengal and expanding territories
• Land revenue systems
• Trade surplus extraction
• Opium and commodity flows

India becomes the **fiscal backbone** of imperial reach—subsidizing
naval presence, colonial administration elsewhere, and London's
position as financial center.

Niall Ferguson frames India as the territory that transformed Britain
from maritime-commercial power into global administrative empire—the
"jewel in the crown" whose revenue and strategic weight justified
imperial commitment at unprecedented scale (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ADMINISTRATIVE LABORATORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
India tests empire's governance methods at scale.

Developments:
• Codified law (common law adapted, not imposed wholesale)
• Railway and telegraph infrastructure
• Civil service professionalization
• Indirect rule through princes and local elites

The Raj becomes Anglia's **governance laboratory**—where procedures
developed in India later inform imperial practice elsewhere, and where
the limits of procedural legitimacy are first exposed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. EXIT AND PARTITION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Withdrawal is procedural; consequence is catastrophic.

Features:
• Legal transfer of sovereignty (1947)
• Partition as unmanaged byproduct
• Administrative handover without civilizational rupture in Anglia
• Empire continues elsewhere; India node severed

India's exit preserves Anglia's core institutions. The cost is
externalized—to South Asia, where Partition imposes lasting trauma.
