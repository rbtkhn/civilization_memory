────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Mexico as **Anglia’s revolutionary counterpart
within North America**: the neighboring polity whose legitimacy grammar,
institutional rhythm, and boundary interaction contrast sharply with
Canada’s gradualist continuity.

Where Canada encodes **procedural evolution without rupture**,
Mexico encodes **sovereignty through rupture, repetition, and reset**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGINS IN REVOLUTIONARY SOVEREIGNTY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mexico emerges through abrupt separation from empire.

Context:
• Anti-colonial revolt against Spain
• Rapid assertion of national sovereignty
• Weak inherited institutions
• Competing elite factions

Legitimacy is declared, not accumulated.

The state is born through negation rather than continuity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CYCLICAL POLITICAL REFOUNDING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mexico experiences repeated constitutional resets.

Patterns:
• Frequent regime change
• Military and caudillo intervention
• Competing visions of republic, empire, and revolution
• Fragile procedural inheritance

Authority reasserts itself episodically.

Stability is achieved temporarily, not structurally.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. TERRITORIAL CONTRACTION & EXTERNAL PRESSURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mexico’s early history includes severe territorial loss.

Developments:
• Loss of northern territories
• Exposure to Anglian-American expansion
• Border defined by defeat rather than agreement

The boundary hardens asymmetrically.

Memory encodes vulnerability rather than buffer logic.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LAW, IDENTITY, AND CENTRALIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mexico substitutes ideology for procedure.

Characteristics:
• Centralized authority
• Symbolic nationalism
• Revolutionary myth as legitimacy source
• Law subordinated to political settlement

Identity carries governance burden.

Procedure remains secondary to narrative.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. REVOLUTION AS RECURRING CORRECTIVE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Mexican Revolution (1910–1920) resets legitimacy again.

Outcomes:
• Land reform
• State-led nationalism
• Institutionalized revolution
• Party dominance replacing plural procedure

Revolution becomes institutional memory.

Stability is achieved through managed mobilization.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. STRATEGIC ROLE IN ANGLIAN SYSTEM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mexico functions as **managed friction**, not continuity partner.

Roles:
• Labor and migration interface
• Industrial adjacency without procedural integration
• Security concern rather than alliance anchor

Interdependence exists without alignment.

Proximity replaces partnership.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. IDENTITY WITH MISSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mexico maintains a strong civilizational narrative.

Traits:
• Emphasis on sovereignty
• Historical grievance memory
• Cultural consolidation
• Suspicion of external procedural dominance

Mission substitutes for procedure.

Legitimacy is emotionally reinforced.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mexico encodes **revolutionary legitimacy with cyclical stability**.

Lessons:
• Sovereignty can be repeatedly reasserted without continuity.
• Narrative cohesion can substitute for procedural inheritance.
• Borders formed by defeat retain frictional charge.
• Revolution can be institutionalized but not transcended.

Mexico is Anglia’s persistent contrast.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Mexico prioritizes sovereignty over procedure.
• Stability is cyclical, not accumulative.
• Identity compensates for institutional weakness.
• Proximity to Anglia amplifies asymmetry.
• The border encodes unresolved legitimacy tension.

Tension preserved without verdict.

XIV. COUNTERFACTUAL BOUNDARY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plausible alternative:
• Mexico evolves into a gradualist constitutional monarchy
  or federated continuity state after independence.

Why it did not occur:
• Weak imperial institutions, elite fragmentation, and
  external pressure favored rupture over inheritance.
