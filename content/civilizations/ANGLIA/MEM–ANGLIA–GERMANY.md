I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Anglia–Germany interface as **kinship that
became rivalry and rivalry that became alliance**: the relationship
between the Germanic homeland and the island kingdom founded by
Anglo-Saxon migrants, bound by dynasty and trade, fractured by
naval competition and two world wars, and reconciled in NATO.

For Anglia, Germany encodes **the cousin who challenged the balance**—
linguistic and cultural kinship did not prevent geopolitical conflict
when Germany sought Continental hegemony. Procedure and coalition
prevailed; postwar reconciliation followed.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. KINSHIP: ANGLO-SAXON ORIGINS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
England (Engla-land) was founded by Germanic migrants—Angles, Saxons,
Jutes. The language, law, and social structures were Germanic. The
Hanseatic Steelyard in London bound German merchants to English trade.
Reformation created Protestant solidarity. Anglia and Germania shared
roots; the relationship was not merely external.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE HANOVERIAN CONNECTION (1714–1837)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
German kings ruled Britain. George I and II barely spoke English;
Hanoverian interests influenced British foreign policy. Victoria
married Albert of Saxe-Coburg-Gotha; the royal house was German in
the male line. Victorian Britain saw Germany as kindred—industrial,
Protestant, orderly. Peter H. Wilson: "Germans and English long saw
themselves as kindred peoples—Protestant, industrious, orderly. The
discovery in 1914 that they were enemies shocked both nations
profoundly."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. NAVAL RIVALRY AND WORLD WARS (1890–1945)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tirpitz's naval program threatened British maritime supremacy.
Britain aligned with France and Russia. August 1914: kinship became
enmity. The royal house renamed Windsor (1917). The Second World War
completed the rupture—total war, unconditional surrender. Britain's
structural interest was clear: no single power could dominate Europe.
Germany's pursuit of hegemony triggered coalition formation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. POSTWAR RECONCILIATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
West Germany joined NATO (1955). Britain supported reunification
(1990). Both were EU members (Britain until 2020). The enmity of
two world wars gave way to alliance. Brexit complicated but did not
reverse the relationship. Kinship without hegemony permits cooperation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Germany encodes **kinship, rivalry, and reconciliation**.

• Anglo-Saxon origins; Hanoverian dynasty; Victorian affinity
• Naval race (1898–1914); two world wars
• Structural lesson: Continental hegemony triggers British opposition
• Postwar: NATO, EU; reconciliation without renewed rivalry
