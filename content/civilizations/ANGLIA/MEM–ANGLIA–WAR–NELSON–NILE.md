I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Battle of the Nile as **Anglia's Mediterranean
restoration**: the engagement that reversed French naval ascendancy,
trapped Napoleon's Army of the Orient in Egypt, and prefigured the
doctrinal template that Trafalgar would later consummate.

The Nile is not merely a tactical success.
It is the moment when Nelson's **option-collapsing method** first achieves
strategic effect: total fleet destruction rather than displacement,
night attack over conventional caution, and delegated initiative as
operational doctrine.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The French expedition to Egypt threatens systemic disruption.

Context:
• Napoleon's Army of the Orient landed July 1798
• French fleet anchored at Aboukir Bay (Alexandria's harbor too shallow)
• British Mediterranean squadron driven from the sea in 1796–1797
• Nelson's search for the French fleet—weeks of pursuit, then contact

Structural logic:
→ French control of Egypt = lever on Ottoman legitimacy, India routes, and Mediterranean commerce.
→ Fleet destruction = army isolation = strategic reversal without land battle.

The question is not whether to fight. It is whether to attack *immediately*, at dusk, against anchored positions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. FORCE DISTRIBUTION & GEOGRAPHIC CONSTRAINT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Aboukir Bay imposes a narrow engagement geometry.

French disposition:
• 13 ships of the line, 4 frigates under Vice-Admiral Brueys
• Anchored in line, assuming landward flank secure
• Flagship *Orient* at center

British force:
• 14 ships of the line under Nelson
• Approached from seaward at 14:00; attacked at dusk

The structural opportunity: a gap between French line and shore.
→ Ships that could pass *between* the enemy and the coast could engage from the unprepared port side.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. NELSON'S DOCTRINAL PREVIEW
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nelson attacks without waiting for daylight or full concentration.

Actions:
• Immediate engagement despite approaching night
• HMS *Goliath* leads inshore passage; splits the British attack
• Half the fleet passes between coast and French line
• French caught in crossfire—engaged from both sides

This is the same logic that Trafalgar will formalize:
→ Break the formation; accept approach risk; destroy, do not displace.

Nelson assumes subordinates will interpret intent. No detailed orders needed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. DECISIVE OUTCOME
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The battle produces near-total French fleet annihilation.

Outcomes:
• *Orient* explodes c. 22:00; Brueys killed
• French fleet shattered; only 2 ships of the line, 2 frigates escape (Villeneuve)
• No British ships lost (218 killed, 677 wounded)
• Napoleon's army cut off from France—no supply, no evacuation

Strategic effects:
• British Mediterranean dominance re-established
• Egyptian campaign becomes a cul-de-sac for France
• Nelson's reputation secured; doctrinal template validated

VII. NILE AS TRAFALGAR PRECURSOR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Nile encodes the same civilizational pattern as Trafalgar:

• Decisive engagement over positional maneuvering
• Delegated initiative over centralized control
• Total destruction over tactical victory
• Risk acceptance within institutional confidence

The difference: scale and theater.
→ Nile restores Mediterranean balance.
→ Trafalgar locks maritime supremacy globally.

Nelson does not innovate at Trafalgar. He **reapplies** what the Nile proved.
