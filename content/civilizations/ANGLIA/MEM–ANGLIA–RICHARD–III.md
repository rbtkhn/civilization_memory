I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Richard III as **Anglia’s terminal Plantagenet**:
the last Yorkist king, defeated at Bosworth (1485), whose fall
completes the Wars of the Roses and enables Tudor legitimation through
the defeat of a “villain” king.

Richard III does not invent civil war.
He **ends** the Plantagenet line—and in ending, encodes the moment when
legitimacy passes from York to Tudor by battlefield verdict.

This file does not adjudicate Richard’s character or the Princes in the
Tower. It preserves *terminal fracture*: the last king to die in battle
on English soil, and the structural close of dynastic conflict that
opens the way for Tudor synthesis.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. BOSWORTH & TERMINAL FRACTURE (PRIMARY + SECONDARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Richard III is killed at the Battle of Bosworth (22 August 1485). Henry
Tudor (Henry VII) claims the throne by conquest and marriage. The act is
framed as liberation and restoration—legitimacy is transferred by
battlefield verdict and later by union with the Yorkist line.

Structural consequence: the Wars of the Roses end not by treaty but by
**elimination**. York fails to close the legitimacy cycle; Tudor
succeeds by defeating the last Plantagenet king and absorbing the
symbolic remainder.

Shakespeare encodes Yorkist rise and fall: “Now is the winter of our
discontent / Made glorious summer by this sun of York” (1.1) and
Richard’s desperate “A horse, a horse! My kingdom for a horse!” (5.4).
The winter/summer metaphor and the battlefield cry fix the moment in
Anglian memory: terminal fracture is both political and dramatic.

III. TUDOR LEGITIMATION THROUGH DEFEAT (SECONDARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Henry VII’s legitimacy rests on defeating Richard III and marrying
Elizabeth of York. The Tudor myth—that a villain king was overthrown and
the roses united—is encoded in Shakespeare’s *Richard III*: Richard as
deformed, ruthless, and doomed. That encoding is cognition glue: shared
recognition that legitimacy can be restored by defeating a “bad” king.

The structural point is not moral. It is that **terminal fracture**
enables a new dynasty to claim both conquest and continuity—union of
Lancaster and York, end of civil war.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Richard III encodes **terminal fracture** and **legitimacy by defeat**.
The last Plantagenet falls; Tudor rises. The “winter of our discontent”
becomes “glorious summer” only when the sun of York is extinguished.

Lessons:
• Civil war can end by elimination, not compromise.
• The last king of a line encodes terminal fracture—dramatic and political.
• Tudor legitimation depends on Richard’s defeat and the union of roses.
• “My kingdom for a horse” encodes the moment when everything is lost.

Richard III is Anglia’s terminal Plantagenet—the figure who made
Tudor synthesis possible by falling.
