────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Singapore** as Anglia's strategic port imperial
node: the territory founded by commercial calculation, developed as
naval fortress, and relinquished through managed decolonization.

Singapore is not a settler colony.
It is **empire as chokepoint**—positional value, procedural exit.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FOUNDING: RAFFLES & THE STRAITS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Singapore enters Anglia's system through deliberate commercial
acquisition.

Context:
• 1819 — Stamford Raffles establishes trading post (Dutch sphere; nominal Johor sovereignty)
• Strategic position: Malacca Strait chokepoint, entrepôt between India and China
• Free port policy attracts regional trade

Raffles operates within East India Company framework. The post is
acquired by treaty and commercial logic—not conquest. Singapore
complements Penang and Malacca (Straits Settlements, 1826).

Entry is **positional**: control the strait, control the trade route.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CROWN COLONY & NAVAL BASE
────────────────────────────────────────────
Singapore becomes Crown Colony (1867) as Straits Settlements pass from
Company to Crown.

Development:
• Railway links to Malaya (commodity hinterland)
• Naval base construction (1920s–1930s) — "Gibraltar of the East"
• Commercial hub for rubber, tin, regional trade

The naval base represents empire at its strategic zenith: Britain
positions forces to dominate the sea route between Indian Ocean and
Pacific. Singapore is the **linchpin** of Eastern empire—until 1942.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. FALL OF SINGAPORE (1942)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Japanese capture (February 1942) shatters imperial confidence.

Significance:
• "Worst disaster in British military history" (Churchill)
• Fortress designed for sea attack; falls to land assault from Malaya
• Surrender of ~80,000 Commonwealth troops

The fall exposes **structural vulnerability**: naval supremacy does not
guarantee territorial defense when geography permits land approach.
Singapore becomes symbol of imperial overreach and strategic misjudgment.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. POSTWAR & DECOLONIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
British reoccupation (1945) restores control; empire does not restore
confidence.

Trajectory:
• 1946 — Singapore separate Crown Colony (Malacca, Penang to Malaya)
• 1959 — Internal self-government
• 1963 — Merger with Malaya, Sarawak, North Borneo → Malaysia
• 1965 — Separation; Singapore independent (expulsion from Malaysia)

Exit is **managed**: Britain negotiates transition rather than
resisting. Singapore's independence is negotiated, not won by
revolution. Procedure preserves metropolitan legitimacy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Singapore encodes **chokepoint empire and its limits**.

Lessons:
• Positional value creates imperial nodes without settlement.
• Naval bases presuppose naval supremacy; land approaches can defeat them.
• Managed decolonization preserves procedure when negotiated in advance.
• Strategic nodes can outlast empire when local elites absorb governance.

Singapore is Anglia's proof that empire builds on geography—and
geography can betray it.
