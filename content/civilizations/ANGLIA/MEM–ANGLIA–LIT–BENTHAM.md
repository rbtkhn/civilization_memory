I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Jeremy Bentham as **Anglia's philosopher of
utility and reform**: the thinker who makes the greatest happiness
of the greatest number the standard of law and policy, who attacks
legal fiction and inherited privilege, and who grounds reform in
empirical calculation rather than tradition or natural rights.

Bentham does not defend the past.
He **measures it by utility**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT & FORMATIVE CONDITIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Bentham writes during Anglia's age of reform and empire.

Context:
• Post–American and French revolutions
• Parliamentary reform pressure
• Legal and penal reform movements
• Imperial administration and codification

Bentham applies reason to institutions.
He treats law and policy as instruments of happiness.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. GREATEST HAPPINESS PRINCIPLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Bentham's first principle: utility.

Core claim:
• Nature has placed mankind under the governance of pain and pleasure
• The right act is that which maximizes happiness (pleasure minus pain)
• Every person counts for one; no one for more than one

This is **democratic utilitarianism**.

Law and government are justified only insofar as they
promote the greatest happiness of the greatest number.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. REFORM AND CODIFICATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Bentham attacks legal fiction and common-law obscurity.

Key ideas:
• Codification of law for clarity and accessibility
• Penal reform: punishment proportional to offence; deterrence and reform
• Constitutional reform: representative democracy, publicity, accountability

He does not romanticize custom.
He **reforms it by calculation**.

PRIMARY SOURCE ANCHOR A — *INTRODUCTION TO THE PRINCIPLES OF MORALS AND LEGISLATION*

Source:
• Jeremy Bentham, *An Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation* (1789)

Verbatim Quote:
"Nature has placed mankind under the governance of two sovereign masters, pain and pleasure. It is for them alone to point out what we ought to do, as well as to determine what we shall do."

Context Function:
This anchors utility as the standard of conduct and policy.
