I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves John Adams as **Anglia’s legal-diplomatic
continuity figure** in the American founding: the founder who
articulated rebellion in Anglian legal and constitutional language,
defended the accused (Boston Massacre), negotiated recognition and
peace, and sustained procedural legitimacy through the first
presidential succession.

Adams does not invent American legitimacy.
He **translates** it—lawyer, diplomat, vice president, president—
within the “first great generation” of American leaders alongside
Washington and Jefferson.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

III. LAWYER & PROCEDURAL REBEL
(PRIMARY + SECONDARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Adams operates inside Anglian legal grammar from the start.

Context:
• Common law training
• Defense of British soldiers (Boston Massacre, 1770)—procedure over faction
• Continental Congress; independence as legal and diplomatic argument
• Drafting and advocacy for the Declaration (with Jefferson and others)

Resistance is framed as **rights and precedent**, not as rupture of form.
Adams embodies the paradox: rebellion in the language of the realm.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. DIPLOMAT & RECOGNITION
(PRIMARY + SECONDARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Adams secures external legitimacy and peace.

Roles:
• Commissioner to France; alliance and recognition
• Negotiator of the Treaty of Paris (1783)—codified exit from empire
• Minister to Britain (1785–1788)—post-war relationship
• Vice President (1789–1797); President (1797–1801)

First peaceful transfer of executive power (Adams to Jefferson, 1801)
encodes **procedure over person**: the republic survives the loss of
office without crisis.
