I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Ireland as **Anglia’s unresolved internal frontier**:
a neighboring island repeatedly incorporated, governed, resisted, and
partially separated, never fully harmonized with Anglia’s legitimacy
grammar.

Ireland is near—but never settled.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

Winston Churchill, in *A History of the English-Speaking Peoples*, Vol. 4 (*The Great Democracies* [1956–1958]), treats Ireland within "The Victorian Age" section—"Home Rule for Ireland"—framing the Home Rule debates as Anglia's persistent struggle with unresolved internal frontier, where procedural legitimacy fails to overcome cultural-religious divergence (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

SOURCE — Churchill, *The Great Democracies* (Vol. 4) (ARC-T-MODERN):
> "When Gladstone in 1880 became Prime Minister for the second time his position was not the comfortable one he had held twelve years before."
> — Winston S. Churchill, *A History of the English-Speaking Peoples*, Vol. 4, *The Great Democracies* (1956–1958), ch. 19 "Home Rule for Ireland" (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

II. GEOGRAPHIC & CIVILIZATIONAL PROXIMITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ireland’s closeness intensifies tension.

Factors:
• Shared archipelago
• Strategic vulnerability
• Cultural distinction
• Religious divergence

Distance is minimal.
Difference is decisive.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. FAILED INTEGRATION PATTERNS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Anglia attempts multiple models.

Approaches:
• Conquest
• Plantation
• Administrative rule
• Legal incorporation

None resolve legitimacy.

Procedure fails where belief diverges.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. RELIGION & IDENTITY FRACTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Religion hardens resistance.

Dynamics:
• Catholic majority
• Protestant ruling minority
• Moral legitimacy conflict

Authority is seen as alien.

Law lacks consent.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. STRATEGIC NECESSITY VS LEGITIMACY COST
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ireland matters to Anglia’s security.

Concerns:
• Invasion routes
• Continental leverage
• Internal instability

Control is required.
Acceptance is absent.

This produces chronic friction.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. PARTIAL SEPARATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Modern settlement fractures the problem.

Outcomes:
• Irish independence
• Northern Ireland retained
• Conflict managed, not erased

Resolution is incomplete.

Peace replaces harmony.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ireland encodes **the limits of procedural legitimacy**.

Lessons:
• Law cannot substitute for belief indefinitely.
• Cultural-religious divergence resists absorption.
• Proximity magnifies unresolved conflict.

Ireland is Anglia’s cautionary case.
