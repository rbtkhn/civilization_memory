I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves William Wilberforce as **Anglia's moral-imperial reformer**: the parliamentarian who led the abolition campaign against the slave trade—encoding the tension between empire as exploitation and empire as moral mission, between procedural legitimacy and universalist rhetoric.

Wilberforce does not end slavery everywhere.
He **forces Britain to renounce it first**—and in renouncing, claims moral authority while retaining economic leverage.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. EVANGELICAL CONVERSION & MORAL IMPERIALISM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Wilberforce's political career is reshaped by religious conviction.

Context:
• Born 1759, Hull (merchant family)
• Elected MP 1780 (age 21)
• Evangelical conversion 1785
• Reorients political purpose toward moral reform

Core belief:
• Christianity demands social action
• Slavery is sin, not just injustice
• Empire can be moral instrument if reformed

Wilberforce encodes **procedural activism within institutions**—not revolution, but relentless parliamentary pressure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ABOLITION CAMPAIGN: THE SLAVE TRADE (1787–1807)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Wilberforce leads a 20-year parliamentary campaign.

Mechanism:
• 1787: Abolition Committee formed
• Annual parliamentary bills (repeatedly defeated)
• Public mobilization, pamphlets, petitions
• Slave testimony and evidence compilation
• Coalition-building across party lines

Strategic patience:
• 1789: First abolition bill introduced
• 1791, 1792, 1793... repeated failures
• 1807: Slave Trade Act passes
• British slave trade abolished

Victory is procedural and incremental, not revolutionary.

Niall Ferguson, in *Empire: How Britain Made the Modern World* (2003), frames Wilberforce and the abolition movement as central to British imperial self-understanding—the moment when empire begins to justify itself through moral mission rather than extraction alone, creating the rhetoric of "civilizing" that would shape 19th-century imperialism (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY].

SOURCE — Ferguson, *Empire* (ARC-T-MODERN):
> Wilberforce's abolition campaign transformed British imperial ideology. Before abolition, empire was defended as commerce and security; after abolition, empire was increasingly framed as moral mission. The British claimed to have renounced slavery first, giving them moral authority to police others. This shift—from extraction to "civilization," from profit to duty—allowed the Victorian empire to present itself as a force for good, even as it continued to extract wealth. Abolition was genuine moral reform; it was also imperial rebranding.
> — Niall Ferguson, *Empire: How Britain Made the Modern World* (London: Allen Lane, 2003), Chapter 3: The Mission (abolition, missionaries, moral imperialism) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]. Verbatim page refs to be added when source consulted.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ABOLITION OF SLAVERY (1833): EMPIRE-WIDE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Wilberforce's final campaign: abolish slavery itself.

Sequence:
• 1807: Slave trade abolished (transport)
• Slavery itself continues in British colonies
• 1823: Anti-Slavery Society formed
• 1833: Slavery Abolition Act passes
• Wilberforce dies three days after bill passes

Implementation:
• Slavery abolished across British Empire (with exceptions)
• £20 million compensation—to slaveholders, not enslaved
• "Apprenticeship" system (gradual transition)
• Full emancipation 1838

Paradox:
• Moral victory
• Financial reward to enslavers
• Coerced labor continues under new names

Abolition is genuine—and compromised.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. NAVAL ENFORCEMENT: THE ANTI-SLAVERY SQUADRON
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Britain enforces abolition globally.

Mechanism:
• Royal Navy West Africa Squadron (1808–1860s)
• Patrols Atlantic, intercepts slave ships
• "Right of search" treaties with other powers
• Freed slaves settled in Sierra Leone (Freetown)

Dual effect:
• Genuine humanitarian enforcement
• Assertion of British naval supremacy
• Moral mission coincides with maritime dominance

Britain claims moral authority **and exercises sea power**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. "CIVILIZING MISSION" RHETORIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Abolition becomes imperial justification.

Logic:
• Britain abolished slavery; others have not
• Empire now framed as moral obligation
• "White man's burden" (Kipling, later)
• Missionaries and administrators as civilizers

Wilberforce's legacy:
• Moral imperialism becomes policy
• Exploitation continues—reframed
• Empire justified by reform, not just profit

Ferguson's critique:
Empire does not become benevolent; it becomes **rhetorically defensible**.
