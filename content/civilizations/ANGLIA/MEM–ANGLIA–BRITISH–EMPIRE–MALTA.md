I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Malta** as Anglia's central Mediterranean naval
node: the territory acquired from Napoleonic France, developed as
fortress, besieged in WWII, and relinquished through managed independence.

Malta is not a settler colony.
It is **empire as strategic island**—positional value, procedural exit.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ACQUISITION: NAPOLEONIC CONTEXT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Malta enters Anglia's system through the French Revolutionary Wars.

Context:
• 1798 — Napoleon seizes Malta from Knights of St. John (en route to Egypt)
• 1799–1800 — Maltese uprising; British blockade; French surrender
• 1802 Treaty of Amiens — Britain to evacuate; never fully implemented
• 1814 Treaty of Paris — Britain retains Malta

Malta's value is **positional**: midway between Gibraltar and Suez,
controlling Mediterranean east-west transit. Britain acquires it as
byproduct of war against France; retains it as strategic necessity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. NAVAL BASE & FORTRESS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Malta becomes Britain's principal Mediterranean naval station.

Development:
• Grand Harbour as fleet anchorage
• Dockyard and coaling infrastructure
• Fortress construction and garrison
• Link in Gibraltar–Malta–Suez–India route

Malta complements Gibraltar (west) and Cyprus (east). The island
enables Britain to project naval power across the Mediterranean—and
to protect the India route via Suez.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. WWII: THE SIEGE (1940–1942)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Axis aerial assault tests British retention.

Significance:
• Sustained bombing (1940–1942); civilian and military casualties
• George Cross awarded to Malta (1942) — collective valor
• Island supplied by convoy; held as Allied base
• Strategic value: Malta-based aircraft interdict Axis supply to North Africa

The siege reinforces **positional endurance**: when naval supply holds,
the fortress holds. Malta becomes symbol of imperial resolve—and of the
cost borne by a small population for strategic necessity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. DECOLONIZATION & INDEPENDENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Postwar pressure and local nationalism drive exit.

Trajectory:
• 1947 — Self-government (internal)
• 1950s — Integration/independence debate
• 1964 — Independence (within Commonwealth)
• British base rights negotiated; phased rundown

Exit is **managed**: Britain negotiates transition. Malta retains
Commonwealth membership; British military presence continues under
agreement before eventual withdrawal. Procedure preserves metropolitan
legitimacy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Malta encodes **strategic island and its limits**.

Lessons:
• Positional value creates imperial nodes without settlement.
• Siege tests endurance; population bears cost of strategic choice.
• Managed decolonization preserves procedure when negotiated.
• Strategic islands can achieve independence while retaining links.

Malta is Anglia's proof that Mediterranean empire could hold under
siege—and could end by agreement.
