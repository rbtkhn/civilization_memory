I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Gibraltar** as Anglia's Mediterranean chokepoint:
the territory ceded in perpetuity by treaty, retained through siege and
diplomacy, and held without scheduled exit.

Gibraltar is not a colony in transition.
It is **empire as permanent fixture**—the node that never leaves.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ACQUISITION: TREATY OF UTRECHT (1713)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Gibraltar enters Anglia's system through the War of Spanish Succession.

Context:
• 1704 — Anglo-Dutch capture from Spain
• 1713 Treaty of Utrecht — Spain cedes Gibraltar to Britain "for ever"
• Perpetual cession; no time limit; no exit clause

The Rock secures Britain's position at the **Strait of Gibraltar**—the
narrow passage between Atlantic and Mediterranean. Control the strait;
control naval access to the inland sea.

Entry is treaty-based. Legitimacy rests on cession, not conquest
narrative. Spain has never accepted the transfer; Britain has never
wavered.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. STRATEGIC VALUE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Gibraltar functions as naval fortress and commercial waypoint.

Functions:
• Naval base — Mediterranean fleet presence
• Coaling station (later refueling) for merchant and military shipping
• Signal station and communications node
• Symbol of endurance (sieges survived: 1727, 1779–1783, WWII)

Strategic logic mirrors Malta, Singapore, Hong Kong: **chokepoint
control** supporting maritime dominance. Gibraltar is the western
anchor of Britain's Mediterranean position.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. SIEGES & RETENTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Gibraltar survives repeated Spanish attempts at reconquest.

Notable:
• Great Siege (1779–1783) — Spanish-French blockade; British hold
• WWII — Fortress holds; Axis denied Strait control

Each siege reinforces **positional resilience**: the Rock is defensible
by sea supply when naval supremacy holds. Gibraltar becomes symbol of
imperial stubbornness—refusal to yield what treaty granted.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. NO EXIT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Unlike Hong Kong, Singapore, or Malta, Gibraltar has no decolonization.

Features:
• Perpetual cession under treaty
• Referendum (1967, 2002): overwhelming preference to remain British
• Spain's sovereignty claim; Britain's refusal to negotiate transfer
• EU exit (Brexit) complicates Gibraltar-Spain frontier

Gibraltar encodes **empire without terminus**—the possession that
endures because treaty, strategy, and local consent align. Anglia does
not exit; it holds.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Gibraltar encodes **empire as permanent strategic asset**.

Lessons:
• Perpetual treaty can create possession without scheduled exit.
• Chokepoints justify retention when local consent endures.
• Not all imperial nodes decolonize; some persist by choice and law.
• Strategic value and treaty formality can override moral pressure.

Gibraltar is Anglia's proof that empire can hold—when geography,
procedure, and population align.
