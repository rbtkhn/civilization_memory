────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Sui dynasty as the **reunification and compression
engine** of Chinese civilization: the short but decisive regime that
**terminated prolonged fragmentation** and **reconstructed imperial
infrastructure**, enabling the Tang synthesis.

Sui China represents **systemic reassembly under extreme strain**—a dynasty
that succeeded structurally while failing politically.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. DYNASTIC OVERVIEW (MEMORY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Founded by Yang Jian (杨坚, Emperor Wen), the Sui reunified China after nearly
four centuries of division following the Han collapse.

Core characteristics:
• Rapid territorial reunification  
• Centralized imperial authority  
• Massive state-led infrastructure  
• Short dynastic lifespan  

Foundational self-framing emphasized order:
> “天下一统。”
(The realm is unified.)

Unity was achieved faster than legitimacy could stabilize.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ADMINISTRATIVE & INSTITUTIONAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Sui rebuilt imperial administration on Han–Wei foundations.

Key features:
• Restoration of centralized prefectural governance  
• Standardized law codes (开皇律)  
• Revival of civil examinations (科举雏形)  
• Reduction of aristocratic autonomy  

Administrative intent emphasized uniform rule:
> “法一而天下治。”
(When the law is unified, the realm is governed.)

Institutional coherence outpaced political endurance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. INFRASTRUCTURE & STATE CAPACITY MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Sui are defined by infrastructure-driven governance.

Major projects:
• Grand Canal (大运河) linking north and south  
• Road and granary systems  
• Military transport integration  

State ambition was explicit:
> “以通天下之利。”
(To connect the benefits of the entire realm.)

Logistical integration reshaped China permanently.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ECONOMIC & LABOR MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sui state capacity relied on intensive mobilization.

Key elements:
• Heavy corvée labor (徭役)  
• Agricultural recovery and redistribution  
• High extraction to fund infrastructure and campaigns  

Economic strain was recognized:
> “民力既竭。”
(The people’s strength was exhausted.)

Productive recovery could not offset coercive overload.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LEGITIMACY & MANDATE MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sui legitimacy rested on performance rather than moral narrative.

Sources of authority:
• Reunification success  
• Administrative order  
• Material improvement  

However, moral legitimacy lagged:
> “政急而民怨。”
(Governance was harsh and resentment grew.)

Mandate erosion was rapid once failures accumulated.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. MILITARY & STRATEGIC MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Sui pursued aggressive external campaigns.

Key features:
• Large conscript armies  
• Failed invasions of Goguryeo (高句丽)  
• Logistical overextension  

Strategic hubris was noted:
> “兵疲而功不成。”
(The troops were exhausted, yet no success was achieved.)

Military failure catalyzed internal collapse.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. INTERNAL STRESS & COLLAPSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Collapse followed compounded strain.

Pressures included:
• Labor exhaustion  
• Military defeats  
• Elite rebellion  
• Popular revolts  

Late judgment was blunt:
> “隋亡于役重。”
(The Sui fell because of excessive burdens.)

The dynasty collapsed within a generation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. FAILURE, ABSORPTION, & LEGACY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sui collapse was political, not structural.

Absorption dynamics:
• Tang inherited Sui institutions wholesale  
• Canal and administrative systems endured  
• Reunification proved irreversible  

Later synthesis summarized:
> “隋亡而唐兴，其制皆隋也。”
(The Sui fell and the Tang rose, but Tang institutions were Sui’s.)

Sui failure enabled Tang success.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Reunification requires compression before synthesis.
• Infrastructure can outlast political legitimacy.
• Speed of reconstruction increases fragility.
• Sui is Qin-like in function, not ideology.

Tension preserved without verdict.
