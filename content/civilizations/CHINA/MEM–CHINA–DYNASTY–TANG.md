I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Tang dynasty as the **high-imperial synthesis of
territorial power, administrative confidence, and cultural cosmopolitanism**.

Tang China represents a phase in which **expansion, legitimacy, and culture
reinforced one another**, creating a civilizational model later dynasties
invoked even when they could not replicate its conditions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. DYNASTIC OVERVIEW (MEMORY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Founded after the collapse of the Sui dynasty (隋朝), the Tang rapidly
re-established imperial authority and projected power across East and
Central Asia.

Core characteristics:
• Large territorial footprint  
• Confident central administration  
• Military superiority in early period  
• Cultural openness and prestige  

Tang political self-understanding emphasized ordered foundations:
> “治天下者，先正其本。”
(Those who govern the realm must first set their foundations in order.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ADMINISTRATIVE & INSTITUTIONAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Tang refined imperial governance through the **Three Departments and
Six Ministries system (三省六部制)**, clarifying policy formulation,
review, and execution.

Key features:
• Codified law as backbone (《唐律》)  
• Balance between aristocratic lineage and merit selection  
• Expansion of examination pathways alongside elite families  

Administrative ideology stressed legal clarity:
> “法令行则国治。”
(When laws are enforced, the state is well governed.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ECONOMIC & INFRASTRUCTURAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tang economic legitimacy relied on agrarian control and infrastructure.

Key systems:
• Equal-field system (均田制) in early Tang  
• State granaries and taxation in kind  
• Canal and road networks  
• Silk Road trade integration  

Provisioning was seen as moral governance:
> “仓廪实而知礼节。”
(When granaries are full, the people understand propriety.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LEGITIMACY & MANDATE MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tang legitimacy combined:
• Military success  
• Confucian moral language  
• Ritual authority  
• Cultural prestige  

Mandate logic was explicit in court ideology:
> “天命不常，惟德是辅。”
(Heaven’s mandate is not constant; it supports virtue.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MILITARY POWER & STRATEGIC POSTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Early Tang power rested on professional armies and frontier garrisons.

Characteristics:
• Cavalry dominance  
• Strategic depth into Central Asia  
• Integration of non-Han forces  

Strategic doctrine favored intimidation through strength:
> “以威服远。”
(Subdue the distant through power.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CULTURAL & COSMOPOLITAN MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tang culture was expansive and outward-facing.

Key elements:
• Religious pluralism (Buddhism, Daoism, foreign faiths)  
• Poetry as elite expression (李白 Li Bai, 杜甫 Du Fu)  
• Foreign merchant and religious communities  

Imperial cultural confidence was explicit:
> “四海之内，皆为唐土。”
(All under Heaven was considered Tang territory.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. EXTERNAL SHOCKS & INTERNAL FRACTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The decisive shock was the **An Lushan Rebellion (安史之乱, 755–763)**.

Consequences:
• Military decentralization  
• Fiscal exhaustion  
• Rise of regional warlords (藩镇割据)  

Later historiography summarized the turning point:
> “自此之后，唐不复盛。”
(From this point onward, Tang never fully recovered.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. FAILURE, DECLINE, & ABSORPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tang decline was gradual rather than catastrophic.

Patterns:
• Court ritual continued as real power waned  
• Provincial autonomy became entrenched  
• Central authority hollowed  

Later dynasties absorbed Tang institutions:
• Legal codes  
• Cultural canon  
• Imperial ideal  

Tang became **memory-as-model**, not memory-as-warning.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Expansion reinforces legitimacy only while administration scales with it.  
• Cultural prestige amplifies power but cannot substitute for control.  
• Military decentralization is difficult to reverse once normalized.  
• Later dynasties emulate Tang symbolism without Tang conditions.
