────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Republic of China (ROC) as a **rupture–continuity
regime** within Chinese civilization: a state that **ended dynastic rule
without successfully resolving the problem of national authority**.

The ROC exists simultaneously as:
• A revolutionary terminus of imperial China  
• An incomplete modern state project  
• A displaced sovereignty preserved on Taiwan  

ROC memory is defined less by governance success than by **structural
interruption**, civil war, and unresolved legitimacy transfer.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. REVOLUTIONARY ORIGIN MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The ROC was founded in 1912 after the collapse of the Qing dynasty.

Core origin features:
• Anti-dynastic revolution  
• Constitutional aspiration  
• Elite-led modernization project  
• Weak coercive capacity  

Sun Yat-sen framed the rupture morally:
> “革命尚未成功。”
(The revolution has not yet succeeded.)

Authority was claimed before it could be enforced.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. LEGITIMACY WITHOUT CONSOLIDATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The ROC asserted national sovereignty but failed to monopolize force.

Key deficits:
• Fragmented military power  
• Regional warlordism  
• Weak fiscal extraction  
• Limited rural penetration  

Modern state form lacked modern state reach:
> “有国而无权。”
(A state exists, but authority does not.)

Legitimacy was ideological, not operational.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. WARLORDISM & CIVIL WAR MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The ROC period was dominated by internal conflict.

Key dynamics:
• Competing regional militaries  
• Party–army fusion without discipline  
• Prolonged civil war with the CCP  

National unification was aspirational:
> “统一未成，革命未竟。”
(Unification unfinished; revolution incomplete.)

Violence substituted for administration.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. JAPANESE INVASION & NATIONAL FRACTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Second Sino-Japanese War (1937–1945) strained the ROC beyond recovery.

Effects:
• Territorial collapse  
• Reliance on foreign aid  
• Inflation and fiscal breakdown  
• Erosion of elite credibility  

War magnified weakness rather than consolidating authority:
> “外侮当前，内政先溃。”
(With foreign invasion present, internal governance collapsed first.)

Victory did not restore legitimacy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. RETREAT TO TAIWAN & DISPLACED SOVEREIGNTY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In 1949, the ROC retreated to Taiwan after defeat by the CCP.

Core transformation:
• Loss of mainland jurisdiction  
• Preservation of constitutional continuity  
• Martial law and security governance  
• Reliance on external protection  

Sovereignty became spatially compressed:
> “法统在此，疆域已失。”
(The legal order remains here, though the territory is lost.)

The ROC survived as **government-in-exile without exile status**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. COLD WAR EMBEDDING & AUTHORITARIAN STABILITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
On Taiwan, the ROC reconstituted authority through control rather than consent.

Key features:
• One-party dominance  
• Security state  
• Land reform and developmental policy  
• U.S. alliance dependence  

Endurance derived from external balance:
• Strategic triangularity transformed compression into viability  

Stability preceded liberalization:
> “先求生存，再谈民主。”
(First seek survival, then speak of democracy.)

Legitimacy was postponed, not abandoned.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. DEMOCRATIZATION & IDENTITY DIVERGENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
From the late 1980s onward, the ROC underwent democratic transition.

Consequences:
• Electoral legitimacy  
• Constitutional persistence  
• Identity pluralization  
• Decoupling from mainland political claims  

Transformation occurred:
• From civilizational claimant to island polity  

Political meaning shifted:
> “中华民国在台湾。”
(The Republic of China is in Taiwan.)

Governance legitimacy no longer required continental representation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. STRATEGIC FUNCTION & CONTEMPORARY MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Today, the ROC functions as:
• Legal–constitutional continuity claim  
• Political container for Taiwan governance  
• Strategic irritant and stabilizer  

Additional roles persist:
• Symbolic reservoir of non-Communist Chinese sovereignty  
• Alternative legitimacy memory during and after civil war  

Its existence complicates closure:
> “存而未定。”
(Existing, yet unresolved.)

The ROC endures through **institutional inertia and external equilibrium**,
not civilizational convergence.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Revolutionary legitimacy without coercion decays rapidly.
• State form cannot substitute for state capacity.
• Displaced sovereignty can persist if externally protected.
• Legal continuity outlives territorial control.
• Weak states can endure when embedded in great-power equilibria.
• Vulnerability can convert into strategic indispensability.

Resolution is deferred, not denied.
