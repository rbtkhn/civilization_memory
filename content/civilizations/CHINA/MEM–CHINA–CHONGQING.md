I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Chongqing as the **interior redoubt** of Chinese
civilizational memory: the space where the ROC government retreated
during the Second Sino-Japanese War (1937–1945), making it the
**wartime capital**—a Yangtze-riverine node beyond easy reach of
coastal invasion, encoding **interior as refuge** when the coast
and Beijing fell. Under the PRC, Chongqing became a direct-controlled
municipality (1997) and a major riverine and industrial node.

Chongqing is remembered not only as a city, but as the **contrast
to coastal humiliation**: when the coast was occupied or
imposed upon (treaty ports, Boxer, Japanese invasion), the
interior—Chongqing on the Yangtze—could function as a
political and military redoubt. No treaty port; no foreign
concession; legitimacy retreated upriver.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC & STRUCTURAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Chongqing sits at the confluence of the Yangtze and Jialing
rivers, in the Sichuan basin approach. It is a **riverine and
overland** node: access to the interior (Sichuan, Chengdu,
upper Yangtze) and to the coast via the Yangtze, but not
a maritime port in the treaty-port sense. Its strategic
value lay in **distance from the coast** and **riverine
supply**: Japanese forces did not take Chongqing; the ROC
government held there until 1946.

Key structural features:
• Wartime capital (1937–1946): ROC government seat; interior redoubt
• Yangtze node: riverine transport; contrast with Shanghai (maritime)
• No treaty port: never opened by unequal treaty; no foreign concession
• PRC municipality (1997–): direct-controlled; industrial and transport hub

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. WARTIME CAPITAL & INTERIOR REFUGE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Chongqing encodes **interior as refuge**: when Nanjing fell
(1937) and the coast was occupied, the ROC moved the capital
inland to Chongqing. The city was bombed but not taken; it
symbolized continued resistance and legitimacy in exile.
For Republican and PRC narrative, Chongqing is evidence that
**authority could retreat** and that the interior could
sustain a government when the coast could not. The contrast
with Beijing (capital struck) and Shanghai (treaty port) is
structural: Chongqing was never a treaty port and never
occupied by a foreign power in the same way.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PRC MUNICIPALITY & RIVERINE NODE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The PRC elevated Chongqing to a direct-controlled municipality
in 1997 (with the absorption of parts of Sichuan), making it
a major administrative and economic node. The Three Gorges
Dam and Yangtze shipping reinforced its role as a riverine
and industrial hub. Chongqing thus also encodes **interior
development**: not a coastal SEZ like Shenzhen, but an
upriver node integrated into PRC growth and party–state
control. No foreign concession; sovereignty uninterrupted.
