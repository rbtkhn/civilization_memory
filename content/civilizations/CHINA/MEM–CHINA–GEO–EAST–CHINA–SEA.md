I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the East China Sea (东海) as the **maritime
corridor and contested space** of Chinese civilizational memory:
the sea between the mainland (Shanghai, Zhejiang, Fujian),
Taiwan (north of the strait), and the Ryukyu Islands (Okinawa/
Japan)—a semi-enclosed sea with major shipping lanes, fisheries,
and subsurface resources. The East China Sea is not only a
trade route but a **theatre of sovereignty assertion**: control
of islands (Senkaku/Diaoyu 钓鱼岛/尖閣諸島), EEZ boundaries,
and sea lanes encodes both historic tributary and treaty-era
logic and contemporary PRC–Japan–ROC contestation.

The East China Sea is remembered as **first-island-chain logic**:
the sea between the mainland and the chain (Japan–Taiwan–
Philippines); whoever controls key passages and features can
constrain or enable projection. Contradictions are preserved
without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC & STRUCTURAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The East China Sea lies between the mainland (Shanghai delta,
Zhejiang, Fujian), Taiwan (north of Taiwan Strait), and the
Ryukyu Islands (Okinawa prefecture, Japan)—bounded to the
north by the Yellow Sea and Korea, and opening to the Pacific
east of Ryukyu. Key structural features: **Senkaku/Diaoyu**
islands (PRC/ROC claim; Japan administers)—small, uninhabited
islets with EEZ and symbolic weight; **continental shelf** and
**EEZ overlap** (PRC, Japan, ROC claims); **shipping lanes**
(Shanghai–Pacific, Northeast Asia–Southeast Asia); **first
island chain**—East China Sea as space between mainland and
chain; projection vs containment.

Key structural features:
• Maritime corridor: historic and contemporary shipping; Shanghai and Zhejiang ports face this sea
• Sovereignty disputes: Senkaku/Diaoyu (PRC/ROC vs Japan); EEZ boundaries contested; no agreed maritime delimitation
• First island chain: East China Sea as interior of chain; mainland projection toward Pacific passes through or near this sea
• Ryukyu/Liuqiu: historic tributary (Liuqiu 琉球) and Japanese incorporation (1879); contemporary Okinawa as US–Japan basing

Terrain enforces **maritime logic**: control of features and lanes shapes who can project and who can deny.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. SEA AS TRADE AND TREATY CORRIDOR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The East China Sea encodes **connectivity and humiliation**:
(1) **Historic trade**—Shanghai, Ningbo, and Fujian ports
faced this sea; tribute and trade with Ryukyu/Liuqiu;
coastal traffic and fishing. (2) **Treaty-era**—Western
and Japanese naval power entered through it; First Opium
War and treaty ports (Shanghai) opened onto this sea;
Japanese expansion (1895 Taiwan, 1879 Ryukyu) reshaped
the maritime perimeter. (3) **Post-1949**—PRC and ROC
both claim Diaoyu; Japan administers Senkaku; US–Japan
alliance and Okinawa basing overlay the eastern edge.
The sea that had been a corridor for trade and tribute
is now a space of **sovereignty assertion and EEZ
contestation**. MEM–CHINA–SHANGHAI (port) opens onto
this sea; MEM–CHINA–TAIWAN–STRAIT connects it to
the South China Sea.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. SEA AS THEATRE OF SOVEREIGNTY AND CONTAINMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The East China Sea encodes **strategic theatre**: (1)
**First island chain**—Japan (Ryukyu), Taiwan form the
northern segment of the chain that can constrain
mainland maritime breakout; the East China Sea is the
**interior** of that chain, where PRC naval and coast
guard presence has grown. (2) **Senkaku/Diaoyu**—
recurrent crises (2010, 2012, ongoing); coast guard
and naval patrols; no armed clash but no resolution;
symbolic weight exceeds material value. (3)
**EEZ and ADIZ**—PRC East China Sea ADIZ (2013);
overlapping EEZ claims; freedom of navigation and
patrols. For civilizational memory, the East China
Sea is the **maritime extension** of the Shanghai
delta and Fujian—the space where treaty-era humiliation
(foreign navies, treaty ports) and reconstitution
(PRC claims, naval modernization) are projected onto
water. MEM–CHINA–TAIWAN and MEM–CHINA–GEO–
TAIWAN–STRAIT are the **adjacent geographic MEMs**;
MEM–CHINA–GEO–SOUTH–CHINA–SEA is the **southern
maritime frame**; this file is the **eastern maritime
frame** between mainland and first chain.

V. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Maritime chokepoint logic applies: control of East China Sea features and lanes shapes mainland projection and first-chain containment.
• Senkaku/Diaoyu dispute encodes unresolved sovereignty and humiliation narrative (Japanese administration since late 19th c.; PRC/ROC claim).
• First island chain makes the East China Sea interior space—PRC must project through or past it; Japan–US hold the eastern edge.
• The sea links treaty-era memory (Shanghai, First Opium) to contemporary EEZ and ADIZ assertion.

Tension preserved without verdict.
