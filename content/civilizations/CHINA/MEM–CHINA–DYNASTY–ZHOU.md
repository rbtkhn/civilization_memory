────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Zhou dynasty as the **foundational moral–cosmological
and political grammar** of Chinese civilization: the era that articulated
**Mandate of Heaven**, ritual legitimacy, feudal ordering, and the intellectual
pluralism later dynasties absorbed.

Zhou functions as the **source layer**—ideas precede institutions; norms
precede administration.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. DYNASTIC OVERVIEW (MEMORY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Zhou overthrew the Shang and justified rule through moral cosmology rather
than lineage alone. Early stability (Western Zhou) gave way to decentralization
and competitive polities (Eastern Zhou).

Core characteristics:
• Moral legitimation over brute force  
• Feudal enfeoffment (封建)  
• Ritual hierarchy (礼)  
• Intellectual pluralism (诸子百家)

Foundational claim:
> “天命靡常。”
(The Mandate of Heaven is not constant.)

This axiom permanently conditioned Chinese legitimacy logic.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. LEGITIMACY & COSMOLOGICAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Zhou legitimacy fused morality, ritual, and cosmic order.

Key features:
• Heaven (天) as moral arbiter  
• Ruler accountable to virtue  
• Loss of virtue equals loss of mandate  

Canonical articulation:
> “惟德动天。”
(Only virtue moves Heaven.)

This doctrine enabled justified rebellion without civilizational rupture.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. POLITICAL–INSTITUTIONAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Western Zhou governance relied on kinship-based feudal bonds.

Key elements:
• Enfeoffed lords owing ritual allegiance  
• Decentralized military authority  
• Custom over codified law  

Structural weakness emerged as kinship diluted:
> “礼坏乐崩。”
(Ritual collapsed; music disintegrated.)

Eastern Zhou fragmentation followed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. RITUAL, LAW, & SOCIAL ORDER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Zhou order prioritized ritual (礼) over punishment.

Doctrine emphasized hierarchy and propriety:
> “非礼勿视，非礼勿听。”
(Do not look or listen to what violates ritual.)

As ritual authority waned, legalist responses emerged in later states.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. INTELLECTUAL & PHILOSOPHICAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Eastern Zhou produced unprecedented intellectual diversity.

Major currents:
• Confucianism (儒家) — moral cultivation, social harmony  
• Daoism (道家) — natural order, non-action  
• Legalism (法家) — law, coercion, state power  
• Mohism (墨家) — universal concern, merit  

Enduring maxim:
> “百家争鸣。”
(A hundred schools contend.)

Later empires canonized selectively.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. MILITARY & STRATEGIC MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Military competition intensified during the Warring States.

Key shifts:
• Mass infantry and conscription  
• Bureaucratized warfare  
• Strategic thought (Sunzi)  

Strategic realism emerged:
> “兵者，国之大事。”
(War is a matter of vital importance to the state.)

This logic fed directly into Qin consolidation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. TRANSITION, COLLAPSE, & ABSORPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Zhou authority eroded without a single terminal collapse.

Absorption dynamics:
• Qin inherited Legalist tools forged in Zhou competition  
• Han absorbed Confucian moral vocabulary  
• Ritual legitimacy persisted across empires  

Retrospective synthesis:
> “周虽旧邦，其命维新。”
(The Zhou were ancient, yet their mandate was renewed.)

Zhou ended, but its grammar endured.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Legitimacy precedes administration in Chinese statecraft.  
• Moral cosmology enables continuity through regime change.  
• Intellectual plurality precedes imperial synthesis.  
• Zhou ideas constrain all later imperial behavior.

Tensions preserved without verdict.
