I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Himalayas (喜马拉雅山脉) as the **southern
barrier and contested frontier** of Chinese civilizational memory:
the space that separates the Tibetan Plateau from the Indian
subcontinent—a **high-altitude barrier** that has limited direct
overland exchange between China and India while framing Tibet's
position as buffer and contested periphery. The Himalayas are not
a trade corridor like the Silk Road; they are **barrier logic**—
whoever controls the passes and the plateau edge can deny or permit
access; the border between China (Tibet) and India (and Nepal,
Bhutan) runs through and along the range. Contemporary disputes
(India–China border, Tibet sovereignty) are encoded in this
geography.

The Himalayas are remembered as **barrier logic**: the range that
imposes discontinuity between Tibetan and Indian space; legitimacy
and control are asserted at the passes and the frontier, not along
a continuous corridor. Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC & STRUCTURAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Himalayas run roughly east–west along the southern edge of the
Tibetan Plateau—separating Tibet (and China) from Nepal, Bhutan,
and India (Sikkim, Arunachal Pradesh, and other border regions).
Key structural features: **high altitude**—passes are few and
seasonal; **barrier**—no equivalent to the Silk Road or Grand Canal
through the range; **frontier**—the India–China border (and Nepal,
Bhutan borders) is disputed or contested in segments. Tibet (MEM–CHINA–GEO–TIBET)
sits **north** of the Himalayas; Indian states sit **south**. The
range thus encodes **discontinuity**—political and cultural—between
Chinese (Tibetan) and Indian space.

Key structural features:
• Barrier: high-altitude range; few passes; limits bulk overland exchange between China and India
• Frontier: India–China border runs along/through the range; contested segments (e.g. Arunachal Pradesh, Aksai Chin linkage to Tibet/Xinjiang)
• Tibet: Himalayas form southern edge of Tibetan Plateau; Tibet incorporation (Qing, PRC) places Chinese control north of the range
• Contrast with Silk Road: Himalayas are barrier, not corridor; no continuous trade route through the range comparable to Central Asian Silk Road

Terrain enforces **barrier logic**: the range imposes discontinuity; control of passes and plateau edge shapes who can project or deny.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. HIMALAYAS AS BARRIER AND TIBET FRONTIER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Himalayas encode **barrier**: the range has **limited** direct
overland exchange between Chinese and Indian civilizational space—
unlike the Silk Road (Central Asia) or the maritime routes (South
China Sea, Indian Ocean). Trade and cultural transmission between
China and India historically went **around** the range (e.g. maritime,
or via Central Asia), not through it. The Himalayas thus function
as **southern limit**—not a corridor to be controlled for bulk
movement, but a **barrier** that defines the edge of the Tibetan
plateau and the frontier with India. MEM–CHINA–GEO–TIBET depends
on the Himalayas as the **southern boundary** of Tibetan space;
Tibet's incorporation (Qing, PRC) places Chinese administration
north of the range and frames the India–China border as running
along or through it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. HIMALAYAS AS CONTESTED FRONTIER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The same range encodes **contested frontier**: the India–China
border (and borders with Nepal, Bhutan) is **disputed** in segments—
Arunachal Pradesh, Aksai Chin (linked to Tibet and Xinjiang), and
other areas. The Himalayas do not resolve the dispute; they **frame**
it—the border is where the two states (and their predecessors)
assert control along the range and the plateau edge. For civilizational
memory, the Himalayas encode **discontinuity and contestation**—
not the connectivity of the Silk Road or the bulk logic of the
Grand Canal, but the **barrier and frontier** logic of a high-altitude
divide. MEM–CHINA–GEO–TIBET and MEM–CHINA–XINJIANG (Aksai Chin
linkage) are the **adjacent geographic MEMs**; this file is the
**geographic frame** for the southern barrier.
