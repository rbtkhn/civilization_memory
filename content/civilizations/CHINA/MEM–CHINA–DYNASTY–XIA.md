────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Xia dynasty as the **foundational narrative layer**
of Chinese civilization: the point at which **kingship, flood control,
hereditary rule, and moral authority** are first integrated into a coherent
civilizational memory.

Xia functions as **origin myth with administrative implications**, not as a
fully verified dynastic record.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STATUS & EVIDENTIARY NOTE (MANDATORY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Xia dynasty is attested primarily through **later textual tradition**.
No contemporaneous written records survive.

Archaeological correlations (e.g., Erlitou culture 二里头文化) are debated
and **not conclusively identified** as Xia.

This file preserves **civilizational memory**, not archaeological certainty.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. DYNASTIC OVERVIEW (MEMORY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Traditional accounts describe the Xia as founded by Yu the Great (大禹),
who tamed catastrophic floods and established hereditary kingship.

Core characteristics:
• Flood-control legitimacy  
• Hereditary succession  
• Early territorial authority  
• Moral kingship narrative  

Foundational claim:
> “禹平水土，定九州。”
(Yu pacified the waters and established the Nine Provinces.)

Legitimacy originates in **service to collective survival**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. LEGITIMACY & MORAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Xia legitimacy is framed as **earned through labor and sacrifice**.

Key elements:
• Kingship as burden, not privilege  
• Authority derived from public works  
• Moral example over coercion  

Canonical maxim:
> “劳而不伐，有功而不德。”
(He labored without boasting; achieved merit without claiming virtue.)

This frames rulership as responsibility.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. POLITICAL & INSTITUTIONAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Xia governance is portrayed as transitional.

Features:
• Shift from abdication to heredity  
• Personal authority over institutions  
• Minimal bureaucratic abstraction  

Succession marks a civilizational turning point:
> “天下为家。”
(The realm became hereditary.)

This moment defines later dynastic tension.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ECONOMIC & ENVIRONMENTAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Environmental management dominates Xia memory.

Key themes:
• Flood control  
• Agricultural stabilization  
• Territorial ordering  

Flood control is civilizational genesis:
> “治水为政之本。”
(Governing the waters is the foundation of rule.)

State authority emerges from environmental mastery.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CULTURAL & SYMBOLIC MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Xia culture is symbolic rather than documented.

Key motifs:
• Bronze precursors  
• Totemic kingship  
• Sacred geography  

Cultural memory emphasizes order from chaos:
> “洪水既平，天下乃定。”
(When the floods were quelled, the realm became stable.)

Symbols precede texts.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. FAILURE, TRANSITION, & OVERTHROW
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Traditional narrative holds that Xia declined through moral failure.

Last ruler Jie (桀) is portrayed as tyrannical.

Canonical judgment:
> “桀失其道。”
(Jie lost the Way.)

This narrative enables **moralized regime change**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. ABSORPTION & CIVILIZATIONAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Xia functions as a **moral precedent** for later legitimacy doctrines.

Absorption dynamics:
• Shang adopts ritual kingship  
• Zhou moralizes overthrow  
• Mandate logic traces back to Xia narrative  

Later synthesis:
> “夏亡而道存。”
(Xia fell, but the Way endured.)

Xia is memory, not administration.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Civilizational origins are framed through environmental mastery.  
• Moral legitimacy precedes law and ritual.  
• Hereditary rule introduces legitimacy risk.  
• Xia supplies the narrative grammar for later dynastic judgment.

Tensions preserved without verdict.
