I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Marco Polo as a **transmission vector** rather than a
governing actor: a figure through whom **Yuan China was rendered legible to
Latin Christendom**, filtered through curiosity, genre conventions, and
selective understanding.

Marco Polo is not a Chinese agent, nor a Mongol ruler, but a **civilizational
interpreter** whose narrative reshaped Europe’s imagination of China for
centuries.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. BIOGRAPHICAL POSITION (MEMORY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Born in Venice, Marco Polo traveled to Asia with his father and uncle and
spent many years within Mongol-ruled territories, including Yuan China under
Kublai Khan (忽必烈).

Key identifiers:
• Venetian merchant-traveler  
• Long-term resident within Mongol/Yuan domains  
• Claimed service in imperial administration  
• Co-author (with Rustichello da Pisa) of *Il Milione*
  (《马可·波罗行记》)

Authorial stance emphasized eyewitness authority:
> “我所见者，非人所能尽信，然皆亲历。”
(What I saw may not all be believed, yet I witnessed it myself.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. RELATION TO YUAN CHINA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Marco Polo encountered China **through Mongol mediation**, not through the
Confucian literati or examination system.

Key interaction points:
• Court of Kublai Khan  
• Exposure to Yuan fiscal and logistical systems  
• Observation of cities, markets, and transport  
• Limited engagement with Chinese moral–textual traditions  

His China was imperial and infrastructural, not philosophical.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PERCEPTUAL FILTER & LIMITATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Structural limits shaped the account:

• Outsider perspective  
• Dependence on interpreters  
• Emphasis on novelty, scale, and marvel  
• Narrative shaped by collaboration with a romance writer  

Notable absences (now better understood):
• Great Wall (largely Ming-era in its later form)  
• Tea, chopsticks, footbinding (less visible or less salient in Yuan context)

Omission reflects **genre and vantage**, not non-presence.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ADMINISTRATIVE & MATERIAL OBSERVATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Marco Polo recorded features later verified as unusually accurate.

Key observations:
• Paper currency (交钞)  
• Salt administration and revenues  
• Postal relay system (驿站)  
• Urban scale and market density  

A striking claim:
> “此国用纸为钱，而民无不信。”
(This country uses paper as money, and the people trust it without doubt.)

Modern scholarship confirms the precision of these descriptions beyond any
other contemporary European source.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LEGITIMACY & AUTHORITY (AS SEEN)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Authority appeared as **imperial efficiency and reach**, not moral cosmology.

Kublai Khan’s power was framed in terms of:
• Administrative penetration  
• Revenue extraction  
• Infrastructure  
• Order without visible terror  

Confucian legitimacy grammar remained largely invisible to the observer.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CULTURAL TRANSLATION EFFECT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Marco Polo translated China into categories intelligible to Europe:

• Wealth  
• Order  
• Scale  
• Administrative sophistication  

Cultural translation compressed complexity into awe.
A later tradition preserves his own awareness of limits:
> “我未言所见之半。”
(I have not told half of what I saw.)

China became **desirable, distant, and semi-mythic** in European memory.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. VERACITY & MODERN SCHOLARSHIP
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Early skepticism (notably 1990s) has been largely overturned.

Current consensus (post-2010s):
• Marco Polo did reach Yuan China  
• Core administrative and fiscal observations are accurate  
• Exaggerations reflect genre, audience, and co-authorship dynamics  

Recent readings frame the text as:
• Collaborative (with Rustichello)  
• Missionary- and court-facing  
• Selectively shaped for Latin audiences  

Truth-value lies in **transmission impact**, not literal completeness.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY & LEGACY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• China’s global image was shaped early by outsiders, not self-representation.  
• Material-administrative power translated more easily than moral systems.  
• The Mongol/Yuan interface enabled transcontinental legibility.  
• Marco Polo reframed China; Europe remembered the frame.  
• The book influenced later exploration—Columbus carried a copy.  
• “Cathay” vs. “China” persisted in European maps for centuries.

Contradictions preserved without verdict.
