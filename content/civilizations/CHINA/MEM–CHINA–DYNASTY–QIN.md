I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Qin dynasty as the **foundational unification shock**
of Chinese civilization: the moment when **fragmented polities were forcibly
compressed into a single administrative state**.

The Qin represents **origin through coercion**—short-lived as a dynasty,
but permanent as an institutional template.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. DYNASTIC OVERVIEW (MEMORY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Qin dynasty was founded by Qin Shi Huang (秦始皇) after the conquest
of the Warring States (战国).

Core characteristics:
• First imperial unification  
• Radical centralization  
• Legalist ideology (法家)  
• Extreme reliance on coercive enforcement  

Imperial self-definition was absolute:
> “普天之下，莫非王土。”
(All under Heaven belongs to the ruler.)

Unification was total, but consent was minimal.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ADMINISTRATIVE & INSTITUTIONAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Qin administration dismantled feudal structures and imposed uniform control.

Key features:
• Commandery–county system (郡县制)  
• Abolition of hereditary aristocracy  
• Standardized laws, weights, measures, script (书同文)  
• Direct appointment of officials  

Legalist doctrine prioritized obedience:
> “法不阿贵，刑过不避大臣。”
(The law does not favor the noble; punishment does not spare ministers.)

This created administrative clarity at the cost of legitimacy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ECONOMIC & LABOR MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Qin mobilized massive labor resources for state projects.

Key elements:
• State-directed agriculture  
• Forced labor (徭役)  
• Monumental construction (Great Wall, roads, tombs)  

Economic extraction was explicit:
> “民以力尽，国以事成。”
(The people exhaust their strength so the state may accomplish its works.)

Productive capacity increased, but social exhaustion followed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LEGITIMACY & MANDATE MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Qin legitimacy rested on:
• Military conquest  
• Administrative order  
• Ideological suppression  

The Qin rejected Confucian moral discourse:
> “焚书坑儒。”
(Burning books and burying scholars.)

Legitimacy was imposed, not cultivated.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MILITARY & SECURITY MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Military power was central to Qin success.

Characteristics:
• Mass conscription  
• Harsh discipline  
• Expansionist security posture  

Strategic logic was uncompromising:
> “以战止战。”
(Ending war through war.)

Once expansion ceased, the system lacked release valves.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CULTURAL & INTELLECTUAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Qin cultural policy was suppressive rather than generative.

Features:
• Legalist dominance  
• Suppression of rival schools  
• Elimination of intellectual pluralism  

Later tradition judged Qin severity harshly:
> “秦以暴亡。”
(Qin perished through brutality.)

This judgment shaped later dynastic moderation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. INTERNAL STRESS & COLLAPSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Internal stress escalated rapidly after unification.

Pressures included:
• Labor exhaustion  
• Elite resentment  
• Popular rebellion (陈胜吴广起义)

Collapse followed quickly:
> “天下苦秦久矣。”
(All under Heaven had long suffered under Qin.)

The dynasty fell within fifteen years.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. FAILURE, ABSORPTION, & LEGACY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Qin collapse was swift, but absorption was total.

Key legacies:
• Administrative unification endured  
• Legal standardization persisted  
• Imperial framework became irreversible  

A later synthesis captured the paradox:
> “秦亡而制存。”
(Qin fell, but its system endured.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Coercion can unify rapidly but cannot sustain legitimacy.  
• Administrative clarity precedes moral authority in Chinese statecraft.  
• Qin created the form; later dynasties supplied moderation.  
• All subsequent empires negotiate with Qin’s legacy.

Tension preserved without synthesis.
