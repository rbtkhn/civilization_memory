I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Tibet as a **civilizational frontier of altitude,
sacral authority, and strategic insulation**—a region where **religion,
geography, and imperial logic intersect without full assimilation**.

Tibet functions neither as a standard province nor as a purely external
other, but as a **highland buffer-space whose control is always indirect,
ritualized, and strategically conditional**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC & ENVIRONMENTAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Tibetan Plateau is defined by altitude, isolation, and logistical
severity.

Core features:
• Extreme elevation and climate  
• Sparse population density  
• Limited agricultural surplus  
• Natural barrier between Inner Asia, South Asia, and China  

Strategic axiom:
> “地高则险，险则难治。”
(High ground creates danger; danger resists governance.)

Geography enforces **distance-based sovereignty**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. EARLY POLITICAL FORMATION & EMPIRE MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Tibetan Empire (7th–9th centuries) emerged as a **militarized plateau
power** capable of contesting Tang China directly.

Key moments:
• Songtsen Gampo (松赞干布)  
• Tibetan occupation of Chang’an (763)  
• Marriage diplomacy with Tang princesses  

Imperial parity was briefly real:
> “吐蕃与唐，势敌也。”
(Tibet and Tang were powers of equal standing.)

Collapse followed fragmentation, not conquest.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. RELIGIOUS–SACRAL AUTHORITY MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tibet’s enduring power lies in **religious legitimacy rather than
administrative capacity**.

Core elements:
• Tibetan Buddhism (藏传佛教)  
• Monastic authority over territory and population  
• Incarnation lineage (转世制度)  

Political authority is sacralized:
> “政教合一。”
(Politics and religion as one.)

This structure resists bureaucratic absorption and substitutes **moral
cosmology for institutional depth**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. IMPERIAL INCORPORATION PATTERNS (YUAN–QING)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Chinese empires incorporated Tibet through **ritual sovereignty rather
than direct administration**.

Patterns:
• Yuan patron–priest relationship (施主–上师)  
• Qing supervision via ambans (驻藏大臣)  
• Local rule preserved under imperial recognition  

Key mechanism:
• **Priest–patron model as a supra-ethnic legitimacy bridge**, allowing
nominal suzerainty without administrative overreach.

Governance maxim:
> “因其俗而治之。”
(Govern by following local custom.)

Control is symbolic, selective, and episodic—yet durable.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MILITARY & SECURITY MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Military control in Tibet has always been **costly and strategically
defensive**, not extractive.

Characteristics:
• Difficult troop sustainment  
• Reliance on deterrence rather than projection  
• Emphasis on border stability over population penetration  

Security logic:
> “守边重于治民。”
(Border defense outweighs population governance.)

Power is about denial, not domination.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. MODERN STATE INCORPORATION & GOVERNANAD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Post-1950 incorporation represents a **qualitative shift**: from episodic
ritual oversight to **permanent administrative and infrastructural presence**,
enabled by technological mitigation of altitude constraints.

Modern features:
• Infrastructure-led integration  
• Demographic and economic insertion  
• Persistent religious-management tension  
• Heavy security and surveillance apparatus  

Modern axiom:
> “稳定压倒一切。”
(Stability overrides all.)

Yet stability remains **managed, not resolved**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. EXTERNAL INTERFACE & GEOPOLITICAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tibet functions simultaneously as:
• Strategic buffer with South Asia  
• Altitude shield for the Chinese interior  
• Observation platform over Himalayan corridors  

Offensive–defensive duality:
• Deny adversary Himalayan access  
• Preserve continental depth and observational advantage for southward
projection if required  

Strategic constant:
> “控其高原，即控其西陲。”
(Who controls the plateau controls the western frontier.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Altitude enforces indirect rule.  
• Sacral legitimacy outlasts political regimes.  
• Empires manage Tibet through ritual before force.  
• Full integration is structurally constrained, not merely political.  
• Empires that accommodate sacral distance endure longer than those that
demand cultural uniformity.  
• Tibet must be governed continuously, not “solved.”

Tension is permanent.
