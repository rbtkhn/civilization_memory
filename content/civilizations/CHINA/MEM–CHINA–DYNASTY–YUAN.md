────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Yuan dynasty as the **foreign-conquest shock phase**
within Chinese civilization—a period in which **external rule tested,
but did not terminate, civilizational continuity**.

The Yuan represents not a civilizational reset, but a **stress-test**:
administrative systems, fiscal practices, and cultural norms were partially
disrupted, selectively adopted, and ultimately reabsorbed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. DYNASTIC OVERVIEW (MEMORY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Yuan dynasty was founded by Kublai Khan (忽必烈) after the Mongol
conquest of the Song.

Core characteristics:
• Non-Han ruling elite  
• Military-conquest origin  
• Continental empire integration  
• Administrative borrowing from prior dynasties  

Yuan legitimacy was framed as universal conquest:
> “一统天下。”
(Unifying all under Heaven.)

Yet this claim sat uneasily atop Chinese governance traditions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ADMINISTRATIVE & INSTITUTIONAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yuan administration combined Mongol hierarchy with inherited Chinese
bureaucratic techniques.

Key features:
• Ethnic stratification (蒙古、色目、汉人、南人)  
• Retention of Song fiscal mechanisms  
• Reduced role of the civil service examinations (科举一度废止)  
• Heavy reliance on appointed administrators  

Chinese officials noted the tension clearly:
> “法制虽存，而用非法。”
(The institutions remained, but were not applied according to their principles.)

Administrative continuity existed, but legitimacy was thin.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ECONOMIC & FISCAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Yuan relied heavily on extraction to sustain imperial demands.

Key elements:
• Tax farming and requisition  
• Extensive use—and abuse—of paper currency (交钞)  
• Maintenance of long-distance trade routes  
• Strain on agrarian producers  

Fiscal breakdown was openly acknowledged:
> “钞法坏而民困。”
(The collapse of the currency system impoverished the people.)

Economic legitimacy eroded faster than administrative control.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LEGITIMACY & MANDATE MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yuan legitimacy rested primarily on **conquest and imperial charisma**,
not Confucian moral authority.

Mandate language existed, but was weakly internalized:
> “得天下者，非德也，力也。”
(Those who obtained the realm did so not by virtue, but by force.)

This marked a sharp divergence from prior dynastic norms.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MILITARY DOMINANCE & CIVILIAN SUBORDINATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Military authority dominated Yuan governance.

Characteristics:
• Mongol military elite at the apex  
• Civilian administration subordinated  
• Persistent use of coercion  

Chinese observers perceived imbalance:
> “兵强而政弱。”
(The military was strong, but governance was weak.)

This imbalance proved unsustainable over time.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CULTURAL & SOCIAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cultural life continued under constraint.

Features:
• Marginalization of the literati  
• Survival of Confucian education outside state power  
• Flourishing of drama and vernacular literature (杂剧)  

Literati response was adaptive rather than revolutionary:
> “隐而不仕。”
(Withdrawing rather than serving.)

Cultural continuity persisted beneath political exclusion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. EXTERNAL POSITION & INTERNAL STRESS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Yuan remained integrated into a continental imperial system.

Pressures included:
• Overextension  
• Fiscal exhaustion  
• Environmental shocks  
• Popular unrest  

A late assessment captured systemic decay:
> “上下离心。”
(Rulers and people were no longer of one mind.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. FAILURE, COLLAPSE, & ABSORPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Yuan collapsed amid rebellion and elite fragmentation.

Key absorption dynamics:
• Ming restoration adopted Song–Yuan administrative practices  
• Mongol political dominance ended, but fiscal lessons endured  
• Civilizational continuity reasserted itself  

Retrospective judgment was blunt:
> “元亡而中国复。”
(The Yuan fell, and China returned.)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Foreign conquest disrupted but did not erase Chinese institutions.  
• Legitimacy without cultural integration decays rapidly.  
• Administrative borrowing cannot substitute for moral authority.  
• Yuan serves as a negative template reinforcing later Sinicization.

Tension preserved without synthesis.

XI.B. SOURCE EVIDENCE — MARCO POLO (ARC–CHINA)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Marco Polo, *Il Milione* (trans. Henry Yule, 3rd ed. rev. Henri Cordier, 1903), Book Second, Part I, Chapter XXIV — paper currency (交钞) under Kublai:

> "With these pieces of paper, made as I have described, he [Khubilai Khan] causes all payments on his own account to be made; and he makes them to pass current universally over all his kingdoms and provinces and territories, and whithersoever his power and sovereignty extends. And nobody, however important he may think himself, dares to refuse them on pain of death. And indeed everybody takes them readily, for wheresoever a person may go throughout the Great Kaan's dominions he shall find these pieces of paper current, and shall be able to transact all sales and purchases of goods by means of them just as well as if they were coins of pure gold."
> — Marco Polo, *Il Milione*, Bk II Pt I Ch XXIV (trans. Yule). ARC–CHINA v1.0.

Contemporary witness to Yuan fiscal practice; paper money as imperial instrument.
