────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Gobi Desert (戈壁) as the **northern barrier
and corridor** of Chinese civilizational memory: the space that
separates the North China Plain and the Yellow River heartland from
the Mongolian steppe—a **arid barrier** that has **limited** direct
sedentary penetration while functioning as **corridor** for nomads,
caravans, and conquest (Xiongnu, Mongol, Yuan). The Gobi is not a
waterway like the Yangtze or a mountain wall like the Himalayas;
it is **desert–steppe logic**—whoever could cross it (nomads) or
skirt it (oasis routes, Hexi corridor) could project or deny; the
Silk Road's northern and central branches ran along the desert's
edge. Mongol unification made the Gobi **interior** to a single
steppe–sedentary polity; Qing and PRC incorporation of Inner
Mongolia placed the desert's southern edge under Chinese administration.

The Gobi is remembered as **barrier and corridor logic**: the desert
that imposed discontinuity between sedentary China and the steppe,
while permitting nomadic mobility and caravan routes along oases
and passes. Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC & STRUCTURAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Gobi Desert spans southern Mongolia and northern China (Inner
Mongolia, Gansu, Xinjiang margin)—a cold, arid basin and plateau
desert. Key structural features: **barrier**—waterless expanse
limits large-scale sedentary movement; **corridor**—nomads crossed
it (mobility, seasonal movement); caravans and armies used **oasis
and edge routes** (Hexi corridor, Silk Road northern branch). The
**Hexi corridor** (Gansu)—between Qilian Mountains and the desert—
linked China proper to the Western Regions (Xinjiang, Central Asia)
and was the **chokepoint** for Silk Road traffic north of the Tibetan
Plateau. The Gobi thus encodes **steppe–sedentary interface**: the
desert is what sedentary China had to **cross or skirt** to reach
the steppe or Central Asia; for steppe polities, the desert was
**traversable** (Mongol mobility) and the southern edge was the
frontier with China.

Key structural features:
• Barrier: arid, waterless; limits sedentary bulk movement; imposes logistics on armies and caravans
• Corridor: nomad mobility; oasis and edge routes (Hexi, Silk Road northern branch); caravan logistics
• Steppe–sedentary interface: Gobi as boundary between North China and Mongolian steppe; Xiongnu, Mongol, Qing frontiers
• Contrast with Taklamakan: Gobi is northern (Mongolia–China); Taklamakan is western (Xinjiang, Silk Road central branch)

Terrain enforces **desert–steppe logic**: control of oases and edge corridors (Hexi) shapes who can project across or along the desert.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. GOBI AS BARRIER AND STEPPE FRONTIER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Gobi encodes **barrier**: the desert has **limited** direct
sedentary penetration from the North China Plain into the Mongolian
steppe—unlike the Yellow River or Grand Canal (bulk supply), the
Gobi does not sustain agriculture or easy bulk transport. Chinese
dynasties (Han, Tang, Ming) built walls and garrisons **south** of
or **along** the desert edge (Great Wall logic); they did not
occupy the Gobi itself. The desert thus functioned as **northern
limit**—not a corridor to be controlled for bulk movement from
the Chinese core, but a **barrier** that defined the edge of
sedentary space and the frontier with the steppe. MEM–CHINA–XIONGNU
and MEM–CHINA–MONGOLIA depend on the Gobi as the **interface**
between Chinese and steppe space; MEM–CHINA–SILK–ROAD used the
Hexi corridor and oasis routes that **skirt** the Gobi (and the
Taklamakan to the west).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. GOBI AS CORRIDOR AND MONGOL HEARTLAND
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The same desert encodes **corridor**: for **nomads** (Xiongnu, Mongol),
the Gobi was **traversable**—mobility, seasonal movement, and
conquest did not require sedentary supply lines in the same way.
The Mongol Empire **unified** the steppe and the Gobi's southern
edge; the Yuan made Dadu (Beijing) the capital **south** of the
desert and extended the Grand Canal to supply it—encoding the
Gobi as **interior** to a single polity that spanned steppe and
sedentary China. The Qing incorporated **Inner Mongolia** (south
of the Gobi); the PRC retains Inner Mongolia. The Gobi thus carries
**both** the barrier narrative (sedentary limit) and the corridor
narrative (nomad mobility, Mongol conquest, Silk Road edge routes).
MEM–CHINA–MONGOL–EMPIRE, MEM–CHINA–WAR–MONGOL–JIN, MEM–CHINA–WAR–SONG–YUAN
depend on the steppe–desert interface; MEM–CHINA–GRAND–CANAL (grain
to Beijing) supplied the capital **south** of the Gobi. MEM–CHINA–MONGOLIA
is the **steppe counterpart**; this file is the **geographic frame**
for the desert–steppe interface.
