I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Iran** as the modern state and territorial container through which Persian civilization has been expressed since the early twentieth century. It does not replace dynasty or regime MEMs (e.g. MEM–PERSIA–IRAN–ISLAMIC–REPUBLIC, MEM–PERSIA–IRAN–ISLAMIC–REVOLUTION) but encodes **continuity of the state**: name (Persia → Iran), territorial integrity (Iranian Plateau), constitutional ruptures (1906, 1979), and the persistence of sovereignty as the central imperative across regime changes.

Iran is the **current name of the polity**; Persia is the civilizational and memory-codex designation. The state densifies after each shock rather than dissolving.

III. NAME, TERRITORY & CONSTITUTIONAL SEQUENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The adoption of "Iran" as the official state name (1935) under Reza Shah tied the country to the plateau and to pre-Islamic identity while asserting independence from the "Persia" label imposed by outsiders. Abbas Amanat frames the continuity:

> "Iran as a geographic and cultural entity has survived numerous
> political transformations, from the Achaemenid Empire to the
> Islamic Republic. What has remained constant is the idea of Iran
> as a distinct civilizational space with a continuous history."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Constitutional milestones—1906 (Majles, limits on monarchy), 1925 (Pahlavi dynasty), 1953 (coup, royal restoration), 1979 (revolution, IRI)—each **compressed** legitimacy into new form rather than erasing the state. Ervand Abrahamian stresses the 1953 rupture as a source of later revolutionary narrative:

> "The 1953 coup left a deep scar on the national psyche. It was
> seen as proof that the West would not permit Iran to control its
> own resources and destiny. The memory of 1953 fueled the
> revolution of 1979."
> — Ervand Abrahamian, *A History of Modern Iran* (2008); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Territorial scope has remained the Iranian Plateau and its borders; regime change did not alter the state's geographic or civilizational frame.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL CONSTRAINTS & SOVEREIGNTY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The state operates under **structural suspicion** of external encirclement (CIV–CORE–PERSIA). Dependency is framed as suffocation; autonomy in energy, finance, and security is non-negotiable. Sanctions and pressure after 1979 (and in earlier phases) **increased institutional density**—the state developed parallel circuits (trade, banking, logistics) rather than capitulating. This aligns with CORE axiom: continuity through compression; loss becomes design.

Homa Katouzian's formulation of arbitrary rule and society applies across the century:

> "In Iran, the state has traditionally been separate from and
> above the society. It has been the state that has determined
> the rules of the game, and society has had to adapt."
> — Homa Katouzian, *State and Society in Iran* (2006); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The Islamic Republic did not break that pattern; it re-encoded it in clerical–republican form. Iran as state container **persists**; the regime is the current compression.
