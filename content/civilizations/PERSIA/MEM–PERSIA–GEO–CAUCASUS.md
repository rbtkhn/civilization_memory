I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Caucasus** from Persia's perspective: the
**northern land barrier** that complements the Caspian Sea (MEM–PERSIA–GEO–CASPIAN)
in CIV–CORE–PERSIA's "Caspian–**Caucasus**–Mesopotamia" buffers. The
Caucasus is the mountain chain between the Black Sea and the Caspian—
high passes, defensible valleys, and the terrain where Persia **lost
territory** to Russia (treaties of Golestan 1813, Turkmanchay 1828):
Georgia, northern Armenia, Nakhchivan, and claims north of the Aras.

For Persia, the Caucasus is **barrier and loss**: historically a
northern shield and corridor (Armenia, Iberia, Albania), and in the
19th c. the direction of sustained territorial cession. The geography
did not change; the balance of power did. MEM–PERSIA–RUSSIA and
MEM–PERSIA–ARMENIA encode the human and political dimension; this
MEM encodes the **terrain**—what the Caucasus does to actors.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

II. MEARSHEIMER STRATEGIC ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.A. MOUNTAIN BARRIER — PASSES AND DEFENSIVE DEPTH

The Caucasus runs roughly northwest–southeast between the Black Sea
and the Caspian. Strabo and ancient sources describe it as rugged
and passage-controlled. Armenia sits on the plateau between Anatolia,
**Mesopotamia, the Caucasus**, and the Iranian heartland (MEM–PERSIA–ARMENIA).
The structural effect: **invasion from the north** (steppe, later Russia)
must force passes; the mountains reward the defender when the defender
has the means to hold them. MEM–PERSIA–GEO–IRANIAN–PLATEAU notes
"Invasion from Mesopotamia, the **Caucasus**, or the east requires
forcing passes." The Caucasus is thus one of three approach axes—and
the one that historically served as **buffer** until Russian military
superiority reversed the equation. فردوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

STRUCTURAL CONSEQUENCE:
• Mountain barrier raises cost of projection in both directions
• Control of passes = control of access; loss of passes = exposure
• When a stronger power (Russia) holds the northern slope, Persia's buffer becomes the other side's advance base

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.B. CASPIAN–CAUCASUS PAIR — SEA AND LAND

CIV–CORE–PERSIA names "Caspian–Caucasus–Mesopotamia buffers." The
**Caspian** is the northern water boundary (closed sea, littoral
control). The **Caucasus** is the northern land boundary. Together
they form the northern rim: sea to the east of the mountains, mountains
to the west of the Caspian's western shore. Persia's northern security
depended on holding or buffering both. Russia's southward expansion
pierced both: Caspian littoral and Caucasus passes. Abbas Amanat:

> "The Qajar state could not match Russian military and logistical
> power. The loss of the Caucasian provinces was a trauma that
> shaped Persian perception of the northern threat for more than
> a century. Russia was the power that had taken Persian land."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The Caucasus is the **terrain of that loss**—Georgia, northern
Armenia, Nakhchivan. The geography did not fail; Persian force
could not hold the passes against Russian concentration.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.C. ARMENIA AND THE CAUCASUS

MEM–PERSIA–ARMENIA encodes Armenia as the buffer between Roman and
Persian spheres. Armenia sits **between** Anatolia, Mesopotamia, the
Caucasus, and the Iranian heartland. The Caucasus thus bounds Armenia
to the north; Armenian highlands are part of the broader Caucasian–
Anatolian complex. When Russia took northern Armenia (Turkmanchay),
the Caucasus became the new border—Persia held the southern slope
and what remained of Persian Armenia; Russia held the north. The
Caucasus as **border** replaced the Caucasus as **buffer**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.D. DEFENSIVE LOGIC — WHEN HELD

When Persia (or its clients) held the southern approaches and passes,
the Caucasus functioned as **defensive depth**: raiders from the north
had to cross mountains; Persian forces could contest passes. The
Sasanians and earlier empires dealt with Caucasian principalities
(Iberia, Albania, Armenia) as clients or vassals. The structural
logic: **buffer value**—client states absorb first impact; the core
(plateau) is not directly exposed. Loss of the buffer (Russian
annexation of Georgian and Armenian territory) eliminated that depth;
the Aras River became the border, and Persia's northern frontier
shrank to the river line.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.E. OFFENSIVE/DEFENSIVE BALANCE

The Caucasus favors the **defender of the passes** when forces are
roughly balanced. When one side has overwhelming logistical and
military advantage (Russia in the 19th c.), the mountains slow
but do not stop conquest. Persia could not reinforce the Caucasian
theater at scale; Russia could. Result: **territorial cession**—
Golestan, Turkmanchay—and the Caucasus as the **direction of
sustained loss** in Persian memory. The terrain is not inherently
Persian or Russian; it is **contested** until one power dominates,
then it becomes the boundary of the weaker power.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ARC TIMELINE EVIDENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

III.A. ARC-T-ANCIENT / MEDIEVAL

Ancient and medieval sources (Strabo, Armenian chronicles) describe
the Caucasus as boundary and corridor. Persian and Roman (Byzantine)
influence extended into Caucasian kingdoms (Iberia, Armenia, Albania).
The mountains were the northern limit of sustained imperial control
from the south.

III.B. ARC-T-MODERN (1800–Present)

Russian expansion into the Caucasus (18th–19th c.) and the treaties
of Golestan (1813) and Turkmanchay (1828) formalized Persian cession
of territory north of the Aras. The Caucasus became the **terrain
of loss**—documented in MEM–PERSIA–RUSSIA. Soviet and post-Soviet
period: the Caucasus remains the northern land boundary of Iran;
Georgia, Armenia, Azerbaijan are independent states, with Azerbaijan
and Armenia bordering Iran. The structural fact: Persia no longer
holds the northern slope; the buffer is gone.

• Golestan and Turkmanchay fixed the Aras as border; the buffer north of the Aras was lost.
• The Caucasus pairs with the Caspian (sea) as the northern rim; loss of both slopes compresses Persia.
