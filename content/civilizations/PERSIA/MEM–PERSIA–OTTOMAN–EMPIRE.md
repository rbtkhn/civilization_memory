I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Ottoman Empire** from the Persian perspective:
the western Islamic empire that succeeded Byzantium (Rūm) and became
Persia's primary **western rival** in the early modern period. The
Ottomans are not the parity peer of Rome—they are the **Sunni caliphate**
against which Safavid Persia defined its Shi'a sovereignty, and the
power that contested Mesopotamia, the Caucasus, and the Levant with
Persia for centuries. The relationship encodes legitimacy contest
(Sunni vs Shi'a), territorial stalemate (Chaldiran, Amasya, repeated
wars), and eventual border crystallization—then, with the Ottoman
collapse and the Turkish republic, a neighbor that is neither existential
threat nor ally.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. OTTOMAN AS SUCCESSOR TO RŪM — WESTERN ISLAMIC COUNTERWEIGHT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In Persian and Persianate encoding, Rome/Byzantium was **Rūm**
(MEM–PERSIA–ROMAN–EMPIRE). The Ottomans inherited that western
seat: Constantinople became Istanbul, and the sultan claimed
caliphal authority over Sunni Islam. Persia under the Safavids
(1501–1722) asserted **Shi'a sovereignty**—Isfahan vs Istanbul,
Shah vs Sultan. The Ottoman in Persian memory is therefore the
**other Islamic empire**: not the infidel Roman, but the Sunni
rival whose legitimacy Persia explicitly rejected. MEM–PERSIA–ISLAM
encodes "suspicion of imperial Sunni centers"; the Ottoman caliphate
is that center. فردوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

Roger Savory frames the structural opposition:

> "The Safavid–Ottoman conflict was not merely territorial. It was
> a struggle for religious legitimacy—Sunni Ottoman caliphate
> versus Shi'a Persian kingship. Each defined itself against the other."
> — Roger Savory, *Iran under the Safavids* (1980); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The Levant (MEM–PERSIA–GEO–LEVANT) and Mesopotamia (MEM–PERSIA–GEO–MESOPOTAMIA)
remained the shared theater: Persian (Safavid, etc.) and Ottoman
contest over Iraq continued the structural logic of western approach
and corridor control.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. SAFAVID–OTTOMAN WARS — CHALDIRAN, MESOPOTAMIA, BORDER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Chaldiran (1514) established the pattern: Ottoman artillery and
discipline defeated Safavid cavalry; the border did not collapse
but crystallized. Mesopotamia (Iraq) and at times the Caucasus
(MEM–PERSIA–GEO–CAUCASUS) were contested; treaties (e.g. Amasya
1555) divided spheres. Neither empire destroyed the other; both
absorbed enormous cost. The structural outcome: **territorial
stalemate**—Persia held the plateau and eastern Iraq at peaks;
the Ottomans held Iraq, Anatolia, and the Levant at theirs. Abbas
Amanat:

> "The Ottoman and Safavid empires fought repeatedly over Iraq and
> the Caucasus, but neither could deliver a knockout blow. The
> result was a long border that shifted with each war and treaty,
> and a mutual exhaustion that left both vulnerable to internal
> and external pressure."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Isfahan (MEM–PERSIA–ISFAHAN) as Safavid capital was the ceremonial
counter to Istanbul; the wars validated that Persia would not be
reabsorbed into a Sunni order. The Ottoman in this encoding is
**the western pressure** that reinforced Shi'a identity and
sovereignty—antagonist, not peer in the Roman sense.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. MEARSHEIMER FRAME — TERRITORIAL LOGIC AND SURVIVAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
From a structural perspective, the Ottoman–Persian contest was
over **corridor control**: Mesopotamia linked the plateau to the
Levant; the Caucasus and eastern Anatolia were buffer and
approach. Both empires had incentive to push the border and
deny the other consolidation; neither had the force ratio to
conquer the other's core. The result is **stable rivalry**—
wars, treaties, border shifts—until external actors (Russia
from the north, European powers) altered the balance. When
the Qajars chose Tehran as capital (1786), distance from
**Ottoman and Russian frontier pressure** was a factor
(MEM–PERSIA–TEHRAN). The Ottoman was one of two land pressures;
Russia was the one that took territory (MEM–PERSIA–RUSSIA).
The Ottoman took land in wars but did not annex the Persian
heartland; the border held.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. QAJAR AND LATE EMPIRE — DUAL FRONTIER, THEN COLLAPSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In the 19th c., Persia faced **Russia and the Ottomans** on
two flanks. Russian expansion cost Persia the Caucasus (Golestan,
Turkmanchay); Ottoman power waned but the western frontier
remained contested until the empire's dissolution. The Great
Game (Britain, Russia) eventually overshadowed the Ottoman–
Persian dyad; by the early 20th c., the Ottoman Empire was
retreating, and the Turkish republic (1922) replaced it.
Persia's western neighbor became Turkey—a secular state,
sometimes pragmatic partner, sometimes tension over Kurdish
and regional issues, but no longer the Sunni caliphate against
which Shi'a sovereignty was defined.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• The Ottoman in Persian memory is the western Islamic rival—successor to Rūm, Sunni caliphate vs Shi'a sovereignty.
• Safavid–Ottoman wars (Chaldiran, Mesopotamia, Caucasus) produced territorial stalemate, not parity in the Roman sense.
• Legitimacy contest (Sunni vs Shi'a) defined both empires against each other; neither absorbed the other.
• Qajar period: Ottoman and Russia as dual frontier pressure; Tehran's capital choice reflected both.
• Russia took Persian territory; the Ottoman did not annex the heartland—border crystallized.
• Ottoman collapse and Turkish republic: neighbor, not existential rival; legacy of contest endures in sectarian and territorial encoding.
