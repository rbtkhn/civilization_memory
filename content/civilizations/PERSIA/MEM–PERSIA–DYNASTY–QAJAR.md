I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Qajar dynasty** as the Persian regime
that **chose Tehran as capital** (1786/1789), lost the Caucasus
and claims north of the Aras to Russia (Golestan 1813, Turkmanchay
1828), and was compressed by the Great Game—British and Russian
zones of influence, concessions (tobacco, oil, railways), and
eventually constitutional stirrings and revolution. The Qajars
represent **continuity under dual pressure**: no reconstitution
of Safavid glory (Isfahan was lost as capital); instead, survival
as a buffer state between two empires, with territorial cession
in the north and sovereignty eroded by concessions in the south
and center.

CIV–CORE–PERSIA continuity: Safavid → **Colonial Trauma** (Qajar
period is the heart of it) → 1953 Coup → Iran–Iraq War. The
Qajar state could not match Russian or British power; it **preserved
the realm** by remaining useful as a buffer while losing land
and control.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

II. FOUNDING — TEHRAN AS CAPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Agha Mohammad Khan Qajar consolidated power and made **Tehran**
the capital in 1786 (coronation 1796). The choice reflected
strategic calculation: proximity to the Alborz, defensible
position, and **distance from Ottoman and Russian frontier
pressure** (MEM–PERSIA–TEHRAN; MEM–PERSIA–OTTOMAN–EMPIRE).
Abbas Amanat:

> "Tehran was a modest town when the Qajars made it their capital.
> Its growth was a function of the court's presence—palaces,
> barracks, bazaars—and the gradual concentration of administration
> and trade that followed."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The Safavid capital (Isfahan) had fallen to the Afghans (1722);
the Afsharid and Zand interregna had not restored a single
ceremonial center. The Qajars chose a **new center**—Tehran—
oriented toward the north (Alborz) and away from the vulnerable
western and northern frontiers. فردوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. TERRITORIAL LOSS — RUSSIA AND THE CAUCASUS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Russian southward expansion brought the Tsarist empire to the
Caspian and the Caucasus. The treaties of **Golestan (1813)** and
**Turkmanchay (1828)** formalized Persian cession of territory
north of the Aras: Georgia, northern Armenia, Nakhchivan (MEM–PERSIA–RUSSIA;
MEM–PERSIA–GEO–CAUCASUS). Abbas Amanat:

> "The Qajar state could not match Russian military and logistical
> power. The loss of the Caucasian provinces was a trauma that
> shaped Persian perception of the northern threat for more than
> a century. Russia was the power that had taken Persian land."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The Qajars did not lose to a peer in a parity war (as with Rome).
They **lost territory to a rising empire** that had no interest
in preserving a buffer. The Ottoman (MEM–PERSIA–OTTOMAN–EMPIRE)
remained a western rival but did not annex the Persian heartland;
Russia did take land. The structural lesson: **northern frontier
compressed**; Caspian and Caucasus became the direction of loss.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. GREAT GAME — BRITAIN AND RUSSIA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In the 19th and early 20th c., Persia was the **buffer** between
Russian and British spheres. Britain projected from India and
the Gulf; Russia from the Caucasus and Central Asia. Ervand
Abrahamian:

> "Iran was the classic buffer state. The British and the Russians
> carved out zones of influence but did not partition the country
> completely—each feared giving the other a decisive advantage."
> — Ervand Abrahamian, *A History of Modern Iran* (2008); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Concessions (tobacco, oil, railways) were extracted by both
(MEM–PERSIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE). The Qajar state sold concessions
to pay for royal and administrative expenses; the **tobacco
revolt** (1890s) showed that the population would resist when
sovereignty was seen to be sold. Oil became the most valuable
and most contested concession. Persia survived as a state because
neither empire wanted the other to dominate—but survival was
**compressed sovereignty**: no territorial annexation by Britain,
but loss of control over resources and agency.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CONSTITUTIONAL STIRRINGS AND DYNASTIC END
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Constitutional Revolution (1906) produced a parliament (Majles)
and a constitution that constrained royal power—a response to
Qajar weakness and foreign pressure. The revolution did not
stabilize the dynasty; Reza Khan (later Reza Shah) staged a coup
(1921) and in 1925 deposed the Qajars and founded the Pahlavi
dynasty. The Qajar period thus ends with **replacement**, not
conquest: an internal military strongman who recentralized power
and claimed modernization. MEM–PERSIA–IRAN encodes the
constitutional sequence; the Qajars are the dynasty that lost
territory and concessions and that was succeeded by Pahlavi.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MEARSHEIMER FRAME — BUFFER AND LOSS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
From a structural perspective, the Qajar state was **buffer value**:
Britain and Russia preferred a weak Persia to direct confrontation.
The incentive structure: neither side would allow the other to
annex Persia entirely. But Russia could take **peripheral territory**
(Caucasus) without triggering British war; Britain could take
**concessions** (oil) without triggering Russian war. The Qajar
state had no ally to restore lost territory; it could only
preserve what remained. Structural outcome: **territorial loss
in the north, sovereignty erosion in the center and south**;
Tehran as capital reflected the need for a defensible center
away from the most exposed frontiers.

VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Qajars chose Tehran as capital (1786); distance from Ottoman and Russian pressure.
• Territorial loss to Russia: Golestan, Turkmanchay; Caucasus and Aras border.
• Great Game: buffer between Britain and Russia; concessions; no partition but compressed sovereignty.
• Tobacco revolt, oil concessions: population resisted when sovereignty was seen sold.
• Constitutional Revolution (1906); dynasty ended by Reza Shah (1925); Pahlavi succeeded.
