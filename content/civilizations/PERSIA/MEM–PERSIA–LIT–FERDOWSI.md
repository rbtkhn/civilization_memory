I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Ferdowsi** as Persia's **epic carrier of
royal and civilizational memory**—the poet who composed the
*Shahnameh* (Book of Kings), the verse chronicle of Iranian
kingship from myth to the Sasanian fall, and who thereby
carried Persian sovereignty narrative across the Islamic
rupture. Ferdowsi is not a dynast or a politician. He is
**legitimacy through narrative**: the figure who made Persian
(Farsi) the language of kingship in the eastern Islamic world
and who encoded the persistence of royal memory when the
imperial state had been absorbed by the Caliphate.

In the Codex, Ferdowsi and the *Shahnameh* are the primary
indigenous-language source (ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL) cited across
MEMs for royal memory, continuity, and the durability of
encoded meaning. This file centralizes that function.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT & FORM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ferdowsi wrote in Tus (Khorasan) under Samanid and later
Ghaznavid patronage. The *Shahnameh* (c. 977–1010) is a long
epic in masnavi form—some 50,000 couplets—narrating the
Iranian royal tradition from the first king (Kayumars) through
the Sasanians to the Arab conquest. The work is in **Persian**,
not Arabic; it reclaims the pre-Islamic past for Persian
speakers and asserts that kingship and fame outlast dynasty.

Structural conditions:
• Post-conquest Persianate society; Arabic as language of state and religion
• Samanid court patronage of Persian letters
• Need for a shared narrative of Iranian identity that does not erase Islam but restores the royal past
• Epic as the form that carries collective memory across rupture

Ferdowsi encodes **civilization as memory**—the seeds sown that save name and reputation from the grave.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CORE CONCERN — ROYAL MEMORY AND FAME
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The prologue to the *Shahnameh* states the poet's claim:

> "I shall not die, these seeds I've sown will save my name and
> reputation from the grave, and men of sense and wisdom will proclaim,
> when I have gone, my praises and my fame."
> — Ferdowsi, *Shahnameh* (prologue), tr. Dick Davis; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

**Original (Farsi):** توانا بود هر که دانا بود / ز دانش دل پیر برنا بود — فردوسی، شاهنامه (دیباچه)، ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

The epic narrates the full arc of Iranian kingship: the just and unjust kings, the heroes (Rostam, Sohrab), the wars, the fall of the Sasanians. It does not deny Islam; it **restores the pre-Islamic past to Persian speech** and makes that past the shared property of the Persianate world. MEM–PERSIA–EMPIRE invokes this when it says: "Ferdowsi's *Shahnameh* carries the memory of kingship across the rupture… Empire falls; civilization persists through institutional and narrative transmission."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ferdowsi establishes:
• **Persian as the language of kingship** in the eastern Islamic world—epic, not only court or religious Arabic
• **Royal memory as transmissible**—kings die; their fame and the narrative of order and chaos survive in verse
• **Continuity across rupture**—the Sasanian fall (651) did not erase Iranian kingship; it was absorbed and retold
• **Indigenous-language anchor** for the Codex—dozens of MEM–PERSIA files cite the Shahnameh prologue for legitimacy, durability, and royal encoding

He functions as **Persia's narrative sovereign**—the one who did not rule but who made rule legible and memorable. No single political actor in the Codex is cited as often for civilizational continuity as Ferdowsi.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. RELATION TO DYNASTY, EMPIRE, AND ISLAM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• **Empire (MEM–PERSIA–EMPIRE):** The Shahnameh carries kingship memory across rupture; "empire falls; civilization persists."
• **Sasanian, Achaemenid, Parthian:** The epic narrates and encodes their place in the royal chain; MEMs cite Ferdowsi when asserting durability of royal or institutional memory.
• **Islam (MEM–PERSIA–ISLAM):** Ferdowsi writes after the conquest; his project is restoration of the Iranian past in Persian, not rejection of Islam—synthesis of royal narrative with Islamic-era patronage.

Ferdowsi does not lead a movement—he **legitimizes** the claim that Persian civilization has a continuous royal and heroic past.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LIMITS & RECEPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• No political treatise—narrative and aesthetic, not programmatic.
• Reception: Persianate courts adopted the Shahnameh as the canonical royal epic; later, nationalist and modern Iranian identity claimed Ferdowsi as father of the Persian language and of Iranian identity.
• Contrast with Nizam al-Mulk (Siyasatnama): Ferdowsi narrates kingship; Nizam al-Mulk advises it. Both are ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

Ferdowsi fills the gap between **power and memory**—he does not explain how power is seized, only how it is remembered and transmitted.
