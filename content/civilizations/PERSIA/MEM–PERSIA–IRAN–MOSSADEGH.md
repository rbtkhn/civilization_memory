I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Mohammad Mossadegh** from the Persian perspective:
the prime minister who nationalized the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company
(1951), reclaimed Iran's oil for the state, and was **overthrown by
a foreign-backed coup** (1953) that restored the Shah. In CIV–CORE–PERSIA,
1953 is a **temporal node** in the continuity engine: Colonial Trauma
→ **1953 Coup** → Iran–Iraq War → Sanctions Era. Mossadegh in Persian
memory is the **sovereign who tried**—and whose fall proved that the
West would not permit Iran to control its own resources. The memory
of 1953 fed the revolution of 1979 (MEM–PERSIA–IRAN–ISLAMIC–REVOLUTION);
Mossadegh is encoded as the nationalist whose defeat was weaponized,
not erased.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. NATIONALIZATION — SOVEREIGN ACT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mossadegh led the movement to nationalize Iranian oil. The Anglo-Iranian
Oil Company (later BP) had operated under a concession that gave Britain
control of extraction and most of the revenue. In 1951 the Majles
passed the nationalization law; Mossadegh became prime minister and
implemented it. The act was **sovereign**: Iran reclaiming control of
its principal resource. Britain (and later the United States) refused
to accept the new order—boycott, pressure, and ultimately regime
change. Abbas Amanat:

> "Mossadegh's nationalization of oil was the most dramatic assertion
> of Iranian sovereignty in the twentieth century before the revolution.
> It challenged not only British interests but the entire structure of
> colonial-era concessions. The response—the coup of 1953—demonstrated
> that such assertions would be met with force."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

III. 1953 COUP — OVERTHROW AND RESTORATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In August 1953 a coup orchestrated by British and American intelligence
(Operation Ajax / TPAJAX) overthrew Mossadegh and restored Mohammad Reza
Shah to full power. Mossadegh was arrested, tried, and imprisoned;
he remained under house arrest until his death (1967). The coup
**reversed sovereign nationalization**: the oil industry was
reorganized with a consortium in which Western companies retained
a dominant role. Ervand Abrahamian:

> "The 1953 coup left a deep scar on the national psyche. It was
> seen as proof that the West would not permit Iran to control its
> own resources and destiny. The memory of 1953 fueled the
> revolution of 1979."
> — Ervand Abrahamian, *A History of Modern Iran* (2008); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

MEM–PERSIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE encodes Britain (with America) as the
agent of the coup; Mossadegh is the **sovereign who paid**—the
nationalist project and the constitutional experiment were
subordinated to royal restoration and renewed dependency.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. LEGITIMACY ENCODING — DEFEAT WEAPONIZED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In Persian civilizational memory, 1953 is not a closed defeat. It is
**proof of Western refusal to permit sovereign control**—encirclement
and dependency made visible. The revolution of 1979 invoked 1953
repeatedly: the Shah was legible as the ruler restored by foreign
powers; sovereignty required breaking that dependency. Mossadegh
himself was a secular nationalist, not an Islamist; but his fate
became a **symbol** for the broader claim that Iran could not
achieve sovereignty under the Pahlavi–Western alignment. CIV–CORE–PERSIA:
"Defeat is weaponized, not erased." Mossadegh's overthrow is the
archetypal instance: the sovereign who acted, the foreign power that
reversed him, and the memory that fueled the next rupture (1979).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. STRUCTURAL FRAME — EXTRACTION AND AGENCY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
From a structural perspective, oil nationalization threatened the
**extraction equilibrium**: Britain (and Western capital) had
benefited from the concession; a sovereign Iranian state controlling
oil threatened that benefit. The coup restored the prior equilibrium—
royal rule, negotiated oil arrangements—at the cost of **agency**:
the Iranian state again appeared as the side that could be overridden
when it challenged great-power interests. The lesson in Persian
encoding: sovereignty cannot be secured by constitutional means
alone when external powers have a stake in the outcome. The IRI
later claimed to have broken that dependency (MEM–PERSIA–IRAN–ISLAMIC–REPUBLIC);
the memory of Mossadegh and 1953 undergirds that claim.
