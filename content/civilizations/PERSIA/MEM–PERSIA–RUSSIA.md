I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Russia** from the Persian perspective: the
northern power that replaced Roman/Byzantine and Mongol pressure
with **sustained territorial encroachment**—Caucasus, Central Asia,
and the treaties (Golestan 1813, Turkmanchay 1828) that cost Persia
Georgia, Armenia, and claims north of the Aras. Russia in Persian
memory is **loss and containment**: the Great Game rival to Britain,
the Soviet neighbor that occupied Iran (1941–46), and the contemporary
power that remains a factor in Persian security calculus without
being the existential conqueror of the Sasanian state (that was
Arabia).

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. TSARIST EXPANSION — CAUCASUS AND CENTRAL ASIA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Russian southward expansion from the 18th c. brought the Tsarist
empire to the Caspian and the Caucasus. The treaties of Golestan
(1813) and Turkmanchay (1828) formalized Persian cession of territory
north of the Aras River—Georgia, northern Armenia, and Nakhchivan.
Abbas Amanat:

> "The Qajar state could not match Russian military and logistical
> power. The loss of the Caucasian provinces was a trauma that
> shaped Persian perception of the northern threat for more than
> a century. Russia was the power that had taken Persian land."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Persia did not lose to a peer in a parity war (as with Rome). It
**lost territory to a rising empire** that had no interest in
preserving a buffer. The Caspian (MEM–PERSIA–GEO–CASPIAN) became
a shared sea; the northern frontier shrank. فردوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. GREAT GAME — RUSSIA AND BRITAIN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In the 19th and early 20th c., Persia was the buffer between Russian
and British spheres. Concessions (e.g. railways, tobacco, oil) were
extracted by both; neither annexed Persia entirely. The structural
logic: **buffer value**—Persia survived in part because Britain
and Russia preferred a weak buffer to direct confrontation. Ervand
Abrahamian:

> "Iran was the classic buffer state. The British and the Russians
> carved out zones of influence but did not partition the country
> completely—each feared giving the other a decisive advantage."
> — Ervand Abrahamian, *A History of Modern Iran* (2008); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Russia in this encoding is **one of two pressures**; Persian
sovereignty is compressed but not erased.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. SOVIET PERIOD — OCCUPATION AND COLD WAR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Soviet and British forces occupied Iran in 1941 (securing supply
lines); the USSR delayed withdrawal from the north until 1946 and
backed separatist movements in Azerbaijan. The experience reinforced
**northern threat** in Persian memory. After the 1979 revolution,
Soviet–Iran relations were tense; the USSR invaded Afghanistan
(1979), and Iran faced a Soviet-backed regime on its eastern flank.
Russia (as USSR successor) inherited that structural position—a
neighbor that had historically taken territory and could project
pressure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CONTEMPORARY — COMPLEX ALIGNMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Post-Soviet Russia and the Islamic Republic have had pragmatic
relations: arms sales, nuclear cooperation (Bushehr), and at times
diplomatic alignment (e.g. Syria). Russia is not an ally in the
sense of shared legitimacy; it is a **tactical counterpart** to
American and European pressure. Persian encoding retains the memory
of territorial loss and northern threat; current cooperation does
not erase the structural lesson: the north has been the direction
of sustained loss.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MEARSHEIMER ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Russia is **northern structural pressure**: a land power that
expanded at Persian expense and that can project force from the
Caucasus and Caspian. Persia cannot balance Russia alone; buffer
logic (Great Game) required a second power (Britain). Contemporary
Iran faces Russia as a potential partner against the West but also
as a power that has historically taken Persian territory. The
Caspian remains a **shared space**—containment and limited
cooperation, not dominion by either side.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. BARNES ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who paid for Russian expansion? The Qajar state and the populations
of the ceded territories. Who defected? Elites who accommodated
Russian demands; the court that signed Turkmanchay. Liability
attached to the sovereign who could not defend the frontier. In
the Great Game, **both empires extracted**; Persia was the party
that paid in concessions. The northern frontier is the **liability
frontier**—where Persian sovereignty was most visibly reduced.
