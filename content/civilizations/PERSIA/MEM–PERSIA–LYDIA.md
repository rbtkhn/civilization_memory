I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Lydia** from Persia's perspective: the wealthy
kingdom of Croesus that Cyrus conquered in 547–546 BC, incorporating
Sardis as the western terminus of the Royal Road and establishing
Persian reach to the Aegean. Lydia is Cyrus's first major western
conquest—and the template for integrative sovereignty (Croesus spared,
incorporated as counselor).

Herodotus's Croesus–Cyrus narrative encodes the Persian ethic of
**conquest followed by recognition**, not annihilation.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CROESUS — THE LYDIAN KING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Croesus rules Lydia at its peak. His wealth is proverbial. Herodotus
records his ambition:

> "Croesus was the first foreigner known to the Greeks who subdued
> some of the Greeks and exacted tribute, and won the friendship of
> others... So it was that Croesus made subject to himself almost all
> the peoples west of the Halys."  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, I.6

Lydia dominates Anatolia. Croesus consults oracles; he decides to
march against Cyrus. The campaign ends in disaster—Cyrus defeats
Croesus, besieges Sardis, and takes the city.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE PYRE — INTEGRATIVE SOVEREIGNTY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Croesus is bound for the pyre. Herodotus records the famous scene—rain
extinguishes the flames; Cyrus, moved, spares him:

> "Croesus, tell me who of all men was it who persuaded thee to march
> upon my land and so to become an enemy to me instead of a friend?"  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, I.87

Cyrus does not execute the conquered king. He incorporates him. Croesus
becomes counselor—the template for Persian integrative conquest. The
pattern prefigures Babylon: conquer, then recognize; do not annihilate.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. SARDIS — ROYAL ROAD WESTERN TERMINUS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sardis becomes the western anchor of the Royal Road. Herodotus documents
the road from Sardis to Susa—the infrastructure that will carry Persian
administration, couriers, and trade across the empire. Lydia's
incorporation gives Persia a gateway to the Aegean and to Greek affairs.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Lydia encodes:
• Integrative conquest: Croesus spared, not destroyed
• Western expansion: Persia reaches the Aegean
• Royal Road origin: Sardis as western terminus
• Wealth transfer: Lydian gold, Greek tribute flow east

Cyrus's treatment of Croesus becomes normative—the model of dignified
conquest that Persian memory preserves.
