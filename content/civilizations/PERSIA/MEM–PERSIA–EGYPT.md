────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Egypt** from the Persian perspective: the
Achaemenid satrapy whose conquest under Cambyses (525 BC) extended
Persian sovereignty to the Nile and whose periodic revolts tested
the integrative limits of imperial governance.

For Persia, Egypt is **the western satrapy**—wealthy, restive, and
culturally distant. The sceptre of Egypt confers prestige and
revenue; it also demands attention.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CAMBYTES' CONQUEST — PELUSIUM AND MEMPHIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cambyses, son of Cyrus, marches against Psamtik III in 525 BC.
Herodotus records the campaign:

> "Cambyses made war on Amasis [actually Psamtik III]... and the
> Egyptians were defeated in a battle at Pelusium, and straightway
> fled in great disorder to Memphis. Cambyses sent a Persian herald
> up the river in a Mytilenean ship to summon the Egyptians to
> surrender; but when they saw the ship entering Memphis, they rushed
> out from the walls, destroyed the ship, and tore the men limb from
> limb, and carried the pieces into the city."  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, III.13–14

The conquest is brutal but not annihilating. Cambyses assumes the
Egyptian royal titulary; he presents himself as legitimate successor
to the pharaohs. Persian propaganda adapts to local expectation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. INCORPORATION — SATRAPY AND TEMPLE POLICY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Egypt becomes a satrapy. Persian governors (satraps) reside in
Memphis; local Egyptian elites retain administrative roles. Temples
and cults are largely undisturbed—Persian policy follows Cyrus's
pattern of respecting local religious institutions.

Pierre Briant observes:

> "The Persian administration in Egypt was characterized by continuity.
> Temples retained their lands and privileges; Egyptian scribes
> continued to function. The main change was the top layer—the
> satrap—and the flow of tribute to the center."  
> — Pierre Briant, *From Cyrus to Alexander*

Lloyd Llewellyn-Jones frames the broader pattern:

> "The Persians never did that. They adopted a more *laisser-faire*
> attitude and, as long as the tribute and the taxes came in, then
> they did not interfere with local systems."  
> — Lloyd Llewellyn-Jones, FiveBooks interview (2020), on *Persians: The Age of the Great Kings*

Egypt tests that *laisser-faire*: rich, proud, and periodically rebellious.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. REVOLTS — DARIUS, XERXES, ARTXERXES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Egypt revolts under Darius (after 486) and again under Xerxes. The
revolts are suppressed; Persian control is reasserted. Under Artaxerxes
I, a major rebellion (460–454 BC) draws Athenian intervention—the
Greek fleet supports the Egyptian insurgents. The revolt fails; Egypt
remains Persian until Alexander.

Herodotus contrasts Persian kings:

> "The Persians have a saying that Darius was a shopkeeper, Cambyses
> a master, and Cyrus a father; for Darius made a small profit from
> everything, Cambyses was harsh and reckless, and Cyrus was gentle
> and provided for all good things."  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, III.89

Cambyses' harshness in Egypt—Herodotus's account of his madness and
sacrilege—may reflect Egyptian or Greek hostile tradition. The
structural fact remains: Egypt is valuable and difficult.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE CANAL — DARIUS CONNECTS NILE AND RED SEA
────────────────────────────────────────────
Darius orders a canal linking the Nile to the Red Sea. The project
demonstrates Persian capacity for large-scale infrastructure and
the strategic value of Egyptian geography. The canal enables maritime
links to Arabia and the Persian Gulf.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ALEXANDER AND THE END OF PERSIAN EGYPT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Alexander enters Egypt in 332 BC. The Persian satrap offers no
resistance; the Egyptians welcome Alexander as liberator from Persian
rule. The Achaemenid sceptre over Egypt ends. Ptolemy will inherit
the kingdom.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Egypt is conquered, not annihilated; incorporation follows Cyrus's pattern.
• Satrapal governance preserves local institutions; tribute flows to center.
• Revolts recur; Egypt tests the limits of Persian integrative capacity.
• Cultural distance and Egyptian pride make Egypt a perpetual management challenge.
• Alexander's welcome reflects Egyptian restiveness, not Persian collapse elsewhere.

Tension preserved without verdict.
