I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **China** from the Persian perspective: the
eastern terminus of the Silk Road—the **silk producer** whose goods
passed through Persian-controlled corridors to Rome and the
Mediterranean (MEM–PERSIA–SILK–ROAD). China was never an Achaemenid
satrapy; it lay beyond the empire's eastern reach. Persia did not
conquer China or fight a parity war with it. The relationship is
**corridor and exchange**: Persia as intermediary, China as distant
origin of luxury and later a modern strategic partner when Western
pressure compels axis optionality (CIV–CORE–PERSIA).

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. SILK ROAD — CHINA AS EASTERN TERMINUS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The overland corridor linked China (and Central Asia) to the
Mediterranean. Persia sat at the **nexus**: silk and spices moved
west through Persian territory or Persian-protected routes; Roman
gold and goods moved east. Rashid al-Din records the continuity:

> "The routes from the east to the west passed through the lands of
> Iran. The caravans brought silk and spices from China and India,
> and the kings of Iran took toll and gave protection. So it was
> in the time of the ancients and so it continued under the Mongols."
> — Rashid al-Din, *Jami' al-Tawarikh*, tr. W.M. Thackston; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

China in this encoding is **supplier**, not rival. Rome sought to
bypass Persia (embassies to China, maritime alternatives); Persia's
interest was to keep the corridor flowing and extract transit value.
فَرْدوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. NO CONQUEST, NO PARITY WAR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Unlike India (eastern satrapies under Achaemenids) or Rome
(seven-century parity war), China was never integrated into Persian
imperial territory nor engaged in existential contest. The distance—
Central Asian steppe and oases between the plateau and China—made
conquest uneconomic. Touraj Daryaee:

> "The Sasanians maintained diplomatic and commercial relations with
> the eastern lands. China was a source of luxury goods and a
> destination for Persian envoys and merchants—not a theater of
> conquest or a peer rival like Rome."
> — Touraj Daryaee, *Sasanian Persia*

The structural logic: **intermediary advantage** depends on both
ends existing and trading; conquering China would have destroyed
the relationship that gave Persia its corridor value.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. MONGOL PERIOD — EURASIAN LINK
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Under the Mongol Ilkhanate, Persia was linked administratively and
commercially to the wider Mongol world—including Yuan China. The
Pax Mongolica temporarily secured the overland routes (MEM–PERSIA–MONGOL–EMPIRE).
Transmission of techniques, ideas, and goods (Eurasian transmission
models: China ⇄ Islam ⇄ Europe) passed through Persian territory.
China in this phase remains **distant pole**—connected by empire
(Pax Mongolica), not by Persian conquest.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MARITIME DISPLACEMENT AND LATER CONTACT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
When maritime routes (Indian Ocean, Cape) displaced overland silk
traffic (c. 14th–15th c.), Persia lost some intermediary advantage—
but the southern maritime corridor (Gulf, Indian Ocean) still linked
Persian ports to Asian trade. China did not disappear from Persian
commercial geography; the **mode of connection** shifted. In the
modern period, diplomatic and trade ties have continued without
the structural hostility that defines Persian relations with some
Western powers.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MODERN PERIOD — STRATEGIC PARTNERSHIP AND AXIS OPTIONALITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Under sanctions and Western pressure, Iran has pursued deeper
economic and strategic ties with China: Belt and Road participation,
energy and infrastructure deals, and diplomatic alignment that
reduces dependency on the West. CIV–CORE–PERSIA and CIV–STATE–PERSIA
encode **Russia, China: alternative partners; axis optionality**.
China in contemporary Persian calculus is not an ally in the sense
of shared legitimacy—it is a **tactical counterpart** that provides
economic exit and diplomatic counterweight. Abbas Amanat:

> "Iran has consistently sought to diversify its partnerships. China
> represents a market, an investor, and a power that does not demand
> ideological conformity—a different calculus from the West."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. MEARSHEIMER ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
China is **eastern terminus and optional partner**: no structural
military threat to Persia (distance, no shared border), and a
potential balancer against Western pressure. Persia's incentive is
to maintain corridor value (historically overland, now also
infrastructure and energy) and to use China as an exit option when
sanctions or containment bind. Force distribution does not pit Persia
against China; it allows **axis optionality**—aligning with China
(or Russia) when alignment with the West is costly.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. BARNES ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who paid for the Silk Road corridor? Both ends (Rome, China) paid
in tolls and goods; Persia extracted. Who defected? When maritime
routes bypassed the overland corridor, Persia lost intermediary
revenue—but that was structural shift, not Chinese defection. In
the modern period, liability for Iran–China deals attaches to
both parties (sanctions risk, reputational cost). China is **low
conflict, high optionality**—a relationship that reduces liability
from Western pressure without creating new liability from Chinese
dominance (China does not seek to govern Iran).
