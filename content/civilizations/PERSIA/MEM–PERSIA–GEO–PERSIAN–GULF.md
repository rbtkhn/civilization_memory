I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Persian Gulf** as Persia's southern maritime
chokepoint: the narrow-mouthed basin where Iran holds the entire
northern littoral, controls the Strait of Hormuz, and projects
leverage over global energy transit. Unlike the Caspian (closed,
containment), the Gulf opens to the Indian Ocean—but only through
a channel Persia can constrict.

For Persia, the Gulf is **asymmetric advantage**: geography grants
littoral dominance and chokepoint control that compensate for
numerical or coalition disadvantage elsewhere.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

II. MEARSHEIMER STRATEGIC ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.A. CHOKEPOINT GEOMETRY — STRAIT OF HORMOZ

The Persian Gulf connects to the Indian Ocean through the Strait of
Hormuz. Ancient and modern sources agree: the mouth is narrow.

Strabo, citing Eratosthenes, records the structural fact:

> "The Persian Gulf is also called the Persian Sea; and Eratosthenes
> describes it as follows: its mouth, he says, is so narrow that from
> Harmozi, the promontory of Carmania, one can see the promontory at
> Macae in Arabia."  
> — Strabo, *Geographica*, Book XVI.3

From Harmozi (Hormuz) to Macae: visibility-range narrowness. The
chokepoint is not a metaphor; it is cartographic. Any power holding
the northern shore commands the exit. Persia has always held the
northern shore.

STRUCTURAL CONSEQUENCE:
• He who controls Hormuz controls Gulf egress
• Persia's coastline is continuous from the Shatt al-Arab to the
  Strait; Arab littoral is fragmented across multiple states
• Naval closure is technologically feasible; the strait is ~21 miles
  at its narrowest
• Global oil transit depends on passage; leverage is structural

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.B. LITTORAL ASYMMETRY — PERSIA HOLDS THE NORTH

George Rawlinson describes the Achaemenid baseline:

> "Persia Proper lay upon the gulf to which it has given name,
> extending from the mouth of the Tab (Oroatis) to the point where
> the gulf joins the Indian Ocean. It was bounded on the west by
> Susiana, on the north by Media Magna, on the east by Mycia, and
> on the south by the sea."  
> — George Rawlinson, *The Seven Great Monarchies*, Vol. 5 (Persia)

Persia gives the Gulf its name. The northern coast—from the Tigris–
Euphrates delta to the strait—is Iranian. The southern coast is
Arab: Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, UAE, Oman. Fragmentation on the south;
unity on the north. Structural advantage accrues to the power that
holds the continuous littoral and the chokepoint.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.C. POWER DISTRIBUTION IMPLICATIONS

The balance of power in the Gulf is not determined by GDP or
population alone. Geography assigns:
• Chokepoint control to Iran
• Oil production concentration to Arab littoral states
• External guarantor (US) dependency to Gulf Arab states
• Denial capability to Iran (mine, missile, swarm)

Iran cannot dominate the Gulf by conventional naval superiority.
It can, however, deny unimpeded passage. Denial is cheaper than
dominance. The terrain rewards the defender of the strait.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.D. OFFENSIVE/DEFENSIVE BALANCE

The Gulf favors denial over projection:
• Narrow waters limit maneuver for large fleets
• Coastal missile batteries can cover the strait
• Asymmetric tactics (fast craft, mines) multiply in confined space
• External powers projecting into the Gulf face long supply lines
  and concentrated Iranian counter–area access

Persia does not need to win a naval battle. It needs to make transit
costly. The geography supports that calculus.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.E. ENERGY TRANSIT — GLOBAL DEPENDENCY

A significant share of global oil passes through the Strait of Hormuz.
Threat of closure—or even harassment—raises premiums. Iran need not
close the strait to extract leverage; the credible threat alters
behavior. Sanctions, blockades, and coalition-building against Iran
must discount this structural card. Persia holds it by geography.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.F. HISTORICAL CONTINUITY — TRADE AND CONTROL

The Gulf has been a trade corridor since antiquity. Darius's canal
linked the Nile to the Red Sea; Persian control of the Gulf littoral
enabled Indian Ocean connectivity. Nearchus's voyage (after Alexander)
passed through the Gulf. The pattern persists: whoever holds the
northern shore influences Gulf transit. Sasanian, Safavid, and
modern Iran inherit that position.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ARC TIMELINE EVIDENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

III.A. ARC-T-ANCIENT (Pre-500 AD)

Strabo on the Gulf's eastern boundary of Arabia Felix:

> "The northern side of Arabia Felix is formed by the above-mentioned
> desert, the eastern by the Persian Gulf, the western by the Arabian
> Gulf, and the southern by the great sea that lies outside both gulfs,
> which as a whole is called Erythra."  
> — Strabo, *Geographica*, Book XVI.3

The Persian Gulf structures the eastern limit of the Arabian
peninsula. It is a boundary, not a void. Nearchus, cited by Strabo,
describes the Persian coast:

> "Nearchus goes on to say that there is an island at the beginning
> of the Persian Gulf where quantities of valuable pearls are to be
> found; and that in other islands there are pebbles of transparent
> and brilliant stones."  
> — Strabo, *Geographica*, Book XVI.3 (quoting Nearchus)

Resources and transit: the ancient template.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

III.B. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL (500–1500 AD)

Medieval Islamic geographers incorporated the Persian Gulf (Baḥr Fāris)
into their world maps and trade-route descriptions. Al-Biruni, in his
*Chronology of Ancient Nations*, and the Persianate cartographic
tradition placed the Gulf as the maritime boundary between the Iranian
world and the Arabian coast. The name "Persian Gulf" reflects this
encoding: the sea belongs to the Persian sphere. Ibn Khaldun, in the
*Muqaddimah*, discusses maritime trade and the routes connecting
India, the Gulf, and the Mediterranean. The Gulf remains a corridor
of commerce and a zone of Persian–Arab interface.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

III.C. ARC-T-EARLY-MOD (1500–1900)

Rawlinson on the Empire's southern boundary:

> "The boundaries of their empire were the desert of Thibet, the
> Sutlej, and the Indus, on the east; the Indian Sea, the Persian
> Gulf, the Arabian and Nubian deserts, on the south."  
> — George Rawlinson, *The Seven Great Monarchies*, Vol. 5 (Persia)

The Persian Gulf as southern frontier: fixed in the Western
historiographical imagination. John Malcolm's *History of Persia*
(1815) and European travelers consistently used "Persian Gulf"—the
naming encodes political geography. The northern littoral is Persian;
the Gulf bears the name.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

III.D. ARC-T-MODERN (1900–Present)

Richard Frye, in *The Heritage of Persia*, situates the Gulf within
the Iranian world's maritime interface—a corridor connecting the
plateau to the Indian Ocean, where littoral position has historically
conferred leverage despite Persia's predominantly land-based traditions.
The structural logic holds: modern Iran inherits Achaemenid and
Sasanian geography.
Oil adds magnitude; the chokepoint adds credibility.
