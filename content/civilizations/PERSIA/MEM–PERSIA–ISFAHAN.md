I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Isfahan** as the Safavid ceremonial and
political capital: the city that Shah Abbas I (1588–1629) transformed
into a showpiece of imperial legitimacy. The saying **"Isfahan is
half the world"** (Esfahan nesf-e jahan) encodes the city as the
center of a cosmos—architecture, bazaar, mosque, and palace fused
into a single statement of sovereignty.

Isfahan is not the modern capital (Tehran) or the clerical center
(Qom). It is **legitimacy in stone**: the Naqsh-e Jahan square,
the Imam Mosque, the Ali Qapu palace, and the bridges over the
Zayandeh River. Safavid power projected through urban design;
the city was the message.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. SHAH ABBAS AND THE NEW CAPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shah Abbas I moved the capital from Qazvin to Isfahan in 1598 and
embarked on a vast building program. The choice reflected strategic
and symbolic logic: Isfahan lay at the crossroads of trade routes,
had a long history, and could be refashioned as a **theater of
kingship**. Roger Savory:

> "Abbas intended Isfahan to be not merely a capital but a symbol
> of the dynasty's power and piety. The great square, the mosques,
> the palace, and the bazaar were designed to impress subjects and
> foreign envoys alike. The city was his manifesto."
> — Roger Savory, *Iran under the Safavids* (1980); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN (Safavid context)

Architecture served legitimacy; the square (Naqsh-e Jahan, "Image
of the World") placed the shah at the center of a visible order.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. NAQSH-E JAHAN — SQUARE AS COSMOS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The great square (today Imam Khomeini Square) framed the key
buildings: the Shah Mosque (south), the Ali Qapu palace (west),
the Sheikh Lotfollah Mosque (east), and the Qeyseriyeh bazaar
entrance (north). Polo was played in the square; processions
and ceremonies displayed the shah's authority. Abbas Amanat:

> "Isfahan's square was the stage for Safavid sovereignty. The
> king watched from the Ali Qapu; the faithful prayed in the
> mosque; the merchants traded in the bazaar. One space united
> political, religious, and economic power."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The square is **constructed majesty**—comparable in function (if
not form) to the Apadana at Persepolis: the realm made visible,
order enacted in space.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. BRIDGES, RIVER, GARDENS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Zayandeh River crosses Isfahan; the Si-o-se-pol (33-arch bridge)
and Khaju Bridge are both functional and ceremonial. Gardens and
avenues (Chahar Bagh) extended the city. Water and greenery encoded
paradise (Persian garden as earthly reflection of divine order).
Lloyd Llewellyn-Jones on Persian architectural encoding:

> "The Persians have always used architecture to tell a story—
> of order, of legitimacy, of the king's place in the cosmos.
> Isfahan under the Safavids is one of the great examples."
> — Lloyd Llewellyn-Jones, *Persians: The Age of the Great Kings* (2022); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Isfahan's survival (ruins restored, city still a cultural symbol)
demonstrates **durability of encoded meaning**—the city outlasted
the Safavid fall (1722) and remains a reference point for Persian
identity. به نام خداوند جان و خرد — فردوسی، ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. FALL AND MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Afghan capture of Isfahan (1722) ended Safavid rule. The city
declined as capital but did not lose its symbolic charge. In the
modern period, Isfahan is a cultural and tourist center—the past
capital whose architecture still encodes "half the world." The
regime (IRI) uses Isfahan as a site of heritage and pilgrimage;
the message is continuity of Persian–Islamic civilization.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MEARSHEIMER ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Isfahan under Abbas was a **force-concentration and display node**:
the capital attracted revenue, talent, and trade; the square and
mosques projected power to subjects and foreigners. The fall of
Isfahan (1722) was the fall of the dynasty—loss of the center
meant loss of the state. Structural logic: the ceremonial capital
was also the fiscal and administrative hub; capture of the city
broke the regime.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. BARNES ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who paid for Isfahan? The treasury and the provinces that supplied
tribute. Who defected when the Afghans came? The periphery and
factions that abandoned the center. The city was the **liability
concentration point**—magnificent but dependent on the regime's
ability to hold the realm. Collapse of the center transferred
liability to the fallen dynasty.
