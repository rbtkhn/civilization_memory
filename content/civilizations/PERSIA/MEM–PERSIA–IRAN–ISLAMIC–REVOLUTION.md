I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **1979 Islamic Revolution** as the rupture that ended the Pahlavi monarchy and established the Islamic Republic of Iran. It encodes the **causes** (dependency, authoritarian modernisation, opposition coalition), **sequence** (mass mobilisation, exile of the Shah, return of Khomeini, referendum), and **outcome** (clerical–republican state, sovereignty restored in anti-Western terms). The revolution is remembered as **civilizational reset**: rejection of foreign imposition and reassertion of dignity through Islamic governance.

Contradictions are preserved: the revolution was at once populist, religious, nationalist, and anti-dependency; it produced both republic and guardianship (velayat-e faqih).

III. CAUSES & OPPOSITION COALITION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The revolution was overdetermined. Nikki Keddie stresses the multiplicity of grievances:

> "The Iranian Revolution was the product of many factors: the
> Shah's authoritarian rule and his dependence on the United
> States; the discontent of the bazaar and the clergy; the
> marginalization of the secular opposition; and the economic
> and cultural dislocations of rapid modernization."
> — Nikki Keddie, *Modern Iran: Roots and Results of Revolution* (2003); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The 1953 coup and subsequent oil and security ties made the Shah's regime **legible as dependency**. Ervand Abrahamian notes how the memory of 1953 fed revolutionary narrative:

> "The 1953 coup left a deep scar on the national psyche. It was
> seen as proof that the West would not permit Iran to control its
> own resources and destiny. The memory of 1953 fueled the
> revolution of 1979."
> — Ervand Abrahamian, *A History of Modern Iran* (2008); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Opposition united around **sovereignty and dignity**; Khomeini's framing (anti-tyranny, Islamic order) captured the coalition while clergy and bazaar provided organisation and sanctuary.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. SEQUENCE: MOBILISATION TO REPUBLIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1978 saw escalating protests, mourning cycles (40-day commemorations amplifying turnout), and strikes that paralysed oil and state. The Shah left in January 1979; Khomeini returned to Tehran in February. The military declared neutrality; the monarchy collapsed. Abbas Amanat summarises the transition:

> "Khomeini's arrival in Tehran on February 1, 1979, marked the
> symbolic takeover of the revolution by the Islamist camp. The
> provisional government, the referendum, and the drafting of the
> constitution followed in quick succession, consolidating clerical
> authority within a republican framework."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The referendum of March–April 1979 produced a majority for an "Islamic Republic"; the constitution (velayat-e faqih, Guardian Council) followed. The revolution **did not** produce a liberal democracy or a left republic; it produced a **sovereign Islamic state** that rejected Western models and dependency.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ENCODING: SOVEREIGNTY, DIGNITY, ANTI-DEPENDENCY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In Persian civilizational memory the revolution encodes **restoration of sovereignty** after perceived subordination (1953, client status, cultural Westernisation). Religion provided the **legitimacy language** and organisational network; the outcome was **state power fused with clerical authority**. Shaul Bakhash describes the institutional result:

> "The constitution of the Islamic Republic created a dual structure
> of power: elected institutions—president, parliament—and
> non-elected institutions—Supreme Leader, Guardian Council,
> Assembly of Experts—that could override or veto the elected
> branch. The non-elected institutions derived their authority
> from the concept of velayat-e faqih."
> — Shaul Bakhash, *The Reign of the Ayatollahs* (1984); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The revolution is thus **continuity through compression**: monarchy and Western alignment were discarded; sovereignty and endurance were re-encoded in Islamic-republican form. CORE axioms (sovereignty sacred; dignity–patience–compute; survival as legitimacy metric) align with this encoding.
