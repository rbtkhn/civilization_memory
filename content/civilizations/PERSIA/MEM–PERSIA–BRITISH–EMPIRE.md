I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **British Empire** from the Persian perspective:
the southern and eastern pressure in the Great Game (Russia was the
north), the power that **extracted concessions**—tobacco, oil,
railways—without annexing Persian territory, and the power that
**overthrew a sovereign government** (1953 coup against Mossadegh).
Britain in Persian memory is **colonial trauma** (CIV–CORE–PERSIA):
encirclement, concession erosion, and 1953 as proof that the West would
not permit Iran to control its own resources. Unlike Russia, Britain
did not take Persian land; it took **sovereignty**—and the memory
of that taking fed the 1979 revolution.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GREAT GAME — BRITAIN AS SOUTHERN PRESSURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In the 19th and early 20th c., Persia was the buffer between Russian
and British spheres. Britain projected from India and the Gulf;
Russia from the Caucasus and Central Asia. Ervand Abrahamian:

> "Iran was the classic buffer state. The British and the Russians
> carved out zones of influence but did not partition the country
> completely—each feared giving the other a decisive advantage."
> — Ervand Abrahamian, *A History of Modern Iran* (2008); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Britain in this encoding is **one of two imperial extractors**.
Persian sovereignty survived because neither empire wanted the
other to dominate—buffer value. But survival was compressed:
concessions, not annexation. به نام خداوند جان و خرد — فردوسی، ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CONCESSIONS — TOBACCO, OIL, RAILWAYS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
British (and Russian) capital extracted concessions: the Tobacco
Regie (1890s), reversed by popular revolt; the Anglo-Persian Oil
Company (1908, later BP), which gave Britain a stake in Persian
oil for decades; railways and telegraph in British and Russian
zones. Abbas Amanat:

> "The Qajar state sold concessions to pay for royal and administrative
> expenses. The tobacco revolt showed that the population would resist
> when sovereignty was seen to be sold. Oil became the most valuable
> concession—and the most contested."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Britain did not conquer Persia; it **purchased and operated** within
it. The distinction—no territorial loss to Britain, but loss of
control over resources—shapes Persian encoding: Russia took land;
Britain took oil and agency.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. 1941 — OCCUPATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Soviet and British forces occupied Iran in 1941 to secure supply
lines to the USSR. The Shah (Reza Shah) was forced to abdicate;
his son (Mohammad Reza) was installed. The occupation was **joint**
—Britain from the south and west, USSR from the north—and reinforced
the lesson: Persia could be entered and its sovereign choices
overridden when great powers agreed. Withdrawal (British sooner,
Soviet delayed until 1946) did not erase the memory of invasion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. 1953 COUP — SOVEREIGNTY RUPTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The nationalization of the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (1951) under
Prime Minister Mossadegh was a sovereign act: Iran reclaiming control
of its oil. Britain (with the United States) responded with a coup
(1953) that overthrew Mossadegh and restored the Shah. Ervand
Abrahamian:

> "The 1953 coup left a deep scar on the national psyche. It was
> seen as proof that the West would not permit Iran to control its
> own resources and destiny. The memory of 1953 fueled the
> revolution of 1979."
> — Ervand Abrahamian, *A History of Modern Iran* (2008); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

In CIV–CORE–PERSIA, 1953 is a **temporal node** in the continuity
engine: Colonial Trauma → 1953 Coup → Iran–Iraq War → Sanctions Era.
Britain (with America) is the agent of that coup. The revolution
encoded 1953 as **proof of Western refusal to permit sovereign
control**—defeat weaponized, not erased.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. POST-IMPERIAL — BRITAIN AS WEST
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After empire, Britain remained part of the "West" that the Islamic
Republic defines itself against: sanctions, diplomatic tension,
hostility to dependency. Britain is not the primary actor in
contemporary US-led pressure, but it is **aligned**—and in Persian
memory it is the power that, with America, overthrew Mossadegh.
The legacy of the British Empire in Iran is concession, occupation,
and coup—not territory, but **sovereignty denied**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. MEARSHEIMER ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Britain was **southern/eastern structural pressure** in the Great
Game: it balanced Russia by maintaining a zone of influence (south,
Gulf, India border) without annexing Persia. The incentive was
buffer and resource extraction, not territorial conquest. When
sovereign action (oil nationalization) threatened extraction,
Britain acted to restore the prior equilibrium—the 1953 coup. The
structural lesson: an imperial power that does not take land can
still **take agency** when the local sovereign challenges its
economic stake. Persia's response (revolution, anti-dependency)
is the long-term counter to that lesson.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. BARNES ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who paid for British concessions? The Persian treasury and the
population (tobacco revolt). Who paid for 1953? Mossadegh, the
nationalist project, and eventually the Pahlavi state—the coup
restored the Shah but made him legible as dependent, and the
revolution claimed to end that dependency. Who was on the hook?
The sovereign who tried to reclaim oil (Mossadegh) and the sovereign
who was restored by foreign intervention (the Shah). Britain (with
America) bears the **liability of the coup** in Persian memory—the
actor that proved the West would not permit sovereign control.
