I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Parthian (Arsacid) dynasty** as the middle
imperial link in Persian sovereignty: the regime that emerged from
Seleucid collapse, governed through confederation and noble accommodation,
established **denial warfare** as doctrine against Rome (Carrhae), and
held parity until Ardashir I's restoration of centralized Iranian
kingship (224 AD).

The Parthians represent **continuity through adaptation**—Iranian
kingship preserved by mobility, intermediation, and refusal to be
absorbed, not by Achaemenid-style bureaucratic scale or Sasanian-style
sacral centralization.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. DYNASTIC ORIGIN & RISE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Arsacid line emerges in the mid-third century BC. Arsaces I
founds the dynasty in Parthia (northeast of the Iranian plateau),
exploiting Seleucid weakness and localizing power in noble houses.

Key origin features:
• Break from Seleucid suzerainty (c. 247 BC)
• Capital at Hecatompylos, later Ctesiphon (Mesopotamia)
• Fusion of Iranian royal tradition with Hellenistic court elements
• No single bureaucratic revolution—authority remains shared with
  regional magnates and clan leaders

The empire expands by **co-opting existing elites and trade corridors**,
not by imposing a uniform satrapal machine. Al-Tabari's chronicle
encodes the long Roman–Persian rhythm that the Parthian phase
inaugurates:

> "The Persians and the Romans fought many wars. When the king of
> kings was victorious he would take the Roman lands and cities; when
> the Romans prevailed they would recover their territory. Thus it
> continued until the rise of Islam."
> — Al-Tabari, *Ta'rikh al-rusul wa'l-muluk*, tr. C.E. Bosworth; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

فردوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد / کز این برتر اندیشه بر نگذرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. GOVERNANCE — CONFEDERATION NOT CENTRALIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Parthian rule is **confederal**. The King of Kings presides over
noble houses and regional rulers who retain armed force, revenue,
and local legitimacy. Central authority is negotiated, not imposed.

Touraj Daryaee frames the contrast that Ardashir's victory would
institutionalize:

> "Ardashir's victory over Artabanus was not merely a dynastic
> coup but the establishment of a new imperial ideology. Where
> the Arsacids had governed through confederation and noble
> accommodation, the Sasanians demanded centralization and
> sacral authority."
> — Touraj Daryaee, *Sasanian Persia*

Adrian Goldsworthy sharpens the military-political distinction (full formulation in MEM–PERSIA–DYNASTY–SASANIAN–SHAPUR–I):

> "The Sassanids were not the Parthians. Where the Arsacids had
> been content to raid and negotiate, Shapur sought conquest and
> permanent humiliation of Rome. He wanted not just victory but the
> destruction of Roman pretensions to universal empire."
> — Adrian Goldsworthy, *Rome and Persia*

The structural implication: Parthia **denies** Rome final victory
without seeking to conquer Rome. Raid, negotiate, hold the frontier.
The Sasanians later choose a different incentive—centralized state,
siege warfare, humiliation of the emperor. Both are Persian; the
institutional form differs.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. DENIAL WARFARE & ROMAN PARITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Parthian military doctrine is **mobility over mass**. Horse archers
and cataphracts avoid set-piece annihilation; they exhaust, harass,
and deny.

Strabo states the logic:

> "The Parthians fight entirely on horseback and avoid close combat,
> retreating when pressed and returning when the enemy is disordered,
> so that their warfare admits no decisive engagement."
> — Strabo, *Geographica*, Book XI

Carrhae (53 BC) validates this doctrine: Crassus invades; Surena's
force chooses the terms. Roman legions cannot close; they are broken
by missile fire and retreat. Seven eagles captured; Crassus killed.
No Parthian occupation of Roman territory—**denial is sufficient**
(MEM–PERSIA–WAR–CARRHAE).

Legitimacy is contested as much as territory. When Parthian nobles
reject the Roman-backed Arsacid Vonones (AD 16), Tacitus records
their appeal to prior denial:

> "Where was the glory of the men who slew Crassus and ejected Antony,
> if a chattel of the Caesar, who had brooked his bondage through all
> these years, was to govern Parthians?"
> — Tacitus, *Annals*, II.2

Parthia refuses to be a Roman province. Kingship is Iranian; the
throne is not a gift of Rome. Parity is **legitimacy contest** as
well as military balance (MEM–PERSIA–WAR–ROME).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. TRADE & INTERMEDIARY ROLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Parthian control of the overland corridor between China/India and
the Mediterranean gives **transit power**. Caravans pay tolls;
Persian and Sogdian merchants intermediate; Roman gold flows east,
silk and spices west (MEM–PERSIA–SILK–ROAD).

The dynasty does not produce silk; it **sits between** producers and
consumers. Denial is possible—close the corridor, and both termini
suffer. Incentive structure favors Parthian intermediation for as
long as overland transit is cheaper or safer than alternatives.
Roman embassies and later maritime efforts reflect the desire to
bypass Parthian (and Sasanian) tolls.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. OVERTHROW — ARDASHIR I AND RESTORATION NARRATIVE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In 224 AD, Ardashir I of Persis defeats the last Arsacid King of
Kings, Artabanus IV, at Hormozdgan. The Parthian dynasty ends; the
Sasanian begins.

Ardashir frames his rise not as revolution but as **restoration of
rightful Persian order**. Hellenistic and Parthian "dilution" is
reversed; Iranian kingship is recentralized. The Arsacid court and
Parthian legitimacy are the losers; Persian sacral authority is
the winner (MEM–PERSIA–DYNASTY–SASANIAN–ARDASHIR–I).

The Parthian period thus encodes a **transition**: confederation
and denial preserved Persian sovereignty for nearly five centuries;
when noble fragmentation and Roman pressure made that model fragile,
the next regime (Sasanian) chose centralization and sacral hardening.
Empire persists; the institutional form changes.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. STRUCTURAL CONTEXT (POWER DISTRIBUTION & INCENTIVE)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**Power distribution:** Parthia held the plateau and Mesopotamia; Rome held the Mediterranean and Anatolia. The Levant was parity zone—neither could absorb it. **Incentive structure:** Parthian incentive was denial (raid, negotiate, hold the frontier), not conquest of Rome. Strabo and Carrhae encode this: mobility over mass, no decisive engagement. What the balance of power told Parthia to do was hold, not conquer. **Structural outcome:** Nearly five centuries of parity; no terminal victory for either side. When confederation and noble fragmentation made the model fragile, the Sasanians chose centralization—incentive shifted, geography did not.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**Who was on the hook:** Artabanus IV (last Arsacid)—defeated at Hormozdgan (224); the King of Kings bore liability for dynastic overthrow. **Who defected:** Noble houses and regional magnates—confederal structure meant liability was distributed; when Ardashir won, the Arsacid court lost. **Who benefited:** Ardashir I and the Sasanian line—restoration narrative, centralization, sacral authority. **Institutional survival:** Parthian doctrine (denial warfare, mobility) was inherited by the Sasanians; Carrhae remained the founding proof. **Irreversibility:** The Arsacid dynasty ended; the Roman–Persian rivalry did not. Liability attached to the last Arsacid; the civilizational layer (denial, parity) transferred to the new regime.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Parthian rule is confederal; noble accommodation over centralization.
• Denial warfare—mobility, retreat, no decisive engagement—validates against Rome (Carrhae).
• Parity with Rome is legitimacy contest as well as military balance.
• Transit power (Silk Road intermediation) extracts value without conquest of termini.
• Overthrow by Ardashir (224) is framed as restoration, not revolution; Sasanians harden authority.
• Empire as form outlives dynasty; Parthian doctrine (denial) is inherited by Sasanians.
