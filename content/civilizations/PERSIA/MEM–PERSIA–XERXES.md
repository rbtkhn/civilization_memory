────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Xerxes I** as the Achaemenid king whose invasions
of Greece (480–479 BC) produced the definitive Persian encounter with
the Hellenic world—and whose memory is encoded differently in Persian
and Greek civilizational narratives.

For Persia, Xerxes represents **frontier expansion, not existential
conflict**. The Greek wars are a western campaign; Thermopylae and
Salamis matter more to Greek identity than to Persian imperial
continuity.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. SUCCESSION & DARIUS'S UNFINISHED BUSINESS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Xerxes succeeds Darius in 486 BC. Egypt has revolted; the Greek
frontier remains unsettled after Marathon. Herodotus frames the
inherited obligation:

> "Darius was angry with the Athenians for their attack on Sardis, and
> the prospect of subduing Greece became his preoccupation. He therefore
> sent messengers to all the cities with orders to prepare an army and
> ships... For he intended to march against Greece and subdue it."  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, VII.1

Xerxes inherits not merely the throne but the Greek question. The
encoding begins here: for Persia, this is continuity of policy; for
Greece, it is invasion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE TRUTH AND THE LIE — AHURAMAZDA AND ARTA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Xerxes continues the Achaemenid ideological framework. The Persian
ethic of *arta* (truth, order) versus *druj* (the lie, chaos) structures
kingship. Rebellious subjects embody *druj*; the king restores *arta*.

Pierre Briant observes:

> "The ideology of the 'lie' is central to Achaemenid royal discourse.
> Rebellions are not merely political; they are cosmic—violations of
> the order that the king, with Ahura Mazda's favor, upholds."  
> — Pierre Briant, *From Cyrus to Alexander*

The Greek campaign is thus framed as restoration of order at the
empire's edge. Persian encoding does not center Greek agency.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. THERMOPYLAE & SALAMIS — GREEK MEMORY VS PERSIAN REALITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Thermopylae (480 BC) blocks the pass; Leonidas falls. Salamis (480 BC)
reverses Persian naval advantage. Plataea (479 BC) ends the land
campaign. Greece celebrates; Persia withdraws.

Herodotus records the scale of Xerxes' host:

> "For who of those in times past has led an army against Greece? ...
> The land army was the greatest of all armies we know of, so that it
> was smaller than that of Darius... but it was not much smaller."  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, VII.20–21

Lloyd Llewellyn-Jones reframes the Greek-centric narrative:

> "I've managed to write the Battle of Marathon in a sentence and a
> half, for instance. It was of very little consequence to the
> Persians."  
> — Lloyd Llewellyn-Jones, FiveBooks interview (2020), on *Persians: The Age of the Great Kings*

If Marathon is minor, Thermopylae and Salamis—though larger—occupy
a different place in Persian memory: frontier setbacks, not civilizational
defeats. The empire survives; the Greek periphery remains contested.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. PERSIAN ENCODING — WHAT PERSIA REMEMBERS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Persia has no Herodotus. The Persian "version" of the Greek wars
survives in inscriptions, reliefs, and later transmission. Xerxes'
failure to annex Greece does not rupture Achaemenid legitimacy; the
court, the satrapies, and the Royal Road persist.

Richard Frye observes that the Greek victories were defensive—they
did not threaten the Persian heartland—and that Greek memory of the
Persian Wars has shaped Western historiography more than Persian
sources ever could (*The Heritage of Persia*). Encoding divergence
is structural: Greek triumph becomes foundational; Persian withdrawal
becomes a footnote.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LATER REIGN — COURT & ASSASSINATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Xerxes returns to Persia. He completes Persepolis; he faces court
intrigue. In 465 BC he is assassinated by the chiliarch Artabanus and
a eunuch. His son Artaxerxes I succeeds after eliminating rivals.

The Greek frontier remains; Persian attention shifts. Xerxes becomes
the king who tried and did not close the western loop.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Xerxes inherits the Greek question; he does not create it.
• Persian encoding of Thermopylae/Salamis differs from Greek triumphalism.
• Frontier expansion fails; imperial core holds.
• The "truth and the lie" frame structures Persian royal ideology.
• Greek memory dominates Western historiography; Persian sources are sparse.

Tension preserved without verdict.
