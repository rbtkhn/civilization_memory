I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Qom** (Qum) as Persia's preeminent Shi'a holy
city and seminary center: the site of the shrine of Fatima Masumeh
(sister of the Eighth Imam), the **hawza** (religious academy) that
trains the clergy, and the locus of clerical authority that undergirds
velayat-e faqih and the Islamic Republic.

Qom is not the political capital—Tehran is. Qom is the **legitimacy
source**: where religious learning, fatwas, and the chain of
authority that validates the Supreme Leader are produced. Khomeini
taught and was exiled from Qom; the city encodes clerical power
independent of—and prior to—the state.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. SHRINE AND PILGRIMAGE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The shrine of Fatima Masumeh (d. 816) made Qom a pilgrimage destination
for Shi'a Muslims. Safavid patronage elevated the city; the shrine
became a symbol of Shi'a sovereignty and piety. Pilgrimage ties
religious devotion to place; the city accumulates **sacral density**.
Abbas Amanat:

> "Qom had long been a center of Shi'i learning and pilgrimage.
> The shrine of Hazrat-e Masumeh drew the faithful; the seminaries
> drew the scholars. Under the Safavids and after, it was the
> religious counterweight to the political capital."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Legitimacy flows from the shrine and the schools. The state may
patronize or pressure Qom; it cannot replace it as the source of
clerical authority.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. HAWZA — SEMINARY AS INSTITUTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The **hawza-ye elmiye** (religious seminary) of Qom is the principal
training ground for Shi'a jurists and scholars. Curriculum, hierarchy
of learning, and the authority to interpret law are produced here.
Roy Mottahedeh:

> "In Qom, the great teachers sat in the courtyards of the shrine
> or in the seminaries, and students came from across the Shi'i
> world. The chain of learning—who studied with whom—determined
> who could claim religious authority."
> — Roy Mottahedeh, *The Mantle of the Prophet* (1985); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The seminary is **institutional continuity**: it predates the IRI
and will outlast any single regime. Velayat-e faqih draws on the
authority of the learned; the learned are certified in Qom.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. KHOMEINI AND REVOLUTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Khomeini taught in Qom from the early 1960s until exile (1964).
His lectures on velayat-e faqih and opposition to the Shah were
disseminated from Qom. The city was the **ideological base** of the
revolution—not the only one, but the one that supplied the
constitutional theory of the Islamic Republic. Ervand Abrahamian:

> "Khomeini's base was Qom. When he spoke, the seminaries listened.
> When the Shah cracked down, it was the Qom clergy who could
> mobilize the bazaar and the pious. The revolution had many
> centers; Qom was the religious heart."
> — Ervand Abrahamian, *A History of Modern Iran* (2008); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Post-revolution, Qom remained the seat of the marja'iyya (source of
emulation) and the Assembly of Experts (which selects the Supreme
Leader). Power flows from Qom to Tehran—and back.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. TEHRAN–QOM AXIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Islamic Republic operates on a **Tehran–Qom axis**: Tehran is
the executive and political center; Qom is the religious and
juridical source. Tension between the two is structural—elected
politicians in Tehran, clerical oversight from Qom—but they are
mutually dependent. فَرْدوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MEARSHEIMER ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Qom is a **non-state power node**: legitimacy is produced there
without being fully controlled by the state. The seminary trains
the personnel who validate the regime; the shrine attracts the
faithful. Structural logic: the regime needs Qom's certification;
Qom can withhold or condition it. Concentration of religious
authority in one city creates a single point of ideological
production—and potential defection.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. BARNES ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who is on the hook in Qom? The senior clergy. Fatwas and succession
(Assembly of Experts) originate here; liability for legitimacy
failure attaches to those who certify the Leader. Defection (a
marja' or faction breaking with the regime) would be declared
from Qom. The city is the **liability source** for clerical
authority.
