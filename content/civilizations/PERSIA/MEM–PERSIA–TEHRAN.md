I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Tehran** as Persia's political capital: the city
chosen by the Qajars (1786) as seat of the shah, retained by Pahlavi
and the Islamic Republic, and encoded as the **locus of sovereign
authority**—where legitimacy is declared, contested, and enforced.

Tehran is not the oldest Persian city. It is the **compression point**
where state power concentrates: palace, parliament, seminary linkage
(Qom is clerical; Tehran is where clerical and executive authority
intersect). Revolution (1979), demonstrations, and succession crises
repeatedly center here.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. QAJAR FOUNDING — CAPITAL CHOICE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Agha Mohammad Khan Qajar made Tehran the capital in 1786 after
consolidating power. The choice reflected strategic calculation:
proximity to the Alborz, defensible position, and distance from
Ottoman and Russian frontier pressure. Abbas Amanat:

> "Tehran was a modest town when the Qajars made it their capital.
> Its growth was a function of the court's presence—palaces,
> barracks, bazaars—and the gradual concentration of administration
> and trade that followed."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The city grew with the state. Legitimacy and geography fused: the
shah's seat defined the center; the center attracted the institutions
that reproduced sovereignty.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PAHLAVI MODERNIZATION — CAPITAL AS SHOWCASE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Reza Shah and Mohammad Reza Shah refashioned Tehran as a modern
capital: broad avenues, monuments, the University of Tehran, and
the symbolic architecture of a secularizing monarchy. Roy Mottahedeh
captures the dual character of the city under the Pahlavis:

> "Tehran was the city where the Shah's portrait hung in every
> office, where the army paraded, and where the Savak had its
> headquarters. It was also the city of the bazaar and the mosque,
> where the clergy maintained their networks."
> — Roy Mottahedeh, *The Mantle of the Prophet* (1985); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Two legitimacies coexisted in one space: royal and religious. When
the revolution came, Tehran was where both were decided.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. 1979 — REVOLUTION IN THE CAPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Islamic Revolution climaxed in Tehran: mass demonstrations,
occupation of key buildings, the flight of the Shah, and Khomeini's
return. The capital did not merely witness the revolution; it **was**
the revolution's stage. Ervand Abrahamian:

> "Tehran was the nerve center. What happened in Tehran determined
> the outcome. The bazaar closed, the oil workers struck, the army
> hesitated—and the regime collapsed in the capital first."
> — Ervand Abrahamian, *A History of Modern Iran* (2008); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Control of Tehran meant control of the state. The revolution encoded
Tehran as the place where sovereignty could be seized—and lost.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ISLAMIC REPUBLIC — DUAL CENTER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Under the IRI, Tehran remains the seat of government (presidency,
Majles, ministries) and the Supreme Leader's office. Qom holds
religious authority; **Tehran is where religious and executive
power meet**. Protests (1999, 2009, 2022) have repeatedly erupted
in Tehran—the capital as both legitimacy display and dissent locus.
فَرْدوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MEARSHEIMER ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tehran is the **force-concentration node**: whoever holds the capital
holds the levers of state—army, media, treasury. The Qajar choice
was structural: a defensible interior base. Revolution and succession
crises are decided in Tehran because that is where power is
concentrated; conquest or defection of the center determines outcome.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. BARNES ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who is on the hook in Tehran? The incumbent. Demonstrations target
the seat of power; liability attaches to whoever fails to hold the
street or the palace. Defection (army, bazaar, clergy) is visible
first in the capital. The city is the **liability amplifier**.
