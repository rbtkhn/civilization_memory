I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Mesopotamia** from Persia's perspective: the
Tigris–Euphrates lowland that is the **western approach to the plateau**,
the seat of Parthian and Sasanian capitals (Ctesiphon/Seleucia), and the
fluvial corridor between the Iranian heartland and the Levant. CIV–CORE–PERSIA
names "Caspian–Caucasus–**Mesopotamia** buffers"; this MEM encodes the
third element—the lowland that connects Persian core to western
theaters and that Roman (and later Palmyrene) armies could reach.

For Persia, Mesopotamia is **capital terrain and approach corridor**:
indispensable for imperial administration and western projection,
but **exposed**—no mountain ring protects Ctesiphon as it protects
the plateau. When Roman or counterweight forces strike Mesopotamia,
the capital is at risk.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

II. MEARSHEIMER STRATEGIC ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.A. FLUVIAL GEOMETRY — TWO RIVERS, OPEN APPROACH

Mesopotamia is the alluvial plain between and along the Tigris and
Euphrates. Strabo describes the connective geography:

> "Syria lies between Cilicia and Egypt, extending inland to the
> Euphrates, and through it pass the roads by which merchandise
> and armies alike move from the sea to the interior and from the
> interior to the sea."
> — Strabo, *Geographica*, Book XVI

The Euphrates marks the **approach line**: Persian armies move west
across it toward the Levant; Roman (and Palmyrene) armies move east
toward Ctesiphon. MEM–PERSIA–WAR–CARRHAE encodes the Euphrates as
implicit boundary—Crassus crosses, is destroyed; the river becomes
the limit of sustainable Roman offensive. From the Persian view,
Mesopotamia is the **forward capital zone**—close enough to the
Levant to project, close enough to the plateau to withdraw. فردوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

STRUCTURAL CONSEQUENCE:
• Capital in lowland = capital at risk when western front collapses
• Euphrates is a line of operation, not a barrier
• Control of Mesopotamia enables projection into Levant; loss of Mesopotamia exposes plateau

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.B. CTESIPHON — CAPITAL VULNERABILITY

Parthian and Sasanian kings placed their principal residence at
Ctesiphon (across from Seleucia on the Tigris). The choice reflected
administrative and strategic logic: Mesopotamia was the nexus of
trade routes, the gateway to the Levant, and the historic center
of Babylonian and Achaemenid western administration. But a capital
in the lowland is **reachable**. Roman armies reached Ctesiphon
multiple times (Trajan, Septimius Severus); Palmyra besieged it
after Valerian's capture (MEM–PERSIA–PALMYRA). Heraclius's counteroffensive
struck deep into Mesopotamia and broke Sasanian resistance. Edward
Gibbon records the Roman capacity to reach the heart:

> "Heraclius resolved to carry the war into the heart of Persia, and
> by a bold enterprise to compel the enemy to recall their armies
> from the conquest of the Roman provinces."
> — Edward Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*, Chapter XLVI

Mesopotamia does not defend itself. It is **corridor and seat**—high
value, high exposure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.C. HINGE BETWEEN PLATEAU AND LEVANT

MEM–PERSIA–GEO–LEVANT frames the Levant as "the hinge between
Mesopotamia and the Mediterranean." Mesopotamia is thus the **eastern
half of that hinge**—the Persian-held approach to the parity zone.
Whoever holds Mesopotamia can project into the Levant (or block
projection). When Persia loses Mesopotamian cohesion (e.g. post-Valerian
chaos), third actors (Palmyra) fill the corridor. When Rome pushes
east, Mesopotamia is the battleground. Touraj Daryaee situates
Sasanian capital choice:

> "Ctesiphon remained the administrative and symbolic center of the
> Sasanian Empire—the place where the King of Kings held court and
> where Roman envoys came to negotiate. Its location in Mesopotamia
> placed it at the crossroads of empire."
> — Touraj Daryaee, *Sasanian Persia*

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.D. EUPHRATES AS BOUNDARY — CARRHAE AND PARITY

The battle of Carrhae (53 BC) and subsequent Parthian doctrine
established the Euphrates as the **effective limit** of Roman
eastward expansion. Plutarch (via MEM–PERSIA–WAR–CARRHAE) records
that the Euphrates "had been passed without result" by Crassus—and
his defeat fixed the lesson. Persia could raid and hold Mesopotamia;
Rome could not hold it permanently. The river is **operational
boundary**—not a wall but the line beyond which Roman occupation
becomes unsustainable. Mesopotamia is therefore **Persian-held
approach terrain** for most of the parity period—until Heraclius
reverses the equation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.E. OFFENSIVE/DEFENSIVE BALANCE

Mesopotamia favors the power that can concentrate force and supply
it. Flat terrain allows cavalry and army movement; rivers supply
water and transport but do not block. Siege of cities (Ctesiphon,
Seleucia) is feasible. The **defender** of Mesopotamia must hold
the Euphrates line and the capital; the **attacker** from the west
must cross the Euphrates and reach the Tigris. Persian structural
advantage: depth—retreat toward the plateau is possible. Roman
advantage: when Persian forces are overextended in the Levant,
Mesopotamia is under-defended (Palmyra, Heraclius). Balance shifts
with force allocation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ARC TIMELINE EVIDENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

III.A. ARC-T-ANCIENT (Pre-500 AD)

Achaemenid administration incorporated Mesopotamia (Babylon, satrapies).
The Royal Road and imperial structure used the lowland as corridor.
Alexander's conquest of Persia passed through Mesopotamia; Seleucia
and Ctesiphon later emerged as Hellenistic and Parthian capitals.

III.B. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL (500–1500 AD)

Islamic conquest (7th c.) transferred Mesopotamia from Sasanian to
Caliphate; Baghdad became the Abbasid capital. The fluvial corridor
remained the hinge between Iranian plateau and Mediterranean/Levant
in Islamic geography. Persian (Safavid, etc.) and Ottoman contest
over Iraq continued the structural logic.

III.C. ARC-T-MODERN (1900–Present)

Modern Iraq occupies the same terrain. Strategic relevance: corridor,
oil, and the enduring fact that the Tigris–Euphrates basin links
the plateau to the Arab Levant. Persia's western approach still
passes through Mesopotamia.
