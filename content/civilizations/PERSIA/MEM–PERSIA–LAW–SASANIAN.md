I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Sasanian law** as Persia's **codified legal
order under sacral kingship**: the fusion of Zoroastrian cosmology
(asha, druj), priestly jurisprudence, and royal authority that
structured inheritance, marriage, contract, crime, and status in the
Sasanian empire. Surviving texts (e.g. *Madigan-i Hazar Dadestan*—
Book of a Thousand Judgments; legal compilations and commentaries)
show a **professionalised legal class** and **written precedent**.
Encoding: **law as cosmic order**—the king and the priests uphold
asha (truth, order); violation is druj (lie, chaos); liability and
redress are part of the same moral-legal frame.

For Persian civilizational memory, Sasanian law represents
**institutional continuity** from Zoroaster (MEM–PERSIA–ZOROASTER)
and Achaemenid royal tradition—hardened into codified form under
the Sasanians and transmitted (in part) into the Islamic period
through Zoroastrian communities and later Persian statecraft.
Contradictions preserved.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. SOURCES AND STRUCTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• **Zoroastrian basis:** Asha (order, truth) vs druj (lie, chaos);
  law as restoration of order; king and mobad (priest) as enforcers
• **Surviving material:** Pahlavi legal texts; *Madigan-i Hazar
  Dadestan* (compilation of case-style judgments and opinions);
  inheritance, marriage, adoption, contract, procedure
• **Institutions:** Royal courts; priestly courts; judges (dadwar);
  written documents and witnesses; hierarchy of legal authority
• **Social encoding:** Status (aristocracy, priests, commoners);
  family and marriage as legal units; property and succession
  tightly regulated

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. LAW AS COSMIC ORDER (MERCOURIS FRAME)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
It seems to me that the crucial point is that Sasanian law was not
secular—it was **part of the same cosmology** that legitimated the
king. The shahanshah upheld asha; the judge applied it. Legitimacy
through order: the realm that judged fairly and punished druj
retained divine favor (khvarenah); the one that allowed injustice
lost it. Institutional continuity: the Sasanian dynasty (MEM–PERSIA–
SASANIAN–DYNASTY) formalised what Zoroaster had set in motion—the
**disciplinary theology of rule** (MEM–PERSIA–ZOROASTER) became
codified law.

> "I shall not die, these seeds I've sown will save my name and
> reputation from the grave, and men of sense and wisdom will proclaim,
> when I have gone, my praises and my fame."
> — Ferdowsi, *Shahnameh* (prologue), tr. Dick Davis; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

Maria Brosius on Achaemenid precedent (applicable to Sasanian
continuity):

> "The king's role as judge and guarantor of justice was central to
> Persian royal ideology. Under the Sasanians this was developed
> into a full legal system with a professional judiciary."
> — Maria Brosius, *The Persians* (2006); contextual

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL FRAME
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Centralisation under the Sasanians (MEM–PERSIA–SASANIAN–DYNASTY)
required **predictable rules**: taxation, land, succession, and
dispute resolution had to be standardised. The incentive structure
favoured codification—royal and priestly authority combined to
produce a law that served both fiscal and ideological control. No
separation of church and state; law was **sacral and royal** at once.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: the king and the priests (mobads, judges); the
**dadwar** (judge) decided cases; appeal and hierarchy existed.
Who was on the hook? The wrongdoer (druj); the king who failed to
enforce order lost khvarenah. Irreversibility: Sasanian law did not
survive as the state law of Iran after the Arab conquest—Zoroastrian
communities retained it in part; Islamic law replaced it in the
public sphere. The **grammar** of law as order (asha) and of royal
responsibility for justice persisted in Persianate political thought
(MEM–PERSIA–LAW–SIYASATNAMA; MEM–PERSIA–ZOROASTER).
