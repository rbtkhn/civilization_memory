────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Persepolis** as Persia's ceremonial heart: the
Achaemenid capital whose terraced platform, audience halls, and relief
sculpture encode imperial legitimacy through architecture. Persepolis
is not merely a city but **constructed majesty**—the place where the
King of Kings received tribute and displayed the order of the realm.

For Persia, Persepolis represents legitimacy made visible. Alexander's
burning (330 BC) ends the Achaemenid ritual center but not its memory.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FOUNDATION — DARIUS'S CEREMONIAL PROJECT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Darius I initiates construction at Parsa (Persepolis) c. 518 BC. The
site lies in the heartland of Persis, the Persian homeland. The
terrace, the Apadana (audience hall), and the gateways proclaim
Achaemenid sovereignty.

Pierre Briant situates the ideological function:

> "Persepolis was not a residential capital in the ordinary sense.
> It was the ceremonial center where the empire displayed itself—
> where tribute-bearers from every province appeared in relief,
> where the king received delegations, and where the ritual of
> imperial unity was performed."  
> — Pierre Briant, *From Cyrus to Alexander*

Architecture serves legitimacy; the building is the message.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE APADANA RELIEFS — EMPIRE AS VISUAL ORDER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Apadana reliefs depict tribute processions: delegates from
twenty-three nations bring gifts to the king. Medes, Egyptians,
Babylonians, Ionians, Indians—each in distinctive dress—enact the
plural imperial order. The king sits at the center; diversity
converges on him.

Lloyd Llewellyn-Jones frames Persian self-presentation:

> "We have to right that wrong. We have to show that the Persians and
> other easterners were sophisticated and cultured, embracing, thoughtful
> and scientific—all the things that a harmful brand of Orientalism says
> that they're not."  
> — Lloyd Llewellyn-Jones, FiveBooks interview (2020), on *Persians: The Age of the Great Kings*

Persepolis embodies that sophistication: precise carving, cosmological
ordering, the empire as harmonious hierarchy. Orientalist caricature
cannot survive the reliefs.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. NOWRUZ AND RITUAL SEASON
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Persepolis likely hosted the New Year (Nowruz) ceremonies. The terrace
and Apadana provided the stage for tribute, feasting, and the
reaffirmation of royal favor. The "Prologue" of Llewellyn-Jones's
*Persians* opens at "Persepolis 488 BCE"—a single day when the empire
gathers. Ritual concentrates legitimacy in time and space.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. XERXES' ADDITIONS — GATE OF ALL NATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Xerxes extends the complex: the Gate of All Nations, the Hall of a
Hundred Columns. Inscriptions declare his piety and his father's
legacy. Persepolis grows under Darius and Xerxes; later kings add
tombs and structures. The site accumulates meaning across reigns.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ALEXANDER'S FIRE — DESTRUCTION AND MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In 330 BC, Alexander enters Persepolis. The sources disagree on
motivation—drunken revenge, strategic signal, or accident—but the
result is fire. The Apadana and palaces burn. Persepolis ceases to
function as living capital.

The destruction was performative—the end of Achaemenid ritual space.
But the ruins remained. Later Persian dynasties, including the
Sasanians, invoked the site as a link to the ancient kings (Canepa,
*The Two Eyes of the Earth*). Destruction does not erase memory;
it concentrates it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Persepolis encodes empire as visual and ritual order.
• Architecture is propaganda; the Apadana reliefs are political theory.
• Ceremonial centrality differs from administrative centrality (Susa, Ecbatana).
• Alexander's fire ends use but not symbolic weight.
• Later Persian regimes invoke Persepolis as legitimacy anchor.

Tension preserved without verdict.
