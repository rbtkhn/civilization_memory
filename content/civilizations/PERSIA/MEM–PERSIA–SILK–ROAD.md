I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Silk Road** from Persia's perspective: the
transcontinental corridor where the Iranian Plateau sits at the **nexus**
between China/Central Asia (east) and the Mediterranean/Rome (west).
Persia does not originate silk; it profits from **transit**. The Royal
Road (Sardis–Susa) prefigures the western leg; Khorasan and the Oxus
valley anchor the eastern approach. Persia holds the middle—and
extracts value from both ends.

For Persia, the Silk Road encodes **intermediary advantage**: control
of the corridor without producing its most famous commodity.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. MEARSHEIMER STRATEGIC ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.A. NEXUS GEOMETRY — PERSIA AT THE CENTER

The Silk Road is not a single route but a network. Key Persian nodes:
• West: Susa, Ecbatana, Ctesiphon (capitals); Palmyra (desert link)
• Center: Rayy, Nishapur, Merv (Khorasan)
• East: Borders Bactria, Sogdiana; connects to Central Asian oases

Persia controls the **bottleneck** between Mediterranean demand and
Central Asian/Chinese supply. Neither end can reach the other at scale
without crossing Persian territory or Persian-protected routes.

STRUCTURAL CONSEQUENCE:
• Transit fees, tolls, and caravan protection generate revenue
• Political leverage: control of corridor = leverage over both termini
• Cultural transmission follows trade: Zoroastrianism, Manichaeism,
  Buddhism, Christianity move along the same paths

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.B. ROYAL ROAD — ACHAEMENID INFRASTRUCTURE

Herodotus documents the Persian imperial road system that later
Silk Road routes would extend and parallel:

> "As regards this road the truth is as follows. Everywhere there are
> royal stages and excellent resting-places, and the whole road runs
> through country which is inhabited and safe."  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, 5.52 (Royal Road, Sardis–Susa)

The Royal Road runs 1,677 miles (2,699 km) with 111 stages. Couriers
could cover it in seven days. The infrastructure—staging posts, guards,
reliable passage—establishes the template for later caravan routes.
What Darius built for administration becomes substrate for commerce.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.C. INTERMEDIARY ADVANTAGE — TRANSIT WITHOUT PRODUCTION

Persia does not produce silk. China does. Rome desires it. Persia
sits between. The structural logic:
• Parthians and Sasanians tax caravans; Sogdian and Persian merchants
  intermediate; Roman gold flows east; silk and spices flow west
• Control of Merv, Rayy, Ctesiphon = control of choke points
• Denial is possible: close the corridor, and both termini suffer

George Rawlinson frames the Achaemenid extent:

> "The boundaries of their empire were the desert of Thibet, the
> Sutlej, and the Indus, on the east; the Indian Sea, the Persian
> Gulf, the Arabian and Nubian deserts, on the south."  
> — George Rawlinson, *The Seven Great Monarchies*, Vol. 5 (Persia)

The empire spans the corridor. East meets west across Persian space.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.D. PARTHIAN AND SASANIAN INTERMEDIARIES

Under Parthian and Sasanian rule, Persia continues to dominate the
overland route. Roman efforts to reach silk sources directly (e.g.,
embassies to China, maritime alternatives) reflect Roman desire to
bypass Persian tolls. The incentive structure favors Persian
intermediation: as long as overland transit is cheaper or safer than
alternatives, Persia extracts value.

Strabo describes the corridor function of Syria—the western terminus:

> "Syria lies between Cilicia and Egypt, extending inland to the
> Euphrates, and through it pass the roads by which merchandise and
> armies alike move from the sea to the interior and from the interior
> to the sea."  
> — Strabo, *Geographica*, Book XVI

Merchandise and armies: the same geography serves commerce and power.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.E. MARITIME DISPLACEMENT — STRUCTURAL EROSION

The Silk Road's decline (c. 14th–15th century) coincides with maritime
routes replacing overland transit. The Persian Gulf, Indian Ocean, and
Red Sea routes bypass the Iranian Plateau. Persia loses its intermediary
advantage when ships carry silk and spices more cheaply than caravans.
MEM–PERSIA–GEO–PERSIAN–GULF and MEM–PERSIA–GEO–INDIAN–OCEAN document
Persia's southern maritime interface—but the overland corridor's
centrality fades. Transit power erodes with technology.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ARC TIMELINE EVIDENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

III.A. ARC-T-ANCIENT (Pre-500 AD)

Herodotus and Strabo establish the road infrastructure and corridor
geography. Xenophon's *Anabasis* describes the march of the Ten Thousand
along roads that would later carry silk caravans. The Achaemenid
courier system ("Neither snow nor rain nor heat nor gloom of night...")
encodes the imperial commitment to rapid communication—and by extension,
to controlled movement of people and goods across the realm.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

III.B. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL (500–1500 AD)

Ibn Khaldun, in the *Muqaddimah*, discusses trade routes and the
civilizational role of cities as nodes of commerce. Al-Biruni's
*Chronology of Ancient Nations* and Islamic geographical works
incorporate the Persian Plateau into the known world's trade networks.
The Sasanian and early Islamic periods see continued caravan traffic;
the Mongol conquests (13th c.) temporarily secure the routes (Pax
Mongolica) before maritime displacement accelerates.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

III.C. ARC-T-EARLY-MOD (1500–1900)

Rawlinson and John Malcolm describe the Persian Empire's historical
extent and the role of roads in imperial cohesion. The Royal Road
and its eastern extensions enter Western historiography as precursors
of the "Silk Road" concept. European travelers document surviving
caravan routes and the decay of overland trade in favor of maritime
alternatives.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

III.D. ARC-T-MODERN (1900–Present)

Richard Frye and Pierre Briant situate the Royal Road and Persian
trade corridors within the longer history of Eurasian connectivity.
Touraj Daryaee addresses Sasanian economic and diplomatic relations
along the eastern frontier. Modern scholarship emphasizes Persia's
role as intermediary rather than originator—transit power as
structural advantage, and its erosion with maritime competition.
