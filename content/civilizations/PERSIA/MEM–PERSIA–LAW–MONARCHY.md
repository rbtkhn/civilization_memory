I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **monarchy** as Persia's **legal and legitimating
form of sovereignty**: the king (shah, shahanshah, padishah) as source
of law and order, not subject to a written constitution until 1906.
From Achaemenid through Qajar (and Pahlavi), the monarch embodied or
channeled divine favor (khvarenah); succession was dynastic, often
contested; the realm was the king's domain. The **law of monarchy**—
who may rule, how succession works, what limits (if any) bind the
sovereign—shifts with the Constitutional Revolution (1906) and ends
as a *state form* with the Islamic Republic (1979); the **encoding**
of kingship in memory (Ferdowsi, mirrors for princes, restoration
narratives) persists.

Contradictions preserved: monarchy as legitimate order vs. monarchy
as oppression; constitution as limit vs. constitution as breach of
divine mandate.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. MONARCHY AS SOURCE OF LAW (PRE-CONSTITUTIONAL)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• **Achaemenid:** King as lawgiver and judge; royal decree (farman);
  local laws preserved under imperial ceiling (Cyrus, Darius); Behistun
  as royal justification. No written limit on the king; legitimacy
  through justice and divine favor (Ahura Mazda).
• **Sasanian:** Sacral kingship; king upholds asha (order); Zoroastrian
  clergy and royal courts; law as cosmic order (MEM–PERSIA–LAW–SASANIAN).
  Succession within the house; assassination and civil war as recurrent
  hazards.
• **Persianate (Seljuk, Safavid, Qajar):** Sultan/shah as sovereign;
  Siyasatnama and mirrors prescribe *how* to rule, not *limits* on
  rule. Monarchy remains the default; the vizier advises, the king
  decides. Farman (royal decree) retains force.

Herodotus on the Persian king and tribute:

> "Darius it was who first fixed the tribute and instructed the peoples
> in the manner of it; and in accordance with his enactment all later
> monarchs have levied tribute."
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, III.89; ARC–PERSIA-T-ANCIENT

III. LEGITIMACY AND SUCCESSION (MERCOURIS FRAME)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
It seems to me that the crucial point is that **monarchy in Persian
law was not contractual**—the king did not hold power by consent of
the governed or by a written constitution. He held it by lineage,
conquest, or designation (and often by all three); legitimacy was
**through order and justice**: the king who upheld asha, who did not
oppress, who restored temples or borders, retained khvarenah. The
law of succession was rarely codified in a way that prevented
dispute; fratricide and civil war were recurrent. Institutional
continuity was the **form** (one king, one realm) and the **grammar**
(restoration, not revolution; dynasty, not republic).

> "I shall not die, these seeds I've sown will save my name and
> reputation from the grave, and men of sense and wisdom will proclaim,
> when I have gone, my praises and my fame."
> — Ferdowsi, *Shahnameh* (prologue), tr. Dick Davis; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CONSTITUTIONAL LIMIT AND TERMINATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• **1906:** The Fundamental Law and Supplementary Law **limit** the
  Qajar shah: Majles must approve legislation and budget; the king
  becomes a constitutional monarch. Monarchy remains; sovereignty is
  **divided** (crown + assembly). The law of monarchy is amended, not
  abolished (MEM–PERSIA–LAW–CONSTITUTION).
• **1925:** Pahlavi coup; Reza Shah replaces the Qajar dynasty. Monarchy
  continues under a new house; constitution (1906) is nominally retained
  but bent to authoritarian practice.
• **1979:** Revolution abolishes the monarchy; the Islamic Republic
  replaces the shah with the faqih. The **law of monarchy** as the
  state form ends; the **memory** of kingship (epic, legitimacy
  narratives, restoration) remains in civilizational encoding.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. STRUCTURAL FRAME
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Structurally, monarchy was the **default sovereign form** for Persian
statehood until 1979. No balance of powers; the king (or sultan/shah)
was the apex. Incentives favoured dynastic consolidation and
avoidance of succession crisis—hence designation of heirs, elimination
of rivals, and the use of marriage and hostage-taking to bind elites.
The constitution of 1906 introduced a **constraint** that the crown
repeatedly tested (1908, 1925, 1953); the law of monarchy in practice
oscillated between nominal constitutionalism and reassertion of royal
prerogative until the form itself was removed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: the monarch held **ultimate jurisdiction**—no court
above the king. Who was on the hook when the realm failed? The king
(loss of khvarenah, rebellion, conquest) or his rivals (usurpers,
assassins). Succession was **irreversible** only in outcome: once a
new king was enthroned and recognised, the previous one was dead or
deposed. The law of monarchy did not specify a single rule of
succession; it encoded **dynastic right** and **victory**. Liability
for breach (e.g. breaking the 1906 constitution) lay with the crown
and the military that enforced the breach; the 1979 revolution
assigned liability to the Pahlavi sovereign and abolished the
institution.
