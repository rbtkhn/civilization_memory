I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Armenia** from the Persian perspective: a
highland buffer between Iranian and Roman spheres, whose dynastic
ties, strategic position, and cultural hybridity made it indispensable
to Persian western security while simultaneously preventing its full
absorption into either empire.

For Persia, Armenia is not peripheral.
It is the **western seam**—the terrain where Persian and Roman
legitimacy collide without resolution.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC LOGIC — MOUNTAIN BUFFER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Armenia occupies the plateau between Anatolia, Mesopotamia, the
Caucasus, and the Iranian heartland.

Strabo defines the terrain:

> "Armenia is everywhere mountainous and difficult of access, a land
> intersected by lofty ridges and deep valleys, so that it is ill-suited
> to the passage of great armies, but well adapted for defense by those
> who are familiar with its roads and strongholds."  
> — Strabo, *Geographica*, Book XI

Geography makes Armenia valuable precisely because it resists conquest.
Persia and Rome both seek influence, not occupation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. IRANIAN SPHERE — DYNASTIC AND CULTURAL TIES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Under the Achaemenids, Armenia is integrated into imperial geography.
Herodotus records its place on the Royal Road:

> "From the Halys to the border of Armenia there are twenty-eight
> stages, making one hundred and four parasangs; and in this country
> there are two fortresses through which all must pass, and rivers
> which they cross on rafts."  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, V.52 (Royal Road, Sardis–Susa)

Armenia sits on the corridor between Anatolia and Mesopotamia—the
western seam of the Persian realm. Persia and Rome later contest it;
Achaemenid administration established the pattern.

Armenia emerges after Alexander as a semi-Hellenized monarchy with
strong Iranian traditions.

Key features:
• Artaxiad and Arsacid dynasties (Arsacid = Parthian house)
• nakharar aristocracy (Iranian-derived structure)
• Cultural hybridity: Iranian, Greek, local

Touraj Daryaee situates Armenia within Sasanian frontier policy:

> "Armenia remained a contested zone between Rome and Persia
> throughout the Sasanian period, with both empires seeking to
> install client kings who would acknowledge their supremacy."  
> — Touraj Daryaee, *Sasanian Persia*

From the Persian view, Armenian kingship is **Iranian-adjacent**—often
bound by dynastic marriage and shared aristocratic culture.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ROME VS PERSIA — ARMENIA AS STAKES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mommsen emphasizes the structural logic:

> "The Roman policy in Armenia was one of limitation; it sought not
> incorporation, but influence, and preferred uncertainty to the
> certainty of endless frontier war."  
> — Theodor Mommsen, *The Provinces of the Roman Empire*, Vol. II

Persia seeks Armenian clients who acknowledge Iranian supremacy;
Rome seeks to deny Persia that alignment. Control of Armenia means
forward defense of the Iranian plateau—or exposure to Roman pressure.

Tacitus (ARC–ROME) captures Armenia's structural role in the Vonones
crisis (AD 16–19). When the Parthians rejected their Roman-backed king:

> "The defeated Vonones found shelter in Armenia, then a masterless
> land between the Parthian and Roman empires—a dubious neighbour to
> the latter owing to the criminal action of Antony."  
> — Tacitus, *Annals*, II.3–4

Armenia as "masterless land between" —the buffer in its pure form.
Vonones flees Parthia; Rome and Parthia contest his refuge. Armenia
is the terrain where neither empire holds definitive sway.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. SASANIAN PERIOD — HARDENED CONTEST
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Under the Sasanians, Armenia becomes a battleground for religious
as well as political allegiance. Christianization (early 4th c.)
aligns Armenia with Rome doctrinally—but elite structure retains
Iranian ties.

Edward Gibbon frames the permanence of contention:

> "Armenia was the perpetual object of contention between the empires
> of Rome and Persia; and its kings were alternately the vassals and
> enemies of both, till the weakness of the country rendered it an
> instrument rather than a power."  
> — Edward Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*, ch. XLII

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. STRATEGIC RESTRAINT — NEITHER EMPIRE ANNEXES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trajan briefly annexes Armenia (114–117); Hadrian restores the
kingdom. Persia never fully absorbs it either.

The rule: **Provincialization destroys buffer value.**

Anthony Kaldellis observes:

> "Armenia functioned as a strategic cushion whose loss or annexation
> exposed the empire to immediate danger; its value lay in not being
> Roman, yet not being hostile."  
> — Anthony Kaldellis, *The New Roman Empire*

For Persia, the same logic holds: Armenia buffers the western
approach; annexation would invite total war with Rome.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. TERMINAL EXHAUSTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
By the 6th–7th centuries, Roman–Persian wars devastate Armenia.
The Arab conquests overrun the region after both empires exhaust
themselves. Armenia ceases to buffer; it becomes exposed terrain.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. MEARSHEIMER ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Armenia is a classic buffer zone: high cost to conquer, low marginal
value to hold. Both empires maximize utility by maintaining client
kings rather than direct rule. The geographic barrier (mountains,
harsh climate) raises the cost of projection for both sides; neither
can afford permanent garrison at scale.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. BARNES ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who pays for Armenian kingship? Both empires, through subsidy and
diplomatic investment. Who benefits from oscillation? Armenian
elites. The buffer logic distributes cost; defection (tilting to
one side) triggers the other's response. Liability is shared;
neither empire can exit without losing face.
