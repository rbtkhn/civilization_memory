I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Seleucid rule** over the former Achaemenid
heartland (Iranian plateau, Mesopotamia, and at times the Levant) as
the **Hellenistic interlude** in Persian civilizational memory: the
Macedonian-Greek dynasty that inherited Alexander's eastern conquests,
retained Achaemenid administrative practice, founded Seleucia-on-Tigris
as a new Mesopotamian capital, and lost the eastern satrapies to the
Arsacid Parthians (c. 247 BC). For Persia, the Seleucids are not a
native dynasty but the **carrier of imperial form** between Achaemenid
collapse and Parthian reconstitution.

The Seleucids represent **continuity of template, discontinuity of
legitimacy**—Greek kingship over Iranian terrain; satrapal structure
preserved; Persian dynastic memory later frames the period as
"dilution" that the Sasanians would reverse.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGIN — FROM ALEXANDER TO SELEUCUS I
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Seleucus I (satrap of Babylon 311/312 BC; king from 305 BC) secured
Mesopotamia and the Iranian plateau after the Wars of the Diadochi.
He did not restore the Achaemenid dynasty; he **inherited its
administrative geography**. The Achaemenid state had ended with
Darius III (MEM–PERSIA–WAR–ALEXANDER); its bureaucratic and
legitimacy grammar endured in Seleucid administration (MEM–PERSIA–ACHAEMENID–DYNASTY). Strabo's geography encodes the scale of the
Seleucid realm:

> "The Seleucidae held the sovereignty over Syria and the country
> of the Babylonians and of all the tribes in the interior as far
> as the tribes of India and the Bactrians and the Arabians."
> — Strabo, *Geographica*, II.5.12 (trans. H.C. Hamilton); ARC–PERSIA-T-ANCIENT

فردوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد / کز این برتر اندیشه بر نگذرد — فردوسی، ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

The Persian tradition does not centre the Seleucids in the *Shahnameh*;
royal memory jumps from the heroic past to the Arsacids and Sasanians.
The Hellenistic period is the **gap**—foreign rule that later
restoration rhetoric would define itself against.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. TERRITORIAL AND ADMINISTRATIVE CONTINUITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Seleucid rule retained:
• Satrapal and provincial structure (inherited from Achaemenid practice)
• Greek-Macedonian colonists and cities (e.g. Seleucia, foundations in Iran)
• Local elites and cults co-opted; no forced Hellenization of the interior
• Mesopotamian and Iranian corridors as the economic and strategic core

The system outlives the Achaemenids—**Seleucid, Parthian, and Sasanian**
(MEM–PERSIA–SATRAPIES). Seleucia-on-Tigris became the principal
Seleucid capital in the east; Ctesiphon later emerged as the Parthian
and Sasanian capital across the river (MEM–PERSIA–GEO–MESOPOTAMIA).
Control of the Iranian plateau passed from Achaemenid to Hellenistic
(Seleucid) from 330 BC to c. 247 BC (MEM–PERSIA–GEO–IRANIAN–PLATEAU).

Adrian Goldsworthy frames the long arc that the Seleucid period
opens:

> "The wars between Rome and Persia were the longest conflict in
> history, lasting almost seven hundred years. They began when
> Roman expansion eastward met the Hellenistic world created by
> Alexander's conquests and the successor kingdoms that emerged
> after his death."
> — Adrian Goldsworthy, *Rome and Persia: The Seven Hundred Year Rivalry* (2023); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The Seleucid kingdom is that successor state on the very terrain
Rome and Parthian (then Sasanian) Persia would contest.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL CONSTRAINTS — FRAGMENTATION AND EASTERN LOSS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**Power distribution:** The Seleucid realm was stretched across
Syria, Mesopotamia, Iran, and at times Anatolia and the Indus
margin. Incentive structure favoured the centre (Antioch, later
Seleucia) and western wars (Ptolemies, then Rome); the eastern
satrapies were distant and harder to hold. **Structural outcome:**
Repeated wars with the Ptolemies, Roman pressure from the west,
and internal succession crises left the east under-resourced.
Arsaces I broke from Seleucid suzerainty (c. 247 BC), exploiting
Seleucid weakness and localizing power in noble houses (MEM–PERSIA–PARTHIAN). The Iranian plateau and much of Mesopotamia passed to
the Parthians; the Seleucid kingdom shrank to Syria and fragments
until Roman annexation (64 BC).

The empire did not fall to a single battle; it **fragmented at the
edges**—eastern satrapies first, then the centre.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LEGITIMACY — GREEK KINGSHIP OVER IRANIAN TERRAIN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Seleucid legitimacy was Macedonian and Greek: conquest by Alexander,
division among the Diadochi, and rule by a dynasty that presented
itself as heir to Alexander's empire, not to the Achaemenids. Local
cults (including Babylonian and Iranian) were tolerated and at times
patronized; the template was **incorporation**, not Persian restoration.
Later Persian dynastic memory (Sasanian) would frame the period as
"Hellenistic and Parthian dilution" and claim a return to rightful
Iranian order (MEM–PERSIA–SASANIAN–DYNASTY; MEM–PERSIA–SASANIAN–
ARDASHIR–I). The Seleucids thus occupy a dual role in the codex:
**transmitter of administrative form**, **other** in legitimacy terms.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**Who was on the hook when the east was lost:** Seleucid kings
(notably Antiochus II and his successors)—succession disputes,
western wars, and failure to hold the eastern satrapies. **Who
defected:** Local satraps and nobles; Arsaces I and the Parthians
broke from Seleucid suzerainty and built a confederal Iranian
kingship. **Who benefited:** The Arsacid Parthians (eastern
terrain, trade corridors, legitimacy as "restorers" of Iranian
autonomy); Roman republic (Seleucid decline eased later Roman
eastward expansion). **Institutional survival:** The Seleucid
state did not endure on the plateau; its administrative and urban
legacy (cities, Greek-Macedonian settlement) was absorbed or
superseded by Parthian and then Sasanian practice. **Irreversibility:**
Seleucid rule over Iran was not restored; reconstitution came
through Parthian and Sasanian lines. Liability attached to the
centre that could not hold the periphery; the civilizational
template passed to Iranian dynasties.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Imperial form can outlast dynasty and legitimacy source.
• The Hellenistic interlude carries Achaemenid administration without Achaemenid legitimacy.
• Eastern loss precedes western loss—periphery fragments first when the centre is overstretched.
• Persian memory encodes the period as dilution; restoration rhetoric requires an other to overcome.
• Seleucid → Parthian transition sets the stage for seven centuries of Roman–Persian contest.
