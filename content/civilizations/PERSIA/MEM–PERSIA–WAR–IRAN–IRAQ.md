I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Iran–Iraq War** (1980–1988) from the
Persian perspective: the conflict that followed the Islamic Revolution,
began with Iraq's invasion of Iranian territory, and ended in stalemate
after eight years of attrition. In CIV–CORE–PERSIA the war is a
**temporal node** in the continuity engine: Colonial Trauma → 1953
Coup → **Iran–Iraq War** → Sanctions Era. For the Islamic Republic,
the war encoded **siege literacy**, martyrdom as legitimacy, and
sovereignty defended against a Western-backed aggressor (Iraq, supplied
by Arab and Western states). The war did not deliver victory to either
side; it **hardened** the IRI's siege doctrine and population's
willingness to absorb pain for sovereignty.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. INVASION AND REVERSAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In September 1980 Iraq invaded Iran, claiming sovereignty over
the Shatt al-Arab and border territories. The invasion was intended
to exploit post-revolutionary disorder and to pre-empt Iranian
export of revolution. Iranian forces, after initial retreat,
stabilized the front and then pushed back. The war became **attrition**—
trench warfare, offensives and counter-offensives, chemical weapons
use by Iraq, and massive casualties on both sides. Ervand Abrahamian:

> "The Iran–Iraq War was the longest conventional war of the twentieth
> century. It cost Iran an estimated 200,000–600,000 dead and reinforced
> the regime's narrative of martyrdom and resistance. The war was framed
> as a defense of the revolution and of Islam against Ba'athist
> aggression backed by the West and the Arab states."
> — Ervand Abrahamian, *A History of Modern Iran* (2008); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Neither side achieved decisive victory. The border returned roughly
to the status quo ante; the human and economic cost was enormous.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. LEGITIMACY AND MARTYRDOM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For the IRI, the war functioned as **legitimacy maintenance**:
external pressure unified the population behind the regime; martyrdom
(basij, war dead) was sacralized and integrated into the Shi'a memory
engine (CIV–CORE–PERSIA: martyrdom → endurance → legitimacy →
sovereignty). The war was not sought as expansion; it was **defense
of the revolution and territory**. Abbas Amanat:

> "The Iran–Iraq War entrenched the Islamic Republic's siege mentality.
> It demonstrated that Iran could withstand a prolonged assault and
> that the population would endure for sovereignty. The cost was
> horrific, but the regime emerged with its narrative of resistance
> strengthened."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

MEM–PERSIA–IRAN–ISLAMIC–REPUBLIC and MEM–PERSIA–IRAN–ISLAMIC–REVOLUTION
encode the regime's legitimacy sources; the Iran–Iraq War is the
**first major test**—invasion repelled, no surrender, stalemate
accepted only when exhaustion forced it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL FRAME — TERRITORY, EXHAUSTION, NO CLOSURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
From a structural perspective, the war was **territorial and
ideological**: Iraq sought to lock in border gains and weaken
the revolution; Iran sought to repel invasion and, at times,
to export revolution or topple Saddam. The balance of forces
and external support (Iraq received arms and intelligence from
Western and Arab states) prevented either side from achieving
decisive victory. The outcome: **exhaustion and stalemate**—
ceasefire (1988) without a formal peace treaty. The lesson in
Persian encoding: sovereignty was defended; the cost was
absorbed; the regime survived. MEM–PERSIA–GEO–MESOPOTAMIA
frames Mesopotamia as the western approach to the plateau;
the Iran–Iraq border is the modern incarnation of that
contested corridor.
