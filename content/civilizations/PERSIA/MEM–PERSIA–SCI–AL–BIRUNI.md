I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **al-Bīrūnī** as Persia's master of **precision and
cross-civilizational inquiry**: astronomer, geodesist, and author of the
*Kitāb taḥqīq mā li-l-Hind* (India)—a systematic study of Indian
sciences, religions, and geography. Born in Khwarezm, he served
Ghaznavid courts; his work encodes **Persianate court science** at its
most outward-looking: measurement, comparison, and the recording of
other civilizations without conquest.

For Persian civilizational memory, al-Bīrūnī represents **institutional
continuity of knowledge** across Islamic absorption—the same impulse
toward order and taxonomy that appears in satrapal administration,
now applied to the heavens and to India. Contradictions preserved.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FORMATIVE CONDITIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Khwarezm (Khiva region); early study in mathematics and astronomy
• Ghaznavid court (Mahmud of Ghazni); accompanied campaigns to India;
  *India* (taḥqīq) based on first-hand inquiry and Sanskrit sources
• Works: *Chronology of Ancient Nations*; *Masʿūdī Canon* (astronomy);
  *Determination of the Coordinates of Cities*; geodesy (earth
  circumference, latitude); trigonometry
• Died in Ghazna (Afghanistan)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PRECISION AND CROSS-CIVILIZATIONAL INQUIRY (MERCOURIS FRAME)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
It seems to me that the crucial point is that al-Bīrūnī did not merely
transmit Greek or Indian knowledge—he **measured** and **compared**.
His geodesy improved on earlier estimates; his *India* presented
Indian thought and practice in its own terms while subjecting it to
critical description. Persianate legitimacy here is **knowledge as
scope**: the court that could sponsor such inquiry demonstrated
capacity to encompass the world in thought. Ferdowsi, his rough
contemporary, gave Persia the epic of kings; al-Bīrūnī gave it the
epistemic reach.

> "I shall not die, these seeds I've sown will save my name and
> reputation from the grave, and men of sense and wisdom will proclaim,
> when I have gone, my praises and my fame."
> — Ferdowsi, *Shahnameh* (prologue), tr. Dick Davis; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

Al-Bīrūnī on the obligation of the scholar:

> "The author of this book has been so eager to present the doctrines
> of the Hindus without bias that he has confined himself to the
> role of a narrator. He has refrained from criticism and preferred
> to leave the reader to form his own judgment."
> — Al-Bīrūnī, *India* (taḥqīq), tr. Sachau; subject verbatim

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL FRAME
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Court patronage supplied the resource base; Ghaznavid expansion into
India supplied access. The incentive structure favoured encyclopedic
and comparative work that enhanced the dynasty's claim to universal
knowledge. Al-Bīrūnī's science was **state-adjacent** but not
weaponised—astronomy and geodesy served calendar, navigation, and
prestige.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
No sovereign-override or liability split. Al-Bīrūnī operated as
scholar under patronage; his legacy is epistemic. Irreversibility:
the *India* and his astronomical works entered the transmission
stream; Persianate science was encoded as precise and comparative.
