────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Medes** as Persia's pre-imperial precursor:
the Iranian people whose hegemony over the Iranian plateau preceded
Cyrus's rise and whose fusion with the Persians created the
Achaemenid imperial base.

For Persia, the Medes represent **the order that Cyrus supersedes**—
and absorbs. Persian identity is forged in relation to Median
supremacy; the overthrow of Astyages is the founding act.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. MEDIAN HEGEMONY — ECBATANA AND THE PLATEAU
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Medes emerge in the seventh century BC. Ecbatana (Hamadan) becomes
their capital. Herodotus describes the Median confederation:

> "The Medes were the first to cast off the yoke of the Assyrians...
> Having won their freedom, they ruled over Asia beyond the Halys for
> one hundred and twenty-eight years, except for the period when the
> Scythians held it."  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, I.95–96

Median power extends across the plateau. The Persians, centered in
Anshan and Pars, are initially subject to Median suzerainty. Cyrus
is the son of a Persian vassal of the Median king Astyages.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PERSIAN–MEDIAN FUSION — CYRUS'S REVOLUTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cyrus overthrows Astyages (c. 550 BC). The sources—Herodotus, Ctesias,
the Nabonidus Chronicle—differ on the mechanism. What is clear: the
Persians do not destroy the Medes. They absorb them.

Herodotus notes the cultural adoption:

> "The Persians are of all men the most ready to adopt foreign customs;
> and they have adopted the dress of the Medes, finding it more
> beautiful than their own."  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, I.135

Pierre Briant frames the structural outcome:

> "The fusion of Persian and Median elites was essential to Achaemenid
> success. Cyrus did not replace one dynasty with another; he integrated
> the Median aristocracy into a new imperial project. Ecbatana remained
> a royal residence; Median nobles retained status."  
> — Pierre Briant, *From Cyrus to Alexander*

Overthrow is followed by incorporation. The Medes become partners,
not erased subjects.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ECBATANA — DUAL CAPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ecbatana remains a summer residence for Achaemenid kings. The Medes
contribute cavalry, administration, and legitimacy. The Persian
empire is, in its early phase, a Persian–Median condominium.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE "PERSIAN" ARMY — MEDIAN ELEMENTS
────────────────────────────────────────────
When Cyrus and his successors campaign, Median cavalry and infantry
form part of the force. The Greek sources often use "Mede" and
"Persian" interchangeably—a slippage that reflects the fusion.
Lloyd Llewellyn-Jones cites Darius's self-description; that martial
identity—bowman, spearman, horseman—draws on both Persian and Median
traditions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MEMORY AND ERASURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Medes fade from prominence in later Achaemenid rhetoric. The
founding narrative centers Cyrus and the Persians; the Medes become
the conquered, even though they were absorbed, not annihilated. This
erasure is partial—Herodotus preserves the memory; the Behistun
inscription lists Media as a satrapy—but the Median contribution to
imperial formation is understated in royal propaganda.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Median hegemony provides the structural precondition for Cyrus's rise.
• Persian–Median fusion creates the imperial base; incorporation, not destruction.
• Ecbatana persists as dual capital; the Medes are partners in empire.
• Cultural adoption (dress, cavalry) runs both directions.
• Royal propaganda understates Median contribution; Herodotus preserves it.

Tension preserved without verdict.
