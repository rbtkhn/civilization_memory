I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **India** from the Persian perspective: the
eastern horizon that supplied Achaemenid satrapies (Gandhara, Indus
valley), the far end of trade corridors (Silk Road, Indian Ocean),
and later a Persianate cultural sphere (Mughal, Deccan) and a modern
neighbor with which Persia has neither parity war nor absorption—
**trade, culture, and strategic distance**.

India in Persian memory is not the western peer (Rome) or the
conquest source (Arabia). It is **eastern extent and corridor**—
integration under Cyrus and Darius, then sustained contact through
sea and overland routes, without the seven-century existential
contest that defines Roman–Persian relations.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ACHAEMENID EAST — SATRAPIES AND TRIBUTE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Achaemenid empire extended into the Indus region. Herodotus
lists "India" (Indoi) among the tribute-bearing peoples; the
Behistun and later inscriptions place the eastern lands in imperial
geography. Pierre Briant:

> "The eastern satrapies—Gandhara, the Indus valley—were integrated
> into the tribute and administrative system. They supplied gold,
> timber, and manpower; they were Persian in the same sense that
> Egypt or Lydia were Persian—incorporated, not erased."
> — Pierre Briant, *From Cyrus to Alexander*

India is **imperial periphery**—valuable for resources and trade,
not the object of parity war. The pattern persists: Persia reaches
east through corridors; it does not seek to conquer the subcontinent.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. TRADE CORRIDOR — SILK ROAD AND INDIAN OCEAN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Silk Road and Indian Ocean linked Persia to India. Parthian and
Sasanian control of overland routes (MEM–PERSIA–PARTHIAN;
MEM–PERSIA–SILK–ROAD) made Persia the intermediary between Mediterranean
and Indian markets. The Indian Ocean (MEM–PERSIA–GEO–INDIAN–OCEAN) is
**peripheral access** for Persia—trade, not dominion. Al-Biruni
(11th c.), writing in Persianate context, composed a major work on
India (*Kitab fi tahqiq ma li-l-Hind*); Persianate scholarship
encoded India as a civilization to be studied and exchanged with,
not conquered. به نام خداوند جان و خرد — فردوسی، ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PERSIANATE INDIA — MUGHAL AND LATER
────────────────────────────────────────────
Mughal India drew on Persian language, administration, and court
culture. Persian was the language of elite and administration;
the flow of scholars, poets, and officials linked the plateau to
the subcontinent. Abbas Amanat:

> "Persianate culture in India was not Persian conquest; it was
> adoption by Indian rulers who found in Persian models a language
> of empire and refinement. The direction of influence was cultural
> and administrative, not military subjugation."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

India in this phase is **receiver and reworker** of Persian
civilizational forms—a different dynamic from Rome (peer rival) or
Arabia (conquest source).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MODERN PERIOD — NEIGHBOR WITHOUT PARITY WAR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
British India and later the Republic of India have shared borders
(or near-borders) and trade with Persia/Iran. There is no
Roman–Persian-style parity war; there is **strategic distance** and
economic and cultural exchange. India in contemporary Persian
encoding is a large neighbor, a market, and at times a diplomatic
counterweight—not an existential threat on the scale of northern
(Russian) or western (Roman historical) pressure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MEARSHEIMER ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
India is **eastern extent**: low structural pressure compared with
the western (Roman) or northern (Russian) fronts. The Hindu Kush
and geography limit large-scale invasion in either direction.
Persia's incentive is corridor control (trade) and cultural
influence, not territorial conquest of the subcontinent. Force
distribution favors other theaters; India remains a secondary
axis in Persian strategic memory.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. BARNES ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who paid for Persian presence in India? Tribute and trade flowed
both ways; cost was distributed. Who defected? Satrapies could
revolt (as in other peripheries), but there was no single
catastrophic defection that defined the relationship. Liability
for the eastern frontier was **lower stakes** than the western
(Rome) or southern (Arab conquest). India is **low-liability
periphery** in Persian memory.
