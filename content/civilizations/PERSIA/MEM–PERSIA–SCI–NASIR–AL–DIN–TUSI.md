I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Naṣīr al-Dīn Ṭūsī** as Persia's **reconstitutor
of knowledge after rupture**: founder of the Maragheh observatory under
the Ilkhanids, author of the *Treatise on the Quadrilateral* (plane
and spherical trigonometry), and architect of planetary models that
influenced later astronomy. Born in Tus, he served Ismaʿili and then
Mongol (Hülegü) patronage; his encoding is **institutional continuity
after catastrophe**—the Persianate template of court science surviving
and advancing under new sovereigns.

For Persian civilizational memory, Tusi represents **post-Mongol
reconstitution**: the observatory as institution, the scholar as
adviser to the conqueror, and the synthesis of Greek and Islamic
astronomy into a single framework. Contradictions preserved.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FORMATIVE CONDITIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Tus (Khurasan); studied in Nishapur, then with Ismaʿilis (Alamut)
• After Mongol capture of Alamut (1256), joined Hülegü; advised on
  astronomy and administration
• Maragheh observatory (from 1259); *Zīj-i Īlkhānī* (Ilkhanic tables);
  *Treatise on the Quadrilateral* (trigonometry); ethics (*Nasirean
  Ethics*); commentaries on Ibn Sina
• Died in Baghdad (or near); buried in Mashhad (Kadhimayn or Tus)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. RECONSTITUTION AFTER RUPTURE (MERCOURIS FRAME)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
It seems to me that the crucial point is that Tusi did not flee
knowledge—he **rebuilt** it under new masters. The Mongols destroyed
Alamut and Baghdad's caliphate; they did not destroy the Persianate
**grammar of court science**. The observatory at Maragheh was both
instrument and symbol: the Ilkhanid sovereign could claim to order
the heavens. Institutional continuity here is **adaptation**: the
scholar serves the conqueror and in return secures the institution.

> "I shall not die, these seeds I've sown will save my name and
> reputation from the grave, and men of sense and wisdom will proclaim,
> when I have gone, my praises and my fame."
> — Ferdowsi, *Shahnameh* (prologue), tr. Dick Davis; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

Tusi on the purpose of the observatory:

> "The construction of the observatory was undertaken so that the
> positions of the stars might be determined with the greatest
> accuracy and that the laws of the heavens might be known."
> — Naṣīr al-Dīn Ṭūsī (attributed; observatory context); subject verbatim tradition

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL FRAME
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ilkhanid patronage supplied land, labour, and authority; Tusi supplied
expertise and programme. The incentive structure favoured **legitimacy
through science**: the Mongol-derived dynasty could present itself as
restorer of order (calendar, prediction). No weaponisation; **astronomical
and mathematical** legacy; Copernicus later used Tusi's geometric device.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tusi served the conqueror; liability for Mongol destruction lies with
the sovereign, not the scholar. His defection from Ismaʿilis to
Hülegü was **institutional survival**: the observatory and the
transmission of knowledge. Irreversibility: Maragheh and the *Zīj*
entered the stream; Persianate science persisted past the conquest.
