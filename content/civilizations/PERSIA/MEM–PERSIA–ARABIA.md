I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Arabia** from the Persian perspective: the
peninsula that was once a named land in Achaemenid geography, a
tribal and client frontier under the Sasanians, and then the **source
of the conquest** that ended the Sasanian state—and that Persia
subsequently absorbed and Persianized (MEM–PERSIA–ISLAM).

Arabia in Persian memory carries a double valence: the periphery
that became, briefly, the center; and the Gulf's southern littoral—
fragmented, Arab, and structurally opposed to Persian northern-shore
dominance (MEM–PERSIA–GEO–PERSIAN–GULF).

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ACHAEMENID AND SASANIAN INTERFACE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Behistun inscription (Darius I) lists Arabia among the lands:

> "King Darius says: These are the countries which came to me; by the
> favor of Ahuramazda I was king of them: Persia, Elam, Babylonia,
> Assyria, Arabia, Egypt, Media, Armenia, Cappadocia, Sardis, Ionia..."
> — Darius I, Behistun Inscription (DB §6), tr. Roland G. Kent; ARC–PERSIA-T-ANCIENT

Arabia is **in the imperial geography**—tribute, corridor, tribal
interface. Under the Sasanians, the Lakhmid clients (al-Hira)
buffered Persia against Arab tribal incursions and Byzantine
proxies (Ghassanids). Touraj Daryaee:

> "The Sasanians relied on the Lakhmids to manage the desert frontier.
> When that client relationship fractured, the southern approach to
> Mesopotamia lay exposed."
> — Touraj Daryaee, *Sasanian Persia*

The peninsula was not conquered; it was **managed at the edge**.
When management failed—and when Roman–Persian exhaustion created
vacuum—Arabia became the source of invasion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE CONQUEST — ARABIA AS RUPTURE SOURCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Arab–Islamic conquests (7th c.) did not emerge from a strong
Persia. They filled the space left by mutual Roman–Persian exhaustion.
Anthony Kaldellis:

> "The Roman–Persian war had emptied both empires. When the Arab
> armies emerged from the peninsula, neither Constantinople nor
> Ctesiphon could muster the resources to resist."
> — Anthony Kaldellis, *The New Roman Empire*

In Persian encoding, Arabia is therefore the **periphery that moved
to the center**—not because the peninsula was stronger, but because
the two empires had spent each other. فَرْدوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

The conquest is rupture (Sasanian state falls) and the start of
absorption (Islam Persianized; MEM–PERSIA–ISLAM). Arabia delivers
the blow; Persia endures by reworking the new universalism.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. GULF DIMENSION — SOUTHERN LITTORAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Persian Gulf divides: **northern littoral Persian**, southern
littoral Arab (Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, UAE, Oman). Arabia in modern
Persian memory includes this southern shore—fragmented into states,
allied with extra-regional guarantors, and structurally in tension
with Iranian chokepoint control (MEM–PERSIA–GEO–PERSIAN–GULF). The
peninsula and the Gulf Arab states are not identical, but they share
the encoding: Arab, Sunni-weighted, and aligned against Persian
hegemony. Arabia is both the historic conquest source and the
contemporary southern shore.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MEARSHEIMER ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Arabia was a **low-cost periphery** until Roman–Persian exhaustion
lowered the cost of invasion. The structural lesson: the periphery
that you do not integrate can become the vector of your defeat when
the center is weak. The Gulf's southern littoral remains a
**fragmented counter-coast**—Persia holds the north and the strait;
Arab states plus external guarantors balance. Arabia (peninsula and
Gulf south) is the enduring southern flank.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. BARNES ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who paid for the conquest? The Sasanian state and the Roman East—
both exhausted. Who defected? Lakhmid and other clients; imperial
cohesion at the frontier failed. Liability attached to the center
that could no longer hold the periphery. In the modern period, who
is on the hook for Gulf tension? Both shores; defection (realignment
of a Gulf state) shifts the balance. Arabia is **shared liability**
and **shared frontier**.
