III. DUAL STRUCTURE: ELECTED AND GUARDIANSHIP
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The constitution creates **two tiers of authority**. Shaul Bakhash:

> "The constitution of the Islamic Republic created a dual structure
> of power: elected institutions—president, parliament—and
> non-elected institutions—Supreme Leader, Guardian Council,
> Assembly of Experts—that could override or veto the elected
> branch. The non-elected institutions derived their authority
> from the concept of velayat-e faqih."
> — Shaul Bakhash, *The Reign of the Ayatollahs* (1984); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

The Supreme Leader (vali-e faqih) holds ultimate authority over military, judiciary, and media; the Guardian Council vets legislation and candidates. The republic (president, Majles) operates **beneath** the guardianship. Roy Mottahedeh captures the fusion of learning and power:

> "The idea that the most learned interpreter of the law should
> lead the community had deep roots in Shi'ism, but Khomeini
> gave it a political reading that made the faqih the default
> ruler of the state."
> — Roy Mottahedeh, *The Mantle of the Prophet* (1985); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

This design **densified** after the revolution: war (1980–88), sanctions, and internal opposition were met with tighter clerical and security control, not liberalisation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. LEGITIMACY: REVOLUTION, RESISTANCE, DIGNITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Legitimacy rests on the revolution (overthrow of the Shah and dependency), on **resistance** to foreign pressure (sanctions, containment, "maximum pressure"), and on **dignity**—willingness to absorb pain for sovereignty. Ervand Abrahamian:

> "The regime has consistently framed itself as the defender of
> national independence and Islamic values against American
> and Israeli aggression. Sanctions and isolation are presented
> as proof of the West's refusal to accept a sovereign Iran."
> — Ervand Abrahamian, *A History of Modern Iran* (2008); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Prosperity is secondary; **survival and autonomy** are the primary legitimacy metrics (CORE axiom 8). The Shi'a memory engine (martyrdom → endurance → legitimacy → sovereignty) reinforces this. فَرْدوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد / کز این برتر اندیشه بر نگذرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. SIEGE ADAPTATION & ASYMMETRIC DOCTRINE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Persistent sanctions and military pressure have **increased** the regime's investment in sanctions-resistant circuits (energy, finance, logistics, compute) and in asymmetric capability (proxy networks, missiles, denial). Expansion is disfavoured; **cost-imposition, deniability, and multi-domain friction** dominate (CORE axiom 6). The state does not recover from shocks by returning to a prior equilibrium; it **densifies** (CORE axiom 3). Abbas Amanat on resilience:

> "The Islamic Republic has survived four decades of war, sanctions,
> and internal unrest. Its ability to adapt—through parallel
> institutions, regional alliances, and ideological reinforcement—has
> made it a durable, if contested, polity."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Internal contradictions (youth, economy, dissent) are **managed**, not resolved; regime survival remains the overriding logic.
