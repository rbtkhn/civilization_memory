I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Darius's Scythian expedition** from Persia's
perspective: the campaign that crossed the Bosporus and Danube to
punish the Scythians for their earlier incursions into Asia—and that
**failed** to force a decisive engagement. The Scythians refused battle;
they withdrew, burned the land, and harassed Persian columns. Darius
retreated. The expedition encodes the **limits of Persian power** against
nomadic denial—a structural lesson that prefigures Carrhae and later
encounters with steppe warfare.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. DARIUS'S MOTIVATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Herodotus records Darius's reason for the campaign:

> "After the taking of Babylon, Darius himself marched against the
> Scythians. For seeing that Asia abounded in men and that he gathered
> from it a great revenue, he became desirous of punishing the Scythians
> for the unprovoked wrong they had done him when they invaded Asia and
> defeated those who encountered them."  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, IV.1

The Scythians had once ruled "the upper country of Asia" for twenty-eight
years; they had invaded Media. Darius seeks vengeance and extension of
Persian authority northward.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE CAMPAIGN — NOMADIC DENIAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Darius crosses the Bosporus (bridge built by Mandrocles) and the Danube.
The Scythians do not offer pitched battle. They withdraw, scorch the
earth, and attack Persian foraging parties. Herodotus records the
Scythian message:

> "Why do you flee from us, Persian? ... If you think yourselves strong
> enough to withstand us, stand and fight; but if not, then acknowledge
> that we are the stronger and give us the tribute of earth and water."  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, IV.126 (paraphrase of Scythian taunt)

Darius cannot pin the Scythians down. He cannot live off the land.
He retreats. The expedition achieves nothing—save the lesson that
nomadic foes resist imperial logic.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL IMPLICATIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Scythian campaign demonstrates:
• Nomadic denial defeats conventional invasion—no cities to take, no
  army to destroy
• Persian logistics and numbers are irrelevant when the enemy refuses
  battle
• The northern frontier has different rules than Mesopotamia or the Levant

This lesson will echo at Carrhae (Parthian cavalry), against Central
Asian nomads, and in later Persian encounters with steppe warfare.
Persia learns: the north resists absorption.
