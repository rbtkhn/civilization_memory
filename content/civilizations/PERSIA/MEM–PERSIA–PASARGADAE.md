I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Pasargadae** as the first Achaemenid ceremonial
capital and the burial place of Cyrus II: the site in the heart of
Persis where Cyrus established his royal seat, the **Gate of All
Nations** (prototype for Persepolis), palace remains, and the tomb
that later conquerors—including Alexander—treated with respect.

Pasargadae is not the scale of Persepolis. It is the **origin point**:
Cyrus's personal capital, simpler and more intimate, encoding kingship
as restraint and legitimacy as continuity. The tomb survives; the
memory of Cyrus as founder anchors Persian sovereignty. Ferdowsi
encodes the durability of royal memory:

> "I shall not die, these seeds I've sown will save my name and
> reputation from the grave, and men of sense and wisdom will proclaim,
> when I have gone, my praises and my fame."
> — Ferdowsi, *Shahnameh* (prologue), tr. Dick Davis; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

توانا بود هر که دانا بود / ز دانش دل پیر برنا بود — فردوسی، شاهنامه (دیباچه)، ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

II. FOUNDATION — CYRUS'S ROYAL SEAT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cyrus founded Pasargadae after his consolidation of power, likely
following the defeat of Astyages of Media (c. 550 BC). The site lies
in the Murghab plain in Persis (Fars), the Persian homeland. Pierre
Briant situates the ideological function:

> "Pasargadae was Cyrus's answer to the need for a royal residence
> in the heart of the Persian land. It was not a city in the
> Mesopotamian sense but a royal complex—palaces, a gate, gardens—
> that expressed the new dynasty's legitimacy at its source."
> — Pierre Briant, *From Cyrus to Alexander*

The architecture is deliberately plural: columned halls show
Ionian and Lydian influence; the garden (paradeisos) prefigures
the Persian garden tradition. Cyrus's empire is already encoded
as **incorporation**—diverse styles at the center.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE TOMB OF CYRUS
────────────────────────────────────────────
Cyrus's tomb stands at Pasargadae: a gabled structure on a stepped
plinth, simple and enduring. Ancient sources (Arrian, Strabo) report
that the tomb bore an inscription—no longer extant—honoring Cyrus
and demanding respect from the viewer. The structure was spared by
Alexander (see below) and later venerated. Lloyd Llewellyn-Jones:

> "The tomb of Cyrus became a site of pilgrimage and legitimacy.
> Persian kings came to Pasargadae to pay homage to the founder.
> The message was clear: we rule because we continue his line and
> his covenant."
> — Lloyd Llewellyn-Jones, *Persians: The Age of the Great Kings* (2022)

The tomb is **legitimacy in stone**: the founder's body anchors
the dynasty; later rulers invoke Cyrus to validate their own
authority.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. GATE, PALACES, GARDENS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Gate of All Nations at Pasargadae (with the winged figure—
perhaps a genius or the king—and the inscription "I am Cyrus the
King, the Achaemenid") is the prototype for the grander gate at
Persepolis. Palace P and Palace S suggest audience and residential
functions; the garden layout (chahar bagh type) reflects cosmological
order. Matthew Canepa on Achaemenid royal space:

> "Pasargadae established the template: royal space as ordered
> landscape, architecture as ideology, the king at the center of
> a visible cosmos. Darius would expand the language at Persepolis;
> Cyrus invented it at Pasargadae."
> — Matthew Canepa, *The Two Eyes of the Earth*

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ALEXANDER AND THE TOMB — RESPECT FOR THE FOUNDER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
When Alexander took Persepolis (330 BC), he visited Pasargadae.
Sources (Arrian, *Anabasis* 6.29) report that Alexander found the
tomb desecrated and ordered it restored—honoring Cyrus as a great
king. The gesture encodes **conqueror paying homage to conquered
founder**: legitimacy of the Persian order acknowledged even in
destruction. The contrast with Persepolis (burned) is deliberate:
Cyrus's resting place was spared; Darius's ceremonial capital was
not. Memory of Cyrus as integrative, dignified ruler survived the
conquest.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LATER RECEPTION — SASANIAN AND BEYOND
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sasanian kings invoked Cyrus and Achaemenid sites (Ardashir, Shapur)
as restoration of Iranian kingship. Pasargadae, like Persepolis,
remained a legitimacy anchor—the place where the first King of Kings
lay. In the Islamic period, the tomb was sometimes identified with
the tomb of Solomon's mother; the site retained sacral charge. The
durability of Pasargadae as symbol outlasts dynasty and religion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. MEARSHEIMER ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pasargadae is a **foundational force node**: the first concentration
of Achaemenid royal authority in Persis. It is smaller and less
defensible than Persepolis would be; its significance is symbolic
and genealogical. Cyrus's choice of site—heartland, not frontier—
established the pattern: legitimacy flows from the Persian core.
Alexander's sparing of the tomb acknowledged that the founder's
memory had structural weight; destroying it would have cost more
legitimacy than it gained.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. BARNES ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who is on the hook at Pasargadae? The founder. Cyrus's tomb makes
him permanently present—every successor who invokes him accepts
the liability of living up to his standard. Defection from that
standard (tyranny, sacrilege) is measured against Cyrus. The site
is the **liability anchor** for dignified kingship: those who
honor it claim his legitimacy; those who desecrate it bear the
cost.

IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Pasargadae encodes Cyrus as founder; tomb = legitimacy anchor.
• First Achaemenid ceremonial complex; Persepolis continues the template.
• Alexander's restoration of the tomb = conqueror acknowledging Persian legitimacy.
• Later dynasties (Sasanian, Islamic-era local lore) reuse the site as sacred.
• Simpler than Persepolis; intimacy and origin over scale.
