I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Iraq** from the Persian perspective: the
modern state that occupies the core of Mesopotamia (MEM–PERSIA–GEO–MESOPOTAMIA)—
the Tigris–Euphrates corridor that linked the Iranian plateau to
the Levant under Achaemenid, Parthian, and Sasanian rule. Iraq is
thus **terrain and polity**: the fluvial lowland where Ctesiphon
stood, the Abbasid caliphate's seat (Baghdad), and the state that
**invaded Iran** in 1980 and fought an eight-year war (MEM–PERSIA–WAR–IRAN–IRAQ).
In Persian encoding, Iraq is the western neighbor that has been
both ancient heartland (Babylon, Ctesiphon) and hostile regime
(Ba'ath, Saddam)—the corridor that Persia once held and that
later held a rival capital, then an invading army.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. MESOPOTAMIA AS PERSIAN CORRIDOR — ANCIENT TO ISLAMIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The territory of modern Iraq was Achaemenid and Sasanian Mesopotamia:
Babylon (MEM–PERSIA–BABYLON), Seleucia, Ctesiphon. The Parthian and
Sasanian capitals lay in Mesopotamia; the Roman–Persian wars were
fought over this corridor (MEM–PERSIA–WAR–ROME; MEM–PERSIA–GEO–MESOPOTAMIA).
Islamic conquest (7th c.) transferred the region to the Caliphate;
Baghdad became the Abbasid capital. Persian (Safavid) and Ottoman
contest over Iraq continued the structural logic (MEM–PERSIA–OTTOMAN–EMPIRE).
Iraq in this long view is **the western approach**—whoever holds it
commands the hinge between plateau and Levant. فردوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. MODERN IRAQ — STATE AND RIVAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The modern state of Iraq (from British mandate, 1920; independence
1932) inherited the Mesopotamian core. Under the Ba'ath (1968–2003),
Iraq was a **Sunni-dominated, Arab-nationalist** rival to the
Islamic Republic. The Shatt al-Arab waterway and border were
disputed. In 1980 Iraq invaded Iran, opening the Iran–Iraq War.
Abbas Amanat:

> "Iraq under Saddam Hussein was the frontline of the Arab and
> Western effort to contain the Iranian revolution. The Iran–Iraq
> War was in part a war over the Shatt al-Arab and regional
> hegemony—but for Iran it was encoded as defense of sovereignty
> against an aggressor backed by the same powers that had imposed
> 1953."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

Iraq in Persian memory is therefore **invader and neighbor**—the
state that attacked in 1980, was repelled, and with which Iran
fought to a bloody stalemate. Post-2003 (US invasion, fall of
Saddam), Iraq became a contested space: Iranian influence expanded
through Shi'a political actors and militia links; the corridor
remains strategic.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL FRAME — CORRIDOR AND BORDER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
From a structural perspective, Iraq occupies the **approach corridor**
to the Iranian plateau from the west. Control of Iraq (or of a
friendly regime in Baghdad) secures the western flank; a hostile
Iraq (Ba'ath, or a Sunni-dominated order) projects threat. The
Iran–Iraq War was a contest over that border and over regional
primacy. Neither side achieved decisive victory; the border
stabilized at roughly status quo ante. Post-2003, Iran's ability
to project influence into Iraq (political, militia, economic)
increased—the corridor is no longer held by a single hostile
state but by a fractured polity in which Iran has leverage.
MEM–PERSIA–SYRIA encodes the "axis of resistance" (Iran–Syria–
Hezbollah); Iraq, in the postwar order, fits into a broader
regional calculus.

V. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Iraq is the modern state in the Mesopotamian corridor—ancient Persian heartland, later caliphal and Ottoman.
• Iran–Iraq War: Iraq as invader (1980); stalemate; Shatt al-Arab and border contested.
• Ba'ath Iraq: Sunni-dominated rival; Western-backed in Persian encoding.
• Post-2003: Iraq fractured; Iranian influence via Shi'a politics and militias; corridor remains strategic.
• Iraq is both territorial neighbor and legitimacy foil—defense against Iraq encoded as defense of revolution.
