I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Ionian Revolt** from Persia's perspective: the
rebellion of Greek cities in Ionia (western Anatolia) against Persian
satraps, supported by Athens and Eretria, which culminated in the
sacking of Sardis and the burning of the temple of Cybele. Darius
suppresses the revolt; the Athenian role triggers the Marathon expedition.
The revolt encodes **satrapal governance limits**—and the costs of Greek
intervention in Persian affairs.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. REVOLT ORIGINS — ARISTAGORAS AND SARDIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Aristagoras of Miletus leads the revolt. Herodotus records Athenian
involvement:

> "The Athenians had sent twenty ships to aid the Ionians... and having
> disembarked, the Athenians and the rest of the allies were induced by
> Aristagoras to march against Sardis... they took the city and besieged
> the acropolis, where sat the satrap Artaphernes with a garrison."  
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, V.99–100

The Greeks take Sardis—but accidentally burn it. The fire destroys
the city; the Ionians withdraw. The revolt spreads; Persian response
is methodical.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PERSIAN SUPPRESSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Darius deploys land and sea forces. The revolt is crushed: Miletus
falls (494 BC); the Persian fleet defeats the Ionian navy at Lade.
Persian authority is restored. The satrapies return to order.

The revolt demonstrates that Persian governance can be challenged—
but that suppression follows. The cost is high; the outcome is imperial
consolidation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. MARATHON AS CONSEQUENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Athens had burned Sardis. Darius does not forget. The Marathon
expedition (490 BC) is the punitive response. MEM–PERSIA–WAR–MARATHON
documents the battle; the Ionian Revolt is its cause. Persian memory
links Athenian insolence (supporting rebels, burning a royal city) to
the need for discipline.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Ionian Revolt encodes:
• Satrapal friction: Greek cities under Persian rule can rebel
• Athenian provocation: support for rebels invites Persian retaliation
• Imperial resilience: revolt is suppressed; order is restored
• Causal chain: Ionian Revolt → Marathon → Xerxes' invasion
