I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Oman** from the Persian perspective: the
southern littoral state that **shares the Strait of Hormuz**—Oman's
Musandam peninsula forms the southern side of the chokepoint while
Iran holds the north (MEM–PERSIA–GEO–PERSIAN–GULF). Oman is therefore
structurally distinct among Gulf Arab states: not only a GCC member
but the **co-guardian of the strait** with Iran, and a maritime
partner in the Indian Ocean corridor (MEM–PERSIA–GEO–INDIAN–OCEAN).

Oman in Persian memory is trade route, occasional rival (Omani naval
power in the Gulf in the 17th–19th c.), and in the modern period
a diplomatic channel—often more neutral toward Iran than Saudi or
UAE. The Gulf of Oman is the water body between the two; the strait
is where both meet.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHY — STRAIT AND GULF OF OMAN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Strait of Hormuz connects the Persian Gulf to the Gulf of Oman;
the Gulf of Oman opens into the Arabian Sea and Indian Ocean. Oman
borders both the Gulf of Oman and, via Musandam, the strait. Strabo
defines the eastern limit of Arabia by the Persian Gulf:

> "The northern side of Arabia Felix is formed by the above-mentioned
> desert, the eastern by the Persian Gulf, the western by the Arabian
> Gulf, and the southern by the great sea that lies outside both gulfs,
> which as a whole is called Erythra."
> — Strabo, *Geographica*, Book XVI.3

Oman lies on the Arabian side of that eastern boundary. From the
Persian view, Oman is the **southern shore of the egress**—whoever
holds Musandam shares physical control of the chokepoint. Iran holds
the north; Oman holds the south. Neither can close the strait without
affecting the other. به نام خداوند جان و خرد — فردوسی، ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. MARITIME TRADE — SIRAF AND OMAN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Persian and Omani sailors have plied the Gulf–Indian Ocean route
for centuries. Al-Biruni records the shared maritime tradition:

> "The sea route from the Persian coast to India follows the monsoon.
> The sailors of Siraf and Oman have plied this route for generations,
> and the kings of Persia have always had an interest in the trade
> that passes through the Gulf to the Indian Sea."
> — Al-Biruni, *Kitab al-Hind* (India), tr. E.C. Sachau; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

Siraf (Persian Gulf coast) and Oman are **linked in the trade
corridor**—not as one polity but as the two littorals that frame
the strait and the route to India. Oman in this encoding is
maritime partner and transit node, not conquest target.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. HISTORIC PHASES — COOPERATION AND RIVALRY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Under the Sasanians, the southern shore (including Oman) was part
of the broader Arabian interface—tribal, client, and trade. After
the Arab–Islamic conquests, Oman became Ibadi Muslim and developed
a distinct identity. In the early modern period, Portuguese then
Omani naval power contested the Gulf; the Omani empire (Zanzibar,
East African coast) made Oman a regional maritime power. Persia
(Safavid, Qajar) and Oman at times cooperated (trade) and at times
clashed (control of Gulf ports, Hormuz island). Richard N. Frye
notes the enduring pattern:

> "The Persian and Omani coasts have always been the two sides of
> the same maritime system. Trade and conflict alternated; neither
> could ignore the other."
> — Richard N. Frye, *The Heritage of Persia*

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MODERN PERIOD — NEUTRALITY AND CHANNEL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In the 20th–21st c., Oman has often maintained a more neutral stance
toward Iran than Saudi Arabia or the UAE—no formal rupture, back-channel
diplomacy (e.g. US–Iran talks hosted in Oman), and a shared interest
in strait stability. Oman does not join Saudi-led confrontations
against Iran to the same degree; it remains a **strait partner** with
an incentive to avoid escalation that would close the waterway. From
the Persian view, Oman is the southern littoral state with which
pragmatic engagement is possible—not an ally, but not a frontline
adversary either.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MEARSHEIMER ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Oman is **structural co-holder of the strait**: geography gives
Oman (Musandam) and Iran (northern shore) shared interest in the
chokepoint. Closure harms both; transit benefits both. Oman's
incentive is to avoid being drawn into a US– or Saudi-led campaign
that would make the strait a war zone. Iran's incentive is to
differentiate Oman from more hostile GCC states—divide the southern
littoral. Oman is the **pivot** on the south: the state that can
tilt toward or away from anti-Iran alignment.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. BARNES ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who is on the hook in the strait? Both sides. If Iran closes or
harasses transit, Oman's economy and reputation suffer; if Oman
hosts hostile forces or joins a blockade, Iran responds. Liability
is **shared**—Oman cannot escape the geography. Defection (Oman
tilting firmly toward Saudi/UAE camp) would shift the southern
balance; Oman's neutrality is an asset that both Iran and Western
powers have an interest in preserving or undermining depending on
calculus. The sultanate bears the liability of being the strait's
southern guardian.
