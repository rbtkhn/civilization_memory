I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Maryam Mirzakhani** as Persia's **modern
mathematical voice**: the first woman and first Iranian to win the
Fields Medal (2014), for work on the dynamics and geometry of moduli
spaces of Riemann surfaces. Born in Tehran, educated at Sharif and
Harvard, she was professor at Stanford until her early death. Her
encoding is **international recognition under constraint**—Iranian
science and diaspora; siege and sanction; and the persistence of
excellence in pure mathematics.

For Persian civilizational memory, Mirzakhani represents **continuity
of the scientific strand** into the Islamic Republic era: the
civilization that produced al-Biruni, Avicenna, and Khayyam also
produces, under very different conditions, a mathematician honoured
globally. Contradictions preserved.

III. RECOGNITION AND CONSTRAINT (MERCOURIS FRAME)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
It seems to me that the crucial point is that Mirzakhani achieved
**global recognition** while Iran was under sanction and while she
worked in the diaspora. The Fields Medal was not awarded for
applications—it was for pure mathematics: moduli spaces, dynamics,
geometry. Persianate legitimacy here is **persistence of the
epistemic strand**: the civilization that valued precision and
system (al-Biruni, Khayyam, Tusi) still produces mathematicians who
are seen as best in the world. The encoding is dual: pride (Iranian
first) and tension (diaspora, sanction, early death).

> "I shall not die, these seeds I've sown will save my name and
> reputation from the grave, and men of sense and wisdom will proclaim,
> when I have gone, my praises and my fame."
> — Ferdowsi, *Shahnameh* (prologue), tr. Dick Davis; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

Mirzakhani on doing mathematics:

> "The beauty of mathematics only shows itself to more patient
> followers. Most of us are not trained to see the beauty of
> mathematics. I think it's like doing a puzzle and putting things
> together. There are different ways the same proof can be viewed."
> — Maryam Mirzakhani (interview, 2014); subject verbatim
