I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Nima Yushij** as the **founder of modern
Persian poetry** (she'r-e now)—the poet who broke the dominance
of classical metre and rhyme and made free verse and
conversational rhythm legitimate in Persian. Nima is not a
politician. He is **legitimacy through form**: the figure who
proved that Persian poetry could enter the twentieth century
without abandoning Persian language or subject matter, and
who thereby gave later poets (Ahmad Shamlou, Forough Farrokhzad,
and others) a new grammar.

In Persian civilizational memory, Nima is the **poet who
changed the rules**—not by rejecting tradition but by
extending it. He wrote in the constitutional and early
Pahlavi period; his influence peaked after his death when
she'r-e now became the dominant mode of serious Persian
poetry.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT & FORM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nima was born in Yush (Mazandaran), educated in Tehran, and
lived through the Constitutional Revolution, Reza Shah's
centralisation, and the early Mohammad Reza period. Classical
Persian poetry (Ferdowsi, Hafez, Saadi) had fixed metres
(aruz) and rhyme schemes. Nima introduced **variable line
length**, **natural speech rhythm**, and **everyday imagery**
while keeping Persian as the medium and Iranian landscape
and society as the matter.

Structural conditions:
• Constitutional and Pahlavi era: modernisation, centralisation, tension between tradition and change
• Persian poetry as the pre-eminent literary form; the novel (Hedayat) emerging in parallel
• Nima's break: formal, not ideological—new music, not new language
• Posthumous influence: the "Nima school" and she'r-e now as the mainstream of twentieth-century Persian poetry

Nima encodes **tradition as extensible**—the past is not erased but the form is freed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CORE INNOVATION — SHE'R-E NOW (NEW POETRY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nima's poems—e.g. "Afsaneh" (Legend), "Mahtab" (Moonlight),
"Quqnoos" (Phoenix)—use irregular lines, run-on sentences,
and imagery drawn from nature and village life. The effect
is closer to speech and to the rhythm of modern experience
than the classical qasida or ghazal. Abbas Amanat:

> "Nima Yushij did for Persian poetry what the Constitutional
> Revolution did for Iranian politics—he opened the door to
> new possibilities without destroying what came before. His
> poetry remained Persian in language and often in theme;
> what changed was the metre and the ear."
> — Abbas Amanat, *Iran: A Modern History* (2017); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

فردوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL (indigenous-language requirement).

Nima did not achieve Tagore-level international fame in his lifetime. His **civilizational function** was internal: he gave Persian poetry a way to be modern without ceasing to be Persian.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nima establishes:
• **She'r-e now as legitimate**—free verse and variable metre enter the Persian canon
• **Poetry as the form that could modernise first**—before the novel, Nima changed the dominant literary art
• **Continuity through extension**—contrast with Hedayat's fracture; Nima extends the line of Persian poetry rather than breaking it
• **Bridge between constitutional and Pahlavi**—he wrote across the 1920s–1950s; his influence shaped the next two generations

He functions as **Persia's poetic moderniser**—the one who made new form acceptable and who enabled the great postwar and post-revolution poets to write in his wake.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. RELATION TO FERDOWSI AND HEDAYAT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• **Ferdowsi (MEM–PERSIA–LIT–FERDOWSI):** Carries royal memory; classical metre and epic. Nima breaks that metre for new content—not to erase Ferdowsi but to add a new branch.
• **Hedayat (MEM–PERSIA–LIT–HEDAYAT):** Same era; prose (novel) vs poetry. Hedayat = fracture and alienation; Nima = formal extension and new rhythm. Both are modernisers; different arts, different temperaments.

Nima does not lead a political movement—he **legitimises** the claim that Persian poetry can speak in a new form to a new time.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LIMITS & RECEPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Limited international reception in his lifetime—unlike Tagore (Nobel) or Hedayat (The Blind Owl translated early). Nima's influence was first domestic.
• Reception in Iran: post-1950s she'r-e now became dominant; Nima revered as founding figure; later poets (Shamlou, Farrokhzad) acknowledged his break.
• Under the IRI: modern poetry (including Nima's legacy) has been both celebrated and at times constrained—poetry as potential counter-voice.

Nima fills the gap between **classical form and modern experience**—he does not abandon the past; he gives it a new metre.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Founder of she'r-e now; broke classical metre; variable line, natural rhythm.
• Tradition as extensible—new form, same language; Persian poetry enters twentieth century.
• Bridge: constitutional and Pahlavi; influence peaked after his death.
• Contrast with Ferdowsi: new branch, not replacement; with Hedayat: poetry vs prose, extension vs fracture.
• Legitimacy through form; enabled Shamlou, Farrokhzad, and the mainstream of modern Persian poetry.
