I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Roman Empire** from the Persian perspective:
the western imperial counterpart with whom Persia fought, traded, and
shared a seven-century parity—the power that could not be permanently
destroyed and that did not permanently destroy Persia.

Rome in Persian memory is not a barbarian periphery. It is the **only
true peer**: the rival whose legitimacy and endurance validated Persian
sovereignty in turn. The relationship encodes trade as well as war,
mutual respect as well as humiliation, and exhaustion as the shared
limit.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ROME AS COUNTERPART — IMPERIAL SYMMETRY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Persia and Rome confront one another as near-equals. Shared traits:
imperial administration, professional armies, tax-based logistics,
ideological legitimacy (law and citizenship vs sacral kingship).
Structural asymmetry: Rome seeks expansion and finality; Persia seeks
balance, denial, and endurance (MEM–PERSIA–WAR–ROME).

Adrian Goldsworthy frames the outcome:

> "Despite their endless clashes, trade between the empires enriched
> them both, and a mutual respect prevented both Rome and Persia from
> permanently destroying the other."
> — Adrian Goldsworthy, *Rome and Persia: The Seven Hundred Year Rivalry* (2023); book description

The war has no terminal victory condition. Neither empire absorbs the
other; both are eventually exhausted and overrun by a third actor
(Arab conquests). In Persian encoding, Rome is therefore the **permanent
pressure rival** that validates siege–compression doctrine—the opponent
worthy of seven centuries of contest.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PERSIANATE ENCODING — RŪM AND THE CHRONICLE TRADITION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Persian and Persianate sources encode Rome as **Rūm** (Romans/Byzantines).
The chronicle tradition records the rhythm of war and reversal, not
moral hierarchy. Al-Tabari's *Ta'rikh* encodes the pattern from the
Persianate perspective:

> "The Persians and the Romans fought many wars. When the king of
> kings was victorious he would take the Roman lands and cities; when
> the Romans prevailed they would recover their territory. Thus it
> continued until the rise of Islam."
> — Al-Tabari, *Ta'rikh al-rusul wa'l-muluk*, tr. C.E. Bosworth; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

فردوسی (شاهنامه، دیباچه): به نام خداوند جان و خرد / کز این برتر اندیشه بر نگذرد — ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL.

Rome is not demonized; it is **the other empire**—alternately victor
and vanquished. Legitimacy contest (e.g. Parthian rejection of
Roman-backed Vonones; Tacitus, *Annals* II.2) reinforces that Persian
kingship refuses to be a Roman province. The Roman view of Persia
mirrors the tension: both sides acknowledge a peer.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. LEGITIMACY CONTEST — NOT CONQUEST ALONE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Roman–Persian parity is not merely military. It is **legitimacy contest**.
Parthia refuses Roman-backed kings (Vonones); Sasanians claim King of
Kings against Roman emperors. Valerian's capture (260) validates
Sasanian khvarenah; Heraclius's reversal (627–628) restores Roman
prestige. The True Cross, Jerusalem, Antioch, Ctesiphon—symbols and
cities pass back and forth. Neither side achieves closure.

Edward Gibbon, from the Roman side, records the humiliation that
Persian memory inverts as triumph:

> "The pride of Rome was humbled to the dust; and the victorious
> monarch of Persia received the submission of a Roman emperor with
> the same haughty indifference with which he would have accepted the
> homage of the meanest of his subjects."
> — Edward Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*, Vol. I, ch. 10 (on Valerian and Shapur)

From the Persian view, Rome is the rival whose submission (when it
occurs) confirms divine favor; whose resilience (when it reverses)
confirms that the contest has no end.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. TRADE, EXHAUSTION, AND THIRD ACTORS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trade between the empires enriched both. The Silk Road and Levantine
corridors linked Roman and Persian commercial and diplomatic worlds.
Mutual destruction was never the sole logic; respect and interest
coexisted with war (Goldsworthy).

Exhaustion is the shared limit. Khosrow II's overextension (Syria,
Palestine, Egypt) and Heraclius's counterstroke exhaust both empires;
within a decade, Arab armies overrun both. Anthony Kaldellis frames
the structural outcome:

> "The Roman–Persian war had emptied both empires. When the Arab
> armies emerged from the peninsula, neither Constantinople nor
> Ctesiphon could muster the resources to resist."
> — Anthony Kaldellis, *The New Roman Empire*

Rome in Persian memory thus carries a double valence: the peer that
validated Persian sovereignty for seven centuries, and the co-exhausted
rival that, with Persia, made way for the next universalism (Islam).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Rome is Persia's only true imperial peer in the classical encoding.
• Parity is legitimacy contest as well as military balance; neither absorbs the other.
• Trade and mutual respect coexist with endless clashes; neither permanently destroys the other.
• Persianate chronicles (e.g. Al-Tabari) encode Rome (Rūm) as the alternating victor/vanquished, not as moral inferior.
• Exhaustion is the shared limit; third actors (Palmyra, then Arabs) fill the vacuum when both are spent.
• Rome validates Persia's siege–compression doctrine—the permanent pressure rival.
