I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **constitutional law** as Persia's **written
limitation and structure of sovereignty**: the 1906–1907 Fundamental
Law and Supplementary Fundamental Law (Mashruta—Constitutional
Revolution) and the 1979 Constitution of the Islamic Republic (as
amended 1989). Two founding moments—one limiting the Qajar monarchy
through a representative assembly and one establishing the dual
structure of republic and guardianship (velayat-e faqih). Encoding:
**law as constraint and as source of legitimacy**—who has jurisdiction,
who may veto, what is irreversible.

Contradictions preserved: 1906 was suspended and battered (1941, 1953);
1979 entrenches a different sovereignty (faqih over parliament). Both
remain in civilizational memory as constitutional.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. 1906 — MASHRUTA (CONSTITUTIONAL REVOLUTION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Qajar dynasty; pressure from clerics, merchants, and reformers for
  a written constitution and a national consultative assembly (Majles)
• December 1906: Muzaffar al-Din Shah signs the Fundamental Law;
  October 1907: Supplementary Fundamental Law (rights, royal veto,
  role of Shi'a jurists)
• Majles-e Shura-ye Melli (National Consultative Assembly); limits on
  royal power; budget and law required assembly approval
• Reversed in practice after 1908 (bombardment of Majles); restored
  after 1909; weakened by 1925 (Pahlavi coup) and 1953 (coup against
  Mossadegh). The **precedent** of written constraint and assembly
  remained; legitimacy of "constitution" entered the grammar.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. 1979 — CONSTITUTION OF THE ISLAMIC REPUBLIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Drafted by Assembly of Experts after the revolution; approved by
  referendum December 1979; amended 1989 (e.g. succession, executive)
• Establishes: velayat-e faqih (Rule of the Jurist); Supreme Leader;
  Guardian Council (veto on legislation and candidates); President;
  Majles; Assembly of Experts; judiciary
• Sovereignty: God; the people exercise it within the framework of
  Islam; the faqih (Supreme Leader) holds ultimate authority
• Dual structure: elected (president, Majles) and guardianship (Leader,
  Guardian Council, Experts). Law must conform to sharia and to the
  constitution; the Council decides conformity.

Shaul Bakhash:

> "The constitution of the Islamic Republic created a dual structure
> of power: elected institutions—president, parliament—and
> non-elected institutions—Supreme Leader, Guardian Council,
> Assembly of Experts—that could override or veto the elected
> branch."
> — Shaul Bakhash, *The Reign of the Ayatollahs* (1984); ARC–PERSIA-T-MODERN

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. LEGITIMACY AND CONSTRAINT (MERCOURIS FRAME)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The crucial point is that both constitutions encode **legitimacy
through procedure**: 1906 by limiting the king through a written
document and an assembly; 1979 by grounding the state in a text that
fuses popular mandate and guardianship. Neither is "law above the
sovereign" in a liberal sense—1906 left the shah as sovereign with
constrained powers; 1979 places the faqih above the elected branch.
Institutional continuity: the **idea** of a constitution as the
source of political order persists across the two moments; the
**content** differs sharply.

> "I shall not die, these seeds I've sown will save my name and
> reputation from the grave, and men of sense and wisdom will proclaim,
> when I have gone, my praises and my fame."
> — Ferdowsi, *Shahnameh* (prologue), tr. Dick Davis; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. STRUCTURAL FRAME
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1906: fiscal and military weakness (Qajar) plus elite demand produced
a concession—the crown accepted a written limit in exchange for
revenue and legitimacy. 1979: revolution produced a new sovereign
(the umma/faqih); the constitution **codified** the victory and
institutionalised the dual structure. Incentive structure: in 1906,
the assembly was the constraint; in 1979, the Guardian Council and
Leader are the constraint on the assembly.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: 1906—the Shah and the Majles; who was on the hook when
the constitution was suspended? The crown (reversing) and the
assembly (dissolved). 1979—the Supreme Leader, Guardian Council,
President, Majles; legislation that fails the Council's sharia check
is void; candidates the Council rejects cannot run. Irreversibility:
1906 was repeatedly breached but the **precedent** of constitutionalism
fueled 1979; 1979 constitution has not been replaced—amendments (1989)
extended it. Liability for breach in 1906 lay with the crown and the
military; in 1979, compliance is enforced by the guardianship
institutions.
