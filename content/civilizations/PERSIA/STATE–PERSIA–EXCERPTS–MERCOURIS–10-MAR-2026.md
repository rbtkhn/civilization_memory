## I. Selected Excerpts

### 1. Contradictory signals / off-ramp vs escalation

> Well, over the last 24 hours we have seen a whole spate of contradictory messages and signals coming from the President of the United States, Donald Trump, and perhaps from members of his administration, which cumulatively do seem to suggest growing doubts about the progress of the military operation against Iran and perhaps the first steps towards seeking some kind of offramp. One needs to be extremely careful what one says about all of this. However, because one also gets messages which still seem to imply a decision to seek further escalation. Of course, the two are not necessarily in contradiction. It might be the case that the president and his officials have begun to understand that the situation is not going according to plan at all. That they are looking for an offramp. They may sense the political and economic damage that the war is causing. But as many people who find themselves in that kind of situation often do, they respond at the same time by escalating by attempting to change the situation to their advantage even as they try to look for a way out.

### 2. Trump initiated call to Putin — "came begging"

> The Russians are saying clearly without any ambiguity, without any doubt that it was Trump who called Putin. It was Trump who initiated the call and called Putin. I've discussed in many programs how in a readout of telephone conversations between governments when a government confirms that it was the other side that sought the call, what the government that publishes the fact is telling us is that the government that initiated the call came begging and that clearly is what happened in this call between Trump and Putin. Trump called Putin because he wants Putin's help.

### 3. Ushakov: "frank" and "businesslike" — contentious call

> I would note at the outset that the conversation was business-like, frank, and constructive… Business like Frank. No words here about the conversation being sincere or warm… Instead, we have businesslike and frank. And as I and several others, notably Larry Johnson, have pointed out, the word frank can often be taken to mean in Russian readouts and descriptions of these kind of conversations that the conversation was contentious, perhaps even heated at times. Well, I would say that in relation to this particular conversation, referring to the meeting as frank and businesslike rather does suggest that this was a contentious call altogether. There were clearly disagreements and push back and perhaps argument.

### 4. WSJ and Israeli officials — war has to end

> We've had reports from the Wall Street Journal that there are officials within the US administration who are now advising the president that this has gone as far as it can go and that the war needs to end. And interestingly, there have been identical reports in Israel itself that there are Israeli officials who are worrying about whether an open-ended war comes with disastrous consequences for the global economy, but also perhaps more pressingly for Donald Trump's political position in the United States. The calculation apparently being that Israel cannot afford the political implosion of a US president because he stood by and supported Israel.

### 5. Putin as sole plausible mediator; terms "far short of Trump's objectives"

> The gulf between the two sides is enormous. But all I will say is if there is any individual who might be in a position to put the pieces back together then it is Vladimir Putin. I cannot at this time think of anyone else.

> So Putin will have told Trump… I am in touch with the Iranians. I am in touch with the Arab Gulf states. I am in a position to mediate between you and them. And I have ideas about how this conflict can be ended. If you are looking for an off-ramp… I might be able to provide it. … [But] it will have to be on terms that fall far short of Trump's objectives, regime change in Iran, and the total disarmament of Iran.

### 6. Hormuz unblock — US will not attempt

> For the record, I think that any operation to unblock the Straits of Hormuz, whilst the war is ongoing, is so dangerous, so high risk that I don't personally think the United States is going to attempt it at all. I've seen all sorts of reports and claims about this, but frankly, I don't think it's going to happen. Just saying.

### 7. Gulf storage filling; Aramco "catastrophic situation"; no decisive fix

> Persian Gulf producers, the Gulf States are increasingly under increasing pressure. Their storage tanks are becoming filled. Their ability to export oil is reducing. Bahrain yesterday followed Qatar by declaring force majeure. Sooner or later the other Gulf states are bound to do the same. And today Saudi Aramco… talked about a catastrophic situation in global energy markets if the war continued for much longer and if the straits of Hormuz remained closed. … [A] 30% reduction in Saudi export of oil combined with a stop in exports by Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, and the UAE remains a catastrophic decline.

> Having said that, declarations that the war will be short, talk about releasing oil from reserves, all of those things can only have an effect for a short time. Somehow or other, in some way or other, something more decisive needs to happen in order to turn the situation around.

### 8. Sanctions on Russian oil — quid pro quo for off-ramp; "effective end of project Ukraine"

> President Trump and his officials have now been talking about relaxing, some say ending all sanctions on Russia's oil export trade, understanding that this oil export trade is essential for general stability in global energy prices, which is in turn essential for the long-term stability of the US economy. … If the United States does lift the sanctions, the oil sanctions on Russia, then it is the effective end of project Ukraine. The whole premise of the sanctions war as the means to get the Russians to change their policy will have gone. … By the way, I am absolutely sure that Vladimir Putin at no point over the course of yesterday's discussion would have suggested to Trump anything like this. The Russians have a consistent policy… they never bring up the topic of sanctions themselves; they only respond when their western interlocutors do so.

## II. STATE–PERSIA Analysis

### A. Material options and discriminators

**Option A (Denial and endurance)**  
Excerpts 6 and 7 support the view that US closure capacity is lacking: Hormuz cannot be forced open at acceptable risk; Gulf storage filling and force majeure (Bahrain, Qatar) show time and geography favour the defender. Excerpt 7’s “something more decisive needs to happen” underlines that declaratory moves (short war, reserve release) do not substitute for a change in the military or political situation — consistent with Option A’s binding constraint (external pressure degrades). Iran’s calibrated denial (Hormuz closed for US/Israel/allies, partial for others) remains in line with DOCTRINE 01; no need to shift to Option C on the basis of this program.

**Option B (Exit-building and negotiated settlement)**  
Excerpts 2, 4, 5 and 8 bear on the settlement track. Trump initiating the call to Putin is treated as a signal that the US is seeking an off-ramp (Excerpt 2) — consistent with M–J 1 (material stress and political cost driving a search for de-escalation). WSJ and Israeli reports (Excerpt 4) that officials advise ending the war and that Israel fears Trump’s political implosion support the same reading and are **DR-8-adjacent** (internal recognition that the war cannot be won or sustained as is). Putin as the only plausible mediator (Excerpt 5) and “considerations” aimed at a “prompt political and diplomatic settlement” point to an **Iran–Gulf–Russia** channel already reflected in Pattern 12 and Gulf buffer logic. The **binding constraint** for Option B remains: settlement would be “on terms that fall far short of Trump’s objectives” (no regime change, no total disarmament), and it is “far from clear either that the Iranians are prepared for an exit ramp or that Donald Trump and the people around him” are prepared to take it. So the program supports **existence of a settlement path** (Russia, Gulf contacts) but **non-closure** on whether either side will take it.

**Option C (Escalation and cost-imposition)**  
Excerpt 1 keeps Option C in the picture: off-ramp search and escalation can coexist (“they respond at the same time by escalating”). The program does not treat Iran as having crossed into Khosrow-band overextension; Hormuz remains partially closed and calibrated. So Option C is **watch** (retaliation level, Houthis, GT11-4), not a shift of assessment.

### B. Pattern and signal alignment

- **Pattern 12 (War-phase signal check):** Excerpts 2, 4, 5 update the **ceasefire/negotiation channel**: US is not formally “engaging” the Iran–Gulf–Russia track in public, but Trump’s call to Putin is a high-level move toward that track. **Coalition cohesion**: Israeli worry about Trump’s domestic position (Excerpt 4) is a fracture signal (Israel has an interest in war termination, not only escalation). **Russia/China posture**: Russia is in a brokering role; “considerations” and “no agreements were reached” (Excerpt 5) fit **bounded but active** — mediation offered, no material military escalation reported.
- **M–J 1 (Off-ramp / material pressure):** The 4-week window from 4 Mar (Qatar force majeure, Brent >$100) is the discriminator. Trump calling Putin and talk of easing Russian oil sanctions (Excerpt 8) are **potential steps** toward de-escalation or market stabilisation; they do not yet constitute the observable “clear step toward ceasefire or de-escalation” that would satisfy M–J 1. Re-run when: (a) US or Israel publicly offers talks or restrains posture, or (b) window closes without such a step.
- **DR-8 (US admission of no victory path):** WSJ “officials advising the president that this has gone as far as it can go and that the war needs to end” (Excerpt 4) is **DR-8-adjacent** (internal advice that the war should end). DR-8 strict is a **public** senior US statement that the US cannot achieve military victory or has no path to war aims. So: **watch** for any on-record or attributable senior US figure saying the war cannot be won or must end.
- **Gulf buffer logic / M–J 4 (Pivot vs collapse):** Bahrain and Qatar force majeure (Excerpt 7); Aramco “catastrophic situation” and 70% Red Sea diversion (30% Saudi export cut) support **material stress** and **Gulf under pressure**. Russia in touch with Gulf leaders (Excerpt 5) and offering mediation fits a **pivot** path (Gulf–Iran–Russia security arrangement) rather than collapse, but the program does not claim any Gulf state has yet committed to that. Re-run on Gulf state statement or action.
- **Exhaustion and external shock:** “Something more decisive needs to happen” (Excerpt 7) and Putin as sole plausible mediator (Excerpt 5) fit the **exhaustion timeline** (neither side has closure; saturation, not victory) and **external shock** (Russia as potential broker). External shock observables (60–90 day) in Section VII — Russia brokered settlement with both sides accepting Russia’s role — are the discriminator; this program supports **watch**, not trigger.

### C. Cross-entity (Russia, Ukraine)

Excerpt 8 (sanctions on Russian oil) ties the Iran war to **Russia’s leverage** and **Ukraine**: US discussion of relaxing or ending oil sanctions is framed as quid pro quo for Russian help on an Iran off-ramp and as “effective end of project Ukraine” if implemented. That fits **Pattern 10** (equilibrium and forced choice): US need for Russian help in the Gulf does not force a Russia–Iran binary yet, but it increases the chance of US concessions (sanctions) that would alter the Ukraine calculus. **Discriminating**: whether any US sanctions relief is formally announced and whether it is explicitly linked to Iran mediation.

### D. Binding constraints and non-closure

- **Option B binding constraint:** A deal must be framed as dignified and reciprocal; US/Israel must move off regime change and total disarmament. Excerpt 5 states that Putin would offer terms “far short of Trump’s objectives” — i.e. the **US would have to move**. The program does not resolve whether Trump or his coalition (e.g. Lindsey Graham) will accept that.
- **Iran’s position:** “I don’t know whether the Iranians would accept [a Russian-brokered off-ramp]. I don’t know whether the supreme leader… would be prepared to agree… I suspect it would be a very tough sell.” So **Iran’s acceptance** is also open; guarantees would need to be “very very strong.”
- **Non-closure preserved:** “we are not yeah there yet and perhaps we never will” — consistent with STATE’s duty to preserve tensions and avoid narrative closure.
