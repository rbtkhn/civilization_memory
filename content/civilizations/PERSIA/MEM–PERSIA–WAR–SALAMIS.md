I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Salamis** from Persia's perspective: the 480 BC
naval battle in the straits between Attica and Salamis island where the
Greek coalition fleet, under Athenian leadership, defeated Xerxes' navy.
Thermopylae had given Persia the pass and the advance to Athens; Salamis
reversed Persian naval superiority and made continued invasion at sea
untenable. For Greece, Salamis is the turning point—Themistocles' ruse,
the narrows negating Persian numbers, the birth of Athenian naval
primacy. For Persia, it is a **frontier naval defeat**—costly but not
existential; the empire withdraws from the Greek theatre by stages;
Plataea will close the land campaign.

Herodotus is the primary source for both encodings.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT — AFTER THERMOPYLAE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
With the pass forced and the Greek fleet withdrawn from Artemisium,
Xerxes advances. Athens is evacuated; the Persians take and burn the
city. The Greek fleet concentrates at Salamis. Herodotus records the
strategic dilemma and the Greek decision to fight in the straits:

> "The Greeks were assembled at Salamis... and the barbarians had
> taken Athens... When the Greeks learned this, such confusion fell
> upon them that some of the captains did not even wait for the
> matter to be put to the vote but embarked and hoisted sail for
> flight... Themistocles... persuaded them to remain and risk a
> sea-fight there."
> — Herodotus, *Histories*, VIII.56, VIII.60 (abridged)

Thucydides, in the voice of the Athenians at Sparta, credits Themistocles and the choice of the narrows:

> "We provided the largest number of ships and the most intelligent general—Themistocles as commander, who was most responsible for the sea-battle being fought in the narrows, which was what above all saved the cause."
> — Thucydides, *History of the Peloponnesian War*, I.74 (tr. Crawley, rev.); ARC–PERSIA-T-ANCIENT

The narrows of Salamis negate Persian numerical advantage; Greek
triremes fight in confined water where coordination and ramming
tactics tell. The Persian fleet—multi-ethnic, many crews pressed
from subject coasts—enters the strait and is broken.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE BATTLE — GREEK VICTORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The battle is fought in the strait. Greek ships hold formation; Persian
numbers cannot be brought to bear; ships crowd and collide. Herodotus
reports heavy Persian losses and the king's withdrawal from the theatre.
Xerxes, watching from shore, sees his fleet defeated. He leaves Mardonius
with a land force and withdraws with much of the army and the remnant
navy. Salamis does not destroy the Persian army—Plataea will decide the
land war—but it ends Persian naval dominance in the Aegean for this
campaign and secures Greek coalition morale.

From the Persian view: a serious reverse. The western naval arm is
mauled; the king's presence in Greece is no longer sustainable; the
campaign continues by land only, and that too will fail at Plataea.
The encoding is not "Persia destroyed" but "frontier campaign failed."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ENCODING DIVERGENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Greece: Salamis = salvation; Themistocles the saviour; narrows as
deliverance; Athenian naval identity born.
Persia: Salamis = naval defeat in a western campaign; withdrawal and
reconsolidation; the Greek wars remain unresolved until Alexander
inverts the dynamic. Thucydides records that the Persian king later
honoured the man who had broken his fleet:

> "Themistocles displayed most emphatically the strength of his natural
> genius ... the best judge of what was needed in the future ... by
> native intelligence and with the least preparation he was the best at
> improvising the right course. The King, it is said, so admired him that
> he bade him ask for whatever he wished."
> — Thucydides, *History of the Peloponnesian War*, I.138 (tr. Crawley, rev.); ARC–PERSIA-T-ANCIENT

Neither encoding is false. Persian memory does not require Salamis to
be absorbed as trauma—the empire endures; the western frontier is one
theatre. The *Shahnameh* encodes kingship and fame across time, not
single battles:

> "I shall not die, these seeds I've sown will save my name and
> reputation from the grave, and men of sense and wisdom will proclaim,
> when I have gone, my praises and my fame."
> — Ferdowsi, *Shahnameh* (prologue), tr. Dick Davis; ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL
>
> **Original (Farsi):** توانا بود هر که دانا بود / ز دانش دل پیر برنا بود — فردوسی، شاهنامه (دیباچه)، ARC–PERSIA-T-MEDIEVAL

**Structural point:** The straits at Salamis function as terrain that
inverts force ratio—Persian numbers become a liability. The same
structural logic appears in reverse when Alexander uses speed and
concentration to defeat Persian mass at Issus and Gaugamela. MEM–PERSIA–WAR–ALEXANDER
encodes the full inversion: Xerxes loses at Salamis and withdraws;
Darius loses the empire.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who is on the hook for the defeat? Xerxes as campaign authority;
naval command was divided (e.g. Artemisia noted in Herodotus). The
decision to fight in the strait was Greek (Themistocles); the decision
to enter it was Persian. Irreversibility: once the fleet was committed
in the narrows, retreat under attack was costly. The mechanism is
terrain and tactics—concentration of Greek force in confined space—
not merely "Greek courage." Liability sits with the king who ordered
the campaign and the admirals who committed to the strait; the
institutional outcome is withdrawal, not regime collapse.
