I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Ayyubid dynasty as Islam’s moral restorer:
the regime that reasserted Sunni legitimacy through sacred-zone recovery,
ethical warfare, and reintegration of fractured Islamic authority during
the Crusader era.

For Islam, the Ayyubids represent legitimacy through restraint and
repair, not empire-building.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGINS: SUNNI REPAIR AFTER SCHISM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Ayyubids arise from the collapse of Fatimid Shi‘a rule in Egypt.

Key transitions:
• Abolition of the Fatimid caliphate (1171)
• Restoration of Abbasid Sunni recognition
• Re-Sunnification of Egypt’s institutions

Legitimacy is repaired by doctrinal realignment, not conquest alone.

Unity precedes expansion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. SALADIN AS MORAL EXEMPLAR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Saladin embodies Ayyubid legitimacy.

Defining traits:
• Personal piety
• Strategic patience
• Avoidance of indiscriminate violence
• Emphasis on unity against external threat

Leadership legitimacy flows from conduct, not lineage.

Saladin becomes memory before institution.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. JERUSALEM & SACRED-ZONE RESTORATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The recapture of Jerusalem (1187) defines the dynasty.

Significance:
• Reversal of Crusader occupation
• Preservation of holy sites
• Mercy toward defeated populations

Sacred recovery is paired with moral restraint.

Islam reclaims Jerusalem without repeating massacre.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LIMITED EMPIRE & DELIBERATE RESTRAINT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Ayyubids resist full imperial consolidation.

Characteristics:
• Regional rule across Egypt, Syria, Hijaz
• Dynastic fragmentation tolerated
• Abbasid caliph acknowledged

The Ayyubids choose legitimacy density over imperial scale.

Their restraint prevents internal legitimacy erosion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. TRANSITION TO MAMLUK CUSTODIANSHIP
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ayyubid authority dissolves without civilizational collapse.

Outcomes:
• Military elites (Mamluks) assume power
• Sacred-zone protection continues
• Abbasid caliphate symbolically preserved

The dynasty ends,
but its legitimacy logic persists.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Sacred-zone recovery can reset legitimacy.
• Moral conduct amplifies military success.
• Restraint can stabilize authority more than domination.
• Unity is a prerequisite for sacred defense.
• The Ayyubids prove that legitimacy can be restored, not invented.

Tension preserved without verdict.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. SOURCE EVIDENCE — PHILLIPS (ARC–ISLAM)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Jonathan Phillips, *The Life and Legend of the Sultan Saladin* (Yale University Press, 2019) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):

Phillips emphasizes that Saladin's moral model endured while the Ayyubid political structure fragmented rapidly after his death. His "courtesy, justice, generosity, and mercy" earned admiration across confessional lines; the dynasty inherited his legitimacy logic but could not institutionalize his personal authority.

"It is impossible to think of another figure from history who dealt such a deep wound to a people and a faith, and yet became so admired."

— Phillips on Saladin's legacy; moral model retained, political structure dissolves; dynastic fragility.
