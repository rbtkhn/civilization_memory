I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Ilkhanate as Islam's **absorption case**: the
Mongol successor state in Persia and Iraq that conquered Baghdad (1258),
ruled through non-Islamic legitimacy, and whose elites converted to Islam
(Ghazan, 1295)—encoding civilizational absorption of the conqueror.

For Islam, the Ilkhanate represents conversion without caliphal restoration.
Power remains Mongol in origin; legitimacy migrates into Islamic grammar.
Iraq under Ilkhanid rule experiences post-destruction recovery.

Grounds: MEM–ISLAM–MONGOL–EMPIRE; MEM–ISLAM–BAGHDAD; Weatherford (ARC).

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGINS: HULEGU & BAGHDAD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Hulegu (grandson of Genghis Khan) establishes the Ilkhanate after the
sack of Baghdad (1258). The Abbasid caliph is executed; the caliphate
relocates to Cairo. Ilkhanid rule extends over Persia, Iraq, Anatolia,
and the Caucasus.

Jack Weatherford, *Genghis Khan and the Making of the Modern World* (2004) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):

"In Baghdad, Hulegu installed a government under Ata Malik Juvaini, a devout Persian Muslim, who governed for most of the next 20 years and whose writings survive as some of the great scholarly works of the Muslim world."

Administration: Persian Muslims serve Mongol masters. The conqueror rules through indigenous elites; legitimacy remains steppe-derived.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. RELIGIOUS PLURALISM & SECULAR STATE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Early Ilkhanid rule is religiously indifferent. Multiple faiths coexist
under Mongol law (yassa).

Weatherford, "Restoring Order," *Asia-Pacific Journal* (2007) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):

"The Mongols spared anyone with a craft, such as carpentry, writing, pottery, weaving or metal working. They fiercely enforced religious freedom, which created an essentially secular state."

"Under Mongol rule Christian, Muslim, Jewish and even Buddhist immigrants poured into the newly conquered Iraq to live under the Great Law of Genghis Khan."

Islam is governed—but not privileged. Power exists without Islamic moral grammar until conversion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. GHAZAN & CONVERSION (1295)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ghazan converts to Islam (1295)—first Ilkhan to do so formally. Conversion
is personal and dynastic; it aligns the ruling elite with the majority
population. No caliphate is restored; no universal Islamic claim emerges.
Islam spreads upward into power without institutional recentering.

MEM–ISLAM–MONGOL–EMPIRE: "The conqueror submits civilizationally—but not retroactively."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. POST-DESTRUCTION RECOVERY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Weatherford ("Restoring Order," 2007):

"Yet under the Mongols — and the legacy of Genghis Khan — Iraq enjoyed a century of peace and a renaissance that brought the region to a level of prosperity and cultural sophistication higher than it enjoyed before or after."

Encoding: Destruction (1258) is followed by recovery under Ilkhanid rule.
The corridor revives; Islam absorbs the conqueror. Tension: civilizational
rupture (caliphate gone) vs material and cultural revival (Iraq flourishes).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. END & FRAGMENTATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ilkhanate dissolves (1335). No clear successor; regional polities emerge
(Timurids, Jalayirids, etc.). Persia and Iraq remain politically fractured.
The Mongol moment ends; Islamic continuity persists—without a restored
caliphate.
