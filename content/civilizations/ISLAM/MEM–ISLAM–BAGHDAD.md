I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Baghdad as Islam's **civilizational consolidator**—
the Abbasid capital where legitimacy was repaired through law, learning,
and inclusion, and whose fall (1258) encodes **civilizational rupture**:
physical destruction by external conquest.

For Islam, Baghdad represents the translation movement, jurisprudence,
and scholarly synthesis. Its end is the sharp break—empire dies;
civilization endures. The caliphate relocates to Cairo, but encoding
stresses rupture despite transfer.

Grounds: CIV–SCHOLAR–ISLAM SYNTHESIS 0006; MEM–ISLAM–DYNASTY–ABBASID;
MEM–ISLAM–MONGOL–EMPIRE.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FOUNDING & CIVILIZATIONAL DEPTH (762–1258)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Abbasids found Baghdad as a new center—deliberate break from
Umayyad geography.

Bernard Lewis, *What Went Wrong?* (2002) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):

"For many centuries the world of Islam was in the forefront of human civilization and achievement."

"For the first thousand years of its history, Islamic civilization's relationship to Christendom was one of dominance."

Legitimacy grammar: repair through law, learning, inclusion. The
translation movement, jurisprudence, scientific synthesis make Islam a
law-centered civilization. Legitimacy migrates from throne to law; the
caliph becomes symbolic as power decentralizes.

Strategic constraint: Mesopotamia concentrates wealth and population.
Tigris–Euphrates basin; Silk Road nodes (Merv, Nishapur). Baghdad is
the natural seat for resource extraction and projection toward Persia
and Central Asia. Power follows revenue.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. FALL BY DESTRUCTION (1258)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Baghdad falls in 1258 by **physical destruction**—the Mongol sack.
The Abbasid caliph is executed; the caliphate becomes ceremonial,
relocates to Cairo.

Jack Weatherford, *Genghis Khan and the Making of the Modern World* (2004) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN): "The Mongol army had accomplished in a mere two years what the European Crusaders from the West and the Seljuk Turks from the East had failed to do in two centuries of sustained effort. They had conquered Baghdad, the heart of the Arab world."

Weatherford, "Restoring Order," *Asia-Pacific Journal* (2007): "The legal justification for the Mongol invasion of Iraq derived from the reluctance of the caliph of Baghdad to control the Shiite Cult of the Assassins, whom the Mongols accused of attempting to kill their khan." "The Mongols also immediately executed the caliph and his sons on charges that they spent too much money on their palaces and not enough defending their nation." "In Baghdad, Hulegu installed a government under Ata Malik Juvaini, a devout Persian Muslim, who governed for most of the next 20 years and whose writings survive as some of the great scholarly works of the Muslim world."

Bernard Lewis, *What Went Wrong?* (2002) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN): "Compared with Christendom, its rival for more than a millennium, the world of Islam had become poor, weak, and ignorant."

Encoding: civilizational rupture. No continuity-through-transition here—
annihilation precedes transfer. External conquest; no internal balance
could have prevented it. Empire dies; civilization persists. The sharp
break contrasts with Damascus and Cairo.
