I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Medina as Islam's **political birthplace**—the site
of the Hijra (622), the Constitution of Medina, and the Prophet's grave.
With Mecca, Medina forms the **Hijaz**; both are non-fungible sacred zones.

For Islam, Medina is where the Ummah became a state—before it became
an empire. Legitimacy precedes scale.

CIV–CORE–ISLAM § IV: "Hejaz (Mecca–Medina)"; sacred zones are
NON-FUNGIBLE.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. HIJRA & STATE FORMATION (622)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Hijra marks Islam's political birth. Muhammad migrates from Mecca
to Medina (Yathrib); the community is reconstituted as a polity.

Bernard Lewis (citing tradition), on the transition:

"By his migration from Mecca to Medina, the Prophet was transformed
from a rebel to a statesman; at the time of his death the state that
he founded was in the process of becoming an empire."

Medinan innovations (MEM–ISLAM–MOHAMMAD):
• Constitution of Medina—plural community order across clans and faiths
• Loyalty redefined from tribe to belief
• Law applied uniformly
• Collective defense obligations

Islam becomes a state **before** becoming an empire. Legitimacy precedes scale.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PROPHET'S GRAVE & CUSTODIANSHIP
──────────────────────────────────────────────
Muhammad is buried in Medina. The Prophet's Mosque (al-Masjid an-Nabawi)
makes the city the second holiest site—after Mecca, before Jerusalem.

Pilgrimage to Medina (ziyara) often accompanies the hajj. Custody of
Medina, like Mecca, has been a caliphal and sultanic responsibility.
Ottoman and Mamluk protection of the holy cities included both.

Bernard Lewis, *The Political Language of Islam* (1988) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):

"The exercise of political power is conceived and presented as a
contract, creating bonds of mutual obligation between the ruler and
the ruled."

Medina embodies that contract's origin—the Constitution of Medina as
early plural order; the Prophet's grave as ongoing sanctity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CALIPHAL SUCCESSION & DAMASCUS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Medina is the first caliphal seat (Rashidun: 632–661). The shift to
Damascus (Umayyad, 661) marks the move from prophetic city to imperial
capital. Medina remains sacred; it ceases to be the political center.

Bernard Lewis, "The Shi'a," *The New York Review of Books* (15 August 1985) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):

"The first four caliphs, known in Muslim historiography as the Rightly
Guided, were chosen from among the companions of the Prophet. Thereafter
the caliphate became hereditary in the house of Umayya."

Medina encodes the **elective precedent**—consensus, companionship,
proximity to the Prophet. Damascus encodes **hereditary succession**.
The tension between the two is never resolved; both persist in memory.
