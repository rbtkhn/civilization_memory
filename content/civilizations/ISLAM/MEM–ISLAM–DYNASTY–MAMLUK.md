I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Mamluk dynasty as Islam’s martial custodians:
the regime that defended the core Islamic heartlands after imperial
collapse by converting slave-soldier institutions into a durable,
legitimacy-preserving state.

For Islam, the Mamluks represent custodianship without dynasty.
They rule not by blood, but by function.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ORIGINS: MILITARY SLAVERY AS INSTITUTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Mamluk system originates under earlier Sunni regimes.

Core features:
• Imported military slaves (Turkic, Circassian)
• Conversion, training, and manumission
• Loyalty to institution, not lineage
• Elite reproduction through purchase, not inheritance

Power is deliberately de-familialized.

Legitimacy derives from service and competence.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. SEIZURE OF POWER & ABBASID RESTORATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Mamluks seize power after Ayyubid fragmentation (1250).

Key actions:
• Defeat of Crusaders
• Decisive victory over Mongols at Ayn Jalut (1260)
• Restoration of Abbasid caliphate in Cairo (symbolic)

Jack Weatherford, *Genghis Khan and the Making of the Modern World* (2004) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN): Mongols sold Crimean captives to Egypt; the Sultan "used them in his slave army—eventually [they] defeated the Mongols at Ain Jalut." The Mamluk institution absorbed those the Mongols displaced and turned them against the conquerors.

The Mamluks rule in the caliph’s shadow.

They protect legitimacy rather than embody it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. SACRED-ZONE DEFENSE AS CORE FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mamluk legitimacy is territorial and defensive.

Primary responsibilities:
• Protection of Mecca and Medina
• Security of pilgrimage routes
• Defense of Cairo–Damascus axis
• Containment of external invaders

They do not claim universality.
They hold the center.

Islam survives because the core is intact.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. GOVERNANCE WITHOUT DYNASTY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mamluk rule resists dynastic continuity.

Characteristics:
• Frequent coups
• Personal military followings
• Weak hereditary principle
• Strong institutional norms

Instability is chronic—
but collapse is avoided.

The system sacrifices elegance for endurance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LIMITS & OVERREACH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Structural weaknesses accumulate.

Constraints:
• Economic rigidity
• Resistance to technological change
• Military conservatism (firearms lag)
• Elite infighting

The custodians can defend—
but cannot modernize.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. ABSORPTION BY OTTOMAN CUSTODIANSHIP
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Ottomans defeat the Mamluks (1516–1517).

Consequences:
• Sacred custodianship transfers smoothly
• Abbasid caliphate relocates to Istanbul
• Institutional memory absorbed, not erased

The Mamluks lose sovereignty,
but not relevance.

They bridge collapse to continuity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Legitimacy can be preserved without dynasty.
• Military institutions can function as civilizational scaffolding.
• Sacred-zone defense outweighs imperial ambition.
• Stability is not the same as order—but can be sufficient.
• The Mamluks keep Islam alive between empires.

Tension preserved without verdict.
