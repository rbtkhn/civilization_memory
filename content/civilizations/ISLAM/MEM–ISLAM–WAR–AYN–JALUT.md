I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Ayn Jalut (1260) as Islam's **first Mongol defeat**:
the battle in which the Mamluk Sultanate halted Mongol advance into the
Levant, preserved the Cairo–Damascus axis, and affirmed that the Islamic
heartland would not fall to steppe conquest.

For Islam, Ayn Jalut encodes:
• Sacred-zone defense succeeds
• Mamluk legitimacy as custodianship
• Structural limit of Mongol expansion

Grounds: MEM–ISLAM–DYNASTY–MAMLUK; MEM–ISLAM–MONGOL–EMPIRE; Weatherford (ARC).

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT: POST-BAGHDAD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Baghdad falls (1258). Abbasid caliph executed. Mongol armies under
Kitbuqa advance into Syria. Damascus falls. The Ilkhanate projects
westward. Cairo—host to the restored Abbasid caliphate (1261)—and the
Nile–Levant corridor face direct threat.

Qutuz (Mamluk Sultan) and Baybars marshal forces. The Mamluks choose
to fight rather than negotiate.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE BATTLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ayn Jalut (Spring of Goliath), Palestine, 3 September 1260. Mamluk army
defeats Mongol force under Kitbuqa. Kitbuqa is killed. Mongol advance
into Egypt is halted. Syria returns to Mamluk control.

First decisive defeat of the Mongols in the Islamic world. The Mongols
would contest the Levant again—but never break the Mamluk defense of
the core.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. MAMLUK ORIGINS & WEATHERFORD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jack Weatherford, *Genghis Khan and the Making of the Modern World* (2004) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):

Mongols sold captives from Crimea to Egypt; the Sultan "used them in his slave army—eventually [they] defeated the Mongols at Ain Jalut."

Encoding: The Mamluk institution absorbed those the Mongols displaced
(through slave trade, captivity, refugee flows). The conqueror's victims
became the conqueror's defeater. Structural irony: steppe-origin slaves,
trained in Egypt, halt steppe-origin conquerors.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. STRATEGIC SIGNIFICANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ayn Jalut establishes:
• Southern limit of Mongol expansion (Levant holds)
• Mamluk claim to sacred-zone defense (Mecca, Medina, pilgrimage)
• Cairo–Damascus axis as defensible core
• No Ilkhanid conquest of Egypt

MEM–ISLAM–DYNASTY–MAMLUK: Custodianship without dynasty. Ayn Jalut is
the founding victory that justifies that role.
