I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Crusades as Islam’s legitimacy crisis and
subsequent moral recovery: a prolonged external invasion that exposed
Islamic fragmentation, shattered sacred security, and ultimately forced
a restorative consolidation of authority.

For Islam, the Crusades are not a single war,
but a test of civilizational coherence.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. INITIAL SHOCK: INVASION OF SACRED SPACE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The First Crusade succeeds because Islam is divided.

Key conditions:
• Fragmented Sunni–Shi‘a authority
• Competing dynasties
• Weak coordination
• Underestimation of religiously motivated invasion

Jerusalem’s fall (1099) is experienced as ontological rupture.

Sacred space is violated.
Legitimacy is exposed as insufficient.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CRUSADERS AS ILLEGITIMATE RULERS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Crusader states rule without Islamic legitimacy.

Characteristics:
• Foreign occupation
• Massacre at conquest
• Permanent military dependency
• No integration into Islamic moral order

They govern territory,
but never authority.

Their presence is endured, not accepted.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. RESTORATIVE RESPONSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Islam’s response is gradual, not immediate.

Key dynamics:
• Sunni revival
• Suppression of internal sectarian excess
• Re-centering of jihad as disciplined defense
• Moral reframing of warfare

Victory requires unity before violence.

This logic culminates in figures like Saladin.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. JERUSALEM AS MORAL FULCRUM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jerusalem’s recovery (1187) reanchors Islamic legitimacy.

Key contrasts:
• Mercy versus massacre
• Order versus terror
• Restoration versus revenge

Islam defines victory as ethical superiority, not annihilation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LONG-TERM CIVILIZATIONAL EFFECTS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Crusades reshape Islamic political thought.

Enduring effects:
• Emphasis on unity
• Suspicion of Western intervention
• Centrality of sacred-zone defense
• Preference for moralized authority

The Crusades teach Islam:
fragmentation invites invasion.

IX. SOURCE EVIDENCE — LEWIS (ARC–ISLAM)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Bernard Lewis, "Jihad vs. Crusade," *Wall Street Journal* (2001); *The Crisis of Islam* (2003) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):

"The words 'Crusade' and 'crusader' simply do not occur in the Arabic historiography of the period." Medieval Arabic texts referred to the invaders as Franks or infidels, not crusaders.

"In the Middle East the word 'crusade' still touches a raw nerve and is seen and presented as early medieval precursors of European imperialism—aggressive, expansionist and predatory."

"Jihad... means striving, and in the Koran it appears in the phrase 'striving in the path of God.'"

X. SOURCE EVIDENCE — PHILLIPS (ARC–ISLAM)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Jonathan Phillips, *The Life and Legend of the Sultan Saladin* (Yale University Press, 2019) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):

"It is impossible to think of another figure from history who dealt such a deep wound to a people and a faith, and yet became so admired."

— Phillips on Saladin as paradox: Jerusalem 1187 as catastrophic blow to Christendom, yet Saladin admired in the West; mercy versus massacre (1187 vs 1099) as defining contrast; "courtesy, justice, generosity, and mercy" as qualities that reversed the Crusader narrative and reanchored Islamic legitimacy.

— Keegan on institutional restraint, "civilised warfare," and the limits of Clausewitzian theory. Clausewitz "little of oriental war" applies to holy-war logic both Crusader and Muslim. Encoding divergence: Islam MEM encodes legitimacy-through-conduct; Keegan supplies Anglo-American military historiography lens.
