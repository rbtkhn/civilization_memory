I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Shi‘a Islam as **Islam’s legitimacy-through-justice
architecture**: the tradition that anchors authority in divinely guided
lineage, moral rectitude, and resistance to illegitimate power.

Shi‘a Islam is not a schism of doctrine.
It is a **permanent moral indictment embedded in history**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. SUCCESSION AS SACRED BREACH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shi‘a identity begins with a refusal.

Core claim:
• Muhammad designated moral authority through his family
• Leadership without legitimacy is usurpation
• Injustice at the origin corrupts all outcomes

Ali is not merely bypassed.
Justice itself is displaced.

Politics becomes a theological wound.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. IMAMATE AS MORAL AXIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Imam is not a ruler by convenience.

Characteristics:
• Divinely guided authority
• Moral and interpretive infallibility (in Twelver doctrine)
• Legitimacy independent of power

Authority does not derive from consensus.
It derives from **truth under oppression**.

Powerlessness does not negate legitimacy.
It confirms it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. MARTYRDOM AS LEGITIMACY ENGINE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Karbala (680 CE) defines Shi‘a time.

Husayn’s death establishes:
• Suffering as proof of righteousness
• Resistance as sacred duty
• Memory as political obligation

Martyrdom is not tragedy.
It is **authority preserved through blood**.

History becomes ritualized accusation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. OPPOSITION AS PERMANENT POSTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Shi‘a Islam institutionalizes dissent.

Traits:
• Suspicion of power
• Emphasis on injustice
• Expectation of betrayal

Quietism and revolt coexist.
Both are framed as ethical responses to illegitimate rule.

Endurance is not acceptance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. OCCULTATION & DEFERRED SOVEREIGNTY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The disappearance of the Imam transforms Shi‘a politics.

Consequences:
• Authority becomes provisional
• Clerics act as deputies, not replacements
• Justice is postponed but demanded

The world is fallen.
Redemption is future.

This produces **strategic patience with moral intensity**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. MODERN REACTIVATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Modern Shi‘a movements reactivate dormant sovereignty.

Features:
• Clerical political leadership
• Fusion of martyr memory and state power
• Persistent revolutionary legitimacy

When Shi‘a Islam governs,
it does so as **corrective history**, not normal politics.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Justice can anchor legitimacy without power.
• Memory can function as permanent resistance.
• Martyrdom outlives empires.
• Authority deferred becomes morally intensified.
• Shi‘a Islam transforms loss into political permanence.

Tension preserved without verdict.
