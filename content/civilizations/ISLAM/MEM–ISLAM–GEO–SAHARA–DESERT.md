────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Sahara** as Islam's **trans-Saharan corridor**: the desert as **flow-space** linking Maghreb (North Africa) to the Sahel (sub-Saharan West Africa) via gold, salt, caravans, and faith. For Islam, the Sahara is not a barrier but a **crossable axis**—oasis nodes and caravan routes make it a zone of trade and transmission.

For Islam:
• Maghreb–Sahel axis: Sijilmasa, Timbuktu, Gao, Kano
• Gold (south) ↔ salt (north); caravan logic
• No single Islamic state controls the whole desert; plural sovereignty
• Desert as corridor, not limit

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC PARAMETERS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sahara ~9,200,000 km²; world's largest hot desert. Key transit corridors: western (Morocco–Mali), central (Libya–Chad), eastern (Egypt–Nubia/Sudan). Oases (Siwa, Ghadames, Kufra, Timbuktu-region nodes) anchor passage. Structural axiom: **crossing requires nodes**; control of nodes = influence over flow, not sovereignty over space.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. STRATEGIC LOGIC — CORRIDOR, NOT EMPIRE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trans-Saharan trade (gold, salt, slaves, ivory, cloth) predates Islam but is **Islamicized** along the route: Berber and Arab traders; later Maliki scholarship in Timbuktu; Sufi networks. Caravans (e.g. camel trains; raiding risk) define the economics. No caliphate or sultanate ever **controls** the full Sahara; Umayyads, Fatimids, Almoravids, Almohads, Ottomans hold **littoral or northern fringe** and key termini. The interior remains **flow-space** with plural actors.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CONTROL SEQUENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pre-Islam: Garamantes (Rome's interface); limited trans-Saharan contact. Early Islam: Umayyad/Abbasid Maghreb; caravan routes extend. Medieval: Almoravids (Sijilmasa–Ghana axis); Mali (Timbuktu, Mansa Musa); Kanem–Bornu. Ottoman: North African regencies (Tripoli, Tunis, Algiers) at northern edge; no deep Saharan control. Colonial: French (Sahel), Italian (Libya); routes disrupted. Modern: Sahel states; jihadist and trafficking use of desert corridors; no single actor on the axis.
