I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Cairo as Islam's **second caliphal capital**—a city
that entered as rival (Fatimid) before hosting the restored Abbasid
caliphate (Mamluk), and that encodes **continuity through multiple
transitions** without destruction.

For Islam, Cairo represents the Nile–Levant axis as sacred-zone defense
core after Baghdad's fall (1258). It is the capital that was never
conquered; power passed through institutional transfer.

Grounds: CIV–SCHOLAR–ISLAM SYNTHESIS 0006; MEM–ISLAM–DYNASTY–FATIMID,
MEM–ISLAM–DYNASTY–MAMLUK.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FOUNDING AS RIVAL CALIPHATE (969)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Fatimids found Cairo (969) as a Shiʿa universal caliphate—the only
sustained attempt to establish a competing caliphal center against
Baghdad.

Bernard Lewis, "The Shi'a," *The New York Review of Books* (15 August 1985) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):

"The Abbasid caliphs, whose origins were among the Shi'a, came to Baghdad via eastern Iran. The Fatimid caliphs, who began as leaders of a radical Shi'ite sect, came to Egypt via North Africa."

Cairo establishes Al-Azhar (originally Ismaʿili), a parallel caliphal
court, competing ritual calendar. Islam has two universalisms. Unity
fractures at the top.

Strategic constraint: The Nile is a single chokepoint. Control Egypt,
control the corridor between Africa and the Levant. Mediterranean trade
and Red Sea access converge here.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. SUNNI REABSORPTION & MAMLUK CUSTODIANSHIP (1171–1517)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Saladin abolishes the Fatimid caliphate (1171). Sunni alignment is
restored. Cairo transitions without destruction.

After Baghdad's fall (1258), the Mamluks restore the Abbasid caliphate
in Cairo (1261)—**symbolic**, not temporal. Jack Weatherford, *Genghis Khan and the Making of the Modern World* (2004) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN): Mongol conquest brought "destruction and shock" followed by "unprecedented rise in cultural communication, expanded trade, and improved civilization" in many conquered lands—yet Baghdad lost the caliphate to Cairo; no institutional continuity in Mesopotamia. The Cairo–Damascus axis becomes the sacred-zone defense core: Mecca, Medina, pilgrimage routes. The Nile and Levant replace Mesopotamia as the Islamic heartland.

Cairo falls by **institutional transfer** (1517)—the caliphate moves to
Istanbul. The city is never conquered; power passes through Ottoman
victory over Mamluks elsewhere. Encoding: multiple transitions
(Fatimid → Ayyubid → Mamluk); institutional handover; continuity
preserved.
