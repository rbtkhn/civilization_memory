────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Balkans as Islam's **European frontier**: the
region where the Ottoman domain "reached deep into Europe's heart,"
governing a multiethnic, multilingual, multireligious territory for
centuries—and where late-Ottoman exclusivity triggered fragmentation.

For Islam, the Balkans represent:
• Ottoman European integration (not separate "East")
• Managed diversity (millet, toleration, conversion)
• 19th C nationalist fragmentation; empire's demise

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC PARAMETERS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Balkans: Southeastern Europe; mountain spine (Dinaric, Balkan, Rhodope);
Danube frontier; Adriatic and Aegean littorals. Key cities: Belgrade,
Sarajevo, Sofia, Thessaloniki, Athens (under Ottoman rule).

Terrain: Mixed—mountain, river valley, coast. Climate: Mediterranean
north; continental interior. Structural: Land bridge; overland route
between Anatolia and Central Europe.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. OTTOMAN ENTRY & CUSTODIANSHIP
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ottomans expand into Balkans (14th–15th C).

Characteristics:
• Conquest of Serbian, Bulgarian, Byzantine territories
• Devşirme (child-levy) from Christian populations
• Millet system for Orthodox, Catholic, Jewish communities
• Conversion and toleration as integration tools

The Balkans become core Ottoman territory—
"Europe's heart," not periphery.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRATEGIC LOGIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Power distribution:
• Danube as frontier with Habsburgs
• Vienna 1683 as limit of Ottoman projection
• Balkan mountains as defensible interior
• Maritime access via Adriatic, Aegean

Control sequence: Byzantine/Serbian/Bulgarian → Ottoman (14th–15th C)
→ Nationalist fragmentation (19th C) → Post-Ottoman states (20th C).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LATE-OTTOMAN FRAGMENTATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
19th C: Nationalist movements; exclusivity replaces inclusion.

Baer: "In the nineteenth century, they embraced exclusivity, leading to
ethnic cleansing, genocide, and the empire's demise after the First
World War."

Balkan wars; WWI; Ottoman exit from Europe.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. SOURCE EVIDENCE — BAER (ARC–ISLAM)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Marc David Baer, *The Ottomans: Khans, Caesars, and Caliphs* (Basic Books, 2021) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):

"The Ottoman Empire has long been depicted as the Islamic, Asian antithesis of the Christian, European West. But the reality was starkly different: the Ottomans' multiethnic, multilingual, and multireligious domain reached deep into Europe's heart."

"The Ottomans pioneered religious toleration even as they used religious conversion to integrate conquered peoples. But in the nineteenth century, they embraced exclusivity, leading to ethnic cleansing, genocide, and the empire's demise after the First World War."

— Baer on Balkans as Ottoman European core; toleration-and-conversion; late-Ottoman exclusivity trajectory.
