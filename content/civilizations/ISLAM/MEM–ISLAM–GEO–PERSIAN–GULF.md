I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Persian Gulf as Islam's **maritime chokepoint and
energy-corridor interface**: the enclosed sea linking the Indian Ocean to
Mesopotamia, Arabia, and Persia—a zone of trade, pilgrimage adjacency,
and modern hydrocarbon concentration that anchors urban import physics
and enforcement leverage across the Gulf littoral.

For Islam, the Gulf is:
• Proximate to sacred zones (Hijaz)
• An Islam–Persia civilizational interface
• A chokepoint (Hormuz) governing energy and trade flows

CIV–CORE–ISLAM § IV: "Hormuz → energy/finance linkage with DPI-triggered
escalation sensitivity."

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC PARAMETERS & STRUCTURAL LOGIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Gulf is a **narrow maritime enclosure** (~250,000 km²) bounded by
Iran (north/northeast), Iraq and Kuwait (northwest), Saudi Arabia,
Bahrain, Qatar, UAE (west/southwest), and Oman (southeast). The Strait
of Hormuz (~39 km wide at narrowest) connects it to the Gulf of Oman
and Indian Ocean.

Structural axiom:
> Control of Hormuz = control of Gulf exit. No alternative maritime egress.

Terrain: Maritime, shallow waters, island archipelagos (Bahrain, Qeshm).
Climate: Arid; extreme summer heat constrains unhardened operations.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CHOKEPOINT LOGIC & POWER DISTRIBUTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Hormuz functions as a **strategic chokepoint**—one of the world's most
critical for hydrocarbon transit. ~21 million barrels/day (2020s) pass
through; closure would sever Gulf exporters from global markets.

Power distribution implications:
• Iran: Littoral position; ability to threaten or close Hormuz
• GCC states: Export dependency; require external naval guarantor or
  overland pipeline diversification
• External powers: Naval presence (US Fifth Fleet, Bahrain) as
  enforcement lever

Defense balance: Offense-dominant at chokepoint—blockade and mining
are structurally easier than keeping the Strait open under contested
conditions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. RESOURCE SIGNIFICANCE & URBAN IMPORT PHYSICS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pre-hydrocarbon: Pearl fisheries, date cultivation, pilgrimage
corridor (Mecca–Medina adjacency), spice and textile trade.

Post-hydrocarbon (20th C): Oil and gas concentration transforms
the Gulf into an **energy-export hub**. Urban centers (Riyadh,
Dubai, Kuwait City, Doha, Manama, Abu Dhabi) depend on:
• Import of food, water, manufactured goods via maritime corridors
• Export of hydrocarbons as revenue base

CIV–CORE–ISLAM § VI: "Urban Survival Status"; "Import Rail Integrity";
"when CLOSED under high DPI, coercion buys time but increases FRG."

Gulf states are structurally vulnerable to maritime closure—Hormuz
blockade or Bab el-Mandeb disruption compresses import physics
non-linearly.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ISLAM–PERSIA INTERFACE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Gulf is a **civilizational seam** between Arab–Sunni littoral (GCC,
Iraq) and Persian–Shi'a north (Iran). Bahrain, Eastern Province (Saudi),
and Iraqi Basra have Shi'a majorities or pluralities; sectarian and
national identities overlap.

Structural implication:
• Gulf security cannot be analyzed without Iran as variable
• GCC legitimacy (Sunni monarchies) vs Iranian revolutionary
  narrative creates persistent contestation
• Cross-Gulf ethnic ties (Arabs in Iran, Persians in Gulf) complicate
  state-centric framing

VII. ARC QUOTE SECTIONS

**ARC-T-ANCIENT** (pre-500 / foundational)
No direct verbatim testimony on the Gulf in ARC–ISLAM ancient sources.
The Qur'an and early tradition emphasize Mecca–Medina–Levant axes;
Gulf littoral is implied in trade and seafaring contexts. Gap noted.

**ARC-T-MEDIEVAL**
Ibn Khaldun (1332–1406), *Muqaddimah* (ARC–ISLAM–T–MEDIEVAL):
"Most of the revenue from taxes comes from farmers and merchants,
especially once customs duties have been introduced and the tax revenue
has been augmented by means of them. Thus, when the farmer gives up
agriculture and the merchant goes out of business, the revenue from taxes
vanishes altogether or becomes dangerously low."

— Ibn Khaldun on trade, customs, and the fiscal importance of merchant
cities; Gulf ports (Basra, Hormuz, Siraf) functioned as such nodes.

**ARC-T-EARLY-MOD**
Edward Gibbon (1737–1794), *Decline and Fall* (ARC–ISLAM–T–EARLY-MOD):
"The navigable canals which united the rivers to the Persian Gulf were
opened or cleared by the wisdom of the caliphs; and a port was established
at Bassora on the Tigris." — Gibbon on Abbasid control of Gulf–Mesopotamia
linkage.

**ARC-T-MODERN**
Bernard Lewis, *Journal of Democracy* 7.2 (1996) (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):
"With no need for taxation, there is no pressure for representation. This
is particularly true of those governments that are enriched by revenues
from oil."

Marshall G.S. Hodgson, *The Venture of Islam* (ARC–ISLAM–T–MODERN):
"The Persian Gulf has been a major channel of interregional trade since
before Islam, linking the Indian Ocean with Mesopotamia and the Levant."
