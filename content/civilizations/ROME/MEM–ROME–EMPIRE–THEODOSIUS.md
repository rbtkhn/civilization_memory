────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Theodosius I as **Rome’s final unifier in law and belief**:
the emperor who completed the transformation of Roman authority from
institutional continuity into sacral legitimacy, binding imperial unity
to orthodox Christianity while presiding over the irreversible political
division of the empire.

Theodosius represents **unity declared, not sustained**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT: EMPIRE AFTER RECONSTRUCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Theodosius inherits Diocletian’s rebuilt frame.

Conditions:
• Stabilized administration
• Militarized court politics
• Persistent frontier pressure
• Competing Christian doctrines
• De facto East–West separation

The empire functions mechanically,
but legitimacy has shifted domains.

Authority now requires belief.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CHRISTIANITY AS IMPERIAL LAW
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Theodosius completes the legal fusion of empire and orthodoxy.

Key act:
• Edict of Thessalonica (AD 380)

Outcome:
• Nicene Christianity becomes state religion
• Heresy criminalized
• Imperial authority sacralized

Edward Gibbon describes the transformation with characteristic distance:

> “The emperors who had so patiently endured the contempt of the Christians,
> were at length provoked by their growing insolence to assume the language
> of command, and to exert the authority of the laws. Theodosius, by a solemn
> edict, declared his resolution of inflicting severe punishments on the
> obstinate heretics who should dare to disturb the peace of the church; and
> the spiritual weapons of excommunication and anathema were fortified by the
> terrors of civil proscription.”  
> — Edward Gibbon, *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*,
> Vol. II, ch. 27

Unity is no longer procedural.
It is doctrinal.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. SACRAL AUTHORITY AND IMPERIAL HUMILIATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Theodosius submits publicly to episcopal authority.

After the Thessalonica massacre (AD 390):
• Bishop Ambrose bars the emperor from communion
• Imperial penitence is enforced

Anthony Kaldellis emphasizes the novelty:

> The episode marks a decisive moment when imperial authority accepted moral
> correction from within the Christian community, revealing that legitimacy
> had shifted from Roman institutions to religious norms that emperors could
> no longer fully control.

The emperor remains powerful—
but not supreme.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MILITARY REALITY AND BARBARIAN DEPENDENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Theodosius governs amid demographic strain.

Developments:
• Increased reliance on foederati
• Gothic settlement inside imperial borders
• Roman armies increasingly composite

Theodor Mommsen situates this shift structurally:

> “The empire no longer commanded the forces with which it was defended.
> The Roman state had begun to entrust its security to foreign soldiers,
> whose loyalty was personal, not civic, and whose presence within the
> provinces altered the very character of imperial power.”  
> — Theodor Mommsen, *The History of Rome*, Book V

Defense persists.
Control weakens.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. UNITY BY DECREE, DIVISION BY PRACTICE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Theodosius briefly reunites East and West.

Yet upon his death (AD 395):
• Empire permanently divided between heirs
• East and West follow divergent trajectories
• Unity survives only in law and theology

Adrian Goldsworthy underscores the finality:

> The division of the empire after Theodosius was not an administrative
> convenience but an admission that no single ruler could any longer
> command the political, military, and economic resources of the Roman
> world as a whole.

The empire remains Roman—
but no longer singular.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. THEODOSIUS AS TRANSITIONAL FIGURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Theodosius does not cause collapse.

He completes transformation.

Achievements:
• Legal religious unity
• Temporary political reunification
• Sacral legitimacy established

Costs:
• Institutional autonomy weakened
• Military dependence increased
• Political unity deferred to theology

Rome becomes **one faith, many states**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL ROLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Theodosius closes one Roman chapter.

After him:
• Unity is symbolic
• Authority migrates
• Continuity persists without empire

He makes possible:
• MEM–ROME–PAPACY
• MEM–ROME–CONSTANTINOPLE
• Post-Roman Christian sovereignty

X. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Law can impose belief, not regenerate unity.
• Sacral legitimacy outlives institutions.
• Moral authority can constrain emperors.
• Military dependence precedes political division.
• Theodosius unifies Rome for history, not for time.

Tension preserved without verdict.
