I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Castile as **the demographic, legal, and coercive
core of post-Roman Spain**: the kingdom through which Roman civilizational
logic—law, territorial authority, and expansionary legitimacy—was
reactivated at scale after the fall of centralized Roman sovereignty.

Castile is not merely a medieval polity.
It is **Rome’s Iberian engine**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CASTILE AS POST-ROMAN SUCCESSOR CORE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After the collapse of Western Roman imperial authority, Roman civilization
persists in Iberia through law, Latin Christianity, and memory.

Castile inherits:
• Roman legal concepts via Visigothic and ecclesiastical transmission
• Latin language as administrative substrate
• Territorial legitimacy grounded in land control rather than city autonomy

Unlike frontier polities, Castile expands by **absorption**, not federation.

Its political imagination remains Roman:
territory is to be unified, governed, and standardized.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. MEDIEVAL LEGAL CONTINUITY (PRIMARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Castilian authority is anchored in law as inheritance.

The *Siete Partidas* articulates Castile’s self-understanding explicitly:

> “Law is the bond by which men live together in kingdoms, and by it kings
> govern their peoples; and this law is drawn from reason and from the
> wisdom of the ancients, tested by long use and confirmed by custom.”
> — Alfonso X, *Siete Partidas*, Prologue

Law precedes empire.
Command follows legitimacy.

This is Roman logic translated into medieval form.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. DEMOGRAPHIC MASS & REPLACEMENT CAPACITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Castile’s distinguishing feature is scale.

Structural traits:
• Large inland agrarian base
• Continuous territorial depth
• Capacity for sustained manpower extraction
• Replacement without regime collapse

This mirrors Rome’s Italian advantage.

Castile absorbs losses without strategic rupture.
Smaller Iberian polities cannot.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. RECONQUEST AS TERRITORIAL NORMALIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Castile’s role in the Reconquista is not episodic warfare, but
**territorial normalization**.

Medieval chroniclers frame expansion as restoration, not innovation.

The *Chronica Adefonsi Imperatoris* records this logic:

> “He labored not merely to conquer, but to restore the land to law and
> order, so that what had been lost might again be governed as one realm.”
> — *Chronica Adefonsi Imperatoris*, Book I

Reconquest is presented as **jurisdictional repair**.

Rome’s imperial grammar persists:
lost land must be made legible again.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CASTILE AND IMPERIAL PROJECTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Castile provides the human and legal substrate of the Spanish Empire.

Functions:
• Supplies administrators and jurists
• Establishes legal norms overseas
• Provides coercive manpower
• Exports Castilian law and language

Public-domain scholarship emphasizes Castile’s centrality.

Edward Gibbon observes:

> “The kingdoms of Spain, and especially Castile, preserved the forms of
> Roman jurisprudence more steadily than other western states, and thus
> were prepared to extend their authority over distant lands with a
> confidence derived from law rather than custom.”
> — Edward Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*, ch. 38

Empire flows outward from Castilian norms.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LIMITS OF CASTILIAN LOGIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Castile’s strengths contain risks.

Structural tensions:
• Coercive uniformity suppresses local variance
• Scale amplifies administrative rigidity
• Legitimacy tied to expansion encourages overreach

Mommsen’s judgment on Roman successors applies directly:

> “Where Roman habits of command survived without Roman moderation, the
> result was often endurance at the cost of flexibility.”
> — Theodor Mommsen, *History of Rome*, Vol. V

Castile inherits Rome’s power—and its danger.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Castile encodes **Roman continuity through mass and law**.

Lessons preserved:
• Empires require demographic cores
• Law scales authority beyond culture
• Territorial legitimacy precedes ideology
• Replacement capacity sustains endurance
• Rome survives where scale meets structure

Castile is Rome translated into Iberian geography.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Tension preserved without verdict.
