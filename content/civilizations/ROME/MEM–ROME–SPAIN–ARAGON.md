I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Crown of Aragon as **Rome’s Mediterranean
successor-form in Iberia**: a polity that reactivated Roman civilizational
logic not through mass territorial consolidation, but through maritime
circulation, legal pluralism, and city-centered imperial reach.

Aragon is not Rome’s demographic engine.
It is **Rome’s maritime grammar reborn**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ARAGON AS POST-ROMAN MARITIME POLITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Following the collapse of Western Roman sovereignty, Roman civilization
persisted unevenly around the Mediterranean basin.

Aragon inherits:
• Roman maritime routes and port hierarchies
• Urban legal traditions transmitted through cities
• Latin Christianity as civilizational envelope
• Roman commercial and diplomatic habits

Unlike Castile, Aragon does not seek uniform territorial absorption.
It governs through **connectivity**, not depth.

This aligns Aragon structurally with Roman Mediterranean practice rather
than Roman Italian demography.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. LEGAL PLURALISM AS GOVERNANCE TECHNOLOGY (PRIMARY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Aragonic authority rests on negotiated law.

The *Usatges of Barcelona* articulate this principle clearly:

> “The prince is bound by law and custom, and the kingdom is preserved
> not by force alone, but by the faithful observance of pacts made with
> cities and peoples.”
> — *Usatges de Barcelona*, Prologue

Power is constrained by compact.
Authority is distributed, not centralized.

This echoes Roman provincial administration more than Roman republican
uniformity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. THE CROWN OF ARAGON AS COMPOSITE EMPIRE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Aragon expands through dynastic union and maritime acquisition.

Components include:
• Aragon
• Catalonia
• Valencia
• Balearic Islands
• Sicily
• Sardinia
• Naples (periodically)

Each territory retains:
• Distinct laws
• Separate institutions
• Local elites

Aragon rules as Rome once ruled the Mediterranean:
through **layered sovereignty**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MEDITERRANEAN ORIENTATION & IMPERIAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Aragon’s strategic center of gravity is the sea.

Functions:
• Control of western Mediterranean lanes
• Naval projection without inland depth
• Commercial arbitration
• Diplomatic balancing among powers

Ramon Muntaner describes this maritime orientation explicitly:

> “The strength of the Crown lay not in a single land, but in the sea
> that bound its realms together, and in the cities whose ships carried
> its power farther than armies could march.”
> — Ramon Muntaner, *Crònica*, ch. 29

This is Roman maritime interiorization in medieval form.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ARAGON VS CASTILE: CIVILIZATIONAL DUALITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Iberian peninsula contains two Roman successor logics.

Castile:
• Territorial depth
• Demographic mass
• Legal uniformity
• Inland expansion

Aragon:
• Maritime connectivity
• Urban density
• Legal pluralism
• Overseas projection

This mirrors Rome’s own duality:
Italy (core) vs Mediterranean (circulation).

Spain emerges from **forced synthesis** of these logics.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. DECLINE THROUGH ABSORPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Aragon’s decline is not collapse, but subsumption.

Key pressures:
• Dynastic union with Castile
• Fiscal centralization
• Legal homogenization
• Shift toward Atlantic priorities

Jerónimo Zurita records the erosion of Aragonic autonomy:

> “The ancient liberties of the kingdoms were not taken at once, but
> diminished by degrees, as the necessities of greater dominion imposed
> a single will where once there had been many.”
> — Jerónimo Zurita, *Anales de la Corona de Aragón*, Book XX

Roman pluralism yields to Roman uniformity—again.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Aragon encodes **Roman continuity through plurality and sea power**.

Lessons preserved:
• Empires can rule without uniform law
• Maritime systems favor negotiation over coercion
• Cities can substitute for heartlands
• Connectivity scales authority differently than mass
• Rome survives longest where law adapts to diversity

Aragon is Rome’s Mediterranean afterlife.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Aragon is Spain’s Mediterranean Rome.
• Legal pluralism is an imperial technology.
• Maritime empires prefer contracts to commands.
• Composite rule outlasts centralized ambition—until absorbed.
• Rome’s sea-logic reappears wherever ports replace plains.

Tension preserved without verdict.
