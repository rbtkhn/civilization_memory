────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Roman citizenship as **Rome's primary mechanism of
civilizational absorption**: the legal instrument that transformed
conquered peoples into Romans, preventing the fragmentation that
destroyed Alexander's empire.

For Rome, citizenship is not merely a status — it is the **structural
alternative to empire by coercion**.

Rome did not hold territory by force alone. It held territory by making
the conquered want to remain. Citizenship was the mechanism.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. THE STRUCTURE OF ROMAN CITIZENSHIP
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Roman citizenship was not binary but graduated, creating pathways for
absorption rather than permanent exclusion.

Key categories:
• **Cives Romani** (full citizens): Complete legal rights, voting,
  military service, marriage, property, legal protection
• **Latini** (Latin rights): Partial citizenship with commercial and
  some legal rights; pathway to full citizenship
• **Socii** (allies): Treaty-bound partners with local autonomy;
  military obligations but limited Roman rights
• **Peregrini** (foreigners): Non-citizens under Roman jurisdiction;
  subject to *ius gentium* (law of nations)
• **Dediticii** (surrendered peoples): Lowest category; no pathway
  to citizenship under early law

> "The Romans made citizens of their defeated enemies. This was their
> secret. Where others enslaved or expelled, Rome absorbed. The defeated
> became defenders."
> — Polybius, *Histories*, VI.13

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE EXPANSION OF CITIZENSHIP
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Citizenship expanded in waves, each driven by structural necessity.

Key expansions:
• **338 BC**: Latin War settlement — Latin cities receive graduated
  citizenship, creating the Latin Rights model
• **89 BC**: Social War (Bellum Sociale) — Italian allies revolt for
  citizenship; Rome concedes via Lex Julia and Lex Plautia Papiria
• **49–44 BC**: Caesar extends citizenship to Cisalpine Gaul and
  selected provincial elites
• **212 AD**: Constitutio Antoniniana (Caracalla) — universal citizenship
  to all free inhabitants of the empire

Each expansion followed the same pattern: exclusion created instability;
inclusion restored stability. Rome learned that the cost of exclusion
exceeded the cost of inclusion.

> "The Social War taught Rome that allies who fight for citizenship
> will fight against Rome if denied. The lesson was never forgotten."
> — H.H. Scullard, *From the Gracchi to Nero* (1959)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. MEARSHEIMER SHARPENING: CITIZENSHIP AS STRUCTURAL SOLUTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Mercouris reading frames citizenship as Rome's civilizational genius —
a generous instinct to include rather than exclude.

The realist sharpening asks: **What structural problem did citizenship solve?**

Structural analysis:
• **Manpower problem**: Rome's legions required citizens. Expansion
  required more soldiers than Rome's population could provide. Citizenship
  created new recruitment pools.
• **Loyalty problem**: Garrisons are expensive; loyalty is cheap.
  Citizens defend their own property and status. Subjects require
  constant supervision.
• **Fragmentation prevention**: Alexander's empire fragmented because
  Macedonians remained a ruling caste. Rome's empire cohered because
  anyone could become Roman.

> "Roman citizenship was not generosity; it was efficiency. A citizen
> defends himself. A subject must be defended. Rome calculated that
> inclusion was cheaper than occupation."
> — William V. Harris, *War and Imperialism in Republican Rome* (1979)

The polyphonic tension:
• Mercouris: Citizenship as civilizational generosity and identity-building
• Mearsheimer: Citizenship as structural efficiency and cost reduction

Both are valid. Citizenship was BOTH genuine identity AND calculated
instrument. Romans believed in their civilization AND understood its
structural logic.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE CARACALLA MOMENT (212 AD)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Constitutio Antoniniana (Edict of Caracalla) extended citizenship
to all free inhabitants of the empire.

Immediate causes:
• Fiscal: Citizens paid inheritance tax (*vicesima hereditatum*);
  expanding citizenship expanded the tax base
• Military: Citizenship distinctions complicated recruitment
• Administrative: Legal uniformity simplified governance

Long-term significance:
• The distinction between Roman and non-Roman collapsed
• Provincial elites could now hold imperial office without special grants
• "Roman" became a legal status rather than an ethnic identity

> "After Caracalla, to be Roman was to be subject to Roman law, not to
> be born in Rome or descended from Romans. The empire became its law."
> — A.N. Sherwin-White, *The Roman Citizenship* (1973)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CITIZENSHIP AND THE FRAGMENT THESIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Per RLL–ROME–0006 (Fragment Inheritance Law):
> Universal authority cannot be wholly inherited after collapse.
> Multiple claimants will each hold fragments.

Citizenship was Rome's **structural prevention** of fragmentation:
• Alexander's generals could claim fragments because Macedonians were
  a ruling caste separate from conquered peoples
• Roman governors could not easily claim fragments because provincials
  were citizens with Roman identity
• The empire cohered because citizens identified with Rome, not with
  their governor

When fragmentation eventually occurred (395, 476, 800, 1453), it
followed fault lines where citizenship had weakened:
• Germanic federates who were never fully absorbed
• Religious division (Chalcedonian vs. non-Chalcedonian)
• Administrative division (East/West) hardened by separate courts

Citizenship delayed fragmentation by centuries. Its erosion enabled it.

IX. SCHOLAR CONSTRAINTS (RLLs)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This MEM demonstrates RLL–ROME–0006 (Fragment Inheritance) by showing
how citizenship PREVENTED the fragmentation that destroyed Alexander's

was denied to key groups (Germanic federates).
