I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Serbia** as Rome's **Balkan rival and Orthodox successor-state**: the medieval Serbian kingdom (Nemanjić dynasty) that grew from Byzantine vassalage to empire (Dušan's short-lived realm), adopted Orthodox Christianity, and contested Byzantine territory in the Balkans—before both were overrun by the Ottomans. For Rome/Byzantium, Serbia is **exposure within the exposure**: Orthodox in faith but politically independent; ally when convenient, expansionist when possible. Michael VIII Palaiologos faces Serbian expansion; the Balkans (MEM–ROME–GEO–BALKANS) encode the theatre.

Encoding: Serbia is not Byzantine territory in the high medieval period; it is **Orthodox competitor**—same confession, rival sovereignty. Collapse of Byzantine and Serbian power in the Balkans opens the way for Ottoman entry.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRUCTURAL CONTEXT — BALKANS EXPOSURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Medieval Serbia: Nemanjić dynasty; Serbian Orthodox Church (autocephalous 1219); expansion at Byzantine expense in Macedonia, Epirus, Albania. Stefan Dušan (r. 1331–1355): "Emperor of Serbs and Greeks"; crowns himself at Skopje; Serbia briefly the dominant Balkan power. Byzantine recovery (e.g. under Michael VIII) checks but does not eliminate Serbian pressure. Pattern: **vassal to rival**—Serbia rises as Byzantium weakens; both later fall to the Ottomans (Kosovo 1389; Constantinople 1453).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. KEY ENCODINGS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**Michael VIII Palaiologos:** Serbian expansion as one pressure among many; Balkan theatre. **Balkans as exposure core:** Serbia exemplifies the Balkans' resistance to stable hegemony—Orthodox polity that could align or oppose Constantinople. **Ottoman conquest:** Serbian and Byzantine territories absorbed by Ottoman expansion (14th–15th c.); Kosovo (1389) encodes Serbian defeat; Serbia becomes Ottoman domain. Pattern: **shared faith, divided sovereignty**; then shared loss.
