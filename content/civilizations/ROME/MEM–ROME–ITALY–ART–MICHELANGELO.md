I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Michelangelo as **Rome's divine sculptor**:
the artist who liberated form from stone, painted the ceiling
of Christendom, and crowned St. Peter's with a dome.

Michelangelo did not merely create art.
He **defined what art could be**.

Called *Il Divino* ("the Divine One") in his own lifetime,
he was the first Western artist to have biographies written
while he still lived.

He represents **the artist as hero**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FLORENTINE FORMATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Michelangelo was born in 1475 in Caprese, near Florence.

Background:
• Minor Florentine nobility (father was a government administrator)
• Raised in Florence after family's return
• Apprenticed to Domenico Ghirlandaio (1488)
• Patronized by Lorenzo de' Medici (lived in Medici household 1490–1492)

Lorenzo placed Michelangelo in the Medici sculpture garden,
where he studied classical antiquities.

Will Durant notes:

> "Lorenzo saw in Michelangelo's early works the fury of genius.
> He took the boy into his own household and treated him like a son.
> Michelangelo ate at Lorenzo's table and absorbed the philosophy
> of the Platonic Academy."
> — Will Durant, *The Renaissance*

When Lorenzo died (1492), Florence fell to Savonarola.
Michelangelo fled.

See: MEM–ROME–ITALY–MEDICI, MEM–ROME–ITALY–SAVONAROLA

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE PIETÀ (1497–1499)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At age 23, Michelangelo carved the *Pietà* in Rome.

The work depicts Mary holding the dead Christ.

Innovations:
• Mary is young — almost Christ's contemporary — not aged
• The composition is pyramidal, stable, serene
• The marble is polished to impossible smoothness
• It is the only work Michelangelo ever signed

Vasari records:

> "It is a miracle that a formless block of stone should have
> been reduced to a perfection that nature herself rarely creates
> in the flesh."
> — Giorgio Vasari, *Lives of the Artists*

The *Pietà* established Michelangelo's reputation before age 25.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. DAVID (1501–1504)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In Florence, Michelangelo was given a damaged block of marble —
"the Giant" — that two sculptors had already abandoned.

From this ruined block, he carved *David*.

The work depicts David before the battle with Goliath:
• 17 feet tall
• Tense, coiled, vigilant
• Not triumphant — preparing

Durant captures the political meaning:

> "The David was Florence's statement: a republic, young and
> defiant, standing against giants. Michelangelo gave Florence
> its self-image in stone."
> — Will Durant, *The Renaissance*

The statue was placed before the Palazzo della Signoria —
the seat of government.
Art became civic identity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE SISTINE CHAPEL CEILING (1508–1512)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pope Julius II commissioned Michelangelo to paint the Sistine ceiling.

Michelangelo resisted:
• He considered himself a sculptor, not a painter
• He suspected rivals (possibly Bramante) had set him up to fail
• The ceiling was 12,000 square feet

He painted it anyway — largely alone — over four years.

The program:
• Genesis narratives (Creation, Fall, Flood)
• Prophets and Sibyls
• Ancestors of Christ
• 300+ figures in total

The central image — *The Creation of Adam* — became the most
reproduced religious image in Western art.

Durant observes:

> "Michelangelo painted the Sistine ceiling lying on his back on
> scaffolding, paint dripping into his eyes, his body twisted in
> agony. He emerged from the work half-blind, his neck permanently
> strained. He had given Rome its vision of God."
> — Will Durant, *The Renaissance*

The ceiling was unveiled in 1512.
Michelangelo was 37.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. THE LAST JUDGMENT (1536–1541)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Twenty-four years after the ceiling, Michelangelo returned
to the Sistine Chapel.

Pope Clement VII (then Paul III) commissioned *The Last Judgment*
on the altar wall.

The work depicts:
• Christ as Judge (muscular, dynamic, classical)
• The damned descending to Hell
• The saved rising to Heaven
• Over 300 figures, many nude

Controversy:
• The nudity offended Counter-Reformation sensibilities
• After Michelangelo's death, Daniele da Volterra was hired
  to paint draperies over the figures (earning him the nickname
  *Il Braghettone* — "the breeches-maker")

The *Last Judgment* is darker than the ceiling.
Michelangelo was 66 when he finished.
He had lived through the Sack of Rome (1527).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. ST. PETER'S BASILICA (1546–1564)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At age 71, Michelangelo was appointed architect of St. Peter's.

He inherited a half-built project begun by Bramante (1506)
and continued by Raphael, Peruzzi, and Sangallo.

Michelangelo's contributions:
• Simplified the plan to a Greek cross
• Designed the dome (completed after his death by Giacomo della Porta)
• Unified the exterior design

He refused payment:

> "I am undertaking this work for the love of God and in honor
> of the Apostle. I want no salary."
> — Michelangelo (attributed)

The dome of St. Peter's became the model for domes worldwide:
St. Paul's in London, the U.S. Capitol, Les Invalides in Paris.

Michelangelo worked on St. Peter's until his death in 1564.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. MICHELANGELO VS. LEONARDO
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Michelangelo and Leonardo were contemporaries and rivals.

| Leonardo | Michelangelo |
|----------|--------------|
| Universal polymath | Specialist in human form |
| Gentle, curious | Fierce, tormented |
| Unfinished works | Completed commissions |
| Science enabling art | Theology inspiring art |
| Left no school | Founded Mannerism |

Leonardo sought to understand nature.
Michelangelo sought to **transcend** it.

Durant captures the difference:

> "Leonardo loved the world and studied it tenderly. Michelangelo
> raged against the world and bent it to his will. Leonardo was
> a scientist who painted. Michelangelo was a mystic who sculpted."
> — Will Durant, *The Renaissance*

Both defined genius differently.
Both were right.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. THE ARTIST AS HERO
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Michelangelo was the first artist treated as a hero.

Evidence:
• Three biographies written during his lifetime
  (Condivi, two editions of Vasari)
• Called *Il Divino* — the Divine — by contemporaries
• Popes negotiated with him, not commanded him
• His letters and poems survive as literary documents

He transformed the artist from craftsman to genius.

Vasari's conclusion:

> "While the best artists were still laboring in the darkness,
> God looked down and sent to earth Michelangelo Buonarroti,
> that in him alone might be contained all the perfections."
> — Giorgio Vasari, *Lives of the Artists*

After Michelangelo, the artist could claim divine status.

X. MEARSHEIMER SHARPENING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Structural analysis:

**What Michelangelo reveals:**
• Papal patronage concentrated resources for monumental art
• Counter-Reformation demanded art as propaganda
• Rome's centrality enabled careers impossible elsewhere
• The dome of St. Peter's was a power statement — Christendom united

**What he conceals:**
• Michelangelo had no political agency; patrons dictated subjects
• The artist's "freedom" was freedom within commission
• Rome's cultural dominance required military and fiscal power to sustain
• The Sack of Rome (1527) showed the fragility beneath the magnificence

Art serves power.
Power enables art.
Neither can be understood without the other.
