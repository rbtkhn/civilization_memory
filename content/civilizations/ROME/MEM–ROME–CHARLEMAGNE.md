I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Charlemagne as the **Western imperial rival**
from the Roman perspective: a Frankish king elevated by papal action
into an emperor whose coronation constituted a **legitimacy rupture**
with the Roman imperial continuity centered in Constantinople.

For Rome, Charlemagne represents **imperial substitution without
imperial succession**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ROMAN IMPERIAL CONTINUITY CLAIM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome maintains that imperial authority is continuous and singular.

Core premises:
• Imperium is indivisible
• Emperor derives legitimacy from Roman continuity, not papal fiat
• Western imperial vacancy does not authorize replacement

From this view, Charlemagne does not inherit empire—he **duplicates** it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PAPAL CORONATION AS LEGITIMACY BREACH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The coronation of 800 AD is treated as a unilateral act.

Roman interpretation:
• Papacy exceeds ecclesiastical mandate
• Sacred authority improperly converted into imperial grant
• Religious sanction used to fabricate political parity

The act creates a second emperor without lawful succession.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. FRANKISH POWER AS DE FACTO RULE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome acknowledges Charlemagne's material power.

Recognized realities:
• Military dominance in the West
• Administrative competence
• Enforcement capacity over territory

However:
→ Power without continuity ≠ legitimate imperium.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. IMPERIAL DUPLICATION PROBLEM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Charlemagne introduces a dual-emperor condition.

Consequences:
• Diplomatic ambiguity
• Titular inflation
• Long-term East–West rivalry
• Precedent for fragmented imperium

Empire shifts from singular continuity to competitive claims.

V.A FIRST FRAGMENTATION (800 AD — NOT 1453)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The 1453 fall of Constantinople is conventionally treated as the end of
Roman continuity. This is temporally correct but structurally misleading.

**Roman political authority first fragmented in 800 AD.**

When Pope Leo III crowned Charlemagne "Emperor of the Romans," he created
a rival claimant that Constantinople never recognized. From 800 onward,
two emperors claimed Roman succession:
• The Eastern emperor in Constantinople (continuous from Augustus)
• The Western emperor crowned by the Pope (discontinuous, papal-dependent)

The 1453 vacancy was specifically **Eastern Roman political authority**.
The Western claim — the Holy Roman Empire — persisted until Napoleon
dissolved it in 1806. Moscow's Third Rome claim competed with both.

This means:
• Roman political fragmentation began 653 years before Constantinople fell
• The Papacy, not the Ottomans, created the first legitimacy rupture
• 1453 ended one fragment; the other persisted until 1806
• Current analysis of "Roman succession" must account for this prior split

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ECCLESIASTICAL–IMPERIAL ENTANGLEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The papacy's role becomes politicized.

Outcomes:
• Church as kingmaker
• Fusion of altar and throne outside Roman control
• Weakening of imperial–ecclesiastical boundary

From Rome's view, this destabilizes Christian order.

VI.A MEARSHEIMER SHARPENING: PAPAL INCENTIVE CALCULATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Mercouris reading frames Leo III's coronation as legitimacy rupture —
a breach of proper Roman order by ecclesiastical overreach.

The realist sharpening asks: **Why did Leo III do it?**

Structural incentives for the Papacy in 800:
• Constantinople was distant and indifferent to papal concerns
• Lombard pressure on Rome required military protection
• Charlemagne had already demonstrated willingness to intervene
• Creating a Western emperor made the Papacy indispensable

The coronation was **liability management**, not theological aspiration.
By making Charlemagne emperor, Leo III:
• Secured Frankish military protection for Rome
• Created a Western power dependent on papal coronation for legitimacy
• Established the Papacy as kingmaker rather than supplicant
• Reduced dependence on unreliable Constantinople

> "Leo III's coronation of Charlemagne was not spiritual overreach but
> political survival. The Pope needed an emperor who needed him back.
> Constantinople offered protection in theory; Charlemagne offered it
> in practice — but only if properly bound by sacred obligation."
> — Rosamond McKitterick, *Charlemagne* (2008)

The polyphonic tension:
• Mercouris: 800 AD as legitimacy rupture violating Roman order
• Mearsheimer: 800 AD as rational papal security calculation

Both readings are valid. The coronation was BOTH breach AND survival strategy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LONG-TERM ROMAN ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Charlemagne's elevation is judged successful in power,
but corrosive in principle.

Roman memory emphasizes:
• Survival of Eastern imperial continuity
• Eventual absorption of Western imperial forms
• Persistence of Roman law and title as final arbiters

The West borrows empire; Rome retains it.

VII.A FRAGMENT INHERITANCE (RLL–ROME–0006 APPLICATION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Per RLL–ROME–0006 (Fragment Inheritance Law):
> Universal civilizational authority cannot be wholly inherited after
> collapse. Multiple claimants will each hold fragments and claim the
> rest, producing permanent rivalry rather than succession.

The Holy Roman Empire was not a successor to Rome — it was a **fragment
claimant** that held:
• Western territorial authority (Frankish, then Germanic lands)
• Papal-dependent legitimacy (coronation required)
• Latin Christian religious alignment

What the HRE did NOT hold:
• Eastern territories (Constantinople's domain)
• Structural continuity from Augustus
• Recognition from the actual Roman Empire (Constantinople)
• Orthodox Christian communion

The HRE's need to call itself "Roman" proves its fragmentary status.
Actual Roman authority in Constantinople never called itself "Eastern" —
it was simply *Rome*. The HRE's qualifier ("Holy," "Roman," "Empire")
compensated for what structure could not provide.

Voltaire's later observation — that the HRE was "neither holy, nor Roman,
nor an empire" — captures the fragment thesis precisely: the claim
exceeded the structural basis.
