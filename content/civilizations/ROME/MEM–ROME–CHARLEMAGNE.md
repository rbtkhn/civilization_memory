I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Charlemagne as the **Western imperial rival**
from the Roman perspective: a Frankish king elevated by papal action
into an emperor whose coronation constituted a **legitimacy rupture**
with the Roman imperial continuity centered in Constantinople.

For Rome, Charlemagne represents **imperial substitution without
imperial succession**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ROMAN IMPERIAL CONTINUITY CLAIM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome maintains that imperial authority is continuous and singular.

Core premises:
• Imperium is indivisible
• Emperor derives legitimacy from Roman continuity, not papal fiat
• Western imperial vacancy does not authorize replacement

From this view, Charlemagne does not inherit empire—he **duplicates** it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PAPAL CORONATION AS LEGITIMACY BREACH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The coronation of 800 CE is treated as a unilateral act.

Roman interpretation:
• Papacy exceeds ecclesiastical mandate
• Sacred authority improperly converted into imperial grant
• Religious sanction used to fabricate political parity

The act creates a second emperor without lawful succession.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. FRANKISH POWER AS DE FACTO RULE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome acknowledges Charlemagne’s material power.

Recognized realities:
• Military dominance in the West
• Administrative competence
• Enforcement capacity over territory

However:
→ Power without continuity ≠ legitimate imperium.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. IMPERIAL DUPLICATION PROBLEM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Charlemagne introduces a dual-emperor condition.

Consequences:
• Diplomatic ambiguity
• Titular inflation
• Long-term East–West rivalry
• Precedent for fragmented imperium

Empire shifts from singular continuity to competitive claims.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ECCLESIASTICAL–IMPERIAL ENTANGLEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The papacy’s role becomes politicized.

Outcomes:
• Church as kingmaker
• Fusion of altar and throne outside Roman control
• Weakening of imperial–ecclesiastical boundary

From Rome’s view, this destabilizes Christian order.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LONG-TERM ROMAN ASSESSMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Charlemagne’s elevation is judged successful in power,
but corrosive in principle.

Roman memory emphasizes:
• Survival of Eastern imperial continuity
• Eventual absorption of Western imperial forms
• Persistence of Roman law and title as final arbiters

The West borrows empire; Rome retains it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Imperium cannot be created by coronation alone.
• Papal sanctification does not equal Roman succession.
• Dual emperors fracture legitimacy grammar.
• Western empire emerges as imitation, not inheritance.
• Roman continuity persists despite Western assertion.

Tension preserved without verdict.
