I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Caspian Sea** as Rome’s most important **known
unknown**: a space fully mapped in imagination but never absorbed into
Roman political geometry.

The Caspian is not Roman space.
It is **conceptual frontier**.

Its memory encodes how empires incorporate distant regions cognitively
without attempting control.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC LOGIC — ENCLOSED BUT INACCESSIBLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Caspian Sea is geographically enclosed yet strategically remote.

Characteristics:
• No Mediterranean linkage
• Surrounded by steppe, mountains, and deserts
• Access mediated by hostile or autonomous powers

Strabo notes Rome’s limits:

> “The Caspian Sea is shut in on all sides by lands difficult to
> traverse, inhabited by warlike tribes; and although known to us by
> report, it is not within the range of our navigation or control.”
> — Strabo, *Geographica*, Book XI

Knowledge does not equal possession.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ROMAN STRATEGIC PERCEPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For Rome, the Caspian is:
• A barrier beyond Parthia and later Persia
• A corridor for nomadic incursions
• A conceptual edge of the inhabited world

Tacitus frames the Caspian as limit-space:

> “Beyond the Caspian lie peoples whom neither arms nor treaties have
> bound, and whose movements are governed by chance and pasture rather
> than law.”
> — Tacitus, *Annals*, Book VI

Rome plans *around* the Caspian, not toward it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PUBLIC DOMAIN SCHOLARSHIP — HORIZON GEOGRAPHY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Gibbon situates the Caspian within Roman fear calculus:

> “The regions beyond the Caspian were regarded with a distant anxiety,
> as sources from which new enemies might descend, but which no Roman
> policy could permanently secure.”
> — Edward Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*, Chapter XXVI

Mommsen emphasizes non-integration:

> “The Caspian basin lay outside the Roman world-system; it belonged to
> the geography of threat, not administration.”
> — Theodor Mommsen, *History of Rome*

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MODERN SCHOLARSHIP — STEPPE INTERFAAD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Peter Heather observes:
> “Rome’s real eastern frontier was psychological as much as military,
> and the Caspian marked the beginning of worlds governed by mobility,
> not cities.”

Anthony Kaldellis adds:
> “The Caspian Sea represents the outer limit of Roman geopolitical
> imagination—mapped, discussed, and feared, but never domesticated.”

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Caspian encodes **strategic awareness without ambition**.

Lessons preserved:
• Mapping precedes restraint
• Some regions exist only as threat vectors
• Control of neighbors matters more than distant conquest
• Empires fail when horizons become objectives

Rome endures by leaving the Caspian alone.
