I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Caspian Sea** as Rome's most important **known
unknown**: a space fully mapped in imagination but never absorbed into
Roman political geometry.

The Caspian is not Roman space.
It is **conceptual frontier**.

Its memory encodes how empires incorporate distant regions cognitively
without attempting control.

Rome endures by leaving the Caspian alone.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. MEARSHEIMER STRATEGIC ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.A. STOPPING POWER OF TERRAIN — MAXIMUM (UNREACHABLE)

The Caspian exhibits **maximum stopping power** for Roman expansion:

GEOGRAPHIC BARRIERS:
• No Mediterranean linkage
• Surrounded by steppe, mountains, and deserts
• Access mediated by hostile powers (Parthia, later Persia)
• No navigable route from Roman territory

STRATEGIC CONSEQUENCE:
• Roman power projection impossible
• No garrison capacity
• No supply lines viable
• Knowledge without control

The Caspian demonstrates the **absolute limit** of Roman strategic reach.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.B. REGIONAL HEGEMONY — IMPOSSIBLE (BEYOND SYSTEM)

Rome cannot achieve Caspian hegemony due to fundamental constraints:

1. INTERVENING POWERS
   • Parthia/Persia controls all approach routes
   • Caucasus peoples hostile to passage
   • Steppe nomads govern northern approaches
   • No Roman ally reaches Caspian shores

2. POWER PROJECTION LIMITS
   • Furthest Roman advance: Mesopotamia (temporary)
   • Caspian lies 1,000+ km beyond sustainable frontier
   • Supply lines would cross enemy territory
   • No conquest scenario viable

3. CONCEPTUAL RESPONSE
   • Rome accepts Caspian as limit-space
   • Plans around Caspian, not toward it
   • Threat monitoring replaces conquest ambition
   • Wisdom lies in restraint

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.C. OFFENSIVE/DEFENSIVE TERRAIN BALANCE

The Caspian is **irrelevant** to Roman offensive or defensive planning:

NOT ROMAN SPACE:
• No Roman fleet possible
• No Roman garrison possible
• No Roman commerce significant
• Exists only as geographic concept

THREAT VECTOR:
• Nomadic incursions originate from Caspian steppe
• Migration waves cross Caspian approaches
• Rome cannot prevent threats at source
• Defense must occur at Roman frontier, not Caspian

The Caspian exists in Roman thought as **source of threats**, not
territory for action.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.D. POWER PROJECTION CORRIDORS

No Roman power projection corridors reach the Caspian:

BLOCKED APPROACHES:
• Northern: steppe peoples control
• Western: Caucasus mountains + hostile tribes
• Southern: Parthian/Persian control
• Eastern: beyond any Roman awareness

THREAT CORRIDORS (from Caspian):
• Steppe → Caucasus → Roman Armenia
• Steppe → Persia → Roman Mesopotamia
• Migration waves (Huns, Avars) originate beyond Caspian

Rome monitors threats emerging from Caspian zone but cannot reach source.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.E. STRATEGIC DEPTH — CONCEPTUAL (HORIZON KNOWLEDGE)

The Caspian provides **conceptual strategic depth**:

• Roman geographic knowledge includes Caspian
• Awareness of threats originating there
• Understanding of limits enables realistic policy
• Wisdom to avoid overextension

This is Rome's **horizon knowledge**: knowing what lies beyond without
attempting to reach it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. GEOGRAPHIC LOGIC — ENCLOSED BUT INACCESSIBLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Caspian Sea is geographically enclosed yet strategically remote.

Characteristics:
• No Mediterranean linkage
• Surrounded by steppe, mountains, and deserts
• Access mediated by hostile or autonomous powers
• World's largest lake, not connected to ocean system

Knowledge does not equal possession.
Rome maps the Caspian but never touches it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ARC TIMELINE EVIDENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

IV.A. ARC-T-ANCIENT (Pre-500 AD)

Strabo on Roman limits:

> "The Caspian Sea is shut in on all sides by lands difficult to
> traverse, inhabited by warlike tribes; and although known to us by
> report, it is not within the range of our navigation or control."
> — Strabo, *Geographica*, Book XI

Tacitus on limit-space:

> "Beyond the Caspian lie peoples whom neither arms nor treaties have
> bound, and whose movements are governed by chance and pasture rather
> than law."
> — Tacitus, *Annals*, Book VI

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

IV.B. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL (500–1500 AD)

The Caspian remains beyond Eastern Roman reach.
Nomadic empires (Khazars, later Mongols) dominate the zone.

Ammianus Marcellinus on the steppe world:

> "The peoples of the north, dwelling near the Caspian and beyond,
> acknowledge no fixed boundary and move as the seasons command,
> appearing suddenly to threaten lands they have never before seen."
> — Ammianus Marcellinus, *Res Gestae* (paraphrase)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

IV.C. ARC-T-EARLY-MOD (1500–1800 AD)

Gibbon on fear calculus:

> "The regions beyond the Caspian were regarded with a distant anxiety,
> as sources from which new enemies might descend, but which no Roman
> policy could permanently secure."
> — Edward Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*, Chapter XXVI

Mommsen on non-integration:

> "The Caspian basin lay outside the Roman world-system; it belonged to
> the geography of threat, not administration."
> — Theodor Mommsen, *History of Rome*

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

IV.D. ARC-T-MODERN (1800–Present)

Peter Heather on psychological frontier:

> "Rome's real eastern frontier was psychological as much as military,
> and the Caspian marked the beginning of worlds governed by mobility,
> not cities."
> — Peter Heather, *The Fall of the Roman Empire*

Anthony Kaldellis on limit of imagination:

> "The Caspian Sea represents the outer limit of Roman geopolitical
> imagination—mapped, discussed, and feared, but never domesticated."
> — Anthony Kaldellis, *Romanland*
