I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Siena as **Florence's eternal rival**:
the Tuscan republic that challenged Florentine dominance through
banking, art, and war — and lost.

Siena was everything Florence was — except victorious.

Where Florence produced the Renaissance, Siena preserved the Gothic.
Where Florence conquered, Siena was conquered.
Where Florence survived, Siena became a provincial capital.

The rivalry defines both cities.
Siena's fall made Florence's Grand Duchy possible.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. MEDIEVAL BANKING POWER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Siena was Italy's leading banking center before Florence.

BANKING DOMINANCE (13th century):
• Sienese bankers served the papacy
• Bonsignori family: major international banking house
• Wool trade financed the banking system
• Monte dei Paschi (founded 1472): oldest surviving bank in the world

The bankruptcy of the Bonsignori (1298) shifted banking power:

Will Durant notes the transition:

> "When the Sienese bankers failed, the Florentines were waiting.
> The Medici built their empire on the ruins of Sienese finance."
> — Will Durant, *The Renaissance*

Siena's banking collapse created Florence's opportunity.
The Medici replaced the Bonsignori as papal bankers.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE GUELPH-GHIBELLINE CONFLICT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Siena was Ghibelline; Florence was Guelph.

FACTIONAL MEANING:
• **Guelph**: Pro-papal, urban commercial interests
• **Ghibelline**: Pro-imperial, feudal/military aristocracy

BATTLE OF MONTAPERTI (1260):
• Siena defeated Florence decisively
• Florentine Guelphs driven into exile
• Ghibelline highpoint in Tuscany

But the Guelph cause revived:
• Charles of Anjou invaded Italy (1266)
• Florentine Guelphs returned
• Siena's Ghibelline moment passed

The rivalry was ideological as well as commercial.
Siena chose the losing side.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. GOTHIC MAGNIFICENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Siena's architecture remains Gothic while Florence turned Renaissance.

MAJOR MONUMENTS:
• **Siena Cathedral** (Duomo): Black and white striped marble,
  unfinished expansion (plague interrupted)
• **Piazza del Campo**: Shell-shaped central square
• **Palazzo Pubblico**: Republican government seat
• **Torre del Mangia**: Bell tower dominating the campo

SIENESE PAINTING:
• Duccio di Buoninsegna (c. 1255–1318): Byzantine tradition refined
• Simone Martini (c. 1284–1344): International Gothic style
• Ambrogio Lorenzetti (c. 1290–1348): *Allegory of Good and Bad Government*

Siena's art looked backward to Byzantium.
Florence's art looked forward to classical revival.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE BLACK DEATH (1348)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The plague devastated Siena more than most Italian cities.

DEMOGRAPHIC COLLAPSE:
• Population fell from ~50,000 to ~15,000
• Cathedral expansion abandoned permanently
• Artistic production declined
• Banking already weakened since 1298

The Lorenzetti brothers both died in 1348.
Their *Allegory of Good Government* became an elegy for a vanished city.

Durant observes:

> "Siena never recovered from the Black Death. The city that had
> challenged Florence in banking and war became a provincial
> backwater, preserved in amber by its own decline."
> — Will Durant, *The Renaissance*

The plague froze Siena in its Gothic moment.
Florence rebuilt and advanced.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. REPUBLICAN SURVIVAL (1348–1555)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Despite plague and decline, Siena's republic survived two centuries.

POLITICAL STRUCTURE:
• Coalition government representing multiple social groups
• More broadly based than most Italian oligarchies
• Progressive taxation combining loans and indirect taxes
• Fiscal resilience despite demographic collapse

Siena's republican institutions proved adaptive:
• No single family dominated (unlike Medici Florence)
• Factionalism persisted but was managed
• Independence maintained through diplomacy

But survival was not victory.
Siena endured; Florence prospered.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. THE SIEGE AND FALL (1554–1555)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Siena's republic ended through Spanish-Florentine conquest.

SEQUENCE:
• 1552: Anti-Spanish revolt in Siena; French garrison invited
• 1553: Cosimo I de' Medici signs secret treaty with Charles V
• 1554 (January): Siege begins
• 1555 (April 21): Siena surrenders after 15-month siege
• 1559: Siena formally annexed to Duchy of Florence

The siege was brutal:
• Starvation within the walls
• Population expelled to reduce food consumption
• French garrison eventually withdrawn

Siena fell because:
• France could not sustain support
• Spain backed Florence
• Cosimo I was determined to unify Tuscany

The Republic of Siena, founded in the 12th century, ended in 1555.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. THE GRAND DUCHY OF TUSCANY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Siena's conquest enabled Florentine hegemony.

CONSEQUENCES:
• Cosimo I became Grand Duke of Tuscany (1569)
• Siena reduced to provincial status
• Tuscany unified under Medici rule
• Sienese institutions abolished

Siena's fall completed what the Black Death began.
The city that had rivaled Florence became Florence's possession.

See: MEM–ROME–ITALY–FLORENCE, MEM–ROME–ITALY–MEDICI

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. THE PALIO
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Palio survives as Siena's defining tradition.

STRUCTURE:
• Horse race around Piazza del Campo
• Held twice yearly (July 2, August 16)
• 17 *contrade* (neighborhoods) compete
• Medieval pageantry preserved

The Palio encodes Sienese identity:
• Neighborhood loyalty over city unity
• Competition as civic ritual
• Continuity despite political defeat

The republic died; the Palio lived.
Siena's independence survives as performance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. MEARSHEIMER SHARPENING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Structural analysis:

**What Siena reveals:**
• Banking collapse precedes political decline
• Plague accelerates existing weaknesses
• Republican institutions can outlast economic power
• Tuscan geography permits only one dominant city

**What Siena conceals:**
• Siena's Ghibelline alignment was a strategic error
• The rivalry with Florence was unwinnable once banking shifted
• French support was unreliable
• Spanish hegemony in Italy made independence impossible

Siena's fall was **structurally determined**:
Once Florence had banking, once Spain controlled Italy,
once the Black Death halved Siena's population —
the outcome was fixed.
