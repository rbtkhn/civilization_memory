I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Fourth Crusade as **Rome’s most catastrophic act
of civilizational self-harm**: the moment when a papally sanctioned
expedition against Islam was redirected into the violent sack of
Constantinople, the capital of Roman imperial continuity.

The Fourth Crusade represents **annihilation turned inward**.

It is not a deviation.
It is a revelation.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRUCTURAL PRECONDITIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Fourth Crusade emerges from accumulated structural failures:

• Papal claims without enforcement capacity  
• Financialization of crusading logistics  
• Venetian commercial leverage over sacred war  
• Dynastic instability in Constantinople  
• Mutual distrust between Latin and Greek Christendom  

Rome authorizes mobilization
but does not control direction.

This mirrors the earlier logic diagnosed in
MEM–ROME–CRUSADES.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. DEBT, VENICE, AND REDIRECTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Crusaders contract with Venice for transport.

When payment fails:
• Venice demands military services
• Crusading purpose becomes negotiable
• Sacred obligation is subordinated to debt resolution

This is not corruption.
It is structural dependency.

Commercial logistics seize command over moral authority.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CATEGORY A — LATE ANTIQUE / ROMAN CONTINUITY FOUNDATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Roman political culture never contemplated legitimacy through
intra-civilizational annihilation.

Ammianus Marcellinus articulates the Roman expectation of imperial unity:

> “The Roman world, though often shaken by internal discord, was bound
> together by a common reverence for law and order; and even in civil
> strife, the destruction of the state itself was never regarded as a
> lawful prize of victory.”  
> — Ammianus Marcellinus, *Res Gestae*, Book XIV

The Fourth Crusade violates this Roman inheritance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE SACK OF CONSTANTINOPLE (1204)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In April 1204, crusader forces breach Constantinople.

Outcomes:
• Widespread slaughter
• Systematic looting
• Desecration of churches
• Destruction of archives
• Dispersal of Roman administrative elites

This is not conquest.
It is **civilizational liquidation**.

Anthony Kaldellis analyzes the Fourth Crusade's devastating impact on Byzantine Roman identity:

> "The sack of Constantinople in 1204 represented the ultimate betrayal of Byzantine Roman identity, as Western Christians destroyed the Christian Roman Empire they claimed to defend, shattering the cultural and institutional continuity that had preserved Roman civilization in the East for nearly a millennium."
> — Anthony Kaldellis, *The New Roman Empire*

The crusade that killed the empire it was meant to save.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CATEGORY D — MEDIEVAL CONTINUITY TESTIMONY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Niketas Choniates records the event as an act beyond pagan cruelty:

> “Even the Saracens are merciful and kind compared with these men who
> bear the cross of Christ on their shoulders. They spared neither age
> nor sex, neither sacred nor profane; they shattered the holy vessels,
> trampled the icons underfoot, and treated the empire of the Romans as
> plunder to be divided among themselves.”  
> — Niketas Choniates, *History*, Book VIII

This testimony is not rhetorical.
It is diagnostic.

The sack destroys trust irreversibly.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CATEGORY B — PUBLIC-DOMAIN SCHOLARSHIP
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Edward Gibbon identifies the structural consequence:

> “The Fourth Crusade inflicted a wound from which the Greek empire
> never recovered. By the hands of Christians, the capital of the East
> was degraded and impoverished; and the empire, instead of being
> strengthened against its enemies, was left exposed to future ruin.”  
> — Edward Gibbon, *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*,
> Chapter LX

Rome is undone here not by Islam,
but by Latin Christianity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CATEGORY C — MODERN SCHOLARSHIP
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jonathan Phillips emphasizes the loss of moral legitimacy:

> “The capture of Constantinople in 1204 destroyed any remaining claim
> that the crusading movement served the unity of Christendom. Instead,
> it revealed the crusades as vehicles for power, profit, and coercion,
> capable of turning violently upon their own civilization.”  
> — Jonathan Phillips, *The Fourth Crusade and the Sack of Constantinople*

Anthony Kaldellis situates the event as Roman political catastrophe:

> “1204 was not simply a defeat, but a disintegration of Roman state
> capacity caused by external occupation and internal fragmentation.
> The Latin conquest shattered the administrative coherence that had
> sustained the empire for centuries.”  
> — Anthony Kaldellis, *The New Roman Empire*
