────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Crete as Rome's **connective island**: a maritime
platform that regulated movement between the central Mediterranean,
the Aegean, and the eastern provinces.

For Rome, Crete is neither an initiation island like Sicily nor a denial
anchor like Cyprus. It is a **circulatory regulator**—its function is
stabilization of flow, not extraction.

Empire survives by managing circulation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. MEARSHEIMER STRATEGIC ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.A. STOPPING POWER — TWO-SIDED MARITIME ACCESS

Crete provides **dual-basin access**:
• Northern coast faces Aegean
• Southern coast faces Egypt/Libya
• Harbors on both sides enable all-direction operations
• Two-sided exposure = strategic value + piracy risk

II.B. REGIONAL HEGEMONY — CIRCULATION DISCIPLINE

Rome annexes Crete (67 BC) for **circulation discipline**:
• Eliminate piracy base that disrupts imperial movement
• Convert predatory node into transit regulator
• Pompey's anti-piracy campaign as systemic intervention
• Transform disorder into governed corridor

II.C. POWER PROJECTION CORRIDORS

Crete controls Mediterranean transit routes:
• Sicily → Crete → Egypt (grain route)
• Italy → Crete → Levant (military movement)
• Aegean → Crete → North Africa (commercial)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ARC TIMELINE EVIDENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

III.A. ARC-T-ANCIENT

Strabo on maritime exposure:
> "Crete lies stretched along the sea with many harbors on either side,
> and because of this situation it became a rendezvous for pirates."
> — Strabo, *Geography*, Book X

Cassius Dio on anti-piracy logic:
> "The Romans resolved to put an end to piracy because no authority
> could be secure while the seas were lawless; thus Crete was subdued."
> — Cassius Dio, *Roman History*, Book XXXVI

III.B. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL

Gibbon on Byzantine reconquest:
> "He reduced the Island of Crete, and extirpated the nest of pirates
> who had so long defied the majesty of the empire."
> — Edward Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*, ch. 52

III.C. ARC-T-MODERN

Kaldellis on systemic role:
> "Emperors were custodians, not owners of the public space; islands
> like Crete served the system, not personal possession."
> — Anthony Kaldellis, *The New Roman Empire*

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Crete encodes **circulation governance**.

Lessons: Not all provinces extract value—some prevent disruption.
Maritime coherence depends on disciplined transit zones. Empire is
sustained by connectors as much as by cores.

Crete is Rome's circulatory island.
