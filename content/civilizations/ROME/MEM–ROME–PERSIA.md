────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Persia** as Rome's **eastern civilizational peer**: the empire that Rome could defeat in battle but never absorb—Parthian then Sasanian—whose sovereign kingship and deep hinterland denied Roman universalism a decisive eastern closure. For Rome, Persia is the **limit case**: the peer that exposed the structural ceiling of expansion and the cost of parity without subordination.

Encoding: Persia is not a province. Persia is **the rival that outlasts**—until both are hollowed by war and Islam absorbs the Sasanian realm. Rome never conquers Persia; Persia falls to another.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRUCTURAL PARITY — TWO UNIVERSALISMS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome and Persia confront one another as civilizational equals: standing armies, bureaucratic taxation, universalizing legitimacy. Rome seeks **incorporation** (law, citizenship); Persia seeks **denial** (sovereign kingship, balance, endurance). Mesopotamian conquests are cyclical—cities taken and lost; no durable client architecture. The frontier is **philosophy's battleground**: universal law vs sovereign kingship. See MEM–ROME–WAR–PERSIA for the conflict narrative.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. SILK ROAD & INTERMEDIARY DEPENDENCY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Persia controls the **middle** of the Silk Road. Rome is demand terminus (Syria, Egypt); Persia holds the corridor. Rome pays in gold for eastern luxuries; Persia profits from transit. Structural consequence: intermediary advantage—whoever holds the middle has leverage over both ends. Rome cannot bypass Persia. See MEM–ROME–SILK–ROAD.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. EXHAUSTION & ISLAMIC DISPLACEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The terminal Roman–Persian war (602–628) exhausts both empires. Heraclius wins; both are hollow. Islamic conquest absorbs Sasanian territory (651) and Roman eastern provinces (Syria, Egypt, Mesopotamia). Persia does not fall to Rome; Persia and Rome both lose to a third force. The encoding: **parity erodes both**; exhaustion is more terminal than defeat.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. BARNES DIMENSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**Jurisdiction:** No single Roman jurisdiction ever extended over Persia; authority stopped at the frontier. **Defection incentive:** Client kingdoms (e.g. Armenia) could flip between Rome and Persia; neither could lock in the other's periphery permanently. **Irreversibility:** Roman failure to absorb Persia is irreversible—Persia is never a Roman province. The liability dimension is **structural** (civilizational limit) rather than personal.
