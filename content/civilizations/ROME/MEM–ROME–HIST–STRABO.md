I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Strabo as **Rome’s cartographer of order**: the thinker
who translates Greek geographic science into a Roman imperial framework,
rendering space legible, governable, and strategically comparable.

Strabo does not merely describe the world.
He **organizes it for rule**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRABO’S POSITION IN ROMAN CIVILIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strabo writes as a Greek intellectual under Roman sovereignty.

Key features:
• Greek language and method
• Roman political horizon
• Augustan-era consolidation
• Geography oriented toward governance

His *Geography* is not neutral science.
It is **imperial intelligence**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. GEOGRAPHY AS POLITICAL KNOWLEDGE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strabo defines geography as a discipline serving rulers.

He states this explicitly:

> “Geography is a subject which one would suppose to be of interest to
> the philosopher, and particularly to him who is engaged in the
> administration of states; for it describes the earth we inhabit, the
> seas that surround it, and the various nations, their positions, and
> their characters.”
> — Strabo, *Geography*, Book I

Knowledge of space precedes command of space.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. LIMITS, BOUNDARIES, AND CIVILIZATIONAL ORDER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strabo is preoccupied with edges.

On the western boundary:

> “At the extremities of the inhabited world stand the Pillars of
> Heracles, which mark the limit of the Inner Sea and the beginning of
> the great ocean beyond.”
> — Strabo, *Geography*, Book III

Boundaries are not failures.
They are **structural decisions**.

Rome defines itself by where it stops.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ETHNOGRAPHY AND GOVERNABILITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strabo links geography to character.

He assesses peoples in terms of:
• Terrain
• Climate
• Customs
• Political receptivity

This supports Roman differentiation between:
• Core regions
• Assimilable provinces
• Perpetual frontiers

Geography becomes predictive of governance cost.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. STRABO AND AUGUSTAN STABILITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strabo’s work aligns with Augustan consolidation.

Themes:
• Completion of conquest
• Stabilization of borders
• Transition from expansion to administration

The world is mapped because it is now **closed**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strabo encodes **spatial rationality**.

Lessons:
• Empires require comparative geography.
• Space must be described before it can be ruled.
• Boundaries are instruments of order.
• Knowledge stabilizes conquest.

Strabo gives Rome a map of itself.
