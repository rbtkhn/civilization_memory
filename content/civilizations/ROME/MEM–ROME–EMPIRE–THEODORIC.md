I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Theodoric as **Rome’s western continuity custodian**:
the ruler who governed Italy after the fall of the Western Empire by
preserving Roman law, administration, and elite culture under barbarian
kingship, without attempting imperial restoration.

Theodoric represents **Roman governance without Roman sovereignty**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT: ROME AFTER IMPERIAL COLLAPSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
By the late fifth century, the Western Roman Empire no longer exists as a
sovereign political entity.

Conditions:
• Imperial office extinguished (AD 476)
• Roman senatorial elite still active
• Administrative habits intact
• Military power held by barbarian foederati

Italy remains Roman in **practice**, not in authority.

Edward Gibbon frames the post-imperial condition with characteristic clarity:

> “The empire of the West was extinguished, but the laws, the manners, and
> the language of Rome still governed the provinces. The barbarian conquerors,
> who respected the arts and institutions of the vanquished, assumed the
> modest title of kings, and were content to rule under the forms of Roman
> administration which they neither understood nor wished to destroy.”
> — Edward Gibbon, *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*,
> Vol. IV, ch. 39

The problem is no longer survival of empire,
but survival of Roman order.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THEODORIC’S POSITION: KING, NOT EMPEROR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Theodoric rules Italy (493–526) as king of the Ostrogoths, formally under
Eastern Roman authorization, but practically autonomous.

He does not:
• Claim the imperial title
• Restore Western emperorship
• Replace Roman law

Instead, he governs **through Roman forms**.

Cassiodorus records Theodoric’s own articulation of rule:

> “Our royalty is an imitation of yours, a copy of the only empire; and in
> so far as we follow your example, we surpass all other nations. Often you
> have commanded us to love the Senate, to accept willingly the laws of past
> emperors, and to unite in our justice the traditions of old Rome with the
> valor of our people.”
> — Cassiodorus, *Variae*

Authority is derived not from conquest alone,
but from **continuity performance**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ADMINISTRATION AS CONTINUITY MECHANISM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Theodoric preserves Roman civil administration almost intact.

Features:
• Roman law applied to Roman subjects
• Gothic law applied to Goths
• Senate retained ceremonial and advisory role
• Taxation and bureaucracy maintained

Theodor Mommsen identifies this as a decisive transformation:

> “The Roman state had perished, but Roman administration survived. In the
> Ostrogothic kingdom the machinery of imperial government continued to
> operate under new masters, and the continuity of Roman legal and fiscal
> practice was preserved more completely than under any earlier barbarian
> rule.”
> — Theodor Mommsen, *The History of Rome*, Book V

Rome no longer commands.
Rome **administers**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATION WITHOUT SOVEREIGNTY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Theodoric rules a divided society.

Structural separation:
• Goths: military elite, Arian Christian
• Romans: civilian population, Nicene Christian
• Law and identity kept parallel, not fused

Anthony Kaldellis emphasizes the novelty of this arrangement:

> “Theoderic did not attempt to make Italy Gothic, nor did he try to revive
> the Western Empire. Instead, he presided over a post-imperial Roman polity
> in which Roman institutions functioned without Roman sovereignty, a state
> that was Roman in its practices but no longer Roman in its source of power.”
> — Anthony Kaldellis, *The New Roman Empire*

This is continuity without universality.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. THEODORIC AS ROMANIZED BARBARIAN KING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Hans-Ulrich Wiemer, the leading modern specialist on Theodoric, emphasizes
the deliberate limits of his rule:

> “Theoderic consciously renounced imperial claims. His aim was not a
> restoration of the Western Roman Empire, but the stabilization of Italy
> through Roman administrative traditions under a Gothic kingship that
> accepted the irreversibility of imperial collapse.”
> — Hans-Ulrich Wiemer, *Theoderich der Große*

Theodoric governs within accepted boundaries:
• No universal claims
• No expansion ideology
• No theological coercion

Stability is achieved by **not exceeding legitimacy capacity**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. RELIGION AS FAULT LINE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Religious division remains unresolved.

Dynamics:
• Theodoric protects Nicene institutions
• Maintains Arian Gothic identity
• Avoids forced conversion

But:
• Eastern Roman suspicion grows
• Papal–imperial relations complicate balance
• Religious unity proves unachievable

Gibbon records the fragility of tolerance:

> “The toleration of Theodoric was dictated by prudence rather than principle;
> and the seeds of discord, which his wisdom had repressed, sprang up with
> fatal vigor when his authority was no longer able to restrain them.”
> — Edward Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*, Vol. IV, ch. 39

Tolerance functions only while authority endures.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. LIMITS & AFTERMATH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After Theodoric’s death (526):
• Succession falters
• Roman–Gothic balance collapses
• Eastern Roman reconquest follows
• Italy is devastated

The continuity Theodoric preserved
cannot survive without him.

Mommsen summarizes the structural weakness:

> “The system depended on the personal authority of the ruler; once that
> authority vanished, the delicate balance between Roman administration and
> barbarian power collapsed, and the attempt to preserve Rome without empire
> was exposed as transitory.”
> — Theodor Mommsen, *The History of Rome*, Book V

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CIVILIZATIONAL ROLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Theodoric demonstrates a final western Roman lesson:

• Civilization can persist without sovereignty
• Administration can outlive empire
• Legitimacy can be borrowed but not inherited
• Continuity without renewal is fragile

He preserves Rome —
but cannot reproduce it.
