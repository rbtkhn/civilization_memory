This file is a **monument**: a reference model for other STATE files and MEMs
to compare against and find connections from. The Roman state ended in 1453;
there are no live material options for Rome. The file documents the concluded
entity, its succession and fragmentation, and the patterns (RLLs, doctrines)
that govern how successor claimants and other civilizations may be analysed
in relation to Roman inheritance. Use this file for: fragment-claimant
comparison; MEM connection discovery (Roman MEMs → other civs); doctrine/RLL
reference when assessing any successor or parallel case; and **historical
simulations**. The entity is frozen in time at 1453 (conclusion), but users
may **traverse anywhere along the timeline** for simulation — e.g. run
STATE–Rome as if at 800 (Charlemagne), 1204 (Fourth Crusade), or earlier
(Republic, Principate) — and explore what-if, counterfactuals, or
period-specific decision contexts grounded in the Roman pattern set.

Duty of Competence: Not applicable to concluded entities. When this file is
loaded in STATE mode for comparison or connection discovery, the system
surfaces inheritance flows, fragment claimants, and pattern references
without generating material options for Rome itself.

MEM DISCOVERY: MEM–RELEVANCE–ROME does not exist. MEM discovery for Roman
content uses this file's Section VII (Decision-Relevant History) and
CIV–INDEX–ROME plus MEM CONNECTIONS as fallback.

I. ENTITY IDENTIFICATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Name: Rome / Roman state (Res publica Romana; Imperium Romanum; Eastern
Roman / Byzantine political authority)
Classification: FRAGMENTED-SOURCE
Active: NO
**Temporal bounds:** 753 BC (mythical founding of the city of Rome) – 1453
(fall of Constantinople). Used for timeline traversal and simulation.

Conclusion: 1453 (fall of Constantinople; Eastern Roman political authority
terminated). Western Roman imperial authority had ended earlier (476/480);
religious and institutional continuity persisted in the Papacy and in
Latin civilizational grammar (CIV–CORE–ROME: transcontinental Latin
civilization).

Phase history (summary):
• Republic (to late 1st c. BC) — senatorial, mixed constitution, expansion
• Principate (Augustus to 3rd c.) — imperial continuity, citizenship expansion
• Dominate (late 3rd c. onward) — formal autocracy, administrative division
• East/West split — political and later doctrinal divergence; Constantinople
  as Eastern capital; Rome (Papacy) as religious and symbolic center in West
• Eastern Roman (Byzantine) state to 1453 — Ottoman conquest; political
  authority not claimed by conqueror (vacancy by declination, RLL–ROME–0002)

The Roman **state** as a single political entity concluded in 1453. The
**civilizational grammar** (law, procedure, legitimacy-first sovereignty,
jurisdictional power, mediation reflex) persists in CIV–CORE–ROME as
transcontinental Latin civilization — a cultural and institutional
inheritance, not a state. This STATE file describes the concluded state
and its fragment claimants; it does not describe the living polities
that operate within the Latin grammar (those have or may have their own
STATE files: e.g. France, Italy, EU-related entities).

II. SUCCESSION AND INHERITANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

INTERNAL SUCCESSION (within Roman political forms):
Republic → Principate → Dominate → Tetrarchy / East–West division →
Eastern Roman Empire (to 1453); Western imperial office lapsed (476/480).
Type: DIRECT_CONTINUATION across state-form changes until territorial
and institutional split; thereafter Eastern line continued until 1453.

FRAGMENTATION (per RLL–ROME–0006, DOCTRINE 06, 07):
Universal authority cannot be wholly inherited after collapse. Multiple
claimants hold fragments and claim the rest; rivalry is structural.

SUCCESSOR / FRAGMENT CLAIMANTS (typed):

• **Papacy (Rome)**
  Type: IDEOLOGICAL_CLAIM + INSTITUTIONAL_TRANSFER
  Fragment: Religious authority in the West; canon law, spiritual primacy.
  Inheritance mechanism: Immobility (Peter's tomb anchored the see when
  political authority relocated to Constantinople); default inheritance
  when mobile institutions defected (RLL–ROME–0003 proposed). Source:
  CIV–SCHOLAR–ROME SYNTHESIS 0018, 0019; MEM–ROME–PAPACY.

• **Holy Roman Empire (800–1806)**
  Type: IDEOLOGICAL_CLAIM
  Fragment: Political "Roman" title in the West; no structural continuity
  with ancient Rome. Created by Papal coronation of Charlemagne (800);
  Constantinople never recognized it. First structural fragmentation of
  Roman political authority (prior to 1453). Source: SYNTHESIS 0019, 0021;
  MEM–ROME–CHARLEMAGNE, MEM–ROME–HOLY–ROMAN–EMPIRE.

• **Moscow (Third Rome)**
  Type: IDEOLOGICAL_CLAIM
  Fragment: Political and Orthodox religious claim to Roman succession
  after 1453. Claimant without possession of Constantinople or institutional
  continuity; fragment claimant. See CIV–STATE–RUSSIA, MEM–RUSSIA–THIRD–ROME.

• **Ottoman state (1453 onward)**
  Type: CONQUEST_ABSORPTION + DECLINATION
  Possessed Constantinople, administration, and structural power but
  **declined** to claim Roman succession as primary legitimacy (Islamic
  identity preferred). Declination created **genuine vacancy** in Roman
  political authority (RLL–ROME–0002: vacancy by declination). Source:
  SYNTHESIS 0029; MEM–ROME–WAR–MEHMED–II.

CONTESTED CLAIMS:
All four above claimed or could claim fragments; none inherited the whole.
Rivalry is permanent under the fragment thesis. No single successor state
to the Eastern Roman political authority.

INHERITED PATTERNS (for reference by other STATE files and MEMs):
• Fragment inheritance (RLL–ROME–0006, DOCTRINE 07): collapse → fragments,
  not succession.
• Vacancy by declination (RLL–ROME–0002, DOCTRINE 06): authority vacant
  when possessor declines to claim.
• Prior fragmentation (800: Charlemagne; 1054: Great Schism): legitimacy
  fractures before visible collapse.
• Absorption vs annihilation (DOCTRINE 11): Carthage annihilated → no
  fragment competition; Constantinople absorbed (territorially) but title
  declined → vacancy and fragment claimants.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. STRATEGIC POSITION (THREE PERSPECTIVES)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
*Applied to the conclusion and fragmentation of the Roman state, not to
live options.*

A) LEGITIMACY AND INSTITUTIONAL CONTINUITY
────────────────────────────────────────────
Roman legitimacy bifurcated: political authority to Constantinople,
religious authority to Rome (Papacy). When Eastern political authority
fell (1453), the religious fragment (Papacy) and the Western political
fragment (HRE) had already established rival claims. Legitimacy did not
vanish; it had already been translated and split (CIV–CORE–ROME: authority
translation over restoration, DOCTRINE 02). The conclusion of the state
revealed the prior fragmentation rather than causing it.

B) POWER DISTRIBUTION AND STRUCTURAL CONSTRAINT
────────────────────────────────────────────
Structurally, no single successor could hold the whole. The Ottomans had
material control of the center but declined the title; Moscow and the HRE
claimed the title without the center. Vacancy by declination (RLL–0002)
describes the actual power dynamic: possession without claim creates
vacancy; claimants without structural basis cannot fill it. Fragment
inheritance (RLL–0006) is the structural law: universal authority
fragments after collapse.

C) LEADERSHIP LIABILITY AND MECHANISM
────────────────────────────────────────────
Who inherited what, and by what mechanism? Papacy inherited by immobility
(could not defect; Peter's tomb). HRE was created by papal act (liability
management: Frankish protection in exchange for imperial coronation).
Ottomans calculated that Islamic legitimacy dominated Roman claim. Moscow
claimed the mantle absent the geography. Each fragment-claimant's behaviour
can be assessed for liability exposure and defection incentive when
analysing successor states (e.g. CIV–STATE–RUSSIA for Third Rome rhetoric).

Tensions preserved: Legitimacy narrative (translation, continuity) vs
structural reality (fragmentation, vacancy) vs liability distribution
(whose claim, whose risk). No resolution required; the entity is concluded.

VII. DECISION-RELEVANT HISTORY (PATTERNS — REFERENCE)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Patterns below are CANONICAL or REFERENCE. They do not drive live
decision options for Rome; they support analysis of successor states
and cross-civilizational comparison. Activation: REFERENCE (use when
assessing fragment claimants or connecting MEMs).

Pattern 1 — Fragment inheritance (RLL–ROME–0006, DOCTRINE 06, 07)
• Universal authority cannot be wholly inherited after collapse. Multiple
  claimants hold fragments; each claims the rest; permanent rivalry.
• Source: CIV–SCHOLAR–ROME SYNTHESIS 0017, 0018–0021; ENTRIES 0030, 0031.
• MEMs: MEM–ROME–PAPACY, MEM–ROME–HOLY–ROMAN–EMPIRE, MEM–ROME–CONSTANTINOPLE,
  MEM–ROME–GREEK–ALEXANDER (Diadochi precedent).
Activation: REFERENCE. Use when assessing any successor or claimant to
Roman or universal authority.

Pattern 2 — Vacancy by declination (RLL–ROME–0002, DOCTRINE 06)
• Political authority becomes genuinely vacant when the possessor of
  structural power declines to claim the title. Ottomans possessed
  Constantinople but declined Roman succession.
• Source: SYNTHESIS 0029; ENTRY 0026; MEM–ROME–WAR–MEHMED–II.
Activation: REFERENCE. Use when assessing whether a conqueror or holder
of assets claims the predecessor's legitimacy.

Pattern 3 — Prior fragmentation (800, 1054)
• Legitimacy fragmentation predates visible collapse. Charlemagne's
  coronation (800) created Western imperial claimant Constantinople
  never recognized; Great Schism (1054) formalized doctrinal split.
• Source: SYNTHESIS 0019, 0021; MEM–ROME–CHARLEMAGNE, MEM–ROME–HOLY–ROMAN–EMPIRE.
Activation: REFERENCE. Use when dating when authority actually split
vs when the final political entity fell.

Pattern 4 — Bifurcation (political vs religious authority)
• Roman legitimacy bifurcated: political to Constantinople, religious to
  Papacy. Immobility (Papacy) inherited religious authority by default.
• Source: ENTRIES 0025, 0027; SYNTHESIS 0016, 0018; RLL–ROME–0003 (proposed).
• MEMs: MEM–ROME–PAPACY, MEM–ROME–CITY.
Activation: REFERENCE. Use when analysing split legitimacy (e.g. caliphate
vs sultanate, spiritual vs temporal).

Pattern 5 — Absorption vs annihilation (DOCTRINE 11)
• Annihilation closes vacancy (no fragments, no claimants). Absorption
  leaves identity intact; Rome absorbed many enemies; Carthage was
  annihilated (146 BC) — no fragment competition.
• Source: CIV–DOCTRINE–ROME 11; MEM–ROME–WAR–THIRD–PUNIC, MEM–ROME–CARTHAGE.
Activation: REFERENCE. Use when comparing how empires end (erasure vs
fragment inheritance).

Pattern 6 — Institutional absorption of shock (DOCTRINE 01)
• Rome survived crises by absorbing defeat into institutional continuity.
  Applicable to successor states that invoke Roman precedent (resilience,
  procedure over vindication).
• Source: CIV–DOCTRINE–ROME 01.
Activation: REFERENCE.

Pattern 7 — Systems defeat genius (DOCTRINE 09)
• Institutional depth prevailed over individual brilliance (e.g. Hannibal
  vs Roman replacement capacity). Reference for assessing structural
  vs operational advantage in any civilization.
• Source: CIV–DOCTRINE–ROME 09; MEM–ROME–WAR–ZAMA, MEM–ROME–WAR–SECOND–PUNIC.
Activation: REFERENCE.
