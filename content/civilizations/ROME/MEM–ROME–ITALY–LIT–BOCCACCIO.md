I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Boccaccio as **Rome's vernacular bridge**:
the figure who translated humanist ideals into Italian prose,
grounded classical learning in contemporary life, and made
the ancient world accessible beyond Latin scholars.

If Petrarch was humanism's prophet,
Boccaccio was its **infrastructure**.

He recovered manuscripts, taught Greek, wrote encyclopedias,
and composed the *Decameron* — a work that made Italian prose
a vehicle for serious literature.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. THE DECAMERON: PLAGUE AS FRAME
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Black Death struck Florence in 1348.

Boccaccio survived. He responded with the *Decameron*:
a hundred tales told by ten young Florentines fleeing the plague.

Structure:
• Frame narrative: flight from death
• Stories: love, wit, fortune, clerical corruption
• Tone: worldly, comic, erotic, unsentimental

The *Decameron* begins with the plague's devastation:

> "In the year 1348 of our Lord, there occurred in the city of
> Florence, the most beautiful of all Italian cities, a deadly
> pestilence... How many valiant men, how many beautiful women,
> how many gallant youths... breakfasted in the morning with
> their relatives and friends, and that same evening dined with
> their ancestors in the other world!"
> — Boccaccio, *Decameron*, Introduction

Civilization survives by telling stories.
The *Decameron* is literature as continuity mechanism.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. NAPLES: FORMATIVE YEARS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Boccaccio spent his youth in Naples (c. 1327–1340).

Naples under the Angevins:
• Courtly culture
• French influence
• Vernacular literature encouraged
• Commercial wealth of Boccaccio's merchant father

The Neapolitan court exposed Boccaccio to:
• Romance literature
• Aristocratic manners
• Women of education and wit

His early works (*Filocolo*, *Filostrato*) emerged from this milieu.

Durant notes:

> "Naples gave Boccaccio what Florence could not — the graces
> of a court, the company of cultured women, the leisure to
> develop a prose style that would charm all Europe."
> — Will Durant, *The Renaissance*

Naples formed the writer.
Florence disciplined the scholar.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PARTNERSHIP WITH PETRARCH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Boccaccio met Petrarch in 1350.

The relationship was asymmetrical:
• Petrarch: elder, famous, Latin-focused
• Boccaccio: younger, admiring, vernacular-talented

But Boccaccio contributed materially:
• Recovered manuscripts
• Promoted Greek learning
• Defended Petrarch's reputation
• Built institutional foundations

Boccaccio brought the Greek scholar Leontius Pilatus to Florence
to lecture on Homer — the first such lectures in Western Europe
in centuries.

Petrarch wrote; Boccaccio organized.

See: MEM–ROME–ITALY–LIT–PETRARCH

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CLASSICAL RECOVERY WORK
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Boccaccio was a manuscript hunter and encyclopedist.

Major contributions:
• *Genealogy of the Pagan Gods* — systematic classical mythology
• *On Famous Women* — biographical encyclopedia
• *On the Fates of Illustrious Men* — moral exempla from history
• Recovery of Tacitus manuscripts at Monte Cassino

These works made classical knowledge usable.
They were reference works, not merely literary performances.

Boccaccio treated antiquity as **information to be organized**,
not just beauty to be admired.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. THE ANTI-CLERICAL VOICE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The *Decameron* is relentless on clerical hypocrisy.

Friars, monks, abbots, and priests populate the tales as:
• Seducers
• Gluttons
• Frauds
• Hypocrites

Boccaccio's satire is social, not theological.
He mocks the men, not the faith.

Example:

> "A monk who had committed a sin deserving of the gravest
> punishment cunningly accused his abbot of the same fault,
> and escaped from his peril."
> — Boccaccio, *Decameron*, Day I, Story 4 (summary)

This is Italian realism before Machiavelli:
institutions are only as good as their operators.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. VERNACULAR LEGITIMATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Dante proved Italian could be sublime.
Boccaccio proved Italian could be **various**.

The *Decameron* contains:
• Tragedy
• Comedy
• Farce
• Romance
• Satire

Boccaccio's prose became the model for Italian narrative.
Chaucer imitated it in English.
European novella traditions descend from it.

Petrarch sought immortality in Latin and found it in Italian sonnets.
Boccaccio sought entertainment in Italian and created a genre.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. LATE-LIFE TURN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In his later years, Boccaccio experienced religious crisis.

He regretted the *Decameron's* worldliness.
He turned to scholarship and piety.
He delivered public lectures on Dante's *Divine Comedy* —
the first such lectures, establishing Dante's canonical status.

Boccaccio's trajectory:
• Youth: courtly romance
• Maturity: comic realism
• Age: scholarly piety

This is the Italian pattern:
worldliness redeemed by learning, learning by devotion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. MEARSHEIMER SHARPENING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Structural analysis:

**What Boccaccio reveals:**
• Plague as accelerant — social disruption enables cultural innovation
• Vernacular prose creates broader audience than Latin scholarship
• Manuscript recovery requires patronage networks and travel
• Encyclopedia-making is infrastructure, not ornament

**What he conceals:**
• *Decameron's* social realism had no political program
• Boccaccio never held office; influence was cultural, not institutional
• Greek revival depended on Byzantine refugees and Italian wealth

Boccaccio made humanism transmissible.
He did not make it governable.
