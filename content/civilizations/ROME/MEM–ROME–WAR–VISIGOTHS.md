I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Visigoths** as Rome’s first successful
**internal secession**: a people transformed from federate dependents
into an autonomous military–political entity operating within imperial
territory.

The Visigoths do not destroy Rome.
They **outgrow Roman control**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FROM GOTHS TO VISIGOTHS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Visigoths emerge from the broader Gothic confederation after AD 378.

Key characteristics:
• Permanent military identity
• Mobile population with leadership continuity
• Dependence on Roman logistics without submission

Ammianus observes the transition:

> “They wandered not as exiles but as an armed nation, carrying their
> households with them, resolved to live where they could no longer be
> commanded.”  
> — Ammianus Marcellinus, *Res Gestae*, XXXI

Rome faces **a people in motion with political memory**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ALARIC & THE FAILURE OF CONTROL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Alaric represents the institutionalization of Visigothic autonomy.

Roman failures include:
• Broken promises of pay and land
• Use of Visigoths without integration
• Diplomatic inconsistency

Zosimus records Roman mismanagement:

> “The barbarians, long deceived by treaties and mocked by delay,
> resolved to seek by arms what had been denied by law.”  
> — Zosimus, *New History*, V

Roman authority collapses through **credibility loss**, not force.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. THE SACK OF ROME (AD 410)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The sack of Rome is symbolic, not strategic.

Key facts:
• Limited physical destruction
• Immense psychological impact
• Shattering of imperial inviolability

Jerome writes:

> “The city which had taken the whole world was itself taken; and the
> conqueror of nations became the prey of a single tribe.”  
> — Jerome, *Letter 127*

The event marks **the end of Roman inevitability**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ROME’S RESPONSE: MANAGEMENT WITHOUT COMMAND
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After 410, Rome shifts policy.

New reality:
• Negotiated autonomy
• Territorial concessions
• Military outsourcing

Gibbon notes:

> “The empire consented to divide its provinces with the Visigoths, and
> acknowledged a power which it could neither destroy nor fully
> command.”  
> — Edward Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*, Chapter XXXI

Rome survives by **ceding monopoly of force**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. SETTLEMENT IN GAUL (AD 418)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Visigothic settlement in Aquitaine formalizes autonomy.

Characteristics:
• Roman legal forms without Roman sovereignty
• Independent military command
• Hybrid Roman–barbarian administration

This is **post-Roman continuity**, not collapse.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. PUBLIC DOMAIN SCHOLARSHIP — TRANSITIONAL LOGIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mommsen frames the shift:

> “The settlement of the Visigoths marks the transformation of Roman
> dominion into a federative order, where sovereignty was shared rather
> than imposed.”  
> — Theodor Mommsen, *History of Rome*

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. MODERN SCHOLARSHIP — INTENTIONAL ADAPTATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Peter Heather argues:

> “The Visigothic kingdom was not an accident of collapse, but the
> product of Roman attempts to manage power shortages through
> delegation.”  
> — Peter Heather, *Empires and Barbarians*

Hans-Ulrich Wiemer emphasizes choice:

> “The late Roman state did not simply fail; it redefined authority in
> ways that preserved order locally while abandoning universal control.”  

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Visigoths encode **managed secession**.

Lessons preserved:
• Empire can survive loss of monopoly
• Autonomy emerges from policy failure
• Symbolic centers fall before systems collapse
• Delegation becomes permanence
• Rome transitions rather than vanishes

The Visigoths are Rome’s first successor.
