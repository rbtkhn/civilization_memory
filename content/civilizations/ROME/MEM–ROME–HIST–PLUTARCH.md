I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Plutarch as **Rome’s moral comparator**: the Greek
philosopher-biographer who interpreted Roman power through ethical
parallelism, pairing Roman lives with Greek exemplars to test whether
imperial success corresponded to moral greatness.

Plutarch does not ask why Rome conquered.
He asks whether Rome *deserved* to.

He is not concerned with systems, logistics, or institutions,
but with **character under the pressure of power**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. POSITION AS CIVILIZATIONAL INTERMEDIARY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plutarch writes as neither Roman official nor subject in revolt.

Context:
• Greek intellectual under Roman imperial peace
• Priest at Delphi
• Educated in Platonic philosophy
• Beneficiary of Roman order without political dependence

He inhabits a civilizational middle space:
• Greek moral tradition
• Roman political reality

Edward Gibbon situates Plutarch precisely in this role:

> “Plutarch, the philosopher of Chaeronea, lived in an age when the
> Greek world had resigned the exercise of political power, but still
> retained the proud consciousness of moral and intellectual superiority.
> In his Parallel Lives, the heroes of Rome are brought before the tribunal
> of Greek philosophy, and judged not by the success of their arms, but by
> the virtue or the vices of their character.”
> — Edward Gibbon, *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*

Rome rules.
Greece evaluates.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PARALLEL LIVES AS ANALYTICAL METHOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plutarch’s defining innovation is **comparison**.

Method:
• Pair one Greek life with one Roman life
• Emphasize character, decision, and ethical disposition
• De-emphasize chronology, causality, and institutions
• Conclude with explicit moral comparison (*synkrisis*)

Plutarch states his intent openly:

> “It is not histories that I am writing, but lives; and in the most
> illustrious deeds there is not always a manifestation of virtue or vice;
> nay, a slight thing like a phrase or a jest often makes a greater
> revelation of character than battles where thousands fall.”
> — Plutarch, *Life of Alexander*

History becomes **ethical laboratory**, not causal science.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. MORAL CAUSATION OVER STRUCTURAL CAUSATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plutarch privileges ethical agency over structural explanation.

For him:
• Virtue precedes power
• Vice corrodes success
• Character determines legacy

This places him in deliberate contrast with:
• MEM–ROME–HIST–POLYBIUS — institutional mechanics
• MEM–ROME–HIST–TACITUS — experiential suffocation
• MEM–ROME–HIST–GIBBON — long-run structural decay

Theodor Mommsen captures Plutarch’s limitation and value:

> “Plutarch’s biographies are not history in the strict sense of the word,
> but moral portraiture. He neither understood nor sought to explain the
> institutions of Rome; yet no other ancient writer has so powerfully
> influenced the ethical judgment which later ages have passed upon Roman
> greatness.”
> — Theodor Mommsen, *The History of Rome*, Book V

Plutarch shapes memory, not mechanics.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ROMAN FIGURES AS MORAL TEST CASES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plutarch’s Roman subjects include:
• Caesar
• Pompey
• Brutus
• Cicero
• Antony

They are not treated as inevitabilities.

Instead:
• Caesar — brilliance without restraint
• Brutus — virtue without efficacy
• Antony — passion without discipline
• Cicero — eloquence without power

Adrian Goldsworthy notes Plutarch’s enduring influence:

> “For all his lack of interest in institutions or military systems,
> Plutarch has probably done more than any other ancient author to shape
> how later generations imagined the great figures of the Roman Republic,
> fixing their virtues and flaws into an enduring moral drama.”
> — Adrian Goldsworthy, *Caesar: Life of a Colossus*

Rome’s crisis becomes a gallery of ethical failures.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. EMPIRE AS MORAL STRESS TEST
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plutarch does not condemn empire outright.

Instead, empire functions as:
• Amplifier of virtue
• Accelerator of vice
• Revealer of limits

Anthony Kaldellis reframes Plutarch’s function:

> “Plutarch did not seek to undermine Roman rule; rather, he translated it
> into Greek moral categories, ensuring that Roman power would be judged
> according to Greek ethical expectations even as it dominated the Greek
> world politically.”
> — Anthony Kaldellis, *Romanland*

Empire magnifies character.
Judgment follows.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LIMITS OF PLUTARCHAN ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What Plutarch omits is deliberate.

Absent:
• Logistics
• Economics
• Administrative capacity
• Military replacement systems

Thus:
• He cannot explain Rome’s rise
• He cannot explain Rome’s endurance
• He can explain Rome’s *moral memory*

Plutarch explains how Rome is **remembered**, not how it functions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Plutarch encodes Rome into the Greek ethical canon.

Functions:
• Makes Roman power legible to Greek philosophy
• Preserves Greek moral authority under Roman rule
• Converts conquest into comparative evaluation

Rome conquers Greece.
Greece judges Rome.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Comparison is a civilizational weapon.
• Empire invites moral auditing.
• Character determines how power is remembered.
• Structural success does not equal ethical success.
• Rome inherits Greek judgment even as it conquers Greece.

Tension preserved without verdict.
