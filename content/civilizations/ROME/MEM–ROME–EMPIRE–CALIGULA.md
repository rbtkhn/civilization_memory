────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Caligula as **Rome’s legitimacy rupture**: the emperor
who exposed how quickly concealed monarchy could devolve into arbitrary
personal domination once institutional restraint failed.

Caligula represents **power without discipline**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CHARISMA WITHOUT GOVERNANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Caligula inherits popular goodwill.

Sources:
• Germanicus’ legacy
• Youth
• Promise of renewal after Tiberius

This goodwill is squandered rapidly.

Gibbon’s judgment is unsparing:

> “The madness of Caligula soon converted the favor of the Roman people
> into abhorrence; and the vices which were concealed by the veil of
> absolute power burst forth with unrestrained violence.”  
> — Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*, ch. 6

Affection accelerates collapse.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. DEGRADATION OF AUTHORITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Caligula weaponizes sovereignty.

Patterns:
• Public humiliation of elites
• Arbitrary executions
• Financial predation
• Theatrical tyranny

Mommsen diagnoses the structural failure:

> “When the ruler no longer respected even the forms of law, the
> Principate ceased to be a system and became mere despotism, resting
> only on fear and chance.”  
> — Mommsen, *The History of Rome*, Book V

Institutions do not break.
They are ignored.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. COURT VS STATE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Governance collapses into performance.

Effects:
• Treasury exhaustion
• Military alienation
• Senatorial hatred
• Praetorian conspiracy

The empire survives **despite** the emperor.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ASSASSINATION AS SYSTEM CORRECTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Caligula is murdered (AD 41).

The act is not ideological.
It is administrative.

Rome removes a malfunctioning node.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL ROLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Caligula proves a negative lesson:

The Principate requires restraint,
or it becomes intolerable.

This prepares the ground for Claudius.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Charisma without discipline is corrosive.
• Absolute power tests personality faster than institutions.
• Fear is an unstable substitute for legitimacy.
• Removal becomes governance when procedure fails.
• Caligula exposes the floor beneath monarchy.

Tension preserved without verdict.
