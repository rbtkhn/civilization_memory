I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Granada as **Rome’s final Iberian counter-memory**:
the last Islamic polity on the peninsula, whose survival depended on
operating within—and ultimately yielding to—the restored Roman-
Christian order.

Granada is not Roman continuity.
It is **Roman pressure made visible**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GRANADA AS RESIDUAL ANDALUSI STATE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Granada emerges after the collapse of Almohad power.

Conditions:
• Mountainous defensibility
• Dense urban culture
• Agricultural specialization
• Tributary dependence on Castile

Granada survives not by dominance,
but by accommodation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ADMINISTRATION, CULTURE, AND LAW
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Granada maintains sophisticated governance.

Institutions:
• Islamic law (Sharia)
• Court bureaucracy
• Taxation systems
• Urban planning and irrigation

Yet it operates inside a Romanizing environment:
• Treaties framed in Latin legal logic
• Tribute agreements formalized juridically
• Borders enforced by Christian powers

Rome defines the **rules of survival**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. TRIBUTE AND SUBORDINATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Granada pays *parias* to Castile.

This relationship signifies:
• Recognition of Christian supremacy
• Survival through submission
• Loss of strategic initiative

Granada is autonomous in culture,
subordinate in power.

Rome governs indirectly.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. FALL OF GRANADA (1492)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Granada’s conquest marks Roman restoration in Iberia.

Key features:
• Treaty-based surrender
• Initial guarantees of religious freedom
• Rapid erosion of protections
• Forced conversion and expulsion

Roman-Christian authority reasserts itself fully.

Pluralism collapses under unification pressure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. GRANADA IN ROMAN MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Granada functions as the **boundary marker** of Roman return.

Its fall signifies:
• End of Islamic sovereignty in Iberia
• Completion of Visigothic–Roman restoration narrative
• Transition from medieval to early modern Spain

Rome does not absorb Granada.
It **replaces** it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Granada encodes **the limits of non-Roman endurance** in Iberia.

Lessons:
• Sophisticated cultures can survive without sovereignty.
• Tribute delays but does not prevent absorption.
• Legal dominance precedes territorial conquest.
• Rome’s return is juridical before military.
• Restoration ends plural equilibrium.

Granada is Rome’s final proof of inevitability.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Granada survives by submission, not strength.
• Roman law frames even Islamic survival.
• Cultural brilliance does not offset power asymmetry.
• Restoration eliminates intermediaries.
• Rome reasserts itself by closing alternatives.

Tension preserved without verdict.
