────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Protestant Reformation as **Rome’s internal
jurisdictional rebellion**: the moment when Papal universalism fractures
not along geography, but within Latin Christendom itself, as states and
theologians reject Rome’s monopoly on legitimacy, law, and salvation.

Unlike the Great Schism, this is not Rome divided.
It is **Rome contested from within**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. PAPAL UNIVERSALISM AT PEAK EXTENSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
By the early 16th century, the Papacy embodies full Roman inheritance.

Characteristics:
• Canon law supremacy
• Universal jurisdiction claims
• Fiscal extraction (indulgences, tithes)
• Moral authority intertwined with politics

Rome is no longer merely spiritual.
It functions as a **transnational governing system**.

This invites resistance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THEOLOGY AS LEGITIMACY WEAPON
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Reformation theology targets Rome’s authority grammar.

Key challenges:
• Sola scriptura undermines legal mediation
• Sola fide bypasses institutional judgment
• Priesthood of believers decentralizes authority

This is not doctrinal nitpicking.
It is a **direct attack on Roman jurisdiction**.

If salvation bypasses Rome,
Rome loses leverage.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PRINTING, LANGUAGE, AND SCALE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Unlike earlier heresies, the Reformation scales.

Enablers:
• Printing press
• Vernacular scripture
• Literate urban classes
• State protection

Roman authority is slow and procedural.
Reform spreads fast and decentralized.

Rome cannot suppress what it cannot monopolize.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. STATE ABSORPTION OF LEGITIMACY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Secular rulers exploit the rupture.

Outcomes:
• Princes become religious arbiters
• Church lands nationalized
• Obedience rerouted to state authority

Roman universality fractures into **sovereign legitimacy zones**.

This marks the birth of the modern state.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. PAPAL RESPONSE & COUNTER-REFORMATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome adapts—but too late for unity.

Responses:
• Council of Trent
• Doctrinal clarification
• Institutional discipline
• Jesuit mobilization

The Papacy survives intact,
but universal reach contracts.

Rome chooses depth over breadth.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LONG CIVILIZATIONAL CONSEQUENCES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Reformation reshapes the West.

Effects:
• End of unified Latin Christendom
• Rise of confessional states
• Permanent pluralism
• Secular sovereignty legitimated

Rome remains powerful—
but no longer singular.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. COMPARISON WITH THE GREAT SCHISM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Great Schism:
• External bifurcation
• Two Roman continuities
• Geographic divide

Reformation:
• Internal rebellion
• Fragmentation within Latin Rome
• Doctrinal–political hybrid conflict

Both reveal limits of universality.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Universal authority invites internal revolt.
• Theology becomes a weapon against jurisdiction.
• Printing breaks monopoly power.
• States absorb legitimacy when Rome weakens.
• Rome survives by narrowing its claim.

Tension preserved without verdict.
