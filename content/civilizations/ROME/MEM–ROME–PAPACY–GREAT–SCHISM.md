────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Great Schism as **Rome’s first irreversible
institutional bifurcation**: the moment when universal Christian
authority, inherited from Roman imperial logic, fractures into rival
jurisdictional grammars—Latin Papal and Greek Orthodox—without a shared
mechanism of reconciliation.

The Schism is not merely theological.
It is **Roman authority splitting along administrative fault lines**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ROMAN UNIVERSALISM AFTER EMPIRE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After the collapse of Western imperial administration, Rome survives
through ecclesiastical universality.

Key inheritances:
• Territorial dioceses
• Legalized doctrine
• Hierarchical authority
• Claims to universality

However, Roman continuity now exists in two centers:
• Rome (Latin, Papal, juridical)
• Constantinople (Greek, Imperial, conciliar)

Both claim Roman legitimacy.
Both inherit Roman memory.
Neither can subordinate the other without destroying universality.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. JURISDICTIONAL INCOMPATIBILITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Schism crystallizes incompatible authority models.

Latin (Papal) logic:
• Primacy of the Bishop of Rome
• Juridical supremacy
• Centralized adjudication
• Authority flows from office

Greek (Orthodox) logic:
• Conciliar equality of patriarchs
• Imperial–ecclesiastical symbiosis
• Authority flows from consensus and tradition

Both models are Roman.
They are **administratively incompatible**.

Universal authority cannot be simultaneously centralized and conciliar.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. THEOLOGY AS PROXY FOR POWER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Doctrinal disputes become stand-ins for authority conflict.

Examples:
• Filioque clause
• Papal primacy
• Liturgical practice
• Language (Latin vs Greek)

These disagreements are real—but escalatory.

The deeper issue:
Who speaks for the universal Church when empire is divided?

Rome’s old imperial unity no longer exists to arbitrate.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. 1054 AS SYMBOL, NOT ORIGIN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The mutual excommunications of 1054 formalize an already advanced split.

Key reality:
• Cultural estrangement predates the event
• Political rivalry hardens positions
• Communication failure compounds distrust

The Schism is not a sudden collapse.
It is a **bureaucratic divorce finalized late**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONSTANTINOPLE & CIVILIZATIONAL DRIFT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Eastern Rome continues as a Christian empire.

Outcomes:
• Orthodoxy binds church to imperial survival
• Papal authority is rejected as external
• Roman continuity remains—but localizes

Western Rome evolves differently:
• Papacy becomes sovereign substitute
• Universalism detaches from empire
• Authority becomes portable and legalistic

Two Romes persist.
They no longer recognize each other’s supremacy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CIVILIZATIONAL CONSEQUENCES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Schism produces lasting effects:

• Permanent East–West civilizational divide
• Failure of unified Christian diplomacy
• Preconditions for 1204 Sack of Constantinople
• Divergent political theologies

Rome fractures **before** the Crusades—
the Crusades expose the fracture.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Universal institutions fracture when arbitration disappears.
• Theology encodes administrative conflict.
• Roman continuity survives—but splits.
• Conciliar and juridical universalisms cannot coexist.
• The Schism is Rome dividing itself, not Christianity dividing Rome.

Tension preserved without verdict.
