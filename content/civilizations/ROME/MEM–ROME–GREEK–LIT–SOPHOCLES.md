────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Sophocles as **Rome's inherited diagnostician of
authority failure**: the tragedian whose works reveal how law, power,
and moral certainty collapse when leaders exceed human limits.

For Rome, Sophocles is not instruction.
He is **warning**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. TRAGEDY AS GOVERNANCE STRESS TEST
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sophoclean tragedy examines authority under pressure.

Core features:
• Leaders acting with partial knowledge
• Law enforced without wisdom
• Moral certainty producing catastrophe
• Irreversibility of action

Tragedy begins where confidence ends.

This aligns with Roman concern for limits.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ANTIGONE: LAW VS LEGITIMACY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
*Antigone* confronts irreconcilable norms.

Conflict:
• State law (Creon)
• Moral/divine law (Antigone)

Creon's failure:
• Rigidity
• Refusal to hear counsel
• Confusion of authority with infallibility

Rome absorbs this lesson deeply:
law must be flexible to endure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. OEDIPUS: KNOWLEDGE AS LIABILITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
*Oedipus Rex* reframes leadership failure.

Insights:
• Intelligence accelerates downfall
• Truth arrives too late to save
• Responsibility persists without intent

Power magnifies error.

Rome later institutionalizes delay,
procedure, and appeal to slow catastrophe.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. TRAGIC IRREVERSIBILITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sophocles insists:
some actions cannot be undone.

This logic later appears in Roman doctrine:
• Exile
• Damnatio memoriae
• Legal finality

Sophocles teaches that **correction is not restoration**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. SOPHOCLES AND ROMAN LEGAL MINDSET
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome inherits tragic caution.

Effects:
• Emphasis on precedent
• Suspicion of absolute authority
• Fear of charismatic certainty

Tragedy becomes implicit legal wisdom.

Rome governs to avoid Sophoclean endings.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTRAST WITH HOMER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Homer:
• Tragedy of war
• Heroic excess

Sophocles:
• Tragedy of rule
• Legal excess

Rome integrates both:
• Homer teaches cost
• Sophocles teaches limit

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sophocles encodes **irreversibility awareness**.

Lessons:
• Authority magnifies error.
• Law without humility destroys legitimacy.
• Knowledge does not prevent tragedy.
• Counsel is structural, not optional.
• Some losses define civilizations.

Sophocles is Rome's silent brake.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Tragedy predicts institutional collapse.
• Rigid law accelerates failure.
• Authority must hear dissent.
• Irreversibility shapes doctrine.
• Rome survives by fearing Sophocles.

Tension preserved without verdict.
