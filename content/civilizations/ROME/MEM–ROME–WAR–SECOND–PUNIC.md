I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Second Punic War as **Rome’s survival trial**:
the conflict in which Rome faced tactical annihilation, repeated
catastrophic defeat, and existential threat—yet refused psychological,
political, or institutional collapse.

The Second Punic War teaches Rome how to **endure catastrophe without
abandoning the system**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. HANNIBAL AND TACTICAL SUPERIORITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Hannibal represents tactical brilliance.

Livy records Rome’s crisis after Cannae:

> “Never before had the Republic been so shaken. The flower of its youth
> lay slain; allies wavered; Italy itself seemed on the brink of revolt.
> Yet the Senate neither yielded to fear nor listened to proposals of
> peace, but resolved that Rome must endure whatever fortune might bring.”  
> — Livy, *Ab Urbe Condita*, Book XXII

Rome loses armies,
not resolve.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. SYSTEMIC RESPONSE TO DEFEAT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome refuses negotiated settlement.

Actions:
• Continues conscription
• Lowers property qualifications
• Appoints dictators and commanders repeatedly
• Accepts extraordinary loss as normal

Rome chooses **attrition over brilliance**.

Richard A. Gabriel identifies the asymmetry:

> “Rome prevailed because it could replace losses indefinitely and
> maintain political cohesion, while Carthage depended on exceptional
> individuals and finite resources.”  
> — Richard A. Gabriel, *Hannibal*

Hannibal wins battles.
Rome wins time.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRATEGIC SHIFT AND GLOBALIZATION OF WAR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome expands the war beyond Italy.

Measures:
• Campaigns in Spain
• Pressure on Carthaginian logistics
• Isolation of Hannibal

Scipio Africanus reverses initiative by threatening Carthage itself.

The war becomes **systemic and global**, not heroic.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. OUTCOME AND TRANSFORMATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Carthage is defeated, but not yet destroyed.

Theodor Mommsen diagnoses the deeper shift:

> “The Second Punic War trained Rome to endure misfortune without
> surrender, to treat human life as expendable material, and to regard
> victory as a matter of time rather than genius.”  
> — Theodor Mommsen, *The History of Rome*, Book III

Rome survives by hardening.

The Republic does not emerge unchanged.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Systems defeat genius over time.
• Refusal to negotiate is a strategic choice.
• Attrition reshapes moral limits.
• Total war normalizes extraordinary loss.
• Rome survives Hannibal—and becomes something harsher.

Tension preserved without verdict.
