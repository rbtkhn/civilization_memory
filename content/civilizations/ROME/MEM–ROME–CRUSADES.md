I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Crusades as **Rome’s attempt to convert spiritual
primacy into territorial command**: a project to reassert papal
universalism through sanctioned violence.

For Rome, the Crusades represent **authority extended beyond capacity**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CATEGORY A — ANCIENT / LATE ANTIQUE FOUNDATIONS
IMPERIAL PRECEDENTS FOR SACRAL AUTHORITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Although the Crusades occur in the medieval period, their logic draws
directly on late Roman precedents that fused law, religion, and command.

Eusebius articulates the late Roman model of sacral rulership:

> “The emperor alone was appointed by the Supreme Sovereign to be the
> interpreter of His will, the guardian of His laws, and the protector of
> the Church; and through him divine authority was translated into human
> governance, so that obedience to the ruler became a form of obedience
> to God Himself.”  
> — Eusebius, *Life of Constantine*, Book IV

This model of sacral command forms the ideological ancestor of papal
claims to universal mobilization.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. PAPAL CLAIM TO UNIVERSAL MOBILIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Crusades originate in papal assertion of transnational authority.

Urban II’s call at Clermont (1095) establishes the premise.

Robert the Monk records the speech in language that fuses salvation with
obedience:

> “Let those who have been accustomed unjustly to wage private warfare
> against the faithful now go against the infidels and end with victory
> this war which should have been begun long ago. Let those who have been
> robbers now become soldiers of Christ. Let those who have fought against
> their brothers now fight lawfully against the barbarians. Let those who
> have served as mercenaries for a few pieces of silver now attain an
> eternal reward.”  
> — Robert the Monk, *Historia Iherosolimitana*

Faith becomes **mobilization infrastructure**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. DELEGATED WARFARE & CONTROL FAILURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome lacks standing armies.

Violence is therefore delegated.

Fulcher of Chartres records the resulting disorder:

> “Each man obeyed his own will rather than the counsel of leaders, and
> the host moved without discipline or unity. Greed, cruelty, and
> impatience accompanied them, so that many things were done which
> dishonored the cause for which they claimed to fight.”  
> — Fulcher of Chartres, *Historia Hierosolymitana*

Rome authorizes war it cannot fully govern.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CATEGORY D — MEDIEVAL CONTINUITY SOURCES
CONSTANTINOPLE & INTERNAL FRACTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The sack of Constantinople (1204) exposes civilizational rupture.

Niketas Choniates records the trauma in unmistakably Roman terms:

> “Even the Saracens are merciful and kind compared with these men who
> bear the cross of Christ on their shoulders. The city, which had long
> been the glory of the Romans, was stripped bare; the laws were mocked,
> the sanctuaries violated, and the empire itself was treated as spoil.”  
> — Niketas Choniates, *History*, Book VIII

Universal authority collapses into fratricide.

Rome claims unity,
but produces schism.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CATEGORY B — PUBLIC-DOMAIN SCHOLARSHIP
FAILURE OF TERRITORIAL UNIVERSALISM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Edward Gibbon diagnoses the structural failure:

> “The principle of religious war was incompatible with the permanent
> interest of the state. The popes had assumed the dangerous privilege of
> arming the people, and the sword, once drawn, was not easily restored
> to its scabbard. The independence of princes was shaken, the discipline
> of society was relaxed, and the Church herself was wounded by the
> excess of her own zeal.”  
> — Edward Gibbon, *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*,
> Chapter LVIII

Territorial ambition exceeds administrative reality.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CATEGORY C — MODERN SCHOLARSHIP
INTERPRETIVE FRAMING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Christopher Tyerman emphasizes the limits of papal control:

> “The crusades were never the expression of a centralized papal war
> machine. They were negotiated enterprises, dependent on persuasion,
> compromise, and the interests of secular elites whose priorities often
> diverged sharply from those of Rome.”  
> — Christopher Tyerman, *God’s War*

Jonathan Riley-Smith highlights the moral paradox:

> “The papacy succeeded in sanctifying violence, but failed to contain
> it. The language of holy war empowered warriors while weakening the
> Church’s capacity to regulate their actions.”  
> — Jonathan Riley-Smith, *The Crusades: A History*

Modern scholarship frames but does not override medieval testimony.

Rome survives,
but chastened.
