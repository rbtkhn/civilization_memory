────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Atlantic Ocean** as Rome's **western limit**: the body of water beyond Gibraltar where the empire holds coasts (Iberia, Gaul, Britain) but does not claim or control the sea itself. For Rome, the Atlantic is not mare nostrum. It is **the beyond**—the exterior against which the Mediterranean interior is defined.

Central thesis: Rome's maritime identity is Mediterranean. The Atlantic is boundary, not heartland. Littoral presence (ports, provinces) does not imply oceanic dominion.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. MEARSHEIMER STRATEGIC ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.A. STOPPING POWER OF THE ATLANTIC — HIGH

The Atlantic exhibits **high stopping power** for Roman-style naval control:

• Open ocean; no enclosed-basin logic (unlike Mediterranean)
• Distance and weather exceed Roman naval doctrine and logistics
• No sustained Roman battle fleet in the Atlantic
• Littoral traffic (Britain–Gaul–Iberia) is coastal and channel-focused

Strategic consequence: Rome **terminates** at the Atlantic-facing coast. The ocean is a barrier to further westward expansion; it is not a Roman lake.

II.B. GIBRALTAR AS GATE — MEDITERRANEAN VS ATLANTIC

The Strait of Gibraltar (Pillars of Hercules) is the **hinge** between Mediterranean (Roman core maritime space) and Atlantic (exterior). See MEM–ROME–GEO–GIBRALTAR.

• Control of the strait governs who enters or leaves the Mediterranean
• Rome holds one or both shores for long periods; the **gate** is Roman, the **ocean beyond** is not
• Atlantic-facing provinces (Baetica, Lusitania, Gallia, Britain) are **edge provinces**—they look outward onto a space Rome does not dominate

II.C. LITTORAL PRESENCE — COAST, NOT OCEAN

Roman engagement with the Atlantic is **littoral**:

• **Iberia:** Atlantic coast (Galicia, Lusitania, Baetica west) — ports and mining; no blue-water fleet
• **Gaul:** Atlantic coast (Armorica, Aquitania) — trade and garrison; Channel and Biscay as margins
• **Britain:** Conquest (Claudius onward); Hadrian's Wall and northern limit; Britain is **terminal**—Rome does not sail beyond it into the open Atlantic

No Roman thalassocracy in the Atlantic. The Mediterranean remains the only "Roman sea."

II.D. POWER PROJECTION — NONE BEYOND THE STRAIT

Rome does not project power **into** the Atlantic basin. Expeditions (e.g. exploration or punitive) are rare and do not establish sustained presence. The structural logic: investment stays in the Mediterranean and its immediate approaches; the Atlantic is **cost without strategic return** at scale.
