────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Vandals** as Rome’s most damaging **strategic
secession**: a people who seized Roman infrastructure intact and turned
it against the empire’s economic and maritime core.

The Vandals do not pressure Rome from the frontier.
They **occupy its arteries**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CROSSING INTO IMPERIAL SPACE   (AD 406)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Vandals enter Roman territory during systemic breakdown.

Key conditions:
• Rhine frontier collapse
• Roman civil war
• Inadequate field armies

Prosper of Aquitaine records:

> “The Vandals crossed the Rhine when no resistance was offered, for
> the Roman forces were consumed by internal conflict and abandoned
> the defense of the provinces.”  

The entry is **opportunistic**, not overpowering.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. MIGRATION THROUGH GAUL & SPAIN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Vandals traverse imperial territory largely unchecked.

Characteristics:
• Minimal pitched battles
• Exploitation of Roman roads
• Reliance on confiscation rather than destruction

This reveals Rome’s loss of **internal coercive capacity**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. AFRICA: SEIZURE OF THE ECONOMIC CORE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The capture of North Africa   (AD 429–439) is decisive.

Consequences:
• Loss of grain supply
• Loss of tax base
• Loss of shipyards
• Loss of naval dominance

Procopius emphasizes the blow:

> “Africa, the nurse of Rome, passed into the hands of the barbarians,
> and with it the revenues and provisions upon which the city depended.”  
> — Procopius, *Wars*, III

Rome loses **metabolic security**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MARITIME POWER & STRATEGIC REVERSAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Vandals convert Roman naval assets into offensive tools.

Effects:
• Control of western Mediterranean sea lanes
• Raids on Italy and Sicily
• Undermining of imperial legitimacy

Gibbon observes:

> “The Vandals became masters of the sea, and Rome, which had ruled the
> waves, now trembled at the approach of their fleets.”  
> — Edward Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*, Chapter XXXVI

This is **infrastructure inversion**, not primitive piracy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. THE SACK OF ROME   (AD 455)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Vandal sack is methodical rather than destructive.

Features:
• Targeted asset extraction
• Minimal urban devastation
• Symbolic humiliation

Hydatius records:

> “The city was despoiled of its treasures, and the captives were led
> away, yet the buildings themselves largely remained standing.”  

Rome loses prestige, not stones.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. FAILED ROMAN RECOVERY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Roman attempts to retake Africa fail.

Causes:
• Naval inferiority
• Fiscal weakness
• Political fragmentation

This seals the fate of the Western Empire.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. PUBLIC DOMAIN SCHOLARSHIP — STRUCTURAL LOSS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mommsen identifies Africa as decisive:

> “With the loss of Africa, the Western Empire lost not merely a
> province, but the material basis of its existence.”  
> — Theodor Mommsen, *History of Rome*

Gibbon concurs:

> “The separation of Africa was a mortal wound, from which the Western
> Empire never recovered.”  

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. MODERN SCHOLARSHIP — STATE SEIZURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Peter Heather writes:

> “The Vandal kingdom was not a chaotic barbarian realm, but a
> deliberate seizure of Roman state machinery.”  

Hans-Ulrich Wiemer notes:

> “What distinguishes the Vandals is not destruction, but appropriation
> — they understood Roman systems well enough to exploit them.”  

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Vandals encode **asset-based collapse**.

Lessons preserved:
• Control of logistics outweighs symbolic authority
• Maritime dominance can reverse imperial hierarchies
• Loss of fiscal core precedes political end
• Secession can be systemic, not chaotic
• Empires fall when infrastructure is captured intact

The Vandals take Rome apart without burning it.
