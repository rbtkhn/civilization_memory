────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Silk Road** as Rome's **eastern trade corridor**: the overland (and maritime-adjacent) network by which luxury goods—above all silk—reached the empire from Parthia, Persia, and ultimately China. Rome is **demand terminus**, not corridor controller. The empire does not hold Central Asian nodes; it receives goods through intermediaries (Parthians, Palmyrenes, later Sasanians). Loss of the eastern provinces to Islam severs Rome's direct link to the corridor.

For Rome:
• Demand for silk and eastern luxuries (Pliny's complaint about outflow of specie)
• Syria and Egypt as reception points; Palmyra as desert link
• Parthia/Persia as structural intermediary—Rome cannot bypass
• No direct control of the Central Asian segment; encoding is **dependency**, not flow-space

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ROMAN ENGAGEMENT — RECEPTION, NOT ROUTE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rome engages the Silk Road at its **western end**. Key dynamics:
• Syrian and Egyptian ports/caravan termini receive goods from the east
• Palmyra: desert entrepôt linking Roman Syria to Parthian-controlled routes
• Pliny the Elder (*Natural History*): Roman gold flows east for silk and luxuries; moral and economic anxiety
• No Roman army or administration in Central Asia; no Roman "Silk Road policy" beyond securing the Syrian frontier

The corridor exists for Rome as **supply line from the other side of Parthia/Persia**. Structural consequence: whoever controls the middle holds leverage over both termini.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. STRUCTURAL CONSTRAINT — INTERMEDIARY DEPENDENCY (MEARSHEIMER)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Power distribution on the route:
• **East:** China/Central Asian producers
• **Middle:** Parthian, then Sasanian, control of Mesopotamia and Iranian plateau—**bottleneck**
• **West:** Roman demand; Syrian/Egyptian reception

Rome cannot circumvent the middle. Military campaigns (e.g. Trajan, Julian) strike at Ctesiphon and Mesopotamia but do not hold them permanently; they do not establish Roman nodes on the Silk Road proper. The structural logic is **intermediary advantage**: Parthia/Persia profits from transit; Rome pays in gold and accepts dependency.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PALMYRA — EXCEPTION AND COLLAPSE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Palmyra functions as Rome's desert link to eastern trade: semi-autonomous, caravan city, Roman client then breakaway. Its destruction (272) removes a key node of Roman-connected eastern trade. The lesson: Rome's link to the corridor was **fragile and node-dependent**; loss of Palmyra tightens intermediary dependency.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LOSS TO ISLAM — SEVERANCE OF THE TERMINUS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Conquest of Syria and Egypt by Islamic forces (7th c.) severs Rome's direct connection to the Silk Road's western termini. Byzantium retains Anatolia and, later, Constantinople as a magnet for trade—but the Syrian and Egyptian reception nodes pass into dar al-Islam. For Rome, the Silk Road encoding shifts from **live dependency** to **memory of lost access**. Islam inherits the western end of the corridor as flow-space; Rome inherits the lesson of intermediary dependency and terminus loss.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. BARNES DIMENSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**Jurisdiction:** No single Roman jurisdiction ever governed the Silk Road route; authority stopped at the frontier (Syria, Egypt). **Defection incentive:** Palmyra's defection (Zenobia) illustrates that the desert node could exit the Roman orbit when central power weakened. **Irreversibility:** Loss of Syria/Egypt to Islam is irreversible for Rome—the terminus is not reconquered. The liability dimension is **structural** (who controls the middle holds leverage) rather than personal; the mechanism is **dependency and severance**.
