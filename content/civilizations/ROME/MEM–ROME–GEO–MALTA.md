I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Malta as **Rome's smallest decisive island**: a
micro-anchor whose value lies not in extraction but in positional ability
to interrupt—or guarantee—central Mediterranean maritime flow.

For Rome, Malta is not an empire-builder.
It is an **interruption-preventer**.

Small nodes stabilize large systems.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. MEARSHEIMER STRATEGIC ANALYSIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

II.A. STOPPING POWER — MICRO-GEOMETRY

Malta demonstrates **micro-geometric** stopping power:
• Central position between Sicily and Africa
• Observation of passage routes
• Emergency harbor for patrols
• Small size, disproportionate value

II.B. REGIONAL HEGEMONY — DENIAL AT MINIMAL COST

Rome holds Malta for **denial at minimal cost**:
• Secured during Second Punic War
• Denies Carthaginian maritime use
• Stabilizes southern Sicily approaches
• Small garrison sufficient

II.C. POWER PROJECTION

Malta supports central Mediterranean projection:
• Anti-piracy staging
• Fleet observation point
• Emergency repair harbor
• Complements Sicily rather than duplicates

III. ARC TIMELINE EVIDENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

III.A. ARC-T-ANCIENT

Strabo on positional value:
> "Near Sicily lies Melitē, possessing harbors useful to those who sail
> between the Syrtes and Sicily; though small, it is advantageously
> placed for those who command the sea."
> — Strabo, *Geography*, Book VI

Polybius on sea control:
> "The command of the sea is made up of many parts; he who neglects
> even the least places himself at the mercy of those who do not."
> — Polybius, *Histories*, Book I

III.B. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL

Cassius Dio on retention logic:
> "When dominion had grown too vast, the rulers were compelled to
> preserve those positions whose loss would expose greater dangers."
> — Cassius Dio, *Roman History*, Book LII

III.C. ARC-T-MODERN

Gibbon on invisible authority:
> "The firm administration of the Roman provinces secured obedience
> not by terror but by the regular exercise of authority."
> — Edward Gibbon, *Decline and Fall*
