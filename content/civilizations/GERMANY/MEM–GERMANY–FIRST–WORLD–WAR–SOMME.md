I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Somme as **Germania's defensive success at
industrial cost**: the battle where deep dugouts and machine guns
defeated the Allied bombardment, 57,000 British fell on the first
day, and Germany held—but at casualty rates that foretold exhaustion.

For Germania, the Somme encodes **industrial attrition without
decision**—the Allies failed to break through; Germany failed to
avoid being bled. The defensive worked tactically; strategically,
both sides lost.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. THE DEFENSIVE SYSTEM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
German positions had been reinforced since 1914.

• Deep dugouts survived the seven-day Allied bombardment
• 1.5 million shells failed to destroy the defense
• Machine-gun positions emerged when the barrage lifted
• Wire remained; infantry advanced into killing zones

John Keegan describes the result:

"The men of the New Armies had been promised that the artillery
bombardment—seven days, 1.5 million shells—would destroy the German
defenses. They were ordered to advance in waves, at walking pace,
carrying sixty pounds of equipment. The bombardment had failed.
The German deep dugouts survived. When the whistles blew at 7:30 AM,
the German machine-gunners emerged from their shelters and found
perfect targets: dense lines of men walking slowly across open ground."
— John Keegan, *The Face of Battle* (1976)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE TANK EMERGENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
15 September 1916: Flers-Courcelette. The first tanks in combat.

Primitive, unreliable, terrifying—but they crossed trenches. Germany
had no equivalent. The tank signaled the RMA that would eventually
break trench deadlock; in 1916, it was not yet decisive.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ATTRITION ARITHMETIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Somme continued 141 days. German casualties: ~450,000–650,000.

Allied logic: Germany cannot replace losses indefinitely; attrition
will break the army. The calculation was partially right—Germany
would exhaust before the Allies—but the Somme also exhausted
Britain's New Armies and prefigured the mutual hemorrhage of 1917–1918.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Somme encodes **defensive competence under industrial pressure**.

• Deep preparation defeated superficial bombardment
• Machine guns defeated massed infantry
• Tactical success (holding the line) coexisted with strategic
  failure (casualties unsustainable)

For Germania, the Somme pairs with Verdun: at Verdun Germany
attacked and was bled; at the Somme Germany defended and was
bled. Industrial war consumed both sides regardless of posture.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: The Somme was an Allied offensive; Germany defended.
No legal or constitutional trigger—pure operational contest. Liability:
Falkenhayn (German chief of staff) had to balance Verdun commitment
with Somme defense; resources split. Haig and Rawlinson bore Allied
exposure for 57,000 casualties on the first day.

Defection incentives: None materialized at the Somme; both sides
held. Institutional survival: The German army held the line; the
British New Armies were blooded. Irreversibility: 141 days, ~1 million
casualties; no territorial decision. The Western Front locked further
into attrition.
