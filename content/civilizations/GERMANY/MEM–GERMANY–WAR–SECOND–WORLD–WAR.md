I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Second World War as **Germania's total
defeat and unconditional surrender**: the conflict that began with
Blitzkrieg victories—Poland, France, the Low Countries—and ended
with Barbarossa's failure, two-front collapse, and the destruction
of the Reich. For Germania, the war encodes **operational success
turned into systemic annihilation**—the lesson of 1914–1918 inverted
(Blitzkrieg worked where Schlieffen failed) but overextension and
ideology produced total defeat.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. 1939–1940: BLITZKRIEG AND WESTERN VICTORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1 September 1939: Germany invaded Poland. Britain and France
declared war. Poland fell in weeks—Soviet invasion from the east
sealed partition. The Western Front remained quiet until May 1940.

Then Blitzkrieg: armoured thrust through the Ardennes; Sedan
crossing; encirclement of Allied forces; Dunkirk; Fall of France.
Six weeks. The Schlieffen Plan's failure in 1914 was reversed—
concentration, tempo, bypass of fixed defences. Hitler had learned
from 1914–1918: avoid attrition; achieve decision before exhaustion.

David Stahel notes the contrast:

"Operation Barbarossa was conceived as a repeat of the victories
in the west—a campaign of movement and encirclement designed to
destroy the Red Army in weeks, not months."
— David Stahel, *Operation Barbarossa and Germany's Defeat in the East* (2009)

The Western campaign succeeded. France signed an armistice at
Compiègne—the same site as 1918, roles reversed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. BARBAROSSA AND THE EASTERN FRONT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
22 June 1941: Operation Barbarossa. Germany invaded the Soviet
Union. The plan: destroy the Red Army in weeks; reach Moscow before
winter. Initial gains were vast—Minsk, Smolensk, Kiev, Leningrad
besieged. But the Red Army did not collapse. Winter 1941–1942;
Moscow held. Stalingrad (1942–1943): encirclement reversed;
Germany's Sixth Army surrendered. Kursk (1943): the last German
strategic offensive in the east failed. The Soviet advance became
irreversible—Oder, Berlin.

Barbarossa repeated the structural error of 1914: two-front war
without closure. Hitler had opened the Eastern Front before Britain
was defeated. The balance of power—America and the Soviet Union
uncommitted in 1940—turned decisively against Germany by 1943.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. 1944–1945: TOTAL DEFEAT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Allied landings: Normandy (June 1944), Provence (August 1944).
Soviet advances: Vistula–Oder offensive (January 1945); Berlin
(April–May 1945). Germany was crushed from east and west. Hitler
committed suicide (30 April 1945). Unconditional surrender: 7–8 May 1945.

Ian Kershaw captures the terminus:

"Germany in May 1945 was a landscape of ruins. Cities had been
reduced to rubble... The Third Reich had ceased to exist."
— Ian Kershaw, *Hitler: 1936–1945 Nemesis* (2000)

No armistice, no negotiated peace. Total defeat. Partition.
Occupation. Denazification. The Reich was erased.

V. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Second World War encodes **Blitzkrieg success and total defeat**.

• 1940 proved that rapid decision could be achieved—Schlieffen's
  failure reversed; France fell in six weeks
• Barbarossa overextended; two-front war returned; attrition
  consumed Germany
• Unconditional surrender: no stab-in-the-back myth could survive
  invasion and occupation
• Post-1945 Germany: constitutional restraint, NATO embedding,
  Holocaust memory as negative foundation

For Germania, 1939–1945 contrasts with 1914–1918: operational
success (1940) did not produce strategic victory; overextension and
ideology guaranteed systemic destruction. The lesson: competence
without political closure produces catastrophe.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: The Third Reich claimed total authority; no external
law constrained. Post-1945: Nuremberg established individual
criminal responsibility; "following orders" was no defence. Whose
law governs aggression and genocide? International law, imposed by
victors.

Personal liability: Hitler, Göring, Speer, Keitel—Nuremberg trials;
suicide, execution, imprisonment. The chain of command bore exposure.
Defection incentives: July 1944 plot; officers who sought to remove
Hitler; civilians who resisted. Institutional survival: The Wehrmacht
dissolved; the Reich abolished; Federal Republic and GDR founded on
repudiation. Irreversibility: Holocaust; eastern territories lost
(Oder–Neisse); partition; unconditional surrender. No negotiated
restoration—the settlement was imposed and permanent.
