I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Germania** as the civilizational and territorial
precursor to Germany: the Roman-era name and the medieval–early-modern
political space that preceded the modern nation-state.

For Germany, Germania encodes **continuity of name and geography without
implying a single sovereign state**. It records what the term denoted,
how East Frankish and Holy Roman structures inherited it, and how the
nineteenth-century German state claimed descent from it.

This file does not adjudicate whether Germany is the "true" heir of
Germania or whether the Holy Roman Empire was a first Reich. It records
the transmission of the name and the structural conditions of that
transmission.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ROMAN GERMANIA — NAME AND SCOPE
───────────────────────────────────────────────
**Germania** was the Latin name for the lands east of the Rhine and
north of the Danube: a geographic and ethnographic category, not a
polity. Tacitus fixed it in Western memory:

> "Germania as a whole is separated from the Gauls, the Raetians and
> Pannonians by the Rhine and Danube rivers; from the Sarmatians and
> Dacians by mutual fear or mountains. The rest is encircled by the
> ocean."
> — Tacitus, *Germania* (c. 98 AD) (ERC-PRIMARY)

The term denoted multiple peoples and no single state. Roman failure to
subdue the region left a lasting structural fact: **no durable imperial
integration from the Mediterranean**. Germanic political forms evolved
without Roman provincial law as a floor.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. EAST FRANKISH AND THE HOLY ROMAN EMPIRE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After the Carolingian partition (843), the **East Frankish kingdom**
inherited the core of what Romans had called Germania. The name
*Germania* persisted in Latin chronicles and in the title *rex
Teutonicorum* (king of the Germans). Otto I’s imperial coronation (962)
fused Germanic kingship with Roman imperial symbolism:

> "The Holy Roman Empire was in practice a German polity… the German
> king was the emperor, and the Empire’s core lands were those of
> medieval Germany."
> — Peter Wilson, *The Holy Roman Empire* (ERC-SECONDARY)

Structural constraint: the Empire remained a **layer above** multiple
principalities rather than a unified territorial state. Germania as
concept outlived Rome; "Germany" as a single sovereign state did not
exist until the nineteenth century.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. FROM GERMANIA TO GERMANY — KEY STEPS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Transmission path:

• **Name:** *Germania* (Latin) → *Deutschland* (vernacular) → Germany
  (English). The civilizational referent stayed the same; the polity
  did not.

• **Institutional break:** Dissolution of the Holy Roman Empire (1806)
  ended the first structure that had claimed the Germanic–Roman legacy.
  The Confederation of the Rhine and later the German Confederation
  were successor forms, not the Empire.

• **Unification (1871):** Bismarck’s Kleindeutsch solution created a
  **German nation-state** that explicitly claimed to complete the
  work of Germania: one state for the German nation. The Second Reich
  presented itself as the heir to both the Holy Roman Empire and the
  Germanic peoples of Tacitus.

The birth of "Germany" is thus the **territorial and sovereign
crystallization** of a name and a cultural–geographic space that had
existed for centuries without a single state.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. TENSION — CONTINUITY VERSUS RUPTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Germania encodes a tension: **continuity of name and space** versus
**discontinuity of institutions**. The Holy Roman Empire was not
Germany; the German Confederation was not the Empire. Yet each
invoked the same geographic and ethnic referent. Modern Germany
selectively claims Germania (language, territory, cultural identity)
while rejecting the Empire’s polycentric, non-national structure. The
memory of Germania thus supports both "we have always been here" and
"we only became a state in 1871."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. STRUCTURAL CONSTRAINTS (MEARSHEIMER LAYER)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Central Europe’s geography—river corridors, lack of natural frontiers,
exposure to east and west—favoured **multiple centres of power** rather
than early unification. The Holy Roman Empire’s elective monarchy and
territorial fragmentation reflected that structure. Only when one
power (Prussia) achieved sufficient dominance and external threats
(Liberal nationalism, French revanche) aligned did a German nation-state
become viable. Germania as concept did not cause unification; structural
incentives and Prussian capacity did.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**Jurisdiction:** No single court or sovereign "owned" the transition
from Germania to Germany. The Holy Roman Empire was dissolved by
Francis II’s abdication (1806)—a **voluntary surrender of title** under
Napoleonic pressure. Liability for the end of the old order rested with
the emperor and the estates that accepted the new order.

**Irreversibility:** Once the Empire was dissolved and the German
Confederation (and later the Kaiserreich) replaced it, the old
constitutional frame could not be restored. The name "Germania" passed
into history as a precursor label; "Germany" became the sovereign
carrier. Who bore the cost of that transition—dynasties, regions,
minorities—is preserved in separate MEMs (e.g. MEM–GERMANY–CONGRESS–VIENNA,
MEM–GERMANY–BISMARCK).

IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Germania was a Roman name for a non-Roman space.
• No single state held the territory until the modern period.
• The Holy Roman Empire carried the name and legacy; it was not
  "Germany."
• Unification in 1871 is the sovereign crystallization of the
  civilizational space called Germania.
• Continuity of name and geography; discontinuity of institutions.
