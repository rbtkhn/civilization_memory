I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the First World War as **Germania's strategic
defeat**: the conflict that began with Schlieffen's plan for quick
victory and ended with armistice, revolution, and Versailles—a
two-front war of attrition that exhausted the Empire and produced
the stab-in-the-back myth that would fuel Weimar instability and
Hitler's rise.

For Germania, the Great War encodes **operational failure and
structural exhaustion**—the Marne foreclosed quick victory; Verdun
and the Somme bled Germany; 1918 brought collapse, not negotiated
peace.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. SCHLIEFFEN AND THE MARNE REVERSAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The plan required France's defeat in six weeks, then pivot to Russia.

By early September 1914, German armies had advanced 300 miles.
Paris was threatened. Then von Kluck's pivot exposed the flank;
the gap between 1st and 2nd Armies opened; Hentsch authorized
retreat. The Schlieffen Plan died. Quick victory became impossible.
The war of movement became the war of trenches.

John Keegan summarizes:

"When Lieutenant Colonel Hentsch arrived from headquarters with
authority to coordinate, he found both army commanders contemplating
retreat. He authorized it. The Schlieffen Plan died in that decision."
— John Keegan, *The First World War* (1998)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. TWO-FRONT ATTRITION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Germany faced France and Britain in the west, Russia in the east.

The Western Front congealed into trench warfare—Marne, Ypres, Verdun,
Somme, Passchendaele. Attrition consumed both sides. Falkenhayn's
design at Verdun to "bleed France white" failed; the casualty ratio
reversed. The Somme demonstrated that deep dugouts and machine guns
could hold—but at cost. Industrial war had its own logic.

The Eastern Front saw German success—Tannenberg, Gorlice-Tarnow,
Brest-Litovsk (1918)—but the West could not be won. American
entry (1917) tipped the balance. Germany exhausted before the
Allies.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. 1918: COLLAPSE AND ARMISTICE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ludendorff's spring offensives (1918) failed to break the Western
Front. Allied counter-offensives drove German armies back. By
November, defeat was imminent. Revolution in Germany; Kaiser
abdicated. Armistice 11 November 1918.

The Empire had not been invaded. The army had not been destroyed
in the field. For many Germans, surrender appeared premature—the
stab-in-the-back myth (Dolchstoßlegende) claimed that politicians
and civilians had betrayed the undefeated army. The myth would
poison Weimar and empower the far right.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. VERSAILLES AND ITS BURDEN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Treaty of Versailles (1919) imposed reparations, territorial
losses (Alsace-Lorraine, Eupen-Malmédy, Danzig, colonies), military
restrictions, and war guilt (Article 231). Germany accepted under
duress. The "dictated peace" (Diktat) became a grievance that
Weimar could never transcend and Hitler would exploit.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The First World War encodes **strategic defeat and its consequences**.

• Schlieffen failed; two-front war could not be won
• Attrition exhausted Germany before the Allies
• Armistice preceded invasion; stab-in-the-back myth emerged
• Versailles imposed terms that poisoned legitimacy
• Weimar instability and Hitler's rise trace to 1918

For Germania, 1914–1918 contrasts with 1870: Franco-Prussian War
achieved quick decision through disciplined tempo; the Great War
degenerated into mutual hemorrhage. The lesson would inform
Blitzkrieg—avoid attrition; achieve decision before exhaustion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: The July crisis posed jurisdictional conflict—Austria
versus Serbia, Russia's mobilization, Germany's blank cheque. Belgian
neutrality violated; whose law governed? The Schlieffen Plan treated
Belgium as obstacle, not sovereign.

Personal liability: Wilhelm II, Bethmann Hollweg, Moltke, Falkenhayn,
Ludendorff—who bore exposure for defeat? The stab-in-the-back myth
displaced liability from military and imperial leadership to politicians
and civilians. Defection incentives: Revolution (November 1918) when
the army could no longer hold; sailors, workers, and soldiers defected
from the war effort. Institutional survival: The Empire collapsed; the
army dissolved; Weimar inherited legitimacy without foundation. Irreversibility:
Armistice, Versailles, territorial losses, Article 231. The settlement
structured Weimar's impossible politics—and Hitler's exploitation of
grievance.
