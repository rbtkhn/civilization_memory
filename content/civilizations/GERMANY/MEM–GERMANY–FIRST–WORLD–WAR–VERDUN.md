────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Verdun as **Germania's attrition strategy that
bled Germany white**: Falkenhayn's design to "bleed France white"
at a favorable casualty ratio failed—the ratio reversed, German
losses approached French, and the battle consumed both armies.

For Germania, Verdun encodes **strategic calculation gone wrong**—
the fortress was chosen because France could not abandon it; the
attrition math was wrong; the battle designed to break France
exhausted Germany and cost Falkenhayn his command.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FALKENHAYN'S DESIGN: OPERATION GERICHT
────────────────────────────────────────────
Falkenhayn's logic: Attack a point France must defend; inflict
casualties at 5:2 (German:French); bleed France until collapse.

Verdun chosen: historic fortress, symbolic value, salient vulnerable
to artillery. France would sacrifice everything to hold it.

John Keegan explains:

"Falkenhayn did not seek to capture Verdun. He sought to create a
killing ground where French soldiers would die faster than Germany
could lose them. The fortress complex was militarily outdated—the
forts had been stripped of guns for use elsewhere—but its symbolic
value was immense. Verdun had been French since 843; it had never
fallen to a German enemy. Falkenhayn calculated correctly that
France would sacrifice everything to hold it."
— John Keegan, *The First World War* (1998)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE OPENING: 21 FEBRUARY 1916
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Unprecedented bombardment: 1,200 guns, 1 million shells first day.
Fort Douaumont captured 25 February by a handful of German soldiers—it
was virtually undefended. The attack seemed to validate the design.

Then Pétain organized the defense: the Voie Sacrée, the noria system,
artillery prioritization. France held.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. THE ATTRITION REVERSAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Falkenhayn's arithmetic failed.

• Planned 5:2 ratio not achieved
• Actual ratio closer to 1:1.1
• German casualties ~337,000; French ~377,000
• France did not break; Germany exhausted itself

"Falkenhayn's attrition strategy failed on its own terms. He had
expected to bleed France at a favorable ratio; instead, German
casualties approached French. The French army endured; the German
army exhausted itself. By autumn 1916, Germany had lost not only
the battle but the strategic initiative. Falkenhayn was replaced
by Hindenburg and Ludendorff in August. Verdun had consumed
Germany too."
— John Keegan, *The First World War* (1998)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. OCTOBER–DECEMBER: THE RECAPTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
French counter-offensive under Nivelle and Mangin. Fort Douaumont
recaptured 24 October; Fort Vaux 2 November. By December, the front
returned approximately to February positions. Ten months, ~700,000
casualties, no territorial gain—but France had proven it would not break.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Verdun encodes **attrition as strategic fallacy**.

• Symbolic targets invite mutual sacrifice
• Casualty ratios cannot be engineered
• Defensive organization (Pétain's noria) can match offensive
  concentration
• Battles designed to break the enemy can break the attacker

For Germania, Verdun pairs with the Somme: at Verdun Germany attacked;
at the Somme Germany defended. Both produced mutual hemorrhage.
Industrial war had its own logic, resistant to generalship.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: Falkenhayn chose Verdun—a point France could not abandon—
as killing ground. The fortress complex was legally French since 843;
symbolic value compelled French defense. No treaty breach; pure strategic
targeting of political constraint.

Personal liability: Falkenhayn bore exposure for the attrition design;
Hindenburg and Ludendorff replaced him (August 1916) when the ratio
reversed. Crown Prince Wilhelm commanded the attacking forces; Pétain
commanded French defense. Defection incentives: Neither side could
quit; symbolism locked both in. Institutional survival: French army
endured; German army exhausted itself. Irreversibility: ~700,000
casualties, front returned to February positions. Falkenhayn's calculation
was irreversibly wrong.
