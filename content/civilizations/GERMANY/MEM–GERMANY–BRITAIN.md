I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Germania–Britain interface as **the maritime
hegemon that blocked German Continental primacy**: Great Britain
(from 1707) as naval power, coalition leader, and—in 1914 and 1939—
the state that chose war to prevent a single power dominating Europe.

For Germania, Britain encodes **the balancer**—the power whose
structural interest was to oppose Continental hegemony regardless
of cultural kinship. Hanover, Victorian affinity, and Protestant
solidarity did not prevent two world wars when Germany sought
"a place in the sun."

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. THE HANOVERIAN SUCCESSION (1714–1837)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
German kings ruled Britain. George I, Elector of Hanover, inherited
the British crown (1714). British resources defended Hanover; Hanoverian
interests shaped British policy. The personal union lasted 123 years.
Victoria's accession (1837) ended it—Salic Law prevented her ruling
Hanover—but the royal house remained German (Saxe-Coburg-Gotha)
until Windsor (1917).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. VICTORIAN KINSHIP AND NAVAL RIVALRY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Prince Albert promoted German culture and industry in Britain. The
two nations seemed natural allies. Then Tirpitz's naval program
(1898–1914) challenged Royal Navy supremacy. Robert Massie: "The
two nations, bound by blood, religion, and culture, marched toward
war because one built a navy and the other could not tolerate the
challenge." Britain aligned with France (Entente Cordiale, 1904)
and Russia (1907). Structural interest overrode kinship.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. TWO WORLD WARS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
August 1914: Britain declared war over Belgian neutrality. The
war produced mass casualties, anti-German hysteria, and the Windsor
renaming. 1939: Britain declared war again. Churchill: "We shall
fight on the beaches... we shall never surrender." Germany sought
hegemony; Britain led the coalition against it. A.J.P. Taylor: When
Germany sought Continental dominance, Britain opposed—regardless of
cultural affinity. Geopolitical interest prevailed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. POSTWAR RECONCILIATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
West Germany joined NATO (1955). Britain supported reunification
(1990). Both were EU members. The enmity of two world wars gave way
to alliance. Brexit (2020) created new complications—but not renewed
rivalry. Germany accepted restraint; Britain accepted a European
Germany. The structural condition for cooperation: no German quest
for hegemony.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Britain encodes **the balancer and eventual ally**.

• Hanover: German kings on British throne
• Naval race: kinship yielded to structural competition
• 1914, 1939: Britain opposed Continental hegemony
• Postwar: NATO, EU; reconciliation
