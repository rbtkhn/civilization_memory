I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Seven Years' War as **Austria's coalition
without victory**: the conflict in which Habsburg diplomacy achieved
the greatest alliance reversal of the eighteenth century yet failed
to recover Silesia or contain Prussia.

For Austria, the war is remembered as **proof that coalition
construction has limits**—that alliance architecture, however
ambitious, cannot substitute for cohesion, commitment, and
administrative endurance on the part of coalition members.

Prussia's encoding of the same conflict (survival under encirclement,
discipline, replacement capacity) is recorded in
MEM–GERMANY–PRUSSIA–SEVEN–YEARS–WAR.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. WAR ENTRY LOGIC: SILESIA REVANCHE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Austria enters the war to reverse the loss of Silesia (1740–1742).

Maria Theresa never accepted the seizure. Her objective was explicit:
recover the richest province of the Habsburg monarchy and restore
the territorial integrity Charles VI had bequeathed.

The traditional alliance system (Britain–Austria) had proved
inadequate. Britain would not fight a continental war for Silesia.

Kaunitz diagnosed the problem as **systemic, not tactical**.
Recovery required new partners.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE DIPLOMATIC REVOLUTION (1756)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Kaunitz engineered the most dramatic alliance reversal of the
eighteenth century.

Core transformation:
• Austria abandons Britain as primary ally
• Austria allies with France (centuries-long rival)
• Russia joins the anti-Prussian coalition
• Prussia isolated with only Britain remaining

Fred Anderson emphasizes the magnitude of this shift:

> "The alliance of France and Austria, enemies for two and a half
> centuries, was the diplomatic equivalent of an earthquake—a
> realignment so profound that contemporaries could scarcely believe it."
> — Fred Anderson, *Crucible of War* (2000)

For Austria, the Diplomatic Revolution was the **precondition**
for war—the structural change that made a second attempt at
Silesian recovery plausible.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. COALITION ADVANTAGES & FAILURES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Kaunitz's coalition was formidable on paper.

Coalition advantages:
• Three-front pressure on Prussia
• Superior combined demographic and fiscal resources
• Diplomatic isolation of Frederick
• French continental commitment (Treaty of Versailles, 1756)

Coalition failures:
• Coordination problems across theaters
• Russian unreliability (Peter III's withdrawal, 1762)
• French distraction in colonial theaters (North America, India)
• Prussian discipline and replacement depth
• Divergent war aims among coalition members

Anderson notes the structural lesson:

> "The coalition that Kaunitz assembled was the most powerful
> combination any European state had ever faced. That Prussia
> survived it at all testified to the fragility of alliances held
> together by hatred rather than common purpose."
> — Fred Anderson, *Crucible of War* (2000)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE OUTCOME: SILESIA REMAINED PRUSSIAN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The war ended without Austrian recovery of Silesia.

The Treaty of Hubertusburg (1763) confirmed the status quo ante
bellum. Prussia retained Silesia. Austria's relative position in
Germany declined permanently.

Martyn Rady observes:

> "The loss of Silesia was the great wound of Habsburg memory. Maria
> Theresa never reconciled herself to it; she merely learned to live
> with the humiliation."
> — Martyn Rady, *The Habsburgs* (2020)

The failure did not discredit Kaunitz's method. It demonstrated
the **limits of coalition without commitment**—and the asymmetry
between diplomatic architecture and administrative-military
endurance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. POST-WAR RECALIBRATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Austria accepted Prussian permanence as a fact.

Post-war adjustments:
• Focus on internal reform (Maria Theresa's administrative modernization)
• Participation in Polish partitions (1772, 1793)
• Maintenance of French alliance
• Redirection of Habsburg ambition (Balkan, Italy)

Peter Wilson observes:

> "Kaunitz's true legacy was not the recovery of Silesia but the
> demonstration that Austria could reshape European diplomacy when
> it chose patience over provocation."
> — Peter Wilson, *Heart of Europe* (2016)

The war taught Austria that coalition construction could reshape
the diplomatic map—but could not, by itself, defeat an adversary
whose administrative state could regenerate faster than the
coalition could coordinate.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Who bore exposure when the coalition failed?

**Maria Theresa** — Sovereign responsibility. She authorized the
war and never disowned the Silesian objective. Personal liability
was real but survivable; the Habsburg monarchy endured.

**Kaunitz** — Architect of the Diplomatic Revolution. His coalition
succeeded in construction, failed in execution. Institutional
survival: he remained State Chancellor until 1792. The failure
did not end his career; it defined the limits of his method.

**Defection incentive** — Russia (Peter III) exited first. France
prioritized colonial theaters. Austria, as the power with the
clearest stake (Silesia), could not defect without abandoning
the objective. **Irreversibility**: Silesia was not recovered;
the territorial outcome was locked in at Hubertusburg.

IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Coalitions can be constructed but not commanded.
• Alliance architecture has limits; cohesion and commitment matter.
• Administrative endurance can defeat coalition numbers.
• Diplomatic revolution can reshape the map but not guarantee outcomes.
• The wound of Silesia persisted; Austria learned to accommodate.
