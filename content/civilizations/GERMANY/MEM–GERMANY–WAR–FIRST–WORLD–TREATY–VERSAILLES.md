I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Treaty of Versailles as **Germania's imposed
settlement**—the Diktat that excluded Germany from negotiation,
assigned war guilt (Article 231), and imposed terms that poisoned
Weimar legitimacy and enabled Hitler's rise.

For Germania, Versailles encodes **closure without Path A**—the
defeated had no voice, no Talleyrand to reframe the outcome as
equilibrium. Vienna had bound France; Versailles crushed Germany.
The contrast is structural.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. EXCLUSION FROM NEGOTIATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Germany was not permitted to negotiate.

• Armistice (November 1918) preceded terms
• Peace conference (Paris 1919) excluded German representatives
• Terms were presented; Germany could sign or face invasion
• "Negotiation" was acceptance under duress

The Congress of Vienna (1815) had included France—Talleyrand
reframed the settlement, gave French elites a stake. Versailles
had no equivalent. Germany's signatories bore domestic liability
as "November criminals" for accepting what they could not refuse.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ARTICLE 231 AND WAR GUILT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Article 231 assigned Germany and its allies sole responsibility
for the war.

The clause was drafted to establish legal basis for reparations—
liability under international law. For Germany, it became **moral
condemnation**—the entire nation branded guilty. No German government
could narrate the settlement as honorable. Legitimacy was impossible.

Christopher Clark frames the consequence:

"The war-guilt clause was not merely a legal device for reparations.
It was a delegitimization of the entire German position. No Weimar
politician could defend Versailles without losing domestic support.
The treaty structured Weimar's impossible politics."
— Christopher Clark, *The Sleepwalkers* (2012)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. TERRITORIAL AND MILITARY PROVISIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The treaty imposed:

• Alsace-Lorraine to France
• Eupen-Malmédy to Belgium
• Danzig free city; Polish corridor
• Saar under League administration
• Rhineland demilitarized
• Army limited to 100,000; no conscription; no tanks, aircraft,
  submarines
• Colonies surrendered as mandates

Germany lost 13% of territory, 10% of population. The Rhine—Germany's
civilizational spine—was stripped of sovereignty. The settlement was
designed to prevent German recovery as a great power.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CONTRAST WITH VIENNA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MEM–GERMANY–CONGRESS–VIENNA encodes the contrast:

Vienna (1815): France included; Talleyrand at the table; restoration
path; settlement narratable as equilibrium. France could accept
honorably.

Versailles (1919): Germany excluded; terms dictated; no restoration
path; settlement unnarratable as honorable. Revision became the
only domestically viable stance. Enforcement exceeded Allied will;
revision exceeded German capacity—until Hitler.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Versailles encodes **the cost of exclusion from settlement design**.

• Closure without inclusion produces grievance
• War guilt delegitimizes the defeated
• Terms imposed by victors cannot be enforced indefinitely when
  the defeated retains revision capacity
• Weimar's instability traces to a treaty no German could defend

For Germania, Versailles is the anti-Vienna—the settlement that
failed because it violated the logic of durable peace: bind the
defeated, do not crush them.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: The victors claimed authority to impose terms. Germany
had no standing to contest structure—only to sign under ultimatum.
International law provided no forum for the defeated.

Personal liability: German signatories (Brockdorff-Rantzau, Müller,
Bell) bore domestic exposure—"November criminals." Erzberger
(armistice) and others were assassinated. Weimar governments that
attempted fulfillment (Stresemann) were attacked; those who resisted
(London ultimatum 1921) faced invasion. Defection incentives: US
defected first (non-ratification); Britain toward accommodation.
Germany had incentive to evade; France had incentive to enforce but
lacked means alone. Institutional survival: Weimar's legitimacy
never recovered. The Republic was identified with Versailles; Nazis
exploited the linkage. Irreversibility: Territorial transfers were
executed; the treaty was signed. But enforcement was reversible—
reparations reduced, Rhineland remilitarized—and so was the settlement.
Versailles was reversed by war.
