I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Spain** as Germania's Habsburg partner in the
encirclement of France—the southwestern arm of a dynastic ring that
unified Empire, Spain, Netherlands, and Austria under one house, and
then broke when Spain passed to the Bourbons.

Spain is not Germania's mirror.
It is **the partner that was lost**—the proof that dynastic accumulation
can reverse, and that composite monarchy fractures when legitimacy
and capacity diverge.

For Germania, Spain encodes the structural lesson: Habsburg universal
monarchy required Spanish resources and Spanish geography; when Spain
left the orbit (1700), the encirclement collapsed and France gained
a dynastic ally instead of a rival.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. HABSBURG COMPOSITE: SPAIN AND EMPIRE UNITED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Charles V inherits Spain (1516) and the Empire (1519).

The union creates the largest European polity since Rome:

> "Charles's empire was not a state but a composite of inheritances,
> each with its own laws, institutions, and expectations. Governing
> required constant negotiation, not command."
> — Peter H. Wilson, *Heart of Europe* (2016)
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

Spain supplies:
• Maritime reach and colonial extraction
• Revenue that continental territories cannot match
• Strategic depth against France (south and west)

The Empire supplies legitimacy; Spain supplies capacity.
Neither can be governed as one.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ENCIRCLEMENT OF FRANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Habsburg territories surround France.

Spain, the Netherlands, Franche-Comté, Austria, and imperial Italy
form a ring. France faces strategic encirclement for two centuries.

> "The Habsburg-Valois rivalry was the first modern great-power
> competition. France faced encirclement by a single dynasty
> controlling Spain, Austria, the Netherlands, and the Empire.
> French policy for two centuries aimed at breaking that ring."
> — A.J.P. Taylor, *The Struggle for Mastery in Europe* (1954)
> [ERC: SECONDARY]

Spain is the southwestern arm. Without Spain, the ring breaks.
With Spain, France is pressured from multiple directions.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PARTITION: PHILIP II AND FERDINAND (1556)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Charles abdicates (1556). The inheritance splits.

• Philip II: Spain, Netherlands, Italy, Americas
• Ferdinand I: Austria, Imperial title

The structural unity fractures. Spain and Empire remain allied
through dynasty but no longer share a ruler.

Spain continues to support Habsburg interests—against France,
against Protestant powers—until the line fails.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. WAR OF THE SPANISH SUCCESSION (1701–1714)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Charles II of Spain dies without heir (1700).

The succession crisis triggers European war:
• Bourbon Philip (grandson of Louis XIV) vs. Habsburg Charles
• Austria and the Empire fight to keep Spain in Habsburg orbit
• Coalition warfare; Germania cannot impose outcome

The Peace of Utrecht (1713–1714) awards Spain to the Bourbons.
Spain leaves the Habsburg system.

Structural consequence: France gains a dynastic ally where it
had faced a Habsburg partner. The encirclement inverts.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. BOURBON SPAIN AND STRATEGIC REALIGNMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Post-1700, Spain aligns with France.

• Family Compact (Pactes de Famille): Bourbon Spain and Bourbon
  France coordinate against common enemies
• Spain no longer pressures France from the southwest
• Germania (Austria, later Prussia) loses Spanish counterweight

Spain's imperial exhaustion—fiscal collapse, colonial overstretch—
continues. But the strategic alignment has flipped.

Germany pursued continental depth; Spain had pursued global span.
Spain's decline is a different lesson: maritime empire exhausts
faster than continental consolidation—a warning Germany internalized
by not replicating Spain's path.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Spain encodes **the reversibility of dynastic accumulation**.

For Germania:
• Habsburg universal monarchy depended on Spain; Spain was lost
• Encirclement of France required Spanish partnership; partnership
  ended when the dynasty failed
• Composite monarchy fractures when inheritance breaks
• Maritime/colonial empire exhausts; continental depth endures
  (Germany learned by contrast—did not pursue Spain's global path)

Spain is Germania's **lost partner**—the piece that made the
Habsburg ring complete, and whose loss realigned European structure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. LIABILITY / MECHANISM (BARNES DIMENSION)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jurisdiction: Who had authority over the Spanish succession?
Charles II's will named Philip of Anjou; Habsburg claims rested on
dynastic right. The question was settled by war, not law.

Personal liability: Charles II bears the structural exposure—no heir,
no clear succession. Louis XIV and Leopold I pursued competing claims;
coalition warfare determined outcome. No single actor "owned" the
result; defection incentives (Dutch, English, Austrian) fragmented
coalition coherence.

Irreversibility: Utrecht was effectively final. Spain remained Bourbon
until the Napoleonic period; the Habsburg-Spanish union was never
restored. The 1700 succession was a one-way door.
