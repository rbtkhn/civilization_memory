I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **India–Islam interface** as a civilizational
contact zone: transmission, conquest, absorption, and legitimacy
across Sultanate, Mughal, and Partition—without reducing the
interface to a single verdict (conflict or harmony).

India–Islam is **transmission and rule**: Islamic sultanates and
empires rule North India (Delhi Sultanate, Mughal); they are
absorbed into Indian civilizational memory as **Indian** regimes
(MEM–INDIA–DELHI–SULTANATE, MEM–INDIA–DYNASTY–MUGHAL). Partition
(1947) severs territory and encodes **exit and rupture**—yet
India’s civilizational continuity is asserted by plural cohesion,
not by erasure of the Islamic thread.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. TRANSMISSION & ENTRY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Islam enters India via trade, conquest, and settlement—the
northwest corridor (MEM–INDIA–GEO–INDUS) again the axis. Early
raids and Ghaznavid pressure (MEM–INDIA–WAR–GHAZNAVID); Delhi
Sultanate (c. 1206–1526) as first sustained Islamic sultanate
in North India (MEM–INDIA–DELHI–SULTANATE).

Key features:
• Islamic authority (sultanic, not caliphal) over predominantly
  non-Islamic society
• Persianate–Indic synthesis; court and administration
• No systematic civilizational erasure; plural coexistence and
  extraction, not replacement

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ABSORPTION — SULTANATE & MUGHAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Delhi Sultanate and the Mughal dynasty are encoded in Indian
memory as **absorbed**—foreign-origin regimes that became Indian
(MEM–INDIA–DELHI–SULTANATE, MEM–INDIA–DYNASTY–MUGHAL). Legitimacy
is sultanic and pragmatic (Sultanate); then Mughal adds dharmic
accommodation and plural governance (Akbar).

India absorbs even its Islamic conquerors. The Mughals are
remembered as legitimate Indian rulers, cultural synthesizers,
administrative predecessors—not as “Islamic occupation.”

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PARTITION — EXIT & RUPTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Partition (1947) encodes **exit and rupture**: imperial withdrawal,
administrative severance, demographic displacement (MEM–INDIA–
PARTITION). Pakistan is created as an Islamic state; India remains
plural and secular in constitutional form.

The India–Islam interface does not end with Partition—it **reframes**:
territorial severance, two states, ongoing encoding of legitimacy
(secular India vs. Islamic Pakistan). India’s civilizational
continuity is asserted by plural cohesion and by the memory of
absorption (Sultanate, Mughal)—not by erasure of the Islamic
presence.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL CONSEQUENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Outcomes:
• India–Islam = transmission, rule, absorption, then Partition-
  era rupture.
• Sultanate and Mughal are encoded as Indian regimes; Islam is
  integrated into Indian political and cultural memory.
• Partition severs territory; it does not erase the memory of
  absorption or the ongoing interface (Muslims in India; India–
  Pakistan; civilizational encoding).

Rule learned:
India can host Islamic rule and absorb it; India can also undergo
territorial rupture (Partition) without dissolving civilizational
continuity. The interface is **plural**—transmission, absorption,
rupture—not a single verdict.
