I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **India–Pakistan wars** as **episodic eruptions
of the Partition wound**—conflicts in which the civilizational fracture
of 1947 recurs without resolution.

The wars are remembered not as separate events but as **one unresolved
process**: Kashmir contested, procedure without consensus, administrative
spine retained by India, Pakistan defined negatively against India.
No decisive closure; no social consensus; managed, not settled.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. WAR SEQUENCE — 1947–1999
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**1947–48 First Kashmir War**
Partition spillover; tribesmen and Pakistan regulars enter Kashmir;
Maharaja Hari Singh accedes to India; Indian troops airlifted;
UN ceasefire (1949); Line of Control (LoC) established. Kashmir
divided; accession incomplete; legitimacy contested.

**1965 Second Kashmir War**
Pakistan infiltration (Operation Gibraltar); India counterattack;
stalemate; Tashkent Agreement (1966). Neither side gains decisive
advantage; Kashmir remains unresolved.

**1971 Bangladesh War**
East Pakistan crisis; India intervenes; Pakistan surrenders;
Bangladesh independence; Shimla Agreement (1972). India achieves
military victory; bilateral procedure (Shimla) replaces multilateral
(Kashmir). Kashmir deferred, not resolved.

**1999 Kargil**
Pakistan infiltration across LoC; India repels; Lahore Declaration
(Feb 1999) already failed. Nuclear underwrite constrains escalation;
stalemate restored; procedure without consensus endures.

III. CIVILIZATIONAL ENCODING — LEGITIMACY & PROCEDURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, the Pakistan wars encode:

• **Partition wound unresolved** — The fracture of 1947 recurs;
  Kashmir is the locus; no civilizational transfer, only rupture.
• **Procedure without consensus** — UN, Tashkent, Shimla, Lahore;
  agreements freeze conflict; they do not resolve legitimacy.
• **Administrative spine retained** — India holds the institutional
  centre; Pakistan lacks depth; asymmetric emergence from Partition
  persists.
• **Managed, not resolved** — Wars end in ceasefire or agreement;
  Kashmir remains constitutional frontier; legitimacy strain endures.

The wars are not accidents; they are **recurrences** of the same
civilizational fracture. India survives; closure does not arrive.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL LOGIC — POWER & CONSTRAINT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Structurally, the India–Pakistan wars reflect **asymmetric emergence**:
India retained the administrative spine of British India; Pakistan
inherited fragments without depth. The balance of incentives favours
India in the long run—demography, economy, institutional continuity—
but Pakistan's negative identity (defined against India) produces
recurrent confrontation. Kashmir is the territorial locus; neither
side can concede without legitimacy collapse. Nuclearization (1998)
adds mutual constraint—escalation is bounded; stalemate is preserved.
The structural logic: **force cannot resolve what legitimacy cannot
close**.
