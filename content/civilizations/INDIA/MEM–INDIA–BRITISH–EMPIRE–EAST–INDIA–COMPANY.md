I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the East India Company as **India's entry
mechanism for corporate–state rule**: the chartered trading body
that transitioned from commerce to revenue to sovereignty—
authority via contracts, courts, and cash before formal Crown
administration.

The Company is remembered as **hybrid actor**—neither purely
merchant nor purely sovereign; the institutional vehicle through
which Britain entered India procedurally rather than militarily.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. INSTITUTIONAL FORM & CHARTER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The East India Company (EIC) is chartered 1600 by Elizabeth I—a
joint-stock company with monopoly on English trade east of the
Cape of Good Hope. Key structural features:

• Commercial charter — Trade, not conquest; profit motive; shareholder
  governance; London-based Court of Directors
• Three presidencies — Madras (1640), Bombay (1668), Bengal (1690);
  each with fort, factory, and eventually territorial revenue
• Sovereign delegation — Right to make war and peace; mint coins;
  administer justice; raise troops; granted incrementally by Crown
  and Mughal farmans

The Company accumulates **state-like functions** without ceasing
to be a commercial corporation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. COMMERCIAL-TO-TERRITORIAL TRANSITION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The pivot is Bengal. Plassey (1757) and Buxar (1764) secure
Company control of Bengal revenue—diwani rights (1765) grant
revenue collection in Bengal, Bihar, Orissa. The Company becomes
**revenue sovereign** before it becomes **political sovereign**.

Sequence:
• Trade (pre-1757) — Factories; commodities; tribute and farmans
• Revenue (1757–1773) — Plassey; Buxar; diwani; extraction replaces
  trade as primary income
• Administration (1773–1858) — Regulating Act (1773); Governor-General;
  Pitt's India Act (1784); Board of Control; Company as Crown agent
• Collapse (1857) — Sepoy mutiny; Company liability; Crown takeover
  (1858); Company dissolved (1874)

Power precedes legitimacy. The Company rules before the Crown
formally administers.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. MECHANISM FAILURE & JURISDICTION SHIFT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Company's structural dependence on Indian troops produces
the 1857 rupture. Bengal Army: ~135,000 Indian (sepoys/sowars),
~24,000 European. When sepoys mutinied, the **invader's proxy**
defected—exposure fell on the Company. MEM–INDIA–WAR–1857
encodes this as third defection type: invader's local force
defects against invader.

Who had compulsory authority over the sepoy force? The Company
had raised and deployed it; when the proxy turned, the Company
was on the hook. Jurisdiction shifted: Crown took over (1858).
Company rule ends; Crown rule begins.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL ENCODING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, the East India Company encodes:
• Corporate conquest — India fell to a trading company, not an
  army; "commercial pirates" (Durant); "jewel in the crown"
  (Ferguson)
• Procedural entry — Contracts, courts, cash before sovereignty;
  elite defection (Plassey); fragmentation exploited (Mughal decline)
• Extraction without absorption — Revenue and trade; administration
  as means to extraction; legitimacy never dharmic
• Jurisdiction follows liability — When the mechanism failed
  (1857), exposure shifted; Crown replaced Company

The Company is remembered as **institutional vehicle**—the mechanism
through which British rule entered and consolidated—not as
legitimate sovereign.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Corporations can accumulate state functions without becoming states.
• Commercial logic precedes political logic; revenue precedes sovereignty.
• Mechanism failure (1857) produces jurisdiction shift, not regime collapse.
• The Company outlives its governing role—dissolved 1874; Crown
  inherits its structure.

Tension preserved without verdict.
