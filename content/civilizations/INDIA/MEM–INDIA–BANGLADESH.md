I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Bangladesh** as **India's eastern neighbour
born of Partition and 1971**—the sovereign state that emerged
from East Pakistan when the Bengal delta's second fracture
(Ganges heartland → East Pakistan → Bangladesh) completed
the administrative separation begun in 1947.

Bangladesh is remembered not as lost territory but as **Bengal
sovereign**—cultural continuity across the border; political
separation; India's 1971 intervention as the decisive moment
that severed East from West Pakistan and created a neighbour
where once there was a province.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. PARTITION TO INDEPENDENCE — TWO FRACTURES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Bangladesh's trajectory runs through two boundary-making events.

**1947 — First fracture**
East Bengal assigned to Pakistan; becomes East Pakistan.
Radcliffe Line severs the Bengal delta (MEM–INDIA–BENGAL;
MEM–INDIA–PARTITION). West Bengal remains in India; East Bengal
in Pakistan. Dhaka as East Pakistan capital. Geographic and
administrative distance from West Pakistan (Karachi, later
Islamabad); linguistic and cultural distance (Bengali vs Urdu).

**1971 — Second fracture**
East Pakistan crisis; India intervenes; Pakistan surrenders;
Bangladesh independence. Shimla Agreement (1972) formalises
bilateral procedure. East Pakistan becomes Bangladesh—a sovereign
state, not a province (MEM–INDIA–WAR–PAKISTAN; MEM–INDIA–PAKISTAN).

The delta is now divided between **two sovereign states** (India,
Bangladesh), not one. Calcutta (India) and Dhaka (Bangladesh)
face each other across a border; civilizational continuity
survives political separation.

III. CIVILIZATIONAL ENCODING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, Bangladesh encodes:

• **Ganges delta sovereign** — Same heartland substrate as West
  Bengal; sacred geography, demography, riverine agriculture;
  now a neighbour, not a province (MEM–INDIA–GEO–GANGES)
• **1971 as decisive moment** — India's intervention; Pakistan's
  surrender; Bangladesh emergence; Shimla as procedural legacy
• **Cultural continuity, political separation** — Bengal divided
  between India and Bangladesh; shared language, tradition,
  river system; distinct sovereignty
• **Partition's second act** — 1947 severed Bengal; 1971 severed
  Pakistan; Bangladesh is the outcome of both
• **Neighbour, not adversary** — Unlike Pakistan (Kashmir locus,
  procedure without consensus), India–Bangladesh relations
  encode cooperation and continuity alongside border management

Bangladesh is remembered as **heartland sovereign**—the delta
divided by boundary-making; cultural continuity survives;
political separation is real.
