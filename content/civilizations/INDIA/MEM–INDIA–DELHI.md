I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Delhi** as **India's capital locus**—the site
where imperial and republican authority concentrates, from the
Delhi Sultanate through Mughal rule, British transfer (1911), and
the Republic.

Delhi is remembered as **capital continuity**—the node to which
authority returns regardless of regime. Empires build capitals
elsewhere; Delhi absorbs them.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CAPITAL SEQUENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Delhi functions as capital across regimes:

• Delhi Sultanate (1206–1526) — Multiple Delhi sites; Tughlaqabad,
  Firoz Shah Kotla; capital of North Indian sultanates
• Mughal (1526–1857) — Shahjahanabad (Old Delhi, 1639); Red Fort;
  imperial ceremonial centre; Mughal capital after Agra
• British (1911–1947) — New Delhi; capital transferred from
  Calcutta (1911); Lutyens' Delhi; Viceroy's residence; imperial
  capital until independence
• Republic (1947–present) — National capital; Parliament; Rashtrapati
  Bhavan; seat of Union government

Delhi encodes **authority concentration**—regimes change; the
capital site persists.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. 1911 TRANSFER — CALCUTTA TO DELHI
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The British transfer of capital (1911) marks a strategic and
symbolic shift.

Reasons:
• Northwestern focus — Frontier defence; Afghanistan; Central Asia
• Mughal symbolism — Delhi as traditional Indian capital;
  legitimacy through site
• Administrative centrality — Geography; rail; centrality to
  subcontinent
• Calcutta's Bengal bias — Delhi as pan-Indian; Calcutta as
  regional

New Delhi (inaugurated 1931) — Lutyens and Baker; imperial
architecture; Rashtrapati Bhavan (Viceroy's House); Parliament;
central vista. British empire appropriates Mughal capital site.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL ENCODING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, Delhi encodes:
• Capital continuity — Sultanate, Mughal, British, Republic;
  authority returns to the same locus
• Imperial-to-republican transition — Same site; legitimacy
  shifts from Crown to Constitution
• Mughal legacy — Old Delhi (Shahjahanabad) and New Delhi
  coexist; layered capital
• Administrative spine — Delhi as Republic capital; institutional
  continuity from British New Delhi

Delhi is remembered as **the place where India is governed**—
regime-neutral capital site.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. RELATION TO CALCUTTA & MUMBAI
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MEM–INDIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE–CALCUTTA preserves Calcutta as imperial
capital (1773–1911); the 1911 transfer ends that primacy.
MEM–INDIA–MUMBAI preserves Mumbai as commercial/financial capital—never
administrative capital. Delhi is **administrative capital**; Mumbai
is **commercial capital**. Calcutta lost administrative primacy to
Delhi; Mumbai never sought it. Tension preserved.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Capital sites persist across regimes; Delhi absorbs successive
  authorities.
• The 1911 transfer encodes British recognition of Delhi's symbolic
  and strategic centrality.
• Administrative and commercial capitals can diverge—Delhi governs;
  Mumbai trades.
• Imperial architecture repurposed—Rashtrapati Bhavan, Parliament—
  legitimacy shifts, site endures.

Tension preserved without verdict.
