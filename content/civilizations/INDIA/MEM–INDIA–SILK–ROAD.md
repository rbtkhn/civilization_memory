I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves India's relationship to the **Silk Road** as
**eastern reach and maritime hinge**—the subcontinent's connection
to transcontinental trade and transmission via overland northwest
and maritime Indian Ocean corridors.

India was never the Silk Road's central corridor (Persia) or eastern
terminus (China). India sat at the **southern and eastern margin**—
a source of goods and ideas, connected by land and sea, absorbing
and transmitting without controlling the main axis.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. INDIA'S SILK ROAD POSITION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
India connects to the Silk Road network through two vectors:

**Overland — Northwest:**
• Khyber Pass, Kabul, Punjab — Entry to Bactria, Sogdiana, Central Asia
• Kashmir — Northern corridor; Buddhist transmission
• Indus corridor — Same axis used by Alexander, Ghaznavids, Mughals

**Maritime — Indian Ocean:**
• Spice Route — Indian ports (e.g. Malabar) to Persian Gulf, Red Sea
• Zaytun–Hormuz (Yuan/Mongol) — Sea link from southern China via
  Indian Ocean to Hormuz; Indian ports as way stations
• Cotton, spices, gems — Indian goods flow west via land and sea

India is **source and way station**, not corridor controller.
Persia holds the nexus; China holds the eastern terminus; India
supplies and transits.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. TRANSMISSION — BUDDHISM & CULTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
India exports ideas along Silk Road vectors.

Key vectors:
• Buddhism — India → Central Asia → China; transmission via
  oasis nodes (Kashgar, Dunhuang); monks, texts, art
• Sanskrit corpus — Preserved and transmitted through Central
  Asian and Tibetan intermediaries
• Cotton, spices, gems — Material goods flow west; ideas flow east

India is **source** of dharma; Silk Road is **conduit**.
Transmission precedes conquest.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. MONGOL PERIOD — PAX MONGOLICA & MARITIME SHIFT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Under Mongol rule (Pax Mongolica), Silk Road corridors unified—
but India deflected overland Mongol conquest (MEM–INDIA–MONGOL–EMPIRE).

Key elements:
• Overland — Chagatai realm along Silk Road; Delhi Sultanate
  resisted; India not part of Mongol khanate
• Maritime — Zaytun–Hormuz sea link; Indian Ocean ports as
  way stations; India connected via sea, not land, to Mongol
  corridor
• India at eastern reach — MEM–PERSIA–SILK–ROAD: "India at
  Silk Road's eastern reach"

India experiences Silk Road connectivity via **maritime** axis
during Mongol apex; overland connection runs through northwest
but India does not control the corridor.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. MARITIME DISPLACEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Silk Road decline (c. 14th–15th century) coincides with maritime
ascendancy.

Effects:
• Cape route (1498) — Portuguese bypass Silk Road; reach India
  by sea; maritime displacement of overland trade
• Indian Ocean primacy — Spice trade, cotton, gems flow via
  sea; India's maritime interface grows
• Overland corridor loses relevance — India's Silk Road
  connection attenuates; maritime routes dominate

India's trade and transmission shift from **overland–maritime
hybrid** to **maritime primacy**. The Silk Road encodes an earlier
phase—source and way station—before maritime displacement.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL ENCODING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, the Silk Road encodes:
• Eastern reach — India at margin, not centre; source of goods
  and ideas; connected but not controlling
• Overland northwest — Same axis as invasion (Alexander,
  Ghaznavid, Mughal); corridor of trade and conquest
• Maritime hinge — Indian Ocean links to Persian Gulf; India
  as way station and supplier
• Maritime displacement — Silk Road declines; India's maritime
  interface grows; overland attenuates

India remembers the Silk Road as **connection without centrality**—
supplier and transmitter, not corridor controller.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. RELATION TO PERSIA & CHINA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MEM–PERSIA–SILK–ROAD preserves Persia as **nexus**—intermediary,
transit power. MEM–CHINA–SILK–ROAD preserves China as **eastern
terminus**—corridor of flow, Mongol unification. This MEM preserves
India as **eastern reach**—source, way station, maritime hinge.
Same network; different encoding. Tension preserved.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• India connects to Silk Road via overland (northwest) and maritime
  (Indian Ocean); never central corridor.
• Source of goods (spices, cotton, gems) and ideas (Buddhism);
  transmission precedes conquest.
• Maritime displacement shifts India's connectivity from hybrid
  to maritime primacy.
• Same northwest axis serves trade and invasion—corridor of flow
  and corridor of force.

Tension preserved without verdict.
