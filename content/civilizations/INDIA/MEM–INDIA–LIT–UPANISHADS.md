────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Upanishads** as India's **philosophical and soteriological core** within the Vedic-Sanskrit layer—the texts that turn from ritual (yajña) to knowledge (jñāna), encode atman–brahman identity, moksha (liberation), and the teacher–disciple transmission that later philosophy (Vedanta, Shankara) refines.

The Upanishads are not statecraft. They are **legitimacy of the inner sovereign**: the claim that ultimate reality and the self are one, that liberation is possible through knowledge, and that this knowledge is transmitted orally from teacher to pupil—"sitting near" (upa-ni-ṣad). They sit between Veda (ritual) and classical darśana (systematic philosophy); they are shruti (revealed) but philosophical.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT & FORM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Principal Upanishads emerge in the late Vedic and early post-Vedic period—forest and hermitage texts attached to Vedic schools (Brahmana/Āraṇyaka). Dialogue form; named teachers and pupils; no single author or court.

Structural conditions:
• Shift from ritual efficacy to knowledge as path to liberation
• Sanskrit as medium; oral then written; plurality of texts (no single "Upanishad" but a corpus)
• No royal patron as primary—tradition carried by lineages and later commentators
• Ganges heartland and north India as generative geography

The Upanishads encode **knowledge over ritual**—without abolishing ritual; they add a second storey (jnana-marga) to the Vedic edifice.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CORE THEMES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• **Atman and Brahman** — Self (atman) and ultimate reality (brahman); identity or relation expressed in great sayings (mahāvākyas): "That thou art" (tat tvam asi); "I am brahman" (ahaṃ brahmāsmi). Monistic tendency that Advaita Vedanta (Shankara) systematizes.
• **Moksha** — Liberation from cycle of rebirth (samsara); release through knowledge, not only sacrifice. The fourth purushartha (alongside dharma, artha, kama)—structural alternative to Arthashastra's artha-first frame.
• **Teacher–disciple** — Transmission by initiation and dialogue; legitimacy of the lineage (parampara). Authority is personal and pedagogical, not institutional in a church sense.
• **Plurality of formulations** — Different Upanishads stress different aspects (monism, cosmology, ethics); no single orthodoxy. "A thousand variations on one monistic theme" (Durant)—continuity through variation.

The Upanishads establish **philosophical legitimacy** for India's civilizational self-description: from "barbarous idolatry" to "the most subtle and spiritual pantheism" (Durant)—the range Hinduism claims.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Upanishads function as:

• **Substrate for Vedanta** — Shankara (8th c. CE) and later commentators read the Upanishads as non-dualist scripture; Advaita becomes dominant philosophical export. Continuity: "from the Upanishads eight centuries before Christ to Shankara eight centuries after him."
• **Legitimacy of renunciate and philosophical tradition** — Sannyasi, forest-dweller, jnana-yogi claim Upanishadic authority. Kingship (Arthashastra, epics) and renunciation (Upanishads) coexist in the same civilizational frame.
• **Textual plurality within Hinduism** — With Vedas, epics, Puranas, the Upanishads are one pillar of "no single canon" (MEM–INDIA–HINDUISM). They supply the philosophical and soteriological layer.
• **Interface with Buddhism** — Shared milieu (samsara, liberation, meditation); Buddhism rejects atman (no-self) while retaining karma and rebirth. The Upanishads are the **contrast set** for Buddhist innovation.

The Upanishads do not found a state; they found **philosophical continuity**—a grammar of ultimate reality and liberation that survives political fragmentation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. STRUCTURAL: TRANSMISSION WITHOUT TERRITORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Upanishadic ideas travel without conquest:

• **Buddhism** — Buddhist thought develops in the same intellectual climate; negation of atman is a direct engagement with Upanishadic atman–brahman. Transmission to Tibet, China, Southeast Asia carries Indian philosophical vocabulary (adapted).
• **Sufi and Persian** — Later translation and dialogue (e.g. Mughal period) bring Upanishadic themes into contact with Islamic philosophy. No Indian state required.
• **European reception** — Schopenhauer, the Romantics, Orientalist scholarship; "India as mother of our philosophy" (Durant). Philosophical export as soft power—no territorial control.

The Upanishads are **diffusion-first**: ideas spread through translation and commentary; legitimacy accrues to the tradition, not to a particular polity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LIMITS & TENSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• **Elite and Sanskrit** — Transmission was originally restricted; vernacular and popular bhakti develop alongside, not always in harmony with jnana-oriented Upanishads.
• **Interpretive plurality** — Advaita (non-dualism), Dvaita (dualism), Vishishtadvaita (qualified non-dualism) all claim Upanishadic warrant. The corpus supports variation; no single doctrinal closure.
• **Ritual continues** — Upanishads supplement, do not replace, Vedic ritual. Tension between jnana and karma (action/ritual) persists in Hindu practice.
• **Artha vs moksha** — Arthashastra encodes state survival and material interest; Upanishads encode liberation. Same civilization, different ends—tension preserved.

The Upanishads are **repressed as sole authority, retained as philosophical core**—they persist as shruti and as source for systematic philosophy.
