I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Ramayana as **India's epic of ideal kingship**—
the narrative where Rama embodies dharma as unambiguous authority
and where legitimacy is achieved through sacrifice, not compromise.

The Ramayana is not the Mahabharata.
Where the Mahabharata interrogates dharma, the Ramayana embodies it.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT & FORM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The epic emerges from oral tradition, attributed to Valmiki. Shorter
than the Mahabharata; more unified in structure. Transmission to
Southeast Asia (Thailand, Indonesia, Cambodia) as civilizational
export—Rama as universal ideal king.

Structural conditions:
• Kingship legitimacy through virtue and sacrifice
• Dharma as clear, not contested
• Exile and return as civilizational template
• Sita as contested figure in reception

The Ramayana encodes ideal rule through renunciation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. RAMA AS IDEAL KING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rama embodies kingship as duty without ambiguity:
• Obedience to father (exile)
• Fidelity to dharma (Sita's trial)
• Victory over disorder (Ravana)
• Restoration of order (return to Ayodhya)

The contrast with Mahabharata is structural: Rama does not fracture;
Yudhishthira does. Rama's kingship is performative legitimacy—
authority through conduct, not negotiation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. TRANSMISSION & RECEPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Ramayana becomes India's ideal-king template:
• Tulsidas, Ramcharitmanas (Hindi devotional)
• Kamban, Ramavataram (Tamil)
• Southeast Asian adaptations (Ramakien, Kakawin Ramayana)
• Modern political appropriation (Rama as Hindu icon)

It functions as portable ideal—endlessly adaptable because Rama
is abstract enough to carry multiple meanings.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. TENSION WITH MAHABHARATA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The two epics encode different civilizational grammars:
• Ramayana: legitimacy through virtue; order through sacrifice
• Mahabharata: legitimacy contested; order through rupture

Neither displaces the other. India holds both.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Ideal kingship is performative—authority through conduct.
• Exile and return as civilizational template.
• Transmission to Southeast Asia = India as cultural exporter.
• Rama and Mahabharata's Yudhishthira encode opposite poles of dharma.
• Political appropriation of Rama = contested reception.

Tension preserved without verdict.
