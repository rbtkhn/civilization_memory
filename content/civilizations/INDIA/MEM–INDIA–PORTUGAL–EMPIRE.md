I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Portugal's presence in India** as an **enclave and maritime empire**—fortified ports, chokepoint control, and trade regulation—rather than territorial conquest of the interior. The Portuguese reach India by the Cape route (1498), establish the Estado da Índia with Goa as capital (1510), and hold coastal strongholds (Goa, Cochin, Diu, Bombay/Seven Islands, etc.) while **never** absorbing the subcontinent. They displace overland Silk Road primacy and precede the British; they cede Bombay to England (1661) and lose Ceylon to the Dutch; Goa outlasts British India and is annexed by the Republic only in 1961.

For India, the Portuguese empire encodes **maritime interface without interior domination**—a first European colonial layer that is administratively shallow and geographically thin.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ENTRY: CAPE ROUTE AND FIRST CONTACT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vasco da Gama reaches Calicut (Kozhikode) in 1498, opening the **Cape route** from Europe to India and bypassing the overland Silk Road. Portuguese crown and papacy have already framed expansion by law and mission (e.g. Romanus Pontifex 1455). The aim is spices, Christian alliance against Islam, and control of sea lanes—not the conquest of Indian polities.

Structural conditions:
• **Maritime primacy** — Portuguese naval power and fortified feitorias (trading posts) secure ports and chokepoints rather than hinterlands.
• **No demographic depth** — Portugal lacks the population to occupy territory; empire is "sea power and fortified enclaves" (Boxer), "negotiation, alliance, and coercion in unequal measure" (Subrahmanyam).
• **Indian Ocean disruption** — Portuguese seize or contest Hormuz, Goa, Malacca; they disrupt existing Islamic and Indian Ocean trade patterns. India's maritime interface shifts toward European-dominated routes.

The Portuguese encode **route control and enclave rule**, not provincial assimilation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ESTADO DA ÍNDIA: GOA, COCHIN, DIU, BOMBAY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Afonso de Albuquerque captures **Goa** (1510), which becomes the capital of the Estado da Índia. Other key nodes:

• **Goa** — Seat of government; Catholic archbishopric; Inquisition; port and fortress; retained until Indian annexation (1961). See MEM–INDIA–PORTUGAL–EMPIRE–GOA.
• **Cochin** — Early ally and base (1503–1663); Portuguese shift here after friction with Calicut; later lost to the Dutch.
• **Diu** — Fortified island (Gujarat); held with local negotiation and force.
• **Bombay (Seven Islands)** — Portuguese from 1534; **ceded to England in 1661** as part of Catherine of Braganza's dowry. Becomes British Bombay Presidency (MEM–INDIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE–BOMBAY).
• **Ceylon** — Portuguese coastal forts (1517–1658); then Dutch, then British (MEM–INDIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE–CEYLON; MEM–INDIA–SRI–LANKA).

The Estado da Índia is **network and node**, not territory. Interior Indian states remain sovereign; the Portuguese tax and regulate trade at the coast.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, the Portuguese presence functions as:

• **Maritime displacement** — The Cape route (1498) bypasses the overland Silk Road; long-distance trade shifts to sea lanes that the Portuguese (then Dutch, British) contest. MEM–INDIA–SILK–ROAD encodes this as "Portuguese bypass Silk Road; reach India by sea."
• **First European colonial layer** — Portuguese precede the British by over a century. They establish the pattern of European fortified ports and naval control on the Indian coast; they do **not** establish territorial revenue and administrative penetration.
• **Legitimacy through treaty and church** — Papal and royal sanction legitimate Portuguese claims; local legitimacy is negotiated with Indian rulers. No claim to replace Indian sovereignty over the interior.
• **Long tail** — Portuguese India (Goa, Daman, Diu) outlasts British India. The Republic of India annexes Goa in 1961 (Operation Vijay), ending 451 years of Portuguese rule.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LIMITS & TENSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• **Dutch and British displacement** — Portuguese lose Ceylon to the Dutch (1658); Bombay ceded to England (1661). They retain Goa, Daman, Diu but cease to be the dominant European power in the Indian Ocean.
• **No absorption of India** — Unlike the British, the Portuguese do not build a subcontinental revenue state. They remain coastal and nodal.
• **Violence and Inquisition** — Portuguese conduct in Goa (Inquisition, forced conversions) is part of the record; the MEM encodes the structural form (enclave, maritime) and leaves moral evaluation open.

VIII. EVIDENCE (EXTERNAL SCHOLARSHIP)

Portuguese imperial form (cited in MEM–ROME–PORTUGAL):

> The Portuguese empire was an empire of sea power and fortified enclaves, dependent less on territorial conquest than on the control of routes, harbors, and commerce.
— C. R. Boxer, *The Portuguese Seaborne Empire*

> Portuguese power overseas was exercised through negotiation, alliance, and coercion in unequal measure, producing an empire that was administratively coherent but socially shallow.
— Sanjay Subrahmanyam, *The Portuguese Empire in Asia*
