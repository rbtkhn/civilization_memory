EMPIRE (Layer 2)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• **Temporal span:** 1526–1857
• **Territorial extent:** North India (Ganges core); expansion under Akbar–Aurangzeb to Deccan, Bengal, northwest; fragmentation post-Aurangzeb
• **Succession:** Delhi Sultanate (defeated at Panipat 1526); Mughal Empire; succession by British East India Company (Plassey 1757, formal end 1857)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Mughal Empire** as **India's early-modern
territorial and administrative state**—the political unit that
unified the Ganges core and much of the subcontinent under a
Persianate–Indic synthesis before Company and Crown succession.

For India, the Mughal Empire encodes **state capacity and then
fragmentation**—revenue, administration, plural governance, and
later overextension and elite defection that enabled British
procedural entry (MEM–INDIA–DYNASTY–MUGHAL; MEM–INDIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE–CLIVE).

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FOUNDING — PANIPAT AND CONSOLIDATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The empire is founded at the First Battle of Panipat (1526):
Babur defeats the Delhi Sultanate; defender cohesion had already
fractured (MEM–INDIA–DELHI–SULTANATE; MEM–INDIA–MONGOL–EMPIRE).

Key elements:
• Babur (r. 1526–1530) — Timurid–Mongol lineage; Kabul–Delhi axis;
  Mughal state as conquest project
• Humayun — Setback and restoration; Safavid refuge
• Akbar (r. 1556–1605) — Consolidation; revenue (zabt); Rajput
  incorporation; religious accommodation; empire as durable
  political unit
• Capitals — Agra, then Fatehpur Sikri, then Lahore; Shah Jahan
  shifts ceremonial centre to Delhi (Shahjahanabad, 1639)
  (MEM–INDIA–DELHI)

The Mughal Empire becomes **the** North Indian state—revenue,
land administration, and elite co-option underwrite territorial
control.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. TERRITORIAL EXTENT AND REVENUE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mughal authority rests on revenue rationalisation and military
capacity.

Key elements:
• Land revenue — Zabt (assessment); mansabdari (rank–grant);
  agricultural surplus as base of power
• Extent — Ganges heartland; Gujarat, Bengal, Deccan under
  expansion; northwest (Kabul, Kashmir) under Mughal orbit
  (MEM–INDIA–GEO–GANGES–RIVER; MEM–INDIA–AFGHANISTAN)
• Plural governance — Hindu and Muslim elites incorporated;
  early Mughal tolerance as stability mechanism (MEM–INDIA–DYNASTY–MUGHAL)
• Persianate administration — Persian court language; record-keeping;
  forms inherited by Company and Raj (MEM–INDIA–PERSIA)

**Structural point:** Empire scale depends on revenue and elite
compliance. When overextension (Aurangzeb's Deccan wars) and
religious rigidity strain legitimacy, regional defection and
fragmentation follow—the same condition the Company exploits at
Plassey (MEM–INDIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE–CLIVE).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CENTRALISATION, OVEREXTENSION, DECLINE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Later emperors tighten control; equilibrium fractures.

Key elements:
• Shah Jahan (r. 1628–1658) — Building programme (Taj Mahal, Red Fort,
  Shahjahanabad); centralisation; succession war
• Aurangzeb (r. 1658–1707) — Deccan expansion; religious policy
  stiffens; legitimacy–hardship coupling weakens; Maratha and
  regional resistance
• Post-Aurangzeb — Succession conflicts; regional governors
  (subahdars) assert autonomy; empire fragments into rival
  centres of power
• 1857 — Last Mughal emperor (Bahadur Shah Zafar) as symbol of
  rebellion; Company/Crown end formal Mughal sovereignty
  (MEM–INDIA–WAR–1857)

The Mughal Empire is remembered as **peak then fragmentation**—
state capacity sufficient to unify, then overextension and
defection sufficient to enable external succession.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. SUCCESSION BY COMPANY — NO CIVILIZATIONAL RESET
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mughal collapse does not erase India's continuity.

The British inherit:
• Revenue and land-administration forms — Mughal scaffolding
  retained by Company and Raj
• Persianate record and elite practice — Administrative continuity
  (MEM–INDIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE)
• Capital symbolism — Delhi (Mughal Shahjahanabad) later chosen
  by British as imperial capital (1911) (MEM–INDIA–DELHI)

**Structural observation:** Succession is by **defection and
fragmentation**—same type as Mughal entry at Panipat (defender
elites). The empire ends as a political unit; its administrative
and cultural forms persist (MEM–INDIA–DYNASTY–MUGHAL; CIV–SCHOLAR–INDIA
SYNTHESIS 0003).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. RELATION TO DYNASTY–MUGHAL, DELHI SULTANATE, BRITISH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MEM–INDIA–DYNASTY–MUGHAL preserves the **absorption and plural
governance** encoding—Mughals as Indian rulers, cultural
synthesizers. This MEM preserves the **empire as territorial
and administrative unit**—founding, extent, revenue, decline,
succession. MEM–INDIA–DELHI–SULTANATE is predecessor (Panipat);
MEM–INDIA–BRITISH–EMPIRE–CLIVE is successor (Plassey, fragmentation).
Same period; different analytical lens. Tension preserved.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Mughal Empire = North Indian state; revenue and elite co-option
  underwrite territorial control.
• Fragmentation (post-Aurangzeb) enables Company procedural entry.
• Administrative inheritance outlives the empire—Raj rules through
  Mughal scaffolding.
• Panipat and Plassey share defection/fragmentation type; empire
  entry and exit are structurally parallel.

Tension preserved without verdict.
