I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Goa** as the **capital of Portuguese India** (Estado da Índia): the western Indian enclave captured by Afonso de Albuquerque in 1510 and held for 451 years until the Republic of India annexed it by military action (1961). Goa is the longest-lived European colonial possession in the subcontinent—outlasting British withdrawal (1947) by 14 years.

Goa encodes **enclave continuity**: a small coastal territory ruled by Portugal through fortress, church, and port, without Portuguese conquest of the Indian interior. It is the seat of the Estado da Índia’s viceroy, the Catholic archbishopric, and (from 1560) the Goa Inquisition. Its end is not negotiated transfer but **forcible annexation** (Operation Vijay), encoding a different decolonization grammar than British India.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CAPTURE AND CONSOLIDATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Afonso de Albuquerque takes Goa from the Bijapur Sultanate (1510); it is briefly lost and retaken. Goa is chosen for its harbour, its position on the Kanara coast, and its value as a naval and commercial base. It becomes the **permanent capital** of the Estado da Índia, replacing Cochin in that role.

Structural conditions:
• **Port and fortress** — Harbour for the India run (Lisbon–Goa); fortified defences against Ottoman and Mughal-backed raids; shipbuilding and repair.
• **Catholic mission** — Archbishopric of Goa; Jesuits (e.g. Francis Xavier); conversion campaigns; from 1560, the **Goa Inquisition** (targeting converts, Hindus, and “heresy”). Legitimacy is tied to faith and crown.
• **No interior expansion** — Portuguese control is limited to the **conquered districts** (Old Goa, later Nova Goa/Panjim). Neighbouring Indian polities (Vijayanagara, Bijapur, Marathas) remain independent; the Portuguese do not become a land power in the Deccan.

Goa functions as **node and symbol**—the heart of Portuguese India—not as the base for territorial empire.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CIVILIZATIONAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, Goa represents:

• **Longest European holdout** — When the British leave India (1947), Portugal retains Goa, Daman, and Diu. The Indian state claims these as integral territory; Portugal refuses to cede. In December 1961 the Indian Armed Forces conduct **Operation Vijay**; Portuguese resistance is brief; Goa, Daman, and Diu are incorporated into the Indian Union. Decolonization here is **annexation**, not transfer—encoding a different resolution than Partition or independence-by-treaty.
• **Enclave, not province** — Unlike British presidencies (Bombay, Madras, Calcutta), Goa is never the seat of a subcontinental revenue or administrative system. It is a **Portuguese overseas province** (Estado da Índia) until the end. The contrast with British India is structural: British = territorial penetration; Portuguese Goa = coastal enclave.
• **Legitimacy contested** — For Portuguese memory, Goa is “Portuguese India” by right of conquest and long occupation. For Indian nationalist memory, it is occupied territory restored. The MEM encodes both the fact of 451 years of Portuguese rule and the fact of forcible end without resolving the legitimacy dispute.
