I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Arthashastra as **India's foundational
statecraft treatise**—the text that encodes governance as system
management, espionage as normal, and state survival as overriding
moral purity.

The Arthashastra is not dharma literature.
It is **realpolitik encoded**: how to found, defend, and expand the
state when ritual and virtue are insufficient.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT & FORM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Attributed to Kautilya (Chanakya), counsellor to Chandragupta Maurya.
The text may be layered—core Mauryan, later accretions. Rediscovered
1905; critical edition 1950s. Parallel to Sunzi in China: strategic
rationalism, stripped of ritual.

Structural conditions:
• Post-Alexander fragmentation; Mauryan consolidation
• State survival as primary imperative
• Espionage, assassination, subversion as tools
• Law as enforcement, not moral code

The Arthashastra encodes **power precedes legitimacy**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CORE DOCTRINES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• State (rajya) survival overrides moral purity
• Espionage (gudhapurusa) is normalized; foreign and domestic
• Mandala theory: circle of states; enemy's enemy as friend
• Seven pillars of state: king, minister, territory, fort, treasury,
  army, ally
• Danda (coercion) as foundation; dharmic overlay as stabilizer

Kautilyan realism becomes **permanent Indian substrate**—reappearing
across regimes under different moral vocabularies.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. COMPARATIVE: ARTHASHASTRA VS SUNZI
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Sunzi: war as calculation; victory without battle; cost minimization
• Arthashastra: statecraft as system; espionage, subversion, alliance;
  administration as central

Both: amoral realism; both: portable doctrine. Arthashastra is
**broader**—domestic administration, economy, law—Sunzi is **sharper**
on war alone.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Arthashastra establishes:
• Statecraft as technical discipline, not moral philosophy
• Mauryan administrative rationality as template
• Realism as Indian tradition—not only dharma and ahimsa
• Later absorption: Mughal, British, republican—forms persist

It fills the gap between **dharma and survival**—when the former
fails, the latter dictates.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LIMITS & TENSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Ashoka overlays dharmic restraint—Arthashastra does not disappear
• Gandhi and Nehru emphasize non-violence—Arthashastra remains
  in strategic discourse
• Modern India: Nehruvian idealism vs. Kautilyan realism—tension
  preserved

The Arthashastra is **repressed, not replaced**—it returns when
survival is at stake.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Statecraft as system management; espionage normalized.
• Power precedes legitimacy; realism as Indian substrate.
• Arthashastra parallels Sunzi—portable, amoral, strategic.
• Mauryan administration = template for later regimes.
• Dharma tempers Arthashastra; does not displace it.

Tension preserved without verdict.
