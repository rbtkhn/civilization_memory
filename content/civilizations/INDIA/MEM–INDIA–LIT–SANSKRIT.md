I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Sanskrit** as India's transmissive and legitimating language layer: the medium through which scripture (Veda, Upanishad), philosophy (darshana), law (dharmashastra), and classical literature (kavya) were composed, fixed, and transmitted across time and space.

Sanskrit is not a state. It is **civilizational infrastructure**—the language in which India's operating system (dharma, artha, kama, moksha; grammar, logic, numerals) was encoded and exported. It functions as legitimacy vehicle (court and temple), transmission vector (Buddhism to Tibet and China; grammar and numerals via Arabs to Europe), and **memory outlasting territory** (corpus preserved abroad when political centres shift).

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. LANGUAGE LAYER: SCRIPTURE TO KAVYA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sanskrit emerges as the prestige and liturgical language of the Vedic and post-Vedic tradition. It carries:

• **Veda and Vedanga** — Liturgy, ritual, phonetics, metre; the "heard" (shruti) and the auxiliary sciences (grammar, astronomy, ritual).
• **Upanishads** — Philosophical and soteriological core; monistic themes that later philosophy (e.g. Shankara) refines. "A thousand variations on one monistic theme from the Upanishads eight centuries before Christ to Shankara eight centuries after him" (Durant).
• **Dharmashastra** — Law and social order encoded; varna, duty, king's obligation. Legitimacy and procedure in one linguistic frame.
• **Kavya** — Classical poetry and drama (Kalidasa, etc.); court aesthetics; Sanskrit as civilizational badge when Gupta power and Sanskrit culture align.
• **Statecraft** — Arthashastra and related treatises; power and administration in the same language as dharma.

Sanskrit thus **unifies** ritual, philosophy, law, and statecraft in one medium—elite and cosmopolitan, not vernacular. It becomes the language of legitimacy (court, temple, scholarship) and the **carrier** of Indian thought outward.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. STRUCTURAL FUNCTION: TRANSMISSION WITHOUT TERRITORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sanskrit travels without an army. Structurally:

• **Buddhism** — Buddhist canon and scholastic tradition in Sanskrit (and Pali) are transmitted to Central Asia, Tibet, China, Southeast Asia. Sanskrit corpus is preserved and extended through translation (e.g. Tibetan, Chinese). India does not control those polities; the **language and content** do.
• **Grammar and logic** — Paninian grammar; Nyaya and other schools. "India has sent to the west, such gifts as grammar and logic, philosophy and fables, hypnotism and chess, and above all numerals and the decimal system" (Durant). Transmission via Arabs and later Europe—Sanskrit as **source**, not as political jurisdiction.
• **Numerals and decimal system** — Global adoption; origin in Indian mathematics, carried in Sanskrit (and Arabic transmission). Structural outcome: Indian intellectual product becomes world infrastructure without Indian territorial control.

Sanskrit thus encodes **soft-power reach**: civilizational content moves where political borders do not. The language is a **vehicle**, not a state—consistent with India's diffusion-first influence model (CIV–CORE–INDIA).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL FUNCTION: LEGITIMACY AND COSMOPOLITANISM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Within India, Sanskrit functions as:

• **Court and temple language** — Legitimacy performed in Sanskrit (inscriptions, patronage, liturgy). Gupta "Sanskrit cosmopolitanism"; later courts (regional kingdoms, Mughal Persian alongside Sanskrit learning) retain Sanskrit for ritual and prestige.
• **Classical credential** — Kalidasa, epics, philosophy as proof of civilizational depth. Colonial and post-colonial: "India as mother of philosophy, grammar, numerals" (Durant) counters narratives that denied India a high culture.
• **Preservation abroad** — When political centres in India fragment or shift, Sanskrit texts are preserved in Tibet, China, Central Asia. **Memory outlasts territory**: the corpus survives outside the subcontinent. Buddhism MEM and Tibet MEM encode this pattern.

Sanskrit is **legitimacy infrastructure**—whoever controls or patronizes it (king, temple, court) claims continuity with the civilizational core. It does not replace power; it **frames** it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LIMITS & TENSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Sanskrit is not a vernacular; it is elite and liturgical. Vernaculars (Hindi, Tamil, Bengali, etc.) carry popular and regional legitimacy. Tension between Sanskrit as "high" culture and vernaculars as living speech.
• Colonial Orientalism: Sanskrit studied, translated, sometimes instrumentalized (e.g. law, Aryan theory). India's classical past used both to admire and to divide.
• Modern India: Sanskrit as heritage and optional language; English as administrative and elite medium. Sanskrit remains **symbol and reservoir**, not daily instrument of state.

Sanskrit is **repressed as lingua franca, retained as canon**—it persists in ritual, scholarship, and civilizational self-description.
