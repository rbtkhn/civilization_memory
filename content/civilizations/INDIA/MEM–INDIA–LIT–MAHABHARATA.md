I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Mahabharata as **India's foundational epic of
dharma under strain**—the civilizational narrative where kingship,
obligation, and moral ambiguity collide without resolution.

The Mahabharata is not a moral tract.
It is **dharma interrogated**: how authority, kinship, and duty fracture
when no choice is innocent.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT & FORM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The epic emerges from oral and bardic tradition, crystallizing during
the late Vedic and early post-Vedic period. Multiple redactions produce
a layered text: martial core, didactic interpolation, devotional accretions.

Structural conditions:
• Fragmented polity and competing lineages
• Kingship legitimacy under question
• Dharma as contested, not given
• War as civilizational rupture

The Mahabharata encodes **conflict as the price of order**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. DHARMA AS CONTESTED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Central insight: dharma is not a fixed code but a **field of competing
obligations**.

Key tensions:
• Svadharma (personal duty) vs. sadharana dharma (universal duty)
• Kinship loyalty vs. righteous rule
• Victory without legitimacy vs. defeat with honour
• Krishna's instrumentalism vs. Arjuna's hesitation

The Bhagavad Gita (embedded) does not resolve the epic—it intensifies
the question: how does one act when every act carries moral cost?

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. KINGSHIP & LEGITIMACY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The epic stages competing models of kingship:

• Yudhishthira: dharma-obsessed, often ineffective; legitimacy through
  virtue, not outcome
• Duryodhana: entitlement, grievance, refusal to yield; legitimacy
  through birth, contested
• Krishna: instrumental counsellor; legitimacy through necessity,
  not law

No single model prevails. The war destroys both sides.

Kingship in the Mahabharata is **legitimacy under permanent strain**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. WAR AS CIVILIZATIONAL RUPTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Kurukshetra is encoded as fratricide, not glory.

Patterns:
• Victory brings ash, not triumph
• Survivors retreat to forest and renunciation
• The winning side is emptied, not exalted
• Order is restored only through exhaustion

The epic remembers war as **cost, not conquest**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. TRANSMISSION & RECEPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Mahabharata becomes India's shared narrative spine:
• Regional retellings (Tamil, Malayalam, Javanese)
• Performance traditions (Kathakali, Yakshagana, Wayang)
• Political appropriation (nationalist, devotional, feminist)
• Philosophical extraction (Gita as standalone)

It functions as **portable civilizational grammar**—endlessly
reabsorbed because it contains no single verdict.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Dharma is contested, not given; legitimacy is always under strain.
• War as civilizational rupture—victory empties the victor.
• Kingship models (virtue, birth, necessity) collide without resolution.
• The epic encodes India's tolerance for moral ambiguity.
• Transmission across regions and centuries = civilizational absorption.

Tension preserved without verdict.
