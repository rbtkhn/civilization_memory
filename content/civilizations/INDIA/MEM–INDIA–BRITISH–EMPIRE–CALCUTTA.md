I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Calcutta as **India's imperial capital under
British rule**—the Bengal presidency seat that functioned as
Company and Crown headquarters from 1773 until the transfer to
Delhi in 1911.

Calcutta is remembered as **administrative core before commercial
node**—authority, law, and extraction, not only trade.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC & CONTROL HISTORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Calcutta (Kolkata) is founded 1690 by Job Charnock for the East
India Company—a trading post at the head of the Hooghly (Ganges
distributary), accessible to ocean-going vessels. Fort William
(1696; rebuilt 1757–1781) secures Company presence after Plassey.

Control sequence:
• Mughal Bengal (pre-1690) — Sutanuti, Kalikata, Govindapur
• East India Company (1690–1858) — Bengal headquarters; Fort William;
  capital of British India (1773); Governor-General seat
• Crown (1858–1947) — Imperial capital until 1911; Bengal Presidency;
  capital transferred to Delhi (1911); Calcutta remains Bengal seat

Calcutta is **imperial capital** for 138 years—the administrative
centre from which the Raj governed India.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ECONOMIC & ADMINISTRATIVE FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Calcutta's colonial role centres on **authority and extraction**.

Key functions:
• Imperial capital (1773–1911) — Governor-General; Viceroy; High
  Court; legislative council; ceremonial centre
• Bengal Presidency seat — Land revenue; opium monopoly (Patna,
  Benares); jute; tea (Assam)
• Ganges delta gateway — Rice, indigo, silk; export via Hooghly
• Legal and administrative core — Codification; census; rail
  headquarters (EIR); telegraph
• 1857 locus — Sepoy mutiny; jurisdiction shift Company→Crown;
  Calcutta as Crown seat after rebellion

Calcutta encodes **administrative extraction**—the seat from which
procedure, law, and revenue flowed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL ENCODING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, Calcutta under the Raj encodes:
• Imperial capital—authority concentrated; legitimacy performed
• Ganges heartland—Calcutta at Ganges delta; connects to MEM–INDIA–GEO–GANGES–RIVER; empires reconstitute along Ganges
• Procedural core—contracts, courts, cash; Plassey and Clive as
  Bengal entry; Company-to-Crown transition
• Partition legacy—1947 severs Bengal; Calcutta becomes West Bengal
  capital; East Bengal (Pakistan) loses Calcutta; wound event

Calcutta is remembered as **administrative centre**—the node from
which British rule was exercised—more than as commercial hub.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. 1911 TRANSFER & AFTER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The transfer of capital to Delhi (1911) marks Calcutta's decline
as imperial centre. Reasons: strategic (northwestern focus);
symbolic (Mughal Delhi as Indian capital); administrative
(centrality). Calcutta remains Bengal presidency seat; loses
all-India primacy.

Post-1947: Calcutta (Kolkata from 2001) remains West Bengal
capital; Partition severs eastern hinterland; refugee influx;
industrial decline and revival. Colonial infrastructure
repurposed—Calcutta High Court, Victoria Memorial, Howrah Bridge.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Imperial capitals are temporary; Ganges basin endures.
• Administrative nodes concentrate authority; commercial nodes
  concentrate trade. Calcutta was both; authority came first.
• The 1911 transfer encodes British recognition of Delhi's
  symbolic centrality—Mughal continuity, not Bengali.
• Calcutta survives empire; loses imperial function; retains
  regional primacy.

Tension preserved without verdict.
