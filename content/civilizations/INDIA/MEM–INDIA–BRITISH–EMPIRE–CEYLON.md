I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **British Ceylon** as **India's southern maritime
outpost under imperial control**—the island that Britain ruled as a
Crown Colony **separate from British India**, severing it from
Indic continuity by administrative design.

For India, Ceylon under Britain encodes **maritime severance**:
same empire, different jurisdiction; island interface converted
into imperial outpost; civilizational proximity, administrative
distance.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. ADMINISTRATIVE SEPARATION — NOT BRITISH INDIA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ceylon was **never part of British India**. It was a distinct
Crown Colony.

Control sequence:
• Portuguese (1517–1658) — Coastal forts; cinnamon trade
• Dutch (1658–1796) — Maritime control; Kandyan kingdom interior
• British (1796–1948) — Ceylon Crown Colony; separate from Madras
  Presidency; Colombo as capital; Trincomalee as naval base

Key fact: British India (Company, then Crown) governed the
subcontinent. Ceylon was governed by the Colonial Office in
London, not the India Office. Same empire; **different jurisdiction**.

Colonialism converts interface into outpost.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ECONOMIC & STRATEGIC FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Ceylon's colonial role centres on **plantation extraction and
maritime control**.

Key functions:
• Tea plantations (1840s onward) — Hill country; British capital;
  Indian Tamil labour migration; Ceylon tea export
• Rubber, coconut — Plantation economy; export-oriented
• Colombo — Colonial capital; port; administrative centre
• Trincomalee — Strategic naval base; natural harbour; Indian
  Ocean control; Royal Navy presence

Ceylon encodes **plantation extraction**—labour from India,
capital from Britain, produce for empire—and **maritime node**
under imperial control.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. INDIAN LABOUR MIGRATION — LINKAGE UNDER IMPERIAL DESIGN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
British Ceylon imports Indian labour for plantations.

Key elements:
• Indian Tamil migration — Hill country tea; rubber; indentured
  and estate labour; linkage created by imperial design, not
  civilizational flow
• Sinhalese–Tamil tension — British census and classification
  harden categories; pluralism subordinated to imperial
  legibility
• Post-independence inheritance — Ceylon (1948) and later
  Sri Lanka inherit ethnic categories; Tamil question rebinds
  island to India

Labour migration creates **structural linkage**—India supplies
labour; Britain extracts surplus; Ceylon becomes hybrid
interface under imperial control.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL ENCODING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, British Ceylon encodes:
• Administrative severance — Ceylon separated from Indian
  continuity by imperial design; same empire, different
  jurisdiction
• Maritime outpost — Island interface under British control;
  Trincomalee as strategic naval base; Colombo as colonial
  capital
• Labour linkage — Indian migration to Ceylon plantations;
  imperial design creates tie that survives independence
• Pre-Partition independence — Ceylon independent 1948; no
  Partition; clean handover; contrast with India–Pakistan
  fracture

British Ceylon is remembered as **island severed by empire**—
the southern interface converted into imperial outpost, not
absorbed into British India.

VII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Same empire can sever as well as unite; Ceylon separated from
  Indian continuity by British design.
• Maritime islands become imperial outposts; administrative
  distance does not erase civilizational proximity.
• Plantation labour migration creates linkage that survives
  empire; imperial legibility hardens ethnic categories.
• Ceylon's clean independence (1948) contrasts with India's
  Partition (1947); no fracture for the island.

Tension preserved without verdict.
