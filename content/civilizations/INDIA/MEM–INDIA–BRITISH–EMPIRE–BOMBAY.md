I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Bombay as **India's western maritime gateway**
under British rule: the presidency seat that functioned as the
Company and Crown's primary port on the Arabian Sea, opposite
Calcutta's Ganges-delta dominance.

Bombay is remembered as **maritime node before administrative capital**—
trade, cotton, and naval projection, not imperial ceremonial centre.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. GEOGRAPHIC & CONTROL HISTORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Bombay (Mumbai) enters British possession in 1661 as part of
Catherine of Braganza's dowry—ceded by Portugal to Charles II.
The East India Company acquires the islands (1668) and develops
the harbour as a trading post and shipbuilding centre.

Control sequence:
• Portuguese (1534–1661) — Seven Islands; fishing and trade
• British Crown (1661–1668) — Dowry transfer
• East India Company (1668–1858) — Bombay Presidency; western
  maritime headquarters; cotton, opium, textiles
• Crown (1858–1947) — Bombay Presidency; rail terminus; Gateway
  of India (1911); port of embarkation for Persian Gulf, East Africa

Bombay remains **secondary to Calcutta** as imperial capital but
**primary as western maritime node**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. ECONOMIC FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Bombay's colonial role centres on **trade and textiles**.

Key functions:
• Cotton export—raw and milled; Lancashire connection; Deccan
  hinterland
• Opium transit—Malwa and Bengal opium for China trade
• Shipbuilding—Bombay Dockyard; naval and merchant construction
• Rail terminus—Great Indian Peninsula Railway; Bombay–Calcutta
  and Bombay–Delhi links
• Port of call—Suez route; troop and cargo embarkation

Bombay does not displace Calcutta as administrative capital—it
complements it as **western extraction and transit hub**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. POLITICAL & ADMINISTRATIVE ROLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Bombay Presidency governs western India—Maharashtra, Gujarat,
Sind (until 1936), parts of Karnataka. The presidency structure
mirrors Bengal and Madras: governor, council, district collectors.

Bombay is **presidency seat**, not imperial capital. Authority
flows from London via Calcutta (until 1911) and Delhi (after);
Bombay executes, does not command.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL ENCODING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, Bombay under the Raj encodes:
• Maritime gateway—open to ocean, not landlocked
• Commercial extraction—cotton, opium, transit; less administrative
  overlay than Bengal
• Western interface—Arabian Sea, Persian Gulf, East Africa; distinct
  from Calcutta's eastern/Ganges orientation
• Post-colonial continuity—Mumbai retains financial and maritime
  primacy; colonial infrastructure repurposed, not rejected

Bombay is remembered as **commercial node**—extraction and transit—
more than as political centre.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Maritime gateways persist beyond colonial exit.
• Commercial nodes outlast administrative capitals.
• Western and eastern presidencies encode different colonial functions.
• Bombay complements Calcutta—neither replaces the other.

Tension preserved without verdict.
