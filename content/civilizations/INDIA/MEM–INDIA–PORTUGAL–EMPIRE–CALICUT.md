I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **Calicut (Kozhikode)** as the **first Indian landfall** of the Portuguese—the port where Vasco da Gama arrives in 1498, opening the Cape route and the European maritime presence in India. Calicut is **not** the enduring Portuguese base; the Zamorin (Samutiri) of Calicut resists, and the Portuguese turn to Cochin (allied ruler) and then to Goa (conquered 1510) as the seat of the Estado da Índia.

Calicut encodes **first contact and failed consolidation**: the place where Europe and India meet by sea for the first time in the Portuguese narrative, and where Portuguese attempts to monopolize trade and impose terms lead to conflict and Portuguese withdrawal to other nodes.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FIRST LANDFALL (1498)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vasco da Gama’s fleet rounds the Cape of Good Hope and reaches the Malabar coast in May 1498; **Calicut** is the first port of call. Calicut is already a major pepper-trading centre under the **Zamorin** (Samutiri Raja), with Arab and Indian Ocean merchant networks. The Portuguese seek spices and Christian allies (against Islam); the Zamorin seeks trade on existing terms.

Structural conditions:
• **Cape route** — The voyage proves that India is reachable by sea from Europe without crossing Ottoman or Mamluk territory. MEM–INDIA–SILK–ROAD encodes this as “Portuguese bypass Silk Road; reach India by sea.” Calicut is the **geographic point** of that bypass’s completion.
• **Asymmetric expectations** — Portuguese want monopoly or preferential treatment; Zamorin operates in a plural trading world. Initial reception is mixed; negotiations and misunderstandings lead to tension.
• **No Portuguese sovereignty** — The Portuguese do not conquer Calicut. They leave, return with force (blockades, bombardments), and eventually concentrate power elsewhere (Cochin, Goa).

Calicut thus marks **contact**, not **conquest**—the beginning of the Portuguese story in India, not its seat.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CONFLICT AND PORTUGUESE SHIFT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Relations between the Portuguese and the Zamorin deteriorate: Portuguese demand for exclusion of Arab traders, seizure of ships, and violence (e.g. 1500 attack on Portuguese by locals; Portuguese reprisals). The Portuguese cultivate **Cochin** as an allied port (raja hostile to Zamorin) and establish a fort there (1503). **Goa** is captured in 1510 and becomes the Estado da Índia’s capital. Calicut remains under the Zamorin; Portuguese conduct blockades and raids but do not hold the city.

Structural outcome: the **first contact node** (Calicut) is not the **institutional node** (Goa). Portuguese India is built from Cochin and Goa, not from Calicut. Calicut encodes **resistance and refusal**—the Zamorin’s polity does not become a Portuguese enclave.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, Calicut represents:

• **Maritime first contact** — The moment when European (Portuguese) maritime expansion reaches India by the Cape route. It is the hinge between “Silk Road / overland” and “Cape route / maritime” in the corpus (MEM–INDIA–SILK–ROAD; MEM–INDIA–ARABIA).
• **Indian agency** — The Zamorin is not absorbed; Calicut is not a Portuguese possession. The pattern of “Portuguese in India” is therefore **selective**: they hold some ports (Goa, Cochin for a time, Diu, Bombay) and fail or choose not to hold others (Calicut). Calicut is the **first** such case.
• **Trade and violence** — First contact mixes trade and coercion; the MEM does not romanticize. Blockades, bombardments, and conflict are part of the record; so is the persistence of Calicut as a non-Portuguese port.
