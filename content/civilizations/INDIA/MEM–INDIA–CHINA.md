I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves **China** as **India's northern civilizational
interface**—the neighbour connected by Silk Road and Buddhism,
separated by the Himalayas, and defined since 1950–51 by Tibet's
buffer loss and since 1962 by territorial dispute.

China is remembered not as ally nor as mirror but as **interface
and pressure**—civilizational transmission (dharma eastward),
strategic ambiguity (Tibet as buffer then platform), and recurring
territorial strain (McMahon Line, Aksai Chin, Himalayan border).

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CIVILIZATIONAL TRANSMISSION — SILK ROAD & BUDDHISM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
India and China connect through **transmission, not conquest**.

Key vectors:
• Buddhism — India → Central Asia → Tibet → China; monks, texts,
  philosophical lineages (MEM–INDIA–SILK–ROAD; MEM–INDIA–TIBET)
• Sanskrit corpus — Preserved and transmitted through Tibetan
  and Chinese intermediaries
• Trade — Cotton, spices, gems west; silk east; India at eastern
  reach, China at eastern terminus

India is **source**; China is **recipient** (of dharma). Cultural
authority flows eastward; political authority does not follow.
Transmission precedes confrontation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. TIBET AS BUFFER — LOST
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Historically, Tibet functioned as **buffer** between India and China
(MEM–INDIA–TIBET).

British India preferred Tibet as autonomous buffer against Qing
and Russian power. India inherited this assumption. 1950–51 marks
a **rupture**.

Perception shift:
• Tibet transitions from buffer to contested frontier
• China fills the highland; India's buffer assumption collapses
• "When the highland fills, the border hardens"
• Tibet ceases to absorb pressure and begins transmitting it

The loss of Tibetan buffer autonomy is remembered as **structural
deterioration**—India and China now face each other across a
militarized Himalayan interface.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. TERRITORIAL DISPUTE — 1962 & AFTER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The India–China border dispute crystallises after Tibet's absorption.

Key elements:
• McMahon Line (1914) — British–Tibetan boundary; India inherits;
  China rejects
• Aksai Chin — Western sector; China controls; strategic link
  (Xinjiang–Tibet road)
• 1962 War — Chinese advance; Indian defeat; ceasefire; territorial
  status quo without settlement
• Himalayan interface — Ladakh, Arunachal Pradesh; recurrent
  standoffs; procedure without resolution

India encodes 1962 as **correction**—the moment idealism (or buffer
assumption, or deferred capacity-building) met reality. Which
encoding India keeps—"don't be idealistic" vs "don't defer without
building capacity" vs "autonomy has a price"—shapes what it learns
(MEM–INDIA–REPUBLIC–NEHRU § IV.A).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL ENCODING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, China encodes:

• **Civilizational interface** — Silk Road; Buddhist transmission;
  India as source, China as recipient; cultural authority eastward
• **Buffer lost** — Tibet (1950–51) as structural rupture; highland
  filled; border hardened
• **Territorial dispute** — McMahon Line; Aksai Chin; 1962; procedure
  without consensus; managed, not resolved
• **Neighbour, not ally** — Bilateral encoding; no bloc alignment;
  strategic autonomy preserved; strain endures
• **Alternative encodings** — Nehru's idealism; inherited buffer
  assumption; cost of autonomy; which lesson India learns from
  1962 remains contested

China is remembered as **interface and pressure**—transmission
partner, buffer destroyer, territorial claimant. Tension preserved.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
