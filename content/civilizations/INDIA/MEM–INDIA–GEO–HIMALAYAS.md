I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the **Himalayas** as India's **northern civilizational
limit**—the mountain barrier that separates Gangetic plains from Inner
Asia and defines the northern frontier of Indian space.

The Himalayas are remembered not as a possession but as **boundary**—
the natural brake on expansion, exchange, and domination in both
directions.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. PHYSICAL GEOGRAPHY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Himalayan arc defines northern India.

Core features:
• ~2,400 km arc; highest peaks on earth; extreme altitude
• Source of Ganges, Indus tributaries; hydrological headwater
• Passes: Nathu La, Shipki La, Lipulekh; narrow corridors of access
• Sparse habitation; logistical fragility; climactic severity

Geography axiom:
> "पर्वतः सीमा भवति।"
(The mountain becomes the boundary.)

Altitude enforces **restraint**—slowing movement, limiting surplus,
separating plains from plateau and steppe.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CIVILIZATIONAL BRAKE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Himalayas function as **civilizational brakes**, not defensive walls.

Effects:
• Two-way restraint — Limits India's northward expansion; limits
  Inner Asian southward penetration; neither side absorbs the other
• Trade and pilgrimage — Passes enable exchange; bulk movement
  restricted; cultural flow possible, imperial integration not
• Buffer zone — Kashmir, Nepal, Bhutan, Sikkim, Tibet sit along
  or north of the range; highland intermediaries

The mountain does not belong; it **separates**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. HIMALAYAN INTERFACE ZONES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
India's Himalayan interface includes:
• Kashmir — Western Himalayas; constitutional frontier; contested
• Nepal — Central Himalayas; buffer kingdom; civilizational overlap
• Bhutan — Eastern Himalayas; treaty relationship; strategic depth
• Tibet — North of Himalayas; plateau; buffer lost 1950–51; MEM–INDIA–TIBET

Each zone encodes different political memory. The **physical barrier**
is shared; the **political encoding** varies.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. STRATEGIC ENCODING
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For India, the Himalayas encode:
• Northern limit — Plains civilization stops at the mountain;
  highlands are interface, not core
• Altitude as defence — Historically, the mountain absorbed pressure;
  infrastructure and airpower erode that function
• "When the highland fills, the border hardens" — Loss of Tibetan
  buffer (1950–51) transformed Himalayas from insulating barrier
  to contested frontier

The Himalayas remain the **physical constant**; their strategic
meaning shifts with who controls the plateau beyond.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. RELATION TO TIBET & KASHMIR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MEM–INDIA–TIBET preserves Tibet as buffer/threshold—political and
cultural entity north of the range. MEM–INDIA–KASHMIR preserves
Kashmir as constitutional frontier—one Himalayan interface zone.
This MEM preserves the **mountain system itself**—the physical
barrier that defines northern India. Same geography; different lens.
