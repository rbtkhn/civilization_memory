I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Kalidasa as **India's classical literary peak**—
the poet-dramatist who encodes court aesthetics, Gupta legitimacy,
and Sanskrit kavya (poetry) as civilizational currency.

Kalidasa is not a political theorist.
He is **aesthetic legitimacy**: kingship and love rendered in perfected
form, at the moment when Gupta power and Sanskrit culture align.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT & FORM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Kalidasa flourishes under Gupta patronage—likely court poet to
Chandragupta II or Kumaragupta. Sanskrit kavya reaches its classical
form: ornament, suggestion (dhvani), rasa (aesthetic emotion).

Structural conditions:
• Gupta consolidation and imperial self-presentation
• Sanskrit as elite and cosmopolitan language
• Court culture as legitimacy display
• Epic themes (Ramayana, Mahabharata) reworked as drama and lyric

Kalidasa encodes **power as aesthetic achievement**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CORE WORKS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Abhijñānaśākuntalam (Recognition of Shakuntala): drama; king,
  ascetic maiden, separation, recognition; dharma and desire reconciled
• Meghadūta (Cloud Messenger): lyric; exile, message, longing;
  geography as emotional map
• Raghuvamśa: epic poem; Rama's lineage; ideal kingship narrated
• Kumarasambhava: epic poem; Shiva and Parvati; cosmic union

The Shakuntala becomes India's most translated play—Goethe's
adaptation; European Orientalist reception.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Kalidasa represents:
• Sanskrit at its most refined—kavya as civilizational badge
• Court patronage as legitimacy mechanism
• India's classical moment—Gupta, not Maurya or Mughal
• Transmission: Europe discovers "Indian Shakespeare"

He functions as **India's classical credential**—proof of civilizational
depth when colonial discourse denied it.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. LIMITS & RECEPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• No political treatise—aesthetic, not programmatic
• Colonial reception: Orientalist admiration, exoticization
• Post-independence: classical canon, cultural pride
• Contrast with Arthashastra: Kalidasa beautifies; Kautilya calculates

Kalidasa fills the gap between **power and beauty**—he does not
explain how power is seized, only how it is displayed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Court aesthetics as legitimacy display; power beautified.
• Sanskrit kavya = civilizational credential.
• Kalidasa = India's classical moment (Gupta).
• Transmission to Europe = India as cultural object.
• Aesthetic legitimacy complements, does not replace, political realism.

Tension preserved without verdict.
