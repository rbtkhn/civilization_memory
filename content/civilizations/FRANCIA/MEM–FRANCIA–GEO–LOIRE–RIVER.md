I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Loire River as **Francia's interior legitimacy
spine**: the river system through which early kingship, territorial
cohesion, agrarian surplus, and symbolic continuity were anchored
prior to Parisian centralization.

The Loire is not Francia's command river.
It is Francia's **legitimation river**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II–VI. STRATEGIC SECTIONS (TERRAIN, POWER, LEGITIMACY)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**II. Roman and late-ancient foundations:** The Loire enters memory as
stabilizing interior corridor. Roman Gaul relied on it for grain
movement, troop redeployment, administrative reach. Order travels inward.

**III. Early kingship & territorial coherence:** The Loire becomes
central to Frankish and Capetian consolidation. Royal itinerancy,
courtly residence networks, church–crown alignment. Legitimacy is
physical and visible.

**IV. Castles, court, and symbolic power:** Loire Valley as Francia's
symbolic heart. Château network (Blois, Amboise, Chambord), court
ritualization, Renaissance transmission. Authority is aestheticized.

**V. Agrarian surplus & social stability:** The Loire underwrites
stability through food security. Grain production, wine cultivation,
market integration. Agrarian reliability cushions regime shocks.

**VI. Decline relative to Parisian centrality:** From the 17th century
Paris supersedes Loire courts; administrative gravity shifts north.
The Loire becomes memory rather than mechanism. Civilizational memory:
legitimacy through presence. The Loire is Francia before Paris.

VII. ARC-T-ANCIENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Sidonius Apollinaris (5th c. AD) on the Loire's civilizing role:

"The Loire carries not only ships, but manners and law, binding the
countryside into the habits of Rome."
— Sidonius Apollinaris, *Epistulae*, Book V (ARC-T-ANCIENT) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Pattern permanence: river as carrier of order and law.

VIII. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL

Suger of Saint-Denis (12th c.) on the Loire and the crown:

"The land watered by the Loire nourishes the crown, for there the king
is seen, obeyed, and remembered."
— Suger, *Vita Ludovici Grossi* (ARC-T-MEDIEVAL) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Pattern permanence: Loire as site of royal presence and legitimacy.

IX. ARC-T-EARLY-MOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Brantôme (16th c.) on majesty along the Loire:

"Along the Loire, majesty was displayed not in fear, but in splendor,
making obedience agreeable."
— Pierre de Bourdeille (Brantôme), *Mémoires* (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Pattern permanence: aesthetic authority and voluntary obedience.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. ARC-T-MODERN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fernand Braudel on the Loire and French identity:

"The Loire is France before Paris—the river of kingship and of
legitimacy through presence. When power moved north to the Seine,
the Loire did not disappear; it became memory and myth, the river
that had made the crown visible before the state became bureaucratic."
— Fernand Braudel, *L'Identité de la France* (1986) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Pattern permanence: legitimacy through presence preceding centralization.
