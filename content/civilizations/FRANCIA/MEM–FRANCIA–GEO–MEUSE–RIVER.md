I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Meuse River as **Francia's most dangerous
riverine boundary**: a narrow corridor that repeatedly converts
geography into vulnerability by enabling rapid military penetration,
coalition maneuver, and buffer collapse.

Unlike the Loire or Seine, the Meuse does not consolidate Francia.
It **exposes it**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II–VI. STRATEGIC SECTIONS (TERRAIN, POWER, LIMIT)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**II. Roman frontier and liminal status:** The Meuse enters memory as
a Roman border-adjacent river—auxiliary supply route, peripheral
administrative zone, link between Rhine frontier systems and interior
Gaul. The Meuse is never fully interiorized; it remains a seam.

**III. Medieval marchland and contested authority:** The Meuse marks
fragmentation—feudal lordship overlap, ecclesiastical territories
(Liège), weak royal penetration. The river separates Francia,
Lotharingian remnants, and German imperial influence. Order is
negotiated, not imposed.

**IV. Early modern buffer illusion:** Francia attempts to stabilize
the Meuse line through fortification networks, proxy control via the
Low Countries, and diplomatic buffering. Vauban identifies the danger:
the Meuse is the open road for invading armies. Buffers delay—but do
not seal—penetration.

**V. 18th–19th century invasion axis:** The Meuse becomes a recurring
invasion route—coalition maneuver space, rapid advance toward Paris
basin, difficulty of sustained defense. Clausewitz: where rivers form
corridors rather than depth, defense must fail unless the enemy is
stopped elsewhere. The Meuse offers speed, not protection.

**VI. Twentieth-century systemic failure:** 1914 German advance through
Belgium; 1940 Ardennes–Meuse breakthrough. Churchill: through the
Ardennes and across the Meuse came disaster with a speed that stunned
command. The river becomes synonymous with surprise. Northeastern
Francia absorbs shock earliest.

VII. ARC-T-ANCIENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tacitus (1st c. AD) on rivers and the Rhine frontier:

"Along the rivers nearest the Rhine, peace was never certain, for
commerce and war passed by the same channels."
— Tacitus, *Germania* (ARC-T-ANCIENT) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Pattern permanence: frontier rivers as corridors for both trade and invasion.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jean Froissart (14th c.) on the Low Countries and rivers:

"In those parts the rivers are the highways of war; and he who holds
the passages may advance or delay an army at his will, until the
flood or the enemy drives him from the bank."
— Jean Froissart, *Chroniques* (ARC-T-MEDIEVAL) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Pattern permanence: Meuse region as contested passage, not barrier.

IX. ARC-T-EARLY-MOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Vauban (late 17th c.) on the Meuse and northeastern defense:

"The Meuse is the open road by which armies may enter the kingdom
before we can close the door."
— Vauban, correspondence on northeastern defenses (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Clausewitz (1832) on rivers and defense:

Pattern permanence: river as corridor invalidates buffer strategy.
