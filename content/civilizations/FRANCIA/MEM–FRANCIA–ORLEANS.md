────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Orléans as **Francia's Loire gateway and siege city**:
the town whose relief (1429) by Joan of Arc preceded the re-coronation
at Reims and became the turning point of the Hundred Years' War's
Valois recovery.

For Francia, Orléans encodes **legitimacy through siege and relief**—
the city that could not fall if the Dauphin's cause was to survive,
and whose relief re-moralized the war before material recovery followed.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT: ORLÉANS ON THE LOIRE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Orléans occupies a strategic crossing of the Loire—the gateway between
the north (Paris, English-held) and the south (Bourges, Dauphin's base).

Conditions (early 15th c.):
• Duchy of Orléans; royal apanage; loyal to Valois
• English and Burgundian forces control the north; Dauphin holds the south
• Orléans is the **chokepoint**: if it falls, the Dauphin's realm is
  split and Bourges exposed

The siege (October 1428–May 1429) is therefore existential: the town
must hold if the Valois legitimacy story is to hold.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE SIEGE (1428–1429) AND JOAN'S RELIEF
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
English forces besiege Orléans; the defense holds; Joan of Arc enters
the city (April–May 1429) and the siege is lifted.

Dynamics:
• Siege from October 1428; garrison and citizens resist
• Joan arrives (April 1429); morale and sacral narrative shift
• Relief of Orléans (May 1429); English withdraw
• Joan then leads the way to Reims (July 1429)—coronation of Charles VII

Verbatim scholarship (ARC-T-MODERN):

"This moment represents not nationalism but **emergency moral
mobilization**, where belief compensates for institutional absence."
— Justine Firnhaber-Baker, *French History: A Very Short Introduction*
(Oxford: Oxford University Press) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Orléans is the **first proof** that the Dauphin's cause can win—relief
before Reims, moral reversal before material recovery. The city becomes
the symbol of Valois resilience.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL SIGNIFICANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For Francia, Orléans encodes:

• Strategic chokepoint: Loire crossing as gateway between north and south;
  control of Orléans controls access to the Dauphin's realm
• Siege as legitimacy test: if Orléans fell, the Valois narrative
  would have collapsed; relief sustains it
• Joan of Arc as catalyst: relief of Orléans precedes Reims; the city
  is the **first** victory in the sequence (Orléans → Reims → material
  recovery 1431–1453)

SYNTHESIS 0004 medieval extension and RLL–FRANCIA–0011: Orléans is
the **site** where legitimacy is re-moralized (Joan, relief) before
institutional recovery—the city where "moral reversal preceding
material recovery" (MEM–WAR–HUNDRED–YEARS) is spatially anchored.
