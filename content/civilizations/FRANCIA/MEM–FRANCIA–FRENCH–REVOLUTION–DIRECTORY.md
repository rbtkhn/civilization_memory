I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Directory as **Francia's post-Terror
constitutional experiment**: the five-man executive (1795–1799) that
sought to stabilize the Revolution after Thermidor but depended on
the army and repeated coups, and that ended with Brumaire (1799) and
the rise of Napoleon.

For Francia, the Directory encodes **legitimacy without consensus**—the
attempt to institutionalize revolutionary order after the Terror
without restoring monarchy or reimposing virtue-by-terror. It fails;
Brumaire replaces it.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

II. ORIGIN: THERMIDOR AND THE REACTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Directory follows the fall of Robespierre (Thermidor, July 1794)
and the Thermidorian reaction.

Conditions (1794–1795):
• Recoil from the Terror; dismantling of revolutionary tribunals and
  Committee of Public Safety
• White Terror; persecution of former Jacobins
• Constitution of the Year III (1795): five Directors, bicameral
  legislature (Council of Ancients, Council of Five Hundred), two-thirds
  rule (incumbents guaranteed two-thirds of seats)
• Directory installed October 1795; Barras, Carnot, and others as
  Directors

The Directory is **Thermidor institutionalized**—power in the hands of
moderates and survivors, not sans-culottes or Robespierrists.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. STRUCTURE AND INSTABILITY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Directory governs through fragile coalitions and coups.

Dynamics:
• Five Directors chosen by the legislature; one replaced each year by
  lot—designed to prevent dictatorship, but producing weak coalitions
• Continued war (First Coalition persists; Napoleon's Italian campaign
  1796–1797); army becomes arbiter of domestic politics
• Financial crisis: assignats collapse; Directory relies on conquest
  and requisition
• Royalist and Jacobin opposition; Directory oscillates between
  repressing left and right

Verbatim scholarship (ARC-T-MODERN):

"The French Revolution [is] the laboratory in which all the
possibilities of modern politics, both positive and negative, were
tested for the first time."
— Jeremy D. Popkin, *A New World Begins: The History of the French
Revolution* (New York: Basic Books, 2019) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

The Directory is one of those **tested possibilities**—constitutional
republic without king, without Terror; it tests whether revolutionary
order can be sustained by procedure alone. The result is chronic
instability and military dependence.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. COUPS AND MILITARY DEPENDENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Directory survives by coup, not by consent.

Key events:
• Vendémiaire (October 1795): royalist uprising in Paris crushed by
  army (Napoleon's "whiff of grapeshot")
• Fructidor (September 1797): Directory purges royalists with army
  support; annuls elections
• Floréal (May 1798): Directory purges Jacobins; annuls elections again
• Prairial (June 1799): legislature pushes back; Directors forced to
  resign

Each coup deepens **military dependency**—the Directory cannot govern
without the army; the army learns it can govern without the Directory.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. BRUMAIRE AND THE END OF THE DIRECTORY
────────────────────────────────────────────
Napoleon's coup of 18–19 Brumaire (November 1799) ends the Directory.

Dynamics:
• Sieyès and other Directors conspire to revise the constitution;
  they need a "sword"—Napoleon returns from Egypt
• Brumaire: legislature intimidated by troops; Directory dissolved;
  Consulate established (Napoleon as First Consul)
• Revolution preserved in form (republic, rights rhetoric); power
  concentrated in one man

The Directory does not fall to royalists or Jacobins. It falls to
**the general it had empowered**—the logical endpoint of military
dependency.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL SIGNIFICANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For Francia, the Directory encodes:

• **Constitutional republic without consensus**—the attempt to
  institutionalize the Revolution after the Terror fails; procedure
  cannot substitute for legitimacy or coercion
• **Military as arbiter**—the army becomes the guarantor of order and
  then the source of authority; Brumaire is the Directory's
  self-consumption
• **Bridge to Napoleon**—the Directory creates the conditions for
  Bonapartist consolidation: war continues, administration centralizes,
  legitimacy remains revolutionary in name

AXIOM FRANCIA-003 (Rupture enables renewal): The Directory is rupture
from the Terror; the renewal it seeks (constitutional stability) does
not arrive—instead, Brumaire delivers a different renewal (Consulate,
Empire).

VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Constitutional design (five Directors, rotation) aimed at preventing dictatorship but produced weak coalitions.
• Army used to crush Vendémiaire; then Fructidor, Floréal; each coup increased military dependency.
• Two-thirds rule (incumbents guaranteed seats) intended to ensure continuity; instead entrenched illegitimacy.
• Assignats collapsed; Directory relied on conquest and requisition; war finance and domestic instability linked.
• Brumaire: Directors (Sieyès) sought a "sword"; Napoleon returned from Egypt; legislature intimidated by troops.
• Directory did not fall to royalists or Jacobins; fell to the general it had empowered.
