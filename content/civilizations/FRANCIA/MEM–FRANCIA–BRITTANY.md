I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Brittany as **Francia's western Celtic periphery**:
the duchy that retained distinct language, law, and identity while
gradually being drawn into the French kingdom—through the War of
Breton Succession (14th c.), the marriages of Anne of Brittany to
Charles VIII (1491) and Louis XII (1499), and the formal union of
duchy and crown (1532).

For Francia, Brittany encodes **legitimacy through marriage and
treaty**—incorporation by dynastic union and negotiated settlement
rather than by prolonged war (Aquitaine) or confiscation (Anjou).
Brittany is absorbed late and by different means; its distinctiveness
persists in memory.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT: BRITTANY BEFORE THE DUCHY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Brittany (Bretagne) is the peninsula west of the Loire—Celtic/Breton
in culture, with ties to Cornwall and Wales.

Conditions (early medieval):
• Breton migration and settlement; resistance to Frankish control
• Carolingian pressure; nominal submission but local dynasties
• Duchy of Brittany emerges (9th c.); semi-autonomous under the king
  of France

Brittany is **periphery by geography and culture**: maritime, Celtic,
and only loosely integrated into Frankish and then Capetian authority.
The dukes pay homage but rule with considerable autonomy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. WAR OF BRETON SUCCESSION AND HUNDRED YEARS' WAR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In the 14th century, succession conflict draws in English and French
crowns.

Dynamics (1341–1365, 1379–1381):
• War of Breton Succession: rival claimants (Montfort vs. Blois);
  England backs Montfort, France backs Blois
• Brittany becomes a **theatre of the Hundred Years' War**—English
  and French intervention; duchy as prize and buffer
• Settlement leaves Brittany under a Montfort duke holding from the
  king of France; autonomy preserved, but the duchy is entangled
  in Anglo-French rivalry

Brittany thus encodes **peripheral entanglement**: the duchy is not
absorbed by war but is shaped by it. When the Hundred Years' War
ends (1453), Brittany remains a separate duchy—not reabsorbed like
Aquitaine, but aligned with the French crown.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ANNE OF BRITTANY AND DUCAL UNION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The marriage of Anne of Brittany to Charles VIII (1491) and then to
Louis XII (1499) binds the duchy to the crown.

Dynamics:
• Anne inherits the duchy (1488); Charles VIII invades and secures
  marriage (1491)—Brittany remains duchy but passes under French
  royal influence
• Anne marries Louis XII (1499) after Charles's death; their daughter
  Claude marries Francis I (1515)
• Union of Brittany and France (1532): Estates of Brittany consent;
  duchy is incorporated into the kingdom with preserved privileges
  (parliament, fiscal particularism)

Verbatim scholarship (ARC-T-MODERN):

"The Capetians consolidated a fragmented realm into the most powerful
monarchy of the Middle Ages, adopting the fleur-de-lys as the emblem of
divine favor and royal continuity. They did not conquer by explosion but
**by patience—outlasting rivals, absorbing fiefs**, and fusing anointing
with hereditary succession so that the king embodied the state."
— Justine Firnhaber-Baker, *House of Lilies: The Dynasty That Made
Medieval France* (London: Allen Lane, 2024) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Brittany fits that pattern: **absorption by marriage and treaty**, not
by decades of war (Aquitaine) or feudal confiscation (Anjou). The
Valois and early Bourbon crown outlast the ducal line; Anne's marriages
and the 1532 union complete the incorporation. Brittany retains
particularism—legal, fiscal, linguistic—but is no longer a separate
sovereign duchy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL SIGNIFICANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For Francia, Brittany encodes:

• **Incorporation by marriage and treaty**—contrast with Aquitaine
  (Hundred Years' War, 1453 reabsorption) and Anjou (Philip Augustus's
  confiscation 1204). Brittany is absorbed late (1532) and by consent
  (Estates) and dynastic union (Anne, Claude, Francis I).
• **Peripheral particularism**—Brittany keeps privileges (parlement,
  fiscal autonomy) and cultural distinctiveness (Breton language);
  integration is real but **differentiated**
• **Maritime and western flank**—Brittany controls Atlantic approaches;
  strategic for trade and naval power; distinct from the Loire/Paris
  core

AXIOM FRANCIA-003 (Rupture enables renewal): The union of 1532 is
rupture for ducal sovereignty and renewal for the French kingdom—
another fief absorbed, but by negotiated path rather than conquest
alone.
