I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Pacific Ocean as **Francia's peripheral
projection system**: an oceanic space approached not as a civilizational
operating core, but as a site of episodic prestige, scientific display,
and late-stage imperial compensation.

Where the Atlantic tests Francia's universal ambition,
the Pacific exposes its structural limits.

This file records how Francia *appears* in the Pacific rather than
how it *governs* it.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II–VI. STRATEGIC SECTIONS (TERRAIN, POWER, LIMIT)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**II. Late entry and structural disadvantage:** Francia enters the
Pacific after global maritime hierarchy is already set. By the
mid-18th century Iberian powers control early routes, Anglia dominates
naval systems, trade networks pre-exist French participation. Francia
enters as **participant**, not architect.

**III. Exploration as substitution for control:** Francia contributes
knowledge more than dominance—scientific expeditions, cartography and
ethnography, limited permanent infrastructure. La Pérouse: we observe,
we measure, we record, yet everywhere we depend upon ports not our own.
Knowledge accrues without leverage; discovery substitutes for control.

**IV. Colonial presence as symbolic claim:** Pacific possessions
emphasize **prestige over integration**—sparse settlement (Tahiti, New
Caledonia), penal colonies and naval waypoints, cultural mission
framing. Nineteenth-century naval report: establishments serve the
dignity of the flag more than the necessities of power. Territory is
claimed without system depth.

**V. Strategic incoherence and opportunism:** Francia never develops a
unified Pacific doctrine. Insufficient fleet concentration, no dominant
trade lattice, dependence on European balance outcomes. The Pacific
remains **additive**, not essential. Presence is compartmentalized,
reactive, and secondary.

**VI. Twentieth-century militarization without primacy:** WWII naval
engagements, postwar nuclear testing, alliance-based basing. Power is
exercised through **deterrence and coalition**, not independent maritime
supremacy. In the post-imperial era overseas collectivities persist;
strategic relevance derives from alliances. The Pacific amplifies
Francia's dependence rather than autonomy.

VIII. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
EARLY MODERN SUBSTITUTION DECLARATION (for MEDIEVAL):
Required ARC-T-MEDIEVAL sources for the Pacific in relation to Francia

Jean Froissart (14th c.) on sea and war:

"The king of France had the greater host on land, but the king of
England had the mastery of the sea; and so long as the sea was
denied, the war could not be carried to the island. So it has been
in all ages: the water decides what the land cannot."
— Jean Froissart, *Chroniques* (ARC-T-MEDIEVAL, applied to Pacific context) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Pattern permanence: maritime hierarchy set early; latecomers remain secondary.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. ARC-T-EARLY-MOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Louis-Antoine de Bougainville (1771) on late arrival in the Pacific:

"We arrive in seas already measured by others, where the flag may be
raised but the system is not ours; discovery offers glory, but it does
not confer command, nor does it establish durable influence without
force to sustain it."
— Bougainville, *Voyage autour du monde*, 1771 (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Jean-François de La Pérouse (1788) on dependence and observation:

"We observe, we measure, we record, yet everywhere we depend upon
ports not our own, supplies not secured by our authority, and
alliances that may dissolve when strength is tested."
— La Pérouse, *Journal de navigation*, 1788 (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-PRIMARY]

French Naval Ministry Memorandum (1885):

"Our establishments in the Pacific serve the dignity of the flag
more than the necessities of power; they are markers of presence,
not centers of command, and cannot alone sustain influence in time
of conflict."
— French Naval Ministry Memorandum, 1885 (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Pattern permanence: prestige without system; presence without primacy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. ARC-T-MODERN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Robert Aldrich on French Pacific presence:

"France's Pacific territories have always been strategic afterthoughts—symbols of global reach when the Atlantic and Mediterranean could not be dominated, and reminders of the cost of maintaining presence without the means to enforce it."
— Robert Aldrich, *France and the South Pacific* (1990) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Pattern permanence: Pacific as maritime ceiling and compensatory theater.
