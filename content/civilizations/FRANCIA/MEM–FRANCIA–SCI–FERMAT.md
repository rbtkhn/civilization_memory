I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Pierre de Fermat as **Francia's prince of
amateurs**: the magistrate who did mathematics in his spare time,
co-founded probability with Pascal, pioneered number theory (Fermat's
Last Theorem, little theorem), and left problems that resisted proof
for centuries.

Fermat is not a system-builder like Leibniz or Descartes.
He is a **problem-poser**—pure mathematics as civilizational
inheritance.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRUCTURAL CONTEXT: MAGISTRATE & AMATEUR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fermat served as conseiller at Toulouse and Castres; he never held
an academic post. His mathematics was conducted by correspondence
(Pascal, Mersenne, Descartes) and in the margins of books.

Conditions:
• No French academy of science yet; correspondence networks
• Descartes' analytic geometry emerging; Fermat's independent
  development
• Probability: problem of the points (Pascal–Fermat, 1654)

Fermat in his own words—on the margin:

"I have discovered a truly marvelous proof of this, which this margin
is too narrow to contain."
— Fermat, margin of Diophantus' *Arithmetica* (c. 1637) (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

The note refers to what became **Fermat's Last Theorem**: no positive
integers a, b, c, n > 2 satisfy a^n + b^n = c^n. The "proof" was
never found; the theorem was proved only in 1995 (Wiles).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. NUMBER THEORY: FERMAT'S LAST THEOREM & LITTLE THEOREM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fermat's number theory includes: Fermat's Little Theorem (a^p ≡ a
(mod p) for prime p); Fermat primes (2^(2^n) + 1); and the claim
that became Fermat's Last Theorem.

Contributions:
• Fermat's Little Theorem (foundation for primality and cryptography)
• Fermat primes; infinite descent (method of proof)
• Fermat's Last Theorem (conjecture; proof 1995)

Pure mathematics as **legacy**: problems that outlive the poser.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PROBABILITY: THE PROBLEM OF THE POINTS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
With Pascal (1654 correspondence), Fermat solves the "problem of
the points": how to divide stakes when a game is interrupted. The
solution establishes expectation and the foundations of probability.

Contributions:
• Division of stakes; expectation
• Foundation for probability theory (with Pascal)

Calculation replaces intuition; **reason** governs chance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ANALYTIC GEOMETRY & OPTICS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fermat developed analytic geometry independently of Descartes (equations
of lines, curves); the two corresponded and sometimes clashed. Fermat's
principle in optics (light follows path of least time) prefigures
variational principles.

Contributions:
• Analytic geometry (independently of Descartes)
• Fermat's principle (optics); variational thinking

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fermat encodes **pure mathematics as civilizational inheritance**:
the amateur magistrate who left problems (Last Theorem) that defined
mathematics for centuries and who, with Pascal, founded probability.

Lessons:
• Mathematics can flourish outside academies—correspondence and margin.
• Problems outlive the poser; Fermat's Last Theorem proved 1995.
• Probability: reason and expectation replace superstition.
