────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Joan of Arc as **Francia's myth carrier in legitimacy
collapse**: the figure through whom sacral intervention and re-coronation
at Reims (1429) restored moral and ritual legitimacy **before** material
recovery (1431–1453). For doctrine 03 and AXIOM FRANCIA-003, Joan is the
test case: rupture enables renewal when the grammar of legitimacy (one
ritual in one place) is re-performed.

Joan does not invent the grammar—Reims and Clovis already established
it. She **re-activates** it when procedural continuity is absent. Belief
compensates for institutional absence; the Dauphin becomes king at Reims
before the realm is territorially secured.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT: COLLAPSE AND THE DAUPHIN'S WEAKNESS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
By 1429 the Valois cause is at nadir. Troyes (1420) has disinherited
the Dauphin; Paris and the north are under Anglo-Burgundian control.
Orléans, under siege, is the chokepoint: if it falls, the Dauphin's
realm is cut in two.

Structural conditions:
• No standing royal army; no fiscal base sufficient to reverse defeats
• Legitimacy contested between Valois (Dauphin) and Lancastrian (Henry VI)
• Reims—the coronation city—is in enemy hands; the Dauphin cannot be
  crowned in the traditional place

Joan enters as **sacral claimant**: she asserts divine mandate to bring
the Dauphin to his coronation and restore the kingdom. She does not
offer administrative reform; she offers **ritual completion** at the
designated site.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. REIMS 1429: LEGITIMACY RESTORED BEFORE TERRITORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Joan's insistence on Reims is civilizational grammar. The king must be
anointed at Reims by the archbishop; only then is he legible as legitimate.
Relief of Orléans (May 1429) opens the route; the march to Reims and
Charles VII's coronation (July 1429) follow.

> "I was sent to bring the Dauphin to his coronation, and to restore
> the kingdom to its rightful lord."
> — Joan of Arc, trial testimony (MEM–FRANCIA–WAR–HUNDRED–YEARS) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Legitimacy is **ritually restored** before material recovery. The moral
reversal—Valois again the sacrally recognized king—precedes the military
and fiscal consolidation of 1431–1453 (Arras, standing army, taille,
territorial reconquest). Justine Firnhaber-Baker frames this as emergency
moral mobilization: belief compensates for institutional absence (*French
History: A Very Short Introduction*).

From a structural perspective, the coronation at Reims changes the
defection calculus. So long as the Dauphin was uncrowned, redefection
to Valois was costly. Once he is anointed at Reims, the legitimacy
shift is **irreversible** in civilizational memory—the same ritual
channel that Clovis and the Capetians used is re-used; the grammar
survives rupture.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. MYTH CARRIER AND DOCTRINE 03
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Joan functions as **myth carrier** (RLL–0011): when procedural continuity
is absent, sacral narrative and ritual reassertion hold legitimacy until
institutions catch up. She does not replace the grammar; she **carries**
it—Reims, anointing, "rightful lord"—into the moment of maximum collapse.

For AXIOM FRANCIA-003 (rupture enables renewal; grammar must survive):
Joan is the proof that renewal can follow rupture **because** the ritual
chain (Reims, coronation) is re-performed. The building (Reims Cathedral)
and the act (coronation) are the same; the dynasty (Valois) is renewed
through the same channel that Capetians and Carolingians used.

Doctrine 03 (CIV–DOCTRINE–FRANCE): legitimacy can be restored through
symbolic and moral reassertion when procedural continuity fails. Joan
and Reims 1429 are the canonical example.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CAPTURE, TRIAL, EXECUTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Joan is captured (1430), tried by ecclesiastical and secular authority
under English/Burgundian control, and executed (1431). Her trial
testimony supplies the quote above; her death does not undo the
coronation. Reims 1429 remains the turning point—legitimacy was
already re-anchored. Material conversion (1431–1453) follows.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
