I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Frisia as **Francia's northern maritime periphery**
in the early medieval period: the coastal region along the North Sea
(modern Netherlands, northwest Germany) that the Franks and Carolingians
conquered and Christianized, and that the partition of the empire
(9th c.) placed outside the West Frankish—later French—core.

For Francia, Frisia encodes **periphery gained and lost**—territory
incorporated under Charlemagne, then lost to the logic of partition and
successor states. Frisia never becomes French; it becomes Dutch and
German. Francia's memory of Frisia is thus **expansion and retreat**,
not consolidation.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT: FRISIA BEFORE FRANKISH EXPANSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Frisia in the early medieval period is a distinct coastal culture—
maritime, trading, and only loosely integrated into any kingdom.

Conditions (6th–7th c.):
• Frisians control North Sea coast from Flanders to Denmark
• Pagan or mixed belief; trade with Anglo-Saxons and Scandinavians
• Merovingian Franks push north; intermittent conflict and tribute

Frisia is a **buffer and prize**: rich in trade, resistant to direct
rule. Frankish expansion is gradual—military pressure, missionary
activity, and political submission—rather than a single conquest.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CAROLINGIAN INCORPORATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Under the Carolingians, Frisia is subdued and Christianized.

Dynamics (8th–9th c.):
• Charles Martel and Pepin III campaign in Frisia; submission of
  Frisian leaders
• Charlemagne completes incorporation; Frisia within the Frankish
  realm, Christianized by force and mission
• Einhard records Charlemagne's wars and the extension of Frankish
  authority

Verbatim scholarship (ARC-T-MODERN):

"Charlemagne's empire was not a nation-state but a **personal and
dynastic construction** held together by military success, ritual
loyalty, and the integration of elites. The periphery—Saxony, Frisia,
Bavaria—was incorporated through conquest and conversion, but the
coherence of the empire depended on the ruler's person and the
loyalty of regional elites."
— Johannes Fried, *The Middle Ages* (Cambridge, MA: Harvard University
Press, 2015) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Frisia under Charlemagne is thus **imperial periphery**: part of the
Frankish realm but dependent on central authority. When that authority
splits (Treaty of Verdun 843, later partitions), Frisia falls to the
Middle Kingdom and then to the eastern and northern successor polities—
not to West Francia (France).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PARTITION AND LOSS TO FRANCIA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The division of the Carolingian empire leaves Frisia outside the
West Frankish core.

Dynamics:
• Treaty of Verdun (843): Frisia assigned to Lothair's Middle Kingdom
• Later partitions and succession: Frisia drifts into the orbit of
  East Frankish (German) and Flemish/Dutch polities
• West Francia (Capetian France) never reasserts claim; Frisia is
  **lost** to the French kingdom

Francia's relation to Frisia after the 9th century is indirect: trade,
occasional alliance, but no territorial hold. The Netherlands (q.v.
MEM–FRANCIA–NETHERLANDS) later become the northwestern friction zone;
Frisia itself is the **earlier** periphery that Francia held briefly
and then did not.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL SIGNIFICANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For Francia, Frisia encodes:

• **Carolingian expansion**—Frankish/Carolingian reach into the North
  Sea coast; conquest and Christianization as imperial logic
• **Periphery lost by partition**—territory incorporated under
  Charlemagne is lost not by defeat but by the fragmentation of the
  empire; West Francia emerges without Frisia
• **Contrast with core consolidation**—Firnhaber-Baker's "consolidation
  by patience" (House of Lilies) applies to the Capetian core
  (Île-de-France, absorption of fiefs). Frisia represents the opposite:
  territory that was briefly Frankish and then ceased to be—expansion
  without lasting incorporation

AXIOM FRANCIA-003 (Rupture enables renewal): For Frisia, the rupture
is partition; the "renewal" is not French but Dutch/German—Francia
does not renew hold over Frisia; it accepts its loss and orients
elsewhere.
