I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves French colonial Africa (excluding Algeria, which
has its own MEM) as **Francia's vast sub-Saharan and North African
protectorate system**: West Africa (AOF), Equatorial Africa (AEF),
Madagascar, Tunisia, and Morocco—where mission civilisatrice,
extraction, and forced labor coexisted, where colonial troops fought
two world wars, and where decolonization was largely peaceful before
giving way to Françafrique.

African empire was never legally "France" like Algeria.
It was **empire at scale**—and the place where formal empire ended
in transfer, not trauma, then continued informally.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONQUEST & COLONIAL CONSTRUCTION (1881–1912)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Francia acquires African territory under Third Republic expansion,
in parallel to Indochina.

Sequence:
• Tunisia (1881): protectorate
• West Africa (AOF, 1895): Senegal, Ivory Coast, Dahomey, Guinea,
  Sudan (Mali), Upper Volta (Burkina Faso), Niger, Mauritania
• Equatorial Africa (AEF, 1910): Gabon, Congo, Ubangi-Shari (CAR),
  Chad
• Madagascar (1896): conquest, colony
• Morocco (1912): protectorate (with Spain in north)

Dakar becomes the capital of AOF; Brazzaville of AEF. The structure
is extraction-oriented: cash crops, minerals, forced labor (prestations,
later *travail forcé*), and the Code de l'Indigénat—arbitrary
punishment without trial. Assimilation rhetoric targets a small
élite; domination applies to the rest.

Robert Aldrich (ARC–FRANCIA) on the Second Empire's scope:

"By 1920, France ruled the second-largest colonial empire in the
world. From Dakar to Saigon, from Algiers to Brazzaville, French
administrators, soldiers, and missionaries governed 110 million
people. The empire was rationalized as mission civilisatrice—the
duty to spread French culture, language, and institutions to peoples
deemed less advanced. The reality was extraction, forced labor, and
systematic racial hierarchy."
— Robert Aldrich, *Greater France* (1996) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

African empire fits the pattern: civilizing rhetoric, extractive
practice.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. COLONIAL STRUCTURE: EXTRACTION, FORCED LABOR, TIRAILLEURS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
French Africa is run as a resource and manpower base.

Characteristics:
• AOF / AEF: federation of colonies and territories; Governor-General
  in Dakar / Brazzaville
• Economy: peanuts, cocoa, cotton, timber, minerals; railways and
  ports for export
• Code de l'Indigénat: forced labor, head taxes, summary punishment
• Tirailleurs sénégalais (and North African units): colonial troops
  recruited for French wars
• Education: limited; *évolués* and *originaires* (e.g. Four Communes
  of Senegal) as exceptions

Assimilation is promised to a few; domination is the rule. Colonial
troops fight for France in both world wars—and return to subjugation,
feeding postwar demands for equality and then independence.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. WORLD WARS: AFRICA AS RESOURCE AND REFUGE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Both world wars make Africa central to Francia's survival.

World War I:
• ~600,000 colonial troops serve (tirailleurs sénégalais, North
  African units)
• African labor in French factories; African resources for the war
  effort
• Sacrifice used to justify continued colonialism—and to fuel
  later claims for citizenship and equality

World War II:
• 1940: After fall of France, French Equatorial Africa (and much of
  AOF after 1942) sides with Free France
• Brazzaville = de facto Free French capital (1940–1943)
• 1944: Brazzaville Conference—reforms promised, but no independence
• Colonial troops in liberation of France; 1944 Sétif massacre in
  Algeria prefigures later trauma

ARC–DISCOVERY: EXPANDED SEARCH
Source: Frederick Cooper, *Citizenship between Empire and Nation* (2014)
Justification: Colonial troops and postwar citizenship demands; no
ARC–FRANCIA listed author provides equivalent synthesis.

"The colonial contribution to both world wars was substantial.
Hundreds of thousands of African and Asian soldiers fought and died
for France. Their sacrifice was used to justify empire—proof that the
colonies were 'part of France.' But it also generated expectations.
Men who had fought for French liberty returned home to colonial
subjugation. The contradiction would explode after 1945."
— Frederick Cooper, *Citizenship between Empire and Nation* (2014) [ERC-SECONDARY]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. DECOLONIZATION: PEACEFUL TRANSFER (1956–1960)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Unlike Indochina or Algeria, most of French Africa gains independence
through negotiation and transfer.

Sequence:
• Tunisia (1956), Morocco (1956): independence after pressure and
  limited violence
• Sub-Saharan Africa (1960): Year of Africa—most AOF and AEF
  territories become independent (Senegal, Ivory Coast, Mali, etc.)
• Madagascar (1960): independence
• Algeria (1962): exception—war and trauma (see MEM–FRANCIA–EMPIRE–ALGERIA)

The contrast is structural: where no large settler population and no
"integral part of France" claim existed, Francia could withdraw
without civil war. Algeria could not be abandoned the same way.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. FRANÇAFRIQUE: INFORMAL EMPIRE AFTER THE FLAGS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Formal empire ends; informal influence persists.

Françafrique (post-1960):
• Military bases and agreements (e.g. Dakar, Libreville)
• French troops intervene regularly (40+ interventions since 1960)
• CFA franc zone: monetary linkage to France
• Support for friendly (often authoritarian) regimes
• Personal and commercial networks between French and African elites

ARC–DISCOVERY: EXPANDED SEARCH
Source: François-Xavier Verschave, *La Françafrique* (1998)
Justification: Post-colonial French Africa policy; no ARC–FRANCIA
listed author provides equivalent critique.

"France did not abandon Africa when the flags came down. The network
known as Françafrique maintained French influence through military
agreements, monetary control, and personal relationships between
French and African leaders. French troops intervened regularly to
sustain friendly governments or evacuate French citizens. Formal
empire ended; informal influence persisted."
— François-Xavier Verschave, *La Françafrique* (1998) [ERC-SECONDARY]

The universalist mission becomes strategic interest—and, in critique,
neocolonial continuity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTRAST WITH ALGERIA AND INDOCHINA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
African empire (excluding Algeria) encodes a different decolonization
path.

Contrasts:
• Indochina: military defeat (Dien Bien Phu), then withdrawal;
  trauma at distance
• Algeria: legally "France," settler colony, war, then traumatic
  separation
• Sub-Saharan Africa (and Tunisia, Morocco): no mass settlement, no
  "part of France" claim; independence by transfer; then Françafrique

African empire (non-Algerian) teaches that formal empire can end
without metropolitan trauma—and that informal empire can outlast the
flags. Algeria teaches the limit of that path when territory is
claimed as home.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
French colonial Africa (non-Algerian) encodes **empire at scale and
its dual end**: transfer where possible, continuity where useful.

Lessons:
• Mission civilisatrice and extraction coexisted across AOF, AEF,
  Madagascar, Tunisia, Morocco.
• Colonial troops fought for France; their sacrifice fueled postwar
  demands and then decolonization.
• Where no settler colony and no "France" claim existed, independence
  could be negotiated—unlike Algeria.
• Françafrique shows that formal empire's end need not mean the end
  of influence—or of critique.

African empire is Francia's imperial scale and its managed
decolonization—before and beside Algerian trauma.
