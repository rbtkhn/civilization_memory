────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves French Indochina as **Francia's first major
colonial defeat**: the Southeast Asian possession (Vietnam, Cambodia,
Laos) where mission civilisatrice met organized resistance, military
overstretch, and—at Dien Bien Phu in 1954—decisive reversal. Indochina
prefigures Algeria's trauma: loss at distance before loss at home.

Indochina was never legally "France."
It was empire at its farthest reach—and the place where that reach
broke.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONQUEST & COLONIAL CONSTRUCTION (1862–1893)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Francia acquires Indochina incrementally, under Third Republic
expansion.

Sequence:
• Cochinchina (southern Vietnam): Saigon 1859, treaty 1862–1867
• Cambodia: protectorate 1863–1864
• Tonkin (northern Vietnam) and Annam (centre): campaigns 1883–1885
• Laos: 1893 (Siamese crisis)

The Union indochinoise (1887) federates Cochinchina (colony),
Annam, Tonkin, Cambodia, Laos (protectorates) under a
Governor-General in Hanoi. The structure is extraction-oriented:
rubber, rice, minerals; limited European settlement; administration
through existing elites where useful, direct rule where not.

Robert Aldrich (ARC–FRANCIA) on the Second Empire's Asian dimension:

"By 1920, France ruled the second-largest colonial empire in the
world. From Dakar to Saigon, from Algiers to Brazzaville, French
administrators, soldiers, and missionaries governed 110 million
people. The empire was rationalized as mission civilisatrice—the
duty to spread French culture, language, and institutions to peoples
deemed less advanced. The reality was extraction, forced labor, and
systematic racial hierarchy."
— Robert Aldrich, *Greater France* (1996) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Indochina fits the pattern: civilizing rhetoric, extractive practice.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. COLONIAL STRUCTURE: EXTRACTION & ASSIMILATION RHETORIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Indochina is run as a profitable colony, not a settler extension.

Characteristics:
• Cochinchina: direct rule, colony
• Annam, Tonkin, Cambodia, Laos: protectorates, indirect rule
• Economy: rubber plantations (Michelin), rice export, mining
• Code de l'Indigénat: arbitrary punishment, forced labor
• Education: French language and culture for small elite; mass
  population largely excluded from citizenship

Assimilation is promised; domination is delivered. Unlike Algeria,
Indochina is never legally integrated as part of France. It remains
"overseas"—which later makes abandonment politically easier than
Algeria, but does not reduce the shock of military defeat.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. WWII: VICHY, JAPANESE OCCUPATION, REASSERTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
World War II shatters the colonial order.

Sequence:
• 1940: Vichy retains nominal control; Japan pressures bases and
  transit
• 1941–1945: Japanese occupation; Vichy administration retained
  until March 1945 (coup de force)
• 1945: Famine in Tonkin; Viet Minh gains support; Japanese
  surrender; Ho Chi Minh declares Vietnamese independence (September)
• 1945–1946: British (south) and Chinese (north) take surrender;
  French forces return; negotiations and then conflict with Viet Minh

The war weakens French authority and legitimizes nationalist
resistance. Francia returns not as restorer of order but as claimant
against a movement that has already declared independence.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. FIRST INDOCHINA WAR (1946–1954)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The First Indochina War is a counterinsurgency that becomes
conventional—and is lost.

Patterns:
• Viet Minh: guerrilla then mobile warfare; Chinese support after
  1949; siege warfare at Dien Bien Phu
• France: expeditionary force (CEFEO), Legion, colonial troops;
  reliance on firepower and fixed positions; U.S. aid (from 1950)
  insufficient to offset political and military weakness
• 1950: Border defeats (RC 4); 1952–1954: Navarre Plan, then
  Dien Bien Phu as "hedgehog" to draw Viet Minh into battle

ARC–DISCOVERY: EXPANDED SEARCH
Source: Bernard Fall, *Hell in a Very Small Place* (1966)
Justification: Indochina-specific military and political analysis;
no ARC–FRANCIA listed author provides equivalent Dien Bien Phu
coverage.

"Dien Bien Phu was the first time a European colonial power was
defeated decisively by Asian forces in pitched battle. The French
had established a fortified base in a valley, expecting to draw the
Viet Minh into conventional battle they would lose. Instead, General
Giap brought artillery through terrain the French thought
impassable and besieged the besiegers. After 57 days, 11,000 French
troops surrendered. The empire's military mystique was broken."
— Bernard Fall, *Hell in a Very Small Place* (1966) [ERC-SECONDARY]

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. GENEVA (1954) & LOSS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Dien Bien Phu forces negotiation; Geneva ends the war.

Outcomes:
• Geneva Accords (July 1954): ceasefire; Vietnam partitioned at 17th
  parallel; French withdrawal from North; elections planned (never
  held); Cambodia and Laos neutral
• France leaves Indochina. The Second Empire's Asian pillar is gone.

Indochina's loss prefigures Algeria: defeat at distance first, then
the trauma of "French" territory. Robert Tombs (ARC–FRANCIA) on
imperial priorities:

"When Choiseul negotiated the Treaty of Paris, he chose to keep the
sugar islands rather than Canada. By the economics of the time, this
was rational. Saint-Domingue alone produced more revenue than all of
New France. Sugar was Europe's most valuable commodity; beaver pelts
were not. The decision revealed imperial priorities: profit over
territory, Caribbean over continent."
— Robert Tombs, *France 1814–1914* (1996) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Applied to Indochina: after 1954, Francia prioritizes Algeria and
Africa; Indochina is abandoned. Profit and prestige could not hold
territory without legitimacy or sufficient force.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CONTRAST WITH ALGERIA: INDOCHINA AS PREFIGURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Indochina and Algeria are paired in memory—first loss, then trauma.

Contrasts:
• Indochina: never legally France; fewer settlers; loss in 1954;
  military defeat, then political withdrawal
• Algeria: legally "part of France"; one million pieds-noirs; loss in
  1962; civil war, coup risk, then De Gaulle's abandonment

Indochina teaches that colonial wars can be lost; Algeria teaches
that "French" territory can be cut away. The first breaks the military
mystique; the second breaks the territorial imagination. Together
they encode **the end of Francia's formal empire**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. POST-IMPERIAL SHADOW & MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Indochina remains a quieter wound than Algeria—distant, no mass
repatriation to France—but not forgotten.

Legacies:
• Pieds-rouges and repatriates (far fewer than pieds-noirs)
• Vietnamese immigration to France (post-1954, post-1975)
• Silence and delayed reckoning (torture, repression)
• Military memory: Dien Bien Phu as symbol of defeat and
  professionalization

The empire ends in Asia before it ends in Africa; the sequence
matters for how Francia remembers imperial collapse.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Indochina encodes **the first major rupture of the Second Empire**.

Lessons:
• Mission civilisatrice does not survive organized resistance and
  military defeat.
• Distance and legal status (not "France") make abandonment
  politically easier—but defeat still breaks mystique.
• Indochina prefigures Algeria: loss at distance, then loss at home.
• Abandonment can be preservative for the metropole even as it
  devastates those who believed in the colonial order.

Indochina is Francia's imperial prelude to Algerian trauma.
