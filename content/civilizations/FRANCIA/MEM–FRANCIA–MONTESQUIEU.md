────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Montesquieu as **Francia’s structural liberal
diagnostician**: the thinker who analyzed power comparatively, exposed
the dangers of concentration, and articulated institutional restraint
as the only durable defense against tyranny.

Montesquieu does not dissolve authority.
He **redistributes it**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FORMATIVE CONTEXT: ABSOLUTISM OBSERVED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Montesquieu writes under mature absolutism.

Context:
• Post–Louis XIV centralization
• Administrative uniformity
• Legal codification
• Court dominance over society

The problem is not chaos.
It is **excessive coherence**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. COMPARATIVE METHOD AS INSIGHT ENGINE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Montesquieu studies systems, not ideals.

Approach:
• Cross-cultural comparison
• Climate, geography, custom analysis
• Institutional diversity emphasized

Law is not universal.
It must fit conditions.

Universalism is treated as error.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. SEPARATION OF POWERS AS DAMAGE CONTROL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Montesquieu’s central contribution is structural restraint.

Principle:
• Power must check power
• No authority can be trusted alone
• Liberty emerges from friction, not virtue

This is not democratic idealism.
It is **elite distrust institutionalized**.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. RELATION TO FRANCIA & ANGLIA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Montesquieu looks outward for solutions.

Observations:
• Anglia’s mixed constitution admired
• Common law flexibility respected
• Parliamentary balance noted

Yet:
• Francia’s culture resists fragmentation
• Centralization remains dominant

Montesquieu diagnoses—but does not cure.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. MONTESQUIEU VS DESCARTES (STRUCTURAL CONTRAST)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Descartes: abstract rational unity
• Montesquieu: contextual plural restraint

Descartes simplifies.
Montesquieu complicates.

One empowers centralized systems.
The other limits them.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. FAILURE OF ADOPTION & REVOLUTIONARY IRONY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Montesquieu is celebrated—but bypassed.

Consequences:
• Separation of powers adopted abroad (Anglia → America)
• Francia chooses abstraction over restraint
• Revolution centralizes power instead

His solution is exported.
His warnings are ignored at home.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. LONG-TERM CIVILIZATIONAL EFFECT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Montesquieu shapes liberal governance externally.

Outcomes:
• Constitutional design influence
• Anti-tyranny doctrine codified
• Institutional pluralism normalized elsewhere

In Francia:
• Centralization persists
• Universalism overrides restraint

He becomes Francia’s missed exit.

Roger Pearson's portrait of Voltaire sharpens the contrast: Voltaire is
"not an original thinker, but a 'great and tireless communicator'"
(*Voltaire Almighty: A Life in Pursuit of Freedom* [London: Bloomsbury,
2005], pp. 507–08) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]. Montesquieu offers
**structure** (comparative method, separation of powers); Voltaire offers
**critique** (ridicule, delegitimation). The MEM's contrast—structure vs.
critique—is thus grounded in secondary scholarship.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Liberty requires institutional friction.
• Power concentrates unless designed not to.
• Universal law ignores context.
• Montesquieu offers structure where Voltaire offers critique.
• Francia studies Montesquieu—and then does the opposite.

Tension preserved without verdict.
