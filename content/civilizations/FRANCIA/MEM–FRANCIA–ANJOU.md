I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Anjou as **Francia's medieval comital and ducal
territory** that became the **Angevin heartland** (Henry II, Plantagenet
empire—England, Normandy, Anjou, Aquitaine) and was then absorbed by the
Capetian crown (1204–1246), ending the cross-Channel bloc.

For Francia, Anjou encodes **legitimacy through dynastic accumulation
and confiscation**—the county that made the Plantagenet king of England
also duke of Normandy and count of Anjou, and whose seizure by Philip
Augustus (1204) broke the Angevin empire on the continent.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT: ANJOU BEFORE THE PLANTAGENETS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The county of Anjou emerged in the 10th–11th centuries.

Conditions:
• Counts of Anjou (Fulk, Geoffrey Plantagenet) expand and consolidate
• Marriage of Geoffrey to Matilda (daughter of Henry I of England)
• Their son Henry (II) inherits England (1154), Normandy, Anjou, and
  gains Aquitaine by marriage to Eleanor (1152)

Anjou is thus the **dynastic pivot** that links England and Normandy to
the French royal domain's western flank. The Capetians hold the Île-de-France;
the Plantagenets hold Anjou, Normandy, Aquitaine—a bloc that overshadows
the king of France.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE ANGEVIN EMPIRE (1152–1204)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Henry II rules England, Normandy, Anjou, Aquitaine—Anjou at the core
of the continental holdings.

Dynamics:
• Anjou is the ancestral seat of the Plantagenet line
• French crown (Philip II Augustus) seeks to break the bloc
• Conflict over jurisdiction, homage, and succession (Richard I, John)
• Philip Augustus exploits John's weakness; Normandy falls (1204),
  then Anjou, Maine, Touraine

Verbatim scholarship (ARC-T-MODERN):

"The Capetians consolidated a fragmented realm into the most powerful
monarchy of the Middle Ages, adopting the fleur-de-lys as the emblem of
divine favor and royal continuity. They did not conquer by explosion but
by patience—outlasting rivals, absorbing fiefs, and fusing anointing with
hereditary succession so that the king embodied the state."
— Justine Firnhaber-Baker, *House of Lilies: The Dynasty That Made Medieval
France* (London: Allen Lane, 2024), introduction (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Anjou (1204) qualifies that pattern: Philip Augustus **seizes** Anjou and
Normandy when the Plantagenet vassal (John) is adjudged to have forfeited
them—confiscation and reversion, not marriage or patience. The Angevin
empire on the continent is dismantled; only Aquitaine (Gascony) remains
in English hands under French suzerainty.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CAPETIAN ABSORPTION AND LATER VALOIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Anjou is integrated into the royal domain or granted as apanage.

Dynamics:
• Royal domain extended; Anjou under direct or indirect Capetian control
• Later granted as duchy (apanage) to cadet lines (e.g. Valois-Anjou)
• By the end of the Hundred Years' War, Anjou is firmly within the
  French kingdom—no English claim

Anjou thus encodes **Capetian reversal** of Plantagenet power: the
county that had anchored the cross-Channel empire is absorbed, and
the king of France no longer faces a vassal who is also king of England
in Normandy and Anjou. Aquitaine remains the contested territory;
Anjou is settled.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. CIVILIZATIONAL SIGNIFICANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For Francia, Anjou encodes:

• Dynastic accumulation as threat—the Plantagenet bloc (Anjou +
  Normandy + Aquitaine) overshadowed the Capetian domain
• Legitimacy through confiscation and reversion—Philip Augustus
  did not conquer by marriage but by exploiting feudal law and
  military opportunity (1204)
• Contrast with Aquitaine: Anjou was absorbed early (1204); Aquitaine
  remained in English hands until 1453—so Anjou is **resolved**
  territory, Aquitaine the long stake of the Hundred Years' War

AXIOM FRANCIA-003 (Rupture enables renewal): The seizure of Anjou
(1204) is rupture for the Plantagenets; renewal for the Capetians—
territorial consolidation that prefigures the later Valois reabsorption
of Aquitaine.
