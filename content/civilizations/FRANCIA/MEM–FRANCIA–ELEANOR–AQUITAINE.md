────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Eleanor of Aquitaine as **Francia's medieval
dynastic pivot**: the heiress whose inheritance and two royal marriages
(Louis VII of France, Henry II of England) made Aquitaine the territorial
stake of Anglo-French conflict for centuries.

For Francia, Eleanor encodes **legitimacy through marriage and
inheritance**—and the failure of Capetian marriage to hold the south,
so that Aquitaine passes to the Plantagenets and becomes the cause of
the Hundred Years' War.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CONTEXT: AQUITAINE AND THE CAPETIANS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Before Eleanor, Aquitaine was the largest and richest ducal domain in
western Francia—nominally under the king but in practice autonomous.

Conditions (early 12th c.):
• Duke William X of Aquitaine dies (1137); Eleanor his heir
• Capetian king Louis VI marries her to his son (Louis VII) to bind
  Aquitaine to the crown
• Marriage brings Aquitaine under French royal orbit—in theory

The marriage is **legitimacy through union**. The Capetians do not
conquer Aquitaine; they absorb it by inheritance and marriage.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. TWO MARRIAGES, ONE DUCHY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Eleanor's two marriages frame the territorial destiny of the Midi.

With Louis VII (1137–1152):
• Second Crusade (1147–1149); marriage strains
• Annulment (1152); Eleanor retains Aquitaine (her inheritance)
• Capetians lose the duchy—rupture by repudiation

With Henry II (1152–1204):
• Eleanor marries Henry Plantagenet; he becomes king of England (1154)
• Aquitaine becomes part of the Angevin empire—England, Normandy,
  Anjou, Aquitaine
• French crown claims suzerainty over Aquitaine; English kings hold it
  as French vassals—the contradiction that drives the Hundred Years' War

Verbatim scholarship (ARC-T-MODERN):

"From the founder of the Capetian dynasty to Eleanor of Aquitaine,
*House of Lilies* tells the story of the dynasty that made France."
— Justine Firnhaber-Baker, *House of Lilies: The Dynasty That Made
Medieval France* (London: Allen Lane, 2024), publisher description
(ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Eleanor thus sits at the hinge of the Capetian narrative—the heiress
whose repudiation by Louis VII and remarriage to Henry II **removed**
Aquitaine from direct Capetian control and made it the prize over which
France and England would fight for generations.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. CIVILIZATIONAL SIGNIFICANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For Francia, Eleanor encodes:

• Legitimacy through marriage and inheritance—and the limits of that
  strategy when marriage fails (annulment returns Aquitaine to Eleanor,
  then to the Plantagenets)
• Territorial pivot: Aquitaine as the stake of Anglo-French conflict
  (Poitiers, Hundred Years' War, Brétigny)
• Dynastic woman as carrier of territory and claim—not merely consort
  but heiress whose choices reshape the map

AXIOM FRANCIA-003 (Rupture enables renewal): The annulment **is** rupture;
renewal for the Capetians comes only later—through war and eventual
reabsorption of Aquitaine (in part) after the Hundred Years' War. Eleanor's
legacy is the long conflict that her second marriage made inevitable.
