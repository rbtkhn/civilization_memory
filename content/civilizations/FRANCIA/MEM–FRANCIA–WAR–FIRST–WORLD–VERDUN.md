II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT: FALKENHAYN'S ATTRITION DESIGN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Verdun is chosen to bleed France white.

Falkenhayn's logic:
• France is the weakest of the Allied powers
• Attack a point France must defend
• Inflict casualties at 5:2 ratio (Germany:France)
• "Bleed France white" until collapse

Why Verdun:
• Historic fortress complex
• Symbolic importance (French soil)
• Salient vulnerable to artillery
• France cannot abandon it politically

John Keegan explains the calculation:

"Falkenhayn did not seek to capture Verdun. He sought to create a
killing ground where French soldiers would die faster than Germany
could lose them. The fortress complex was militarily outdated—the
forts had been stripped of guns for use elsewhere—but its symbolic
value was immense. Verdun had been French since 843; it had never
fallen to a German enemy. Falkenhayn calculated correctly that
France would sacrifice everything to hold it."
— John Keegan, *The First World War* (1998)

Niall Ferguson frames the attrition logic in cold arithmetic:

"Falkenhayn's aim was not to break through. It was to kill. He had
calculated that France could not sustain casualties at the rate
Verdun would impose; that the French army would crack before the
German; and that the war would be won in the Morgue. The arithmetic
was wrong—the casualty ratio favoured neither side—but the design
was industrial slaughter."
— Niall Ferguson, *The Pity of War* (1998) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

The calculation was strategically rational and morally monstrous.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE OPENING: OPERATION GERICHT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
21 February 1916: Germany launches the greatest bombardment in history.

The scale:
• 1,200 guns on 8-mile front
• 1 million shells in first day
• Twelve hours of bombardment before infantry advance
• French forward positions obliterated

The assault:

"The German bombardment of 21 February 1916 was unprecedented.
Nothing on this scale had ever been attempted. French positions
were pulverized; trenches disappeared; men were buried alive in
their dugouts. When the German infantry advanced, they found
the forward French lines destroyed. Fort Douaumont, the largest
fort in the complex, was captured on 25 February by a handful
of German soldiers who found it virtually undefended—a French
failure that scandalized the nation."
— Alistair Horne, *The Price of Glory: Verdun 1916* (1962)

Fort Douaumont's fall shocked France. The nation demanded response.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PÉTAIN AND THE VOIE SACRÉE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pétain organizes the defense.

25 February 1916: Pétain takes command.

His measures:
• Organizes the "Voie Sacrée" (Sacred Road)
• Single supply route from Bar-le-Duc
• 3,000 trucks per day; one every 14 seconds
• Rotational system (noria) to preserve units
• Artillery defense prioritized

The Voie Sacrée:

"The Voie Sacrée was Verdun's lifeline. A single road, fifty miles
long, carried everything the defense needed: men, shells, food,
equipment. Pétain organized it with mechanical precision. Trucks
ran continuously; broken vehicles were pushed off the road without
stopping. 3,900 trucks passed each day at peak; territorial troops
spread gravel constantly to prevent the road from disintegrating.
Without the Voie Sacrée, Verdun would have fallen."
— John Keegan, *The First World War* (1998)

Pétain's defensive genius saved Verdun. His phrase "Ils ne passeront
pas" (They shall not pass) became national motto.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE NORIA: ROTATING THE ARMY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pétain's rotation system spreads the sacrifice.

The noria (water-wheel) system:
• Units serve limited time in the line
• Fresh divisions replace exhausted ones
• 70 of 96 French divisions serve at Verdun
• Entire French army shares the experience

The effect:

"Pétain's noria ensured that Verdun became the shared experience
of the French army. Unlike the Germans, who kept the same divisions
in the line until destroyed, Pétain rotated units out before they
broke. This preserved the army's fighting power—but it meant that
almost every French soldier experienced Verdun. The memory was
distributed across the entire nation. Every village, every regiment,
every family knew Verdun."
— Alistair Horne, *The Price of Glory* (1962)

The noria made Verdun France's collective trauma.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. THE FIGHTING: TEN MONTHS OF HORROR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Verdun becomes hell on earth.

Conditions:
• Constant artillery bombardment
• Terrain churned to mud and corpses
• Water sources contaminated
• Gas attacks routine
• No shelter from shellfire
• Bodies never recovered

The experience:

"Verdun was unlike any battle before or since. The concentrated
artillery fire created a landscape of craters and corpses. Men
described the sound as a continuous roar that drove them mad.
The dead could not be buried; they were ground into the mud and
became part of the terrain. Soldiers drank from shell holes that
contained decomposing bodies. The fighting was not for territory
but for survival in a place that seemed designed to kill."
— John Keegan, *The First World War* (1998)

The Mort-Homme (Dead Man) and Côte 304 became synonymous with slaughter.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. FORT VAUX AND THE LIMIT OF ENDURANCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fort Vaux's defense becomes legend.

June 1916:
• German forces surround Fort Vaux
• Major Raynal commands ~600 defenders
• Week-long siege without water
• Men drink their own urine
• Raynal surrenders when defense impossible
• Germans honor his courage

The surrender:

"When Major Raynal finally surrendered Fort Vaux on 7 June, after
a week without water, the German Crown Prince sent him back his
sword as a mark of respect. The defenders had fought beyond
human endurance. They had signaled for relief by carrier pigeon;
the last bird, Le Vaillant, died delivering its message and was
later awarded the Légion d'honneur. Vaux became a symbol of
French resistance to the last extremity."
— Alistair Horne, *The Price of Glory* (1962)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. THE GERMAN FAILURE: ATTRITION REVERSED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Falkenhayn's calculation fails.

The arithmetic:
• Planned 5:2 casualty ratio (German:French) not achieved
• Actual ratio closer to 1:1.1
• German losses nearly equal French
• French army not broken; German army exhausted

The failure:

"Falkenhayn's attrition strategy failed on its own terms. He had
expected to bleed France at a favorable ratio; instead, German
casualties approached French. The French army endured; the German
army exhausted itself. By autumn 1916, Germany had lost not only
the battle but the strategic initiative. Falkenhayn was replaced
by Hindenburg and Ludendorff in August. Verdun had consumed
Germany too."
— John Keegan, *The First World War* (1998)

The battle designed to bleed France bled Germany equally.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. THE RECAPTURE: OCTOBER-DECEMBER 1916
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
France retakes the lost ground.

October-December 1916:
• French counter-offensive under Nivelle/Mangin
• Fort Douaumont recaptured (24 October)
• Fort Vaux recaptured (2 November)
• Front line restored approximately to February positions
• French claim victory

The counter-attack:

"Nivelle and Mangin organized the French counter-offensive with
careful artillery preparation. The creeping barrage—a technique
learned at terrible cost—allowed infantry to advance behind a
moving curtain of shells. Fort Douaumont fell with relatively
light casualties; Fort Vaux was found abandoned. By December,
the front line was approximately where it had been in February.
Ten months of fighting, 700,000 casualties, and nothing had changed
except that France had proven it would not break."
— Alistair Horne, *The Price of Glory* (1962)

XI. PÉTAIN'S LEGACY: HERO TO TRAITOR
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Verdun creates Pétain; 1940 destroys him.

At Verdun:
• Pétain saves France through defensive organization
• "Ils ne passeront pas" becomes his motto
• National hero; the defensive genius

After 1940:
• Pétain becomes Vichy head of state
• Collaboration with Nazi Germany
• Post-war trial, death sentence (commuted)
• Memory becomes poisoned

The paradox:

"Pétain's tragedy was that the qualities that saved France at
Verdun—caution, defensive thinking, care for soldiers' lives—
became the qualities that led him to collaboration in 1940.
He could not imagine another war of attrition; he surrendered
to avoid it. The hero of Verdun became the traitor of Vichy,
and France has never resolved the contradiction."
— Robert Tombs and Isabelle Tombs, *That Sweet Enemy* (2006)
