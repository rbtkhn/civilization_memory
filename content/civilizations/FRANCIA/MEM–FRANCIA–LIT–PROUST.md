I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Marcel Proust as **Francia's architect of memory
as civilizational form**: the writer who made involuntary memory the
engine of narrative, who dissolved the boundary between past and
present, and who encoded the end of the aristocratic world and the
triumph of time over habit.

Proust does not record history.
He **makes memory the medium of loss and recovery**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. FORMATIVE CONTEXT: BELLE ÉPOQUE & PARIS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Proust emerges from the Parisian haute bourgeoisie and aristocratic
salons of the Third Republic.

Formative traits:
• Asthma, hypersensitivity, withdrawal and observation
• Dreyfusard engagement (moral and social fracture)
• Immersion in Guermantes and Verdurin worlds
• Conversion of salon life into raw material for art

Core belief:
Reality is not in the present—it is **recovered only through memory**
and art.

The world he describes is already passing as he writes.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. À LA RECHERCHE DU TEMPS PERDU: MEMORY AS STRUCTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
*À la recherche du temps perdu* (1913–1927) is a single work in seven
volumes: not a chronicle of events but an anatomy of memory, habit,
love, jealousy, and the recovery of lost time through involuntary
recall and art.

Proust in his own words—on the recovery of the past:

"And suddenly the memory returns. The taste was that of the little
piece of madeleine which on Sunday mornings at Combray... my aunt
Léonie used to give me, dipping it first in her own cup of tea or
tisane.... The past is hidden somewhere outside the realm, beyond
the reach of intellect, in some material object."
— Marcel Proust, *À la recherche du temps perdu*, I (*Du côté de chez Swann*, 1913) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

On habit and the blindness it induces:

"Habit is a second nature that prevents us from knowing the first, of
which it has neither the cruelties nor the enchantments."
— Marcel Proust, *À la recherche du temps perdu* (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Memory is not storage—it is **discovery**. Habit is the enemy of
perception; involuntary memory is the only route back to what was
actually lived.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. INVOLUNTARY MEMORY: THE MADELEINE AND TEMPS RETROUVÉ
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The madeleine episode establishes involuntary memory as the novel's
structural principle: the past is not narrated chronologically but
**recovered** through sensation—taste, smell, touch—that bypasses
voluntary recall.

Proust on the real voyage of discovery:

"The real voyage of discovery consists not in seeking new landscapes,
but in having new eyes."
— Marcel Proust, *À la recherche du temps perdu* (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

On the only real paradise:

"The only real paradise is the paradise we have lost."
— Marcel Proust, *À la recherche du temps perdu* (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

For Francia, Proust encodes **loss as the condition of recovery**:
the aristocratic and bourgeois world of the Belle Époque is already
lost when he writes it; the First World War and time complete the
dissolution. The novel is the recovery—through art—of what cannot
be lived again.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ART AND PERCEPTION: THE WORK AS REDEMPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In *Le Temps retrouvé* (1927), the narrator understands that the
only durable conquest of time is the work of art itself. Life is
wasted in habit and social performance; art alone recovers and
fixes the past.

Proust on suffering and completion:

"We are healed of a suffering only by experiencing it to the full."
— Marcel Proust, *À la recherche du temps perdu* (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

The civilizational memory function:
Proust gives Francia a grammar for **memory as civilizational
resource**—not archive but involuntary return, not history but
sensation that restores the past. The Republic and the salons,
Combray and Paris, are preserved only inside the work.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Proust encodes **Francia's self-understanding of time and loss**:
the past is not behind us but hidden in material objects and
sensations; habit blinds us to it; art alone recovers it.

For Francia:
• Memory is not chronology—it is involuntary recovery.
• The only real paradise is the one we have lost.
• The Belle Époque and the aristocratic world are preserved in
  the novel because they are already gone in fact.
• The work of art is the only victory over time.

Proust does not explain the state or revolution—he explains **how
Francia remembers**: through sensation, through loss, through the
book that outlives the world it describes.
