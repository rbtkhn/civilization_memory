────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Franco–Mexican War as **Francia’s late-imperial
misprojection episode**: an attempt to translate universalist legitimacy,
dynastic symbolism, and financial coercion into durable authority outside
the European balance system.

The conflict tests whether Francia can project order without coalition
consent or material preponderance.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT: UNIVERSALISM BEYOND EUROPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The intervention emerges from overlapping motives:
• Debt enforcement and financial leverage
• Dynastic restoration (Habsburg monarchy)
• Prestige repair after continental constraint
• Exploitation of U.S. distraction during civil war

Napoleon III frames the venture as civilizational uplift rather than
territorial conquest. In his public justification to the Corps législatif,
he insists:

> “France does not seek conquest in Mexico; she seeks the establishment
> of a stable government which, by restoring order and respect for law,
> will guarantee the interests of all nations and assure to that country
> the benefits of civilization and independence.”  
> — Napoleon III, message to the Corps législatif, 1862

Legitimacy is declared in advance of capacity.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. COALITION ENTRY AND RAPID FRACTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The intervention begins as a tripartite operation with Britain and Spain.
The coalition dissolves rapidly once Francia’s ambitions become clear.

Spanish and British withdrawal reflects:
• Divergent war aims
• Aversion to regime imposition
• Sensitivity to U.S. reaction

Francia proceeds alone, converting a financial dispute into an imperial
project without coalition insulation.

Coalition blindness replaces coalition management.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. IMPERIAL INSTALLATION: MAXIMILIAN AS SYMBOL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Francia installs Archduke Maximilian of Habsburg as emperor of Mexico,
seeking legitimacy through dynastic symbolism and European recognition.

Maximilian’s own correspondence reveals the fragility of this foundation:

> “My position rests neither on the confidence of the Mexican people
> nor on sufficient force to impose obedience everywhere. I depend
> almost entirely on French bayonets, and without them the edifice
> we have erected cannot stand.”  
> — Maximilian I, letter to Empress Charlotte, 1865

Authority is symbolic, not endogenous.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. RESISTANCE, SPACE, AND ATTRITION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mexican republican forces exploit:
• Vast terrain
• Local legitimacy
• Decentralized resistance
• External moral support from the United States

Benito Juárez articulates resistance as sovereign endurance:

> “Among individuals, as among nations, respect for the rights of others
> is peace. Mexico has the right to be independent and to govern herself;
> this right cannot be annulled by force nor by the will of foreigners,
> however powerful they may be.”  
> — Benito Juárez, proclamation, 1865

Francia confronts a legitimacy deficit it cannot repair militarily.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. U.S. REASSERTION AND STRATEGIC ISOLATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
With the end of the American Civil War, U.S. pressure becomes decisive:
• Diplomatic isolation of Francia
• Material support to Mexican republicans
• Invocation of the Monroe Doctrine

Francia faces a choice:
• Escalate without coalition backing
• Withdraw and accept symbolic defeat

Imperial ambition collides with hemispheric constraint.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. WITHDRAWAL AND TERMINATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
French forces withdraw beginning in 1866.
Maximilian is captured and executed in 1867.

The collapse is rapid once French support ends.
No institutional residue survives.

The episode ends not with negotiated settlement, but with moral and
symbolic repudiation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Franco–Mexican War encodes **the outer failure boundary of French
universalism**:

• Legitimacy cannot be exported without local buy-in
• Dynastic symbolism fails outside shared civilizational grammar
• Coalition absence magnifies exposure
• Moral framing accelerates reputational loss when enforcement fails

This is universalism without ballast.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Declarative legitimacy fails without endogenous acceptance.
• Coalition insulation is essential for extra-continental projection.
• Dynastic solutions misfire in republican environments.
• External theaters punish prestige-driven escalation.
• Franco–Mexican War anticipates later overreach failures.

Tension preserved without verdict.
