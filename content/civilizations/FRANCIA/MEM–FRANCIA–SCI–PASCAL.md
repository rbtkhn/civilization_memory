────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Blaise Pascal as **Francia's tension between
reason and faith**: the polymath who advanced mathematics and physics
(probability, pressure) while insisting that the heart has reasons
reason does not know, and who defended Jansenism against Jesuit
casuistry.

Pascal is not a system-builder like Descartes or Leibniz.
He is a **boundary-figure**—reason and revelation in unresolved tension.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRUCTURAL CONTEXT: POST–WAR EXHAUSTION & JANSENISM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pascal emerges from Francia in the wake of religious war and
Richelieu's consolidation.

Conditions:
• Thirty Years' War memory
• Jansenism vs Jesuit controversy
• Descartes' rationalism ascendant
• Port-Royal as intellectual and spiritual pole

Pascal in his own words—on the limits of reason:

"The heart has its reasons, which reason does not know."
— Pascal, *Pensées* (1670, posth.) (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

He does not reject reason—he **limits its domain**. Certainty in
mathematics; elsewhere, the wager and the heart.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. MATHEMATICS: PROBABILITY & THE TRIANGLE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pascal co-founds probability theory with Fermat (correspondence 1654).

Contributions:
• Pascal's triangle (binomial coefficients)
• Expectation and decision under uncertainty
• Foundation for actuarial and rational choice

Pascal on the wager—reason applied to faith:

"If you win, you win everything; if you lose, you lose nothing. Wager
then, without hesitation, that He exists."
— Pascal, *Pensées* (1670, posth.) (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Calculation does not prove God—it **rationalizes the bet**. Reason
serves the heart's stake.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. PHYSICS: PRESSURE & THE VACUUM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pascal establishes that pressure in a fluid is transmitted equally
(Pascal's law) and defends the possibility of vacuum against
Aristotelian and scholastic objection.

Contributions:
• *Traité de l'équilibre des liqueurs*
• Pascal's law (hydrostatics)
• Experimental demonstration (Puy de Dôme, 1648)

Science and faith coexist: the same mind that measures pressure
asserts that "the eternal silence of these infinite spaces frightens me."

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE PROVINCIALES & POLEMIC
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
*Lettres provinciales* (1656–1657) attack Jesuit casuistry and
moral laxity in the name of Jansenist rigor.

Pascal on authority and truth:

"I have made this letter longer than usual only because I did not have
the time to make it shorter."
— Pascal, *Lettres provinciales* (1656–57) (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Rhetoric and mathematics share a discipline: economy, clarity. The
polemicist and the geometer are one.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pascal encodes **Francia's refusal to choose between reason and faith**.

Lessons:
• Mathematics and physics can coexist with theology—without synthesis.
• The heart has reasons; reason has limits.
• Probability can rationalize the wager but not replace it.
• Jansenism and Jesuit controversy shape civilizational tension.

Pascal is the counterpoint to Descartes: certainty in geometry;
elsewhere, the bet and the silence of infinite spaces.
