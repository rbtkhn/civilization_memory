I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Marie Curie as **Francia's embodiment of science
as civilizational achievement**: the first woman to win a Nobel Prize,
the only person to win in two sciences, and the builder of the Institut
du radium—radioactivity as French institutional and moral capital.

Curie is not only a discoverer.
She is a **institution-builder**—science as national resource.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRUCTURAL CONTEXT: THIRD REPUBLIC & SCIENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Curie emerges in a Francia that had made science a pillar of republican
legitimacy and that still restricted women's access to higher education.

Conditions:
• Sorbonne opening to women (late 19th c.)
• Pierre Curie partnership; widowhood (1906)
• Scandals (Langevin affair); xenophobia and misogyny
• WWI mobilization of science

Curie in her own words—on work and perseverance:

"Nothing in life is to be feared; it is only to be understood."
— Marie Curie (attributed; ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Understanding replaces fear; science replaces superstition. The
civilizational claim is explicit.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. RADIOACTIVITY: DISCOVERY & ISOLATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
With Pierre Curie and building on Becquerel, Marie isolates polonium
(1898) and radium (1898–1902), coins "radioactivity," and establishes
its atomic basis.

Contributions:
• Systematic study of uranium radiation
• Isolation of radium (tonnes of pitchblende; shed at École de physique)
• Two Nobel Prizes (Physics 1903; Chemistry 1911)

Curie on the nature of scientific work:

"I was taught that the way of progress was neither swift nor easy."
— Marie Curie, *Pierre Curie* (1923) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Progress is labor—material and intellectual. The Institut du radium
institutionalizes that labor.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. INSTITUTIONALIZATION: INSTITUT DU RADIUM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
After Pierre's death, Marie secures funding (Pasteur-style) and builds
the Institut du radium (1914), later Curie Institute—research and
medical application (radiotherapy).

Strategic logic:
• Science as public good
• Institutional continuity beyond the individual
• Francia as host for European science (Polish immigrant as French icon)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. WWI: "PETITES CURIES" & MOBILIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Curie organizes mobile X-ray units ("petites Curies") for frontline
hospitals—radiology as war medicine. She drives, trains technicians,
and exposes herself to radiation.

Civilizational function:
Science serves the nation in war; the nation later honors the scientist.
Curie becomes **Francia's moral and scientific symbol**—immigrant,
woman, Nobel, patriot.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Curie encodes **science as civilizational achievement and national
resource**: two Nobels, institution-building, war service, and the
persistent tension between acclaim and scandal (gender, origin).

Lessons:
• Science can be institutionalized as national capital.
• Immigration and gender do not preclude becoming a national icon.
• War mobilizes science; science legitimizes the state.
