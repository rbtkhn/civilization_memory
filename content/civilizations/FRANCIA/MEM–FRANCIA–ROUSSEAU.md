I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Jean-Jacques Rousseau as **Francia’s moral
absolutization engine**: the thinker who redefined legitimacy as
collective virtue, transformed popular sovereignty into an ethical
imperative, and supplied the conceptual grammar for revolutionary
totalization.

Rousseau does not erode authority.
He **reconstitutes it as moral command**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

Descartes had already relocated certainty from tradition to the thinking subject:

> "I think, therefore I am."
> — Descartes, *Discours de la méthode* (1637) (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Rousseau opens *Du contrat social*:

> "Man is born free; and everywhere he is in chains. One thinks himself the master of others, and still remains a greater slave than they."
> — Rousseau, *Du contrat social* (1762) (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Will Durant (ARC–FRANCIA) on Rousseau's influence:

"Rousseau gave the Revolution its vocabulary: the general will, the
sovereignty of the people, the belief that law must express the common
good. He did not cause the Revolution, but he armed it with a theory
that made the transfer of sovereignty from king to nation seem not only
possible but just. The Jacobins would quote him; the Declaration of
Rights would echo him."
— Will Durant, *Rousseau and Revolution* (1967) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

II. FORMATIVE ORIGINS & PSYCHOLOGICAL POSTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rousseau emerges from marginality and resentment.

Formative traits:
• Outsider status
• Hostility to elite culture
• Romanticization of innocence
• Moral intensity

Core belief:
Man is born good; society corrupts him.

Legitimacy must therefore remake society.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. GENERAL WILL AS SOVEREIGNTY SUBSTITUTE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rousseau’s central innovation is the General Will.

Key claims:
• Sovereignty is indivisible
• The people are morally unified
• Dissent equals error or corruption
• Freedom is obedience to collective virtue

Legitimacy becomes ethical, not procedural.

Pluralism becomes deviance.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. MORAL POLITICS & COERCIVE VIRTUE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Politics is moral pedagogy.

Implications:
• Education as ideological formation
• Law as moral instrument
• Coercion justified to enforce freedom

The state acquires ethical authority over the individual.

Moral unanimity is prioritized over restraint.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. RELATION TO REVOLUTIONARY PRACTIAD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rousseau provides the grammar later actors weaponize.

Connections:
• Jacobin moral absolutism
• Revolutionary tribunals
• Virtue–terror synthesis

He does not design institutions.
He supplies justification.

Theory outruns feasibility.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. LEGITIMACY ACCELERATION & DANGER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rousseau offers a replacement legitimacy more dangerous than what he
destroys.

Effects:
• Absolutization of popular authority
• Moralization of violence
• Collapse of legal restraint
• Zero-sum politics

Ethics becomes command.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LONG-TERM CIVILIZATIONAL EFFECT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Rousseau’s legacy is dual and volatile.

Outcomes:
• Democratic rhetoric universalized
• Revolutionary totalism enabled
• Moral politics normalized

He replaces dynastic sacrality with civic sacrality.

The altar moves from king to people.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Popular sovereignty can become absolutist.
• Moral unanimity destroys plural legitimacy.
• Freedom framed ethically invites coercion.
• Rousseau supplies the logic of revolutionary terror.
• Robespierre is Rousseau’s executor, not his distortion.

Rupture preserved without absolution.
