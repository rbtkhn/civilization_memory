I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Tours as **Francia's foundational civilizational
defense**: the battle that halted Islamic expansion into Western Europe
and established the Carolingian dynasty's legitimacy through military
victory.

For Francia, Tours is **the origin moment**—the battle that made
Charles Martel "the Hammer" and positioned the Carolingians to replace
the Merovingians. The victory's civilizational significance has been
debated, but its foundational role in French self-conception endures.

Note: This battle is also called the Battle of Poitiers (732), distinct
from the 1356 battle. We use "Tours" to avoid confusion.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT: UMAYYAD EXPANSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Umayyad Caliphate reaches its maximum extent.

Expansion trajectory:
• Arab conquest of Iberia (711-718)
• Visigothic kingdom destroyed
• Raids across Pyrenees begin (720s)
• Narbonne captured; Aquitaine raided repeatedly
• 732: Abd al-Rahman leads major expedition

The scope of the threat:

"The Umayyad forces that crossed the Pyrenees in 732 were not
merely raiders. Abd al-Rahman led a major expedition—perhaps the
largest Islamic army to enter Gaul. His objective was Tours, one
of the richest cities in Francia, where the shrine of St. Martin
promised enormous plunder. Whether this was conquest or raid
remains debated, but Charles Martel treated it as existential.
He marched south with every warrior he could muster."
— John Keegan, *A History of Warfare* (1993)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CHARLES MARTEL: THE HAMMER FORGED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Charles Martel represents Frankish military professionalization.

His position:
• Mayor of the Palace (real power behind Merovingian figurehead)
• Controls Austrasia (northeastern Francia)
• Has spent decades fighting rival factions
• Commands loyal, experienced warriors
• Not yet "Carolingian" dynasty—that name comes from his grandson

Military reforms:

"Charles Martel understood what Frankish kings before him had not:
that infantry could defeat cavalry if properly led. The Franks at
Tours did not charge; they formed a defensive mass—what later
sources call a 'wall of ice'—and let the Arab cavalry break against
them. This required discipline that earlier Frankish armies lacked.
Charles had spent twenty years building that discipline through
constant campaigning. Tours was the product, not the origin, of
his military system."
— John Keegan, *A History of Warfare* (1993)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. THE BATTLE: INFANTRY DEFEATS CAVALRY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tours demonstrates disciplined infantry's power.

The engagement:
• Charles positions on high ground between Tours and Poitiers
• Frankish infantry forms dense phalanx-like formation
• Arab cavalry charges repeatedly
• Franks hold position; cavalry cannot break formation
• Battle continues for days (sources vary: one day to seven)
• Abd al-Rahman killed in final assault
• Umayyad army withdraws overnight

The tactical key:

"The Frankish victory at Tours was not won by cavalry—the Franks
had little effective cavalry in 732—but by infantry discipline.
Arab cavalry, which had destroyed armies across three continents,
could not break the Frankish line. Men stood shoulder to shoulder,
shields overlapping, refusing to pursue, refusing to break formation.
When Abd al-Rahman was killed leading a charge, his army lost
cohesion. They withdrew to protect their plunder, and Charles—
characteristically—did not pursue."
— Hugh Kennedy, *The Great Arab Conquests* (2007)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ABD AL-RAHMAN'S DEATH: THE DECISIVE MOMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The commander's death ends the campaign.

The sequence:
• Abd al-Rahman leads cavalry charge personally
• Killed in the assault (details unknown)
• Umayyad forces lose coordination
• Night falls; Arabs withdraw to camp
• Next morning, Franks find camp abandoned

The effect:

"Abd al-Rahman's death broke the Umayyad will to continue. Without
their commander, the various Arab and Berber contingents had no
coordinating authority. They had also accumulated enormous plunder
that they wished to preserve. Rather than renew the battle, they
withdrew southward, taking what they could carry. Charles, with
his characteristic caution, did not pursue. He had achieved his
objective: the raiders were gone."
— John Keegan, *A History of Warfare* (1993)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. CIVILIZATIONAL SIGNIFICANCE: DEBATED BUT FOUNDATIONAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tours' importance has been contested.

The maximalist view (Gibbon):
• Tours saved Western Christendom from Islam
• Without Tours, mosques in Oxford
• Decisive civilizational turning point

The minimalist view (modern scholarship):
• This was a raid, not an invasion
• Umayyad caliphate was already weakening
• Other factors would have stopped expansion
• Tours was one battle among many

The balanced assessment:

"Tours did not 'save' Western Europe in any simple sense. The
Umayyad Caliphate was already overstretched; internal conflicts
would soon fragment it. Arab expansion had natural limits. But
Tours mattered because of what it made possible. Charles Martel's
victory established the military prestige that allowed his
descendants to replace the Merovingians. Without Tours, there is
no Charlemagne. The battle's civilizational importance lies in
what it founded, not what it prevented."
— Chris Wickham, *The Inheritance of Rome* (2009)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. THE CAROLINGIAN FOUNDATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tours creates the dynasty that creates medieval Europe.

The succession:
• Charles Martel (d. 741) → Pepin III → Charlemagne
• Pepin deposes last Merovingian (751)
• Charlemagne crowned Emperor (800)
• Carolingian Renaissance; medieval Europe takes shape

The legitimacy chain:

"Charles Martel earned his epithet—Martellus, the Hammer—at Tours.
That victory gave him the prestige to dominate Francia despite not
being king. His son Pepin could depose the Merovingians because the
family had proven themselves militarily. His grandson Charlemagne
could claim the Imperial title because the Carolingians had
defended Christendom. Tours was the foundation stone of a dynasty
that would shape European civilization for three centuries."
— John Keegan, *A History of Warfare* (1993)

IX. FRANKISH INFANTRY: PROTO-MEDIEVAL WARFARE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tours represents an infantry moment before the cavalry age.

The paradox:
• 732: Frankish infantry defeats Arab cavalry
• By 800: Carolingian army is primarily cavalry
• Charles Martel himself promoted cavalry development
• The infantry that won Tours was superseded

The transition:

"Tours was won by infantry, but Charles Martel understood that
cavalry was the future. He spent his later years developing
Frankish heavy cavalry, seizing church lands to finance it,
creating the mounted warrior class that would become medieval
knighthood. Tours was the last great Frankish infantry victory;
the future belonged to the horseman. Charles made it so."
— John Keegan, *A History of Warfare* (1993)
