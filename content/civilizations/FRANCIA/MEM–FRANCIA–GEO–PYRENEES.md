I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Pyrenees as **Francia's southern strategic
barrier**: the mountain chain that separates Gaul from Iberia, defines
the limit of French territorial absorption, and has repeatedly
punished overreach—from Roncevaux to Napoleon's Peninsular disaster.

The Pyrenees do not unite Francia and Iberia.
They **divide** them—and punish those who forget it.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II–VI. STRATEGIC SECTIONS (TERRAIN, POWER, LIMIT)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**II. Roman and ancient framing:** Strabo fixes the Pyrenees in
geographic consciousness as the boundary between Gaul and Iberia—not
easily crossed, so that peoples on either side develop differently
despite proximity. The mountains are a civilizational divider before
they are a military obstacle. Roman administration respects the
divide; neither province is fully extended across the range.

**III. Medieval passes and the Roncevaux lesson:** The Pyrenees are
crossable at passes (Roncevaux, Somport, Cerdanya)—but crossing
exposes armies to ambush and retreat to catastrophe. Charlemagne's
rear guard is destroyed at Roncevaux (778); the pass enters legend as
the place where Frankish power meets its limit. The mountains reward
defense and punish projection.

**IV. Early modern frontier and Treaty of the Pyrenees (1659):** The
war between France and Spain ends with the Treaty of the Pyrenees:
territorial adjustments (Roussillon to France), marriage (Louis XIV–
Maria Theresa), and the formalization of the Pyrenees as the
recognized border. The frontier is fixed; the cultural divide
persists. Francia gains territory up to the mountains but does not
absorb Iberia.

**V. Napoleonic overreach and Peninsular disaster:** Napoleon
attempts to incorporate Spain and Portugal—crossing the Pyrenees with
armies and imposing a client regime. The result is guerrilla war,
British intervention, and prolonged attrition. The mountains do not
stop invasion; they enable resistance. Control fails without
legitimacy. The Pyrenees record the limit of French administrative–
military logic to the south.

**VI. Modern border and symbolic divide:** The Pyrenees remain the
state border between France and Spain (and Andorra). No French
regime has sought to absorb Iberia since Napoleon. The mountains
symbolize durable separation—civilizational wall, not bridge.

VII. ARC-T-ANCIENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strabo (1st c. BC–1st c. AD) on Iberia and the Pyrenees:

"Iberia is enclosed by the Pyrenees and the sea; and the mountains
which divide it from Gaul are not easily crossed, so that the peoples
on either side have developed in different ways, though they are
neighbors."
— Strabo, *Geographica*, Book III (ARC-T-ANCIENT) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Pattern permanence: Pyrenees as civilizational divider and barrier to absorption.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Einhard (9th c.) on Roncevaux and the Pyrenees:

"While the army was marching in column through the passes of the
Pyrenees, the Basques, who had set an ambush in the highest part of
the mountains … attacked the rear guard and the baggage train, and
threw them into confusion. In the battle that followed, the Basques
killed their opponents to the last man."
— Einhard, *Vita Karoli Magni*, ch. 9 (ARC-T-MEDIEVAL) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Pattern permanence: Pyrenees passes as site of ambush and limit of Frankish power.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. ARC-T-EARLY-MOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Voltaire (18th c.) on France, Spain, and the Pyrenees:

"Spain had the New World before France had a single ship capable of
holding it. France imitated, but the Pyrenees had already made two
nations that could never be one."
— Voltaire, *Essai sur les mœurs* (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Pattern permanence: Pyrenees as maker of two nations; geography enforces separation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. ARC-T-MODERN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fernand Braudel on the Pyrenees and French identity:

"The Pyrenees are not a wall that France built; they are a wall that
nature built, and that history has confirmed. France and Spain have
lived on either side, sometimes at war, never merged."
— Fernand Braudel, *L'Identité de la France* (1986) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Pattern permanence: mountains as natural and historical confirmation of separation.
