────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Papacy as **Francia’s legitimacy multiplier
and rival arbiter**: an external sacral authority alternately used,
contained, instrumentalized, and resisted in the construction of
French state sovereignty and universalist claims.

For Francia, the Papacy is not merely religious power.
It is a **competing source of universal legitimacy** that must be
managed rather than obeyed.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. PAPAL ALLIANCE & EARLY LEGITIMACY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Francia’s early monarchy aligns closely with Rome.

Functions:
• Sacral coronation
• Dynastic validation
• Moral continuity after Roman collapse

Papal sanction stabilizes early kingship.

Legitimacy flows downward through anointing.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. UNIVERSALISM COMPETITION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As Francia consolidates, rivalry emerges.

Dynamics:
• Competing claims to moral universality
• Papacy asserts spiritual supremacy
• Monarchy asserts temporal sovereignty

Conflict is doctrinal but restrained.

The question becomes:
→ Who defines Christendom?

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. GALLICANISM & SOVEREIGN CONTAINMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Francia develops legal–theological independence.

Key features:
• Gallican Church traditions
• Limitation of papal interference
• Royal control over appointments
• Nationalized ecclesiastical authority

The Church is subordinated without destruction.

Sacral legitimacy is domesticated.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. PAPACY AS INSTRUMENT OF STATECRAFT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Francia uses Rome selectively.

Patterns:
• Papal backing sought when useful
• Papal authority ignored when obstructive
• Religion subordinated to raison d’état

Richelieu exemplifies this logic.

Faith becomes policy terrain.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. REVOLUTIONARY BREAK & LEGITIMACY RUPTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Revolution severs papal authority.

Outcomes:
• Confiscation of Church property
• Civil Constitution of the Clergy
• Papal legitimacy rejected

Universalism is re-authored in secular terms.

The altar moves from Rome to the Republic.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. LONG-TERM CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Papacy becomes a **former legitimizer**, not a governor.

Legacy:
• Template for managing external moral authority
• Proof that universalism can be nationalized
• Model for subordinating sacral power to state interest

Francia learns to replace, not obey, Rome.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• External sacral authority can stabilize early states.
• Universalism invites rivalry with other universalists.
• Gallicanism is sovereignty disguised as theology.
• The Papacy is Francia’s first legitimacy competitor.
• Secular universalism inherits papal ambition without papal restraint.

Tension preserved without verdict.
