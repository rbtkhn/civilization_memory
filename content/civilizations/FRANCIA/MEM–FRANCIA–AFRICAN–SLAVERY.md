I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves African slavery as **Francia’s deepest imperial
moral contradiction**: a system that financed global ambition and
colonial prestige while directly undermining Francia’s universalist
claims of reason, equality, and moral authority.

Slavery is not peripheral to Francia’s empire.
It is **embedded in its Atlantic failure**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. SLAVERY AS ECONOMIC FOUNDATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Francia’s Atlantic economy depends heavily on coerced labor.

Core features:
• Plantation slavery in Saint-Domingue and Caribbean
• Sugar, coffee, and indigo as fiscal engines
• Integration with mercantile and court finance
• Reliance on the Code Noir (1685)

Wealth flows through coercion.

Universal rhetoric coexists with unfreedom.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CODE NOIR & LEGAL RATIONALIZATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Slavery is legalized and regulated, not ignored.

Characteristics:
• Centralized legal codification
• Christian paternal framing
• Racial hierarchy formalized
• State authority asserted over bodies

The state manages slavery as administration.

Moral abstraction is replaced with bureaucratic control.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. ENLIGHTENMENT TENSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Philosophical critique emerges unevenly.

Contradictions:
• Enlightenment elites critique tyranny
• Economic dependence constrains reform
• Abolition delayed by colonial profitability

Reason exposes slavery intellectually—
but power preserves it materially.

Critique without enforcement breeds hypocrisy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. REVOLUTIONARY RUPTURE & HAITI
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The French Revolution detonates the contradiction.

Dynamics:
• Rights declared universal
• Enslaved populations claim inclusion
• Saint-Domingue rebellion escalates

Haiti exposes the impossibility of partial universalism.

Liberty, once declared, cannot be contained.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. ABOLITION, RESTORATION, AND REVERSAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Francia oscillates under pressure.

Timeline:
• 1794: Revolutionary abolition
• 1802: Napoleonic restoration
• 1848: Final abolition

Authority vacillates between morality and control.

Empire fractures under inconsistency.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. CIVILIZATIONAL MEMORY FUNCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
African slavery encodes **the fatal tension between universalism and extraction**.

Lessons:
• Economic dependence corrodes moral authority.
• Legal rationalization deepens contradiction.
• Universal claims invite universal challenge.
• Slavery accelerates imperial delegitimation.

Slavery weakens Francia from within.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. CONTINUITY INSIGHTS (RAW)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Universalism collapses when selectively applied.
• Plantation wealth delays reform but magnifies collapse.
• Haiti is slavery’s verdict on Francia.
• Moral authority cannot coexist with mass coercion.
• Slavery haunts Francia’s revolutionary legacy.

Tension preserved without verdict.
