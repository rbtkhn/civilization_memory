────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Trafalgar as **Francia's naval death**: the battle
that eliminated French sea power, forced Napoleon to continental

II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT: THE INVASION PLAN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trafalgar results from Napoleon's invasion scheme.

The plan:
• Concentrate French and Spanish fleets
• Lure British fleet to Caribbean
• Return to Channel with combined fleet
• Cover invasion force crossing to Britain
• 150,000 troops at Boulogne await crossing

The scheme's failure:

"Napoleon's invasion plan required French naval concentration in the
Channel—something France had never achieved against British opposition.
Villeneuve was ordered to combine the Toulon and Cádiz squadrons with
the Spanish fleet, lure Nelson to the Caribbean, then return to cover
the invasion. The plan was intricate, precise, and wholly unrealistic.
It assumed the British would cooperate by being in the wrong place.
They did not."
— John Keegan, *The Price of Admiralty* (1988)

By October 1805, Napoleon had abandoned invasion and marched to Ulm.
Trafalgar destroyed a fleet no longer needed for its original purpose.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. VILLENEUVE: THE RELUCTANT ADMIRAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pierre-Charles Villeneuve commands against his own judgment.

His situation:
• Knows Franco-Spanish fleet inferior in training
• Knows Nelson's tactics will be aggressive
• Ordered to sea by Napoleon despite objections
• Fears both defeat and Napoleon's wrath

The impossible position:

"Villeneuve was a competent officer trapped by circumstances. He
understood that his fleet—French ships with depleted crews, Spanish
ships with untrained men—could not match British seamanship. He had
written to Paris explaining the odds. Napoleon replied with orders
to sail and implicit threats. Villeneuve chose battle over disgrace,
knowing he would probably lose. 'The enemy will not confine himself
to forming a line of battle parallel to ours,' he warned his captains.
'He will endeavour to envelop our rear, to break through our line,
and to direct his ships in groups upon such of ours as he shall
have cut off.' He predicted Nelson's tactics exactly—and could do
nothing to prevent them."
— John Keegan, *The Price of Admiralty* (1988)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. NELSON'S TACTICS: BREAKING THE LINE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nelson abandons conventional naval tactics.

Traditional approach:
• Fleets form parallel lines
• Ships exchange broadsides
• Battle of attrition; rarely decisive

Nelson's innovation:
• Attack in two columns perpendicular to enemy line
• Break through line at multiple points
• Create mêlée where British gunnery excels
• Accept initial vulnerability for decisive result

The tactical revolution:

"Nelson's approach at Trafalgar was calculated risk. By sailing
directly at the Franco-Spanish line, his leading ships would endure
raking fire they could not return. But once through the line, each
British ship would engage enemies from leeward, preventing escape
and forcing close action where British gun crews—trained to fire
three rounds to their opponents' one—would prove decisive. Nelson
was betting that his ships would survive the approach long enough
to make the gamble worthwhile. He was right."
— John Keegan, *The Price of Admiralty* (1988)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE BATTLE: FOUR HOURS OF DESTRUCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trafalgar is decided by gunnery superiority.

The sequence:
• 11:45: "England expects that every man will do his duty"
• 12:00: First shots fired at Royal Sovereign
• 12:30: Nelson's Victory breaks Franco-Spanish line
• 13:15: Nelson mortally wounded by sniper
• 14:30: Villeneuve's Bucentaure surrenders
• 16:30: Firing largely ceases
• 17:30: Last French ship strikes

The gunnery differential:

"British victory at Trafalgar was won by gunnery. The rate of fire—
three broadsides to the enemy's one—was the product of constant
drill at sea, something French and Spanish crews rarely experienced.
When ships closed to point-blank range, this differential was
devastating. French and Spanish vessels were dismasted, their guns
silenced, their crews slaughtered by carronades firing grapeshot
through shattered hulls. Twenty-two Allied ships were taken or
destroyed; the British lost none."
— John Keegan, *The Price of Admiralty* (1988)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. NELSON'S DEATH: BRITISH MARTYRDOM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nelson dies at the moment of victory.

The death:
• Shot by musket from Redoutable's mizzentop
• Ball enters shoulder, lodges in spine
• Lives three hours; learns of victory
• "Thank God I have done my duty"

The meaning for Francia:

"Nelson's death gave Britain a martyr and confirmed a legend. For
France, it was small consolation. The admiral who had destroyed the
French fleet at the Nile, who had demonstrated British naval
superiority at Copenhagen, who had now annihilated French sea power
at Trafalgar—he was dead, but his work was complete. France would
not rebuild a battle fleet capable of challenging Britain for a
century. Nelson had made British maritime supremacy permanent."
— John Keegan, *The Price of Admiralty* (1988)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. STRATEGIC CONSEQUENCES: CONTINENTAL SYSTEM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Trafalgar forces Napoleon to economic warfare.

The logic:
• Invasion impossible after Trafalgar
• British fleet controls all seas
• Only option: destroy British trade
• Continental System: close Europe to British goods
• Requires control of entire European coastline

The strategic shift:

"Trafalgar did not end Napoleon's war with Britain; it defined its
character. Unable to invade, Napoleon turned to economic strangulation.
The Continental System—closing all European ports to British trade—
became his strategy. But this required controlling every coastline
from Portugal to Russia. The pursuit of that control produced the
Peninsula War, the invasion of Russia, and ultimately Napoleon's
destruction. Trafalgar did not defeat Napoleon; it determined the
form his defeat would take."
— Andrew Roberts, *Napoleon: A Life* (2014)

IX. VILLENEUVE'S FATE: SUICIDE OR MURDER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Villeneuve dies under mysterious circumstances.

The sequence:
• Captured at Trafalgar; held in Britain
• Released on parole (April 1806)
• Returns to France
• Found dead in Rennes hotel (22 April 1806)
• Six stab wounds to the chest
• Official verdict: suicide

The suspicion:

"Villeneuve's death was ruled suicide, but the circumstances remain
questionable. Six stab wounds to the chest is an unusual method of
self-destruction. Napoleon had made clear his fury at the admiral's
failure. Whether Villeneuve killed himself in anticipation of
disgrace or was murdered by agents of the Emperor has never been
established. What is certain is that Napoleon could not forgive
Trafalgar, and Villeneuve could not survive his displeasure."
— John Keegan, *The Price of Admiralty* (1988)
