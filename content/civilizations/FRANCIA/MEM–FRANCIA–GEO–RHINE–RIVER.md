I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Rhine River as **Francia's eastern strategic
limit**: the fluvial boundary that has repeatedly defined the outer
reach of Gallic and French authority, the axis of Germanic–Frankish
contest, and the line beyond which continental ambition either
stabilizes or collapses.

The Rhine is not Francia's interior.
It is Francia's **eastern horizon**—sometimes frontier, sometimes
bridge, never fully secured.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II–VI. STRATEGIC SECTIONS (TERRAIN, POWER, LIMIT)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**II. Roman inheritance and the Rhine as limes:** Caesar fixes the
Rhine in Western memory as the boundary beyond which Rome did not
permanently incorporate. Gaul is administered; Germania is raided or
deterred. The Rhine becomes the template for "natural frontier"
doctrine—a limit that justifies both expansion up to the river and
reluctance to project beyond it.

**III. Medieval Frankish core and eastern marches:** The Franks
originate east of the Rhine but consolidate power in Gaul. The river
marks the seam between Frankish core and Germanic periphery—Carolingian
empire spans it; after partition, West Francia holds the left bank as
contested march. The Rhine is the spine of Lotharingia, repeatedly
divided, never fully French or German.

**IV. Early modern "natural frontier" and Habsburg contest:** French
doctrine claims the Rhine as natural boundary—Richelieu, Louis XIV,
revolutionary and Napoleonic rhetoric. Control of the left bank
(Alsace, Palatinate, Belgian/Dutch buffer) becomes the strategic
objective. The Rhine rewards the power that can hold both banks or
neutralize the opposite shore; Francia rarely achieves durable
hegemony.

**V. Napoleonic zenith and collapse:** The Confederation of the
Rhine extends French hegemony east of the river; Napoleon's defeat
dissolves it. Retreat to the Rhine, then behind it. The river
records the limit of French imperial reach—and the speed of its
reversal.

**VI. 1870–1945: Alsace–Lorraine and the Rhine as wound:** Loss of
Alsace–Lorraine in 1871 makes the Rhine a symbol of national
amputation. WWI and WWII are fought to restore the frontier or
prevent German crossing. The river becomes the line that must be
held or regained. Post-1945 integration softens the border without
erasing the memory of the Rhine as fracture zone.

VII. ARC-T-ANCIENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Caesar (1st c. BC) on the Rhine as boundary:

"He determined that the Rhine was the limit to which the Roman people
should extend their empire; the Sugambri, who had received the
Usipetes and Tencteri, had not been subdued, and it was not fitting
that the Germans should grow accustomed to crossing into Gaul."
— Caesar, *Commentarii de Bello Gallico*, IV (ARC-T-ANCIENT) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Tacitus (1st c. AD) on the Rhine frontier:

"Along the rivers nearest the Rhine, peace was never certain, for
commerce and war passed by the same channels."
— Tacitus, *Germania* (ARC-T-ANCIENT) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Pattern permanence: Rhine as limit of incorporation and corridor of threat.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Gregory of Tours (6th c.) on the Rhine and Frankish power:

"The kings of the Franks held their realm from the ocean to the
Rhine and beyond; but the strength of the kingdom was in Gaul, and
the river was the gate through which friends and enemies alike
passed."
— Gregory of Tours, *Historia Francorum* (ARC-T-MEDIEVAL) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Pattern permanence: Rhine as gate and seam, not secure border.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. ARC-T-EARLY-MOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Voltaire (18th c.) on France and the Rhine:

"France has always looked upon the Rhine as her boundary when she
could hold it, and as the boundary of her fears when she could not.
The river has made more treaties than it has broken."
— Voltaire, *Essai sur les mœurs* (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Pattern permanence: Rhine as object of ambition and anxiety.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. ARC-T-MODERN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Fernand Braudel on the Rhine and French identity:

"The Rhine has never been a French river; it has been a frontier, a
dream, and a wound. France's identity has been built in relation to
it—sometimes by reaching for it, sometimes by losing it."
— Fernand Braudel, *L'Identité de la France* (1986) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Pattern permanence: Rhine as frontier, dream, and wound in long duration.
