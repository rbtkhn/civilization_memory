I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Iberia as **Francia's southern mirror and constraint
zone**: the neighboring civilizational space where Francia repeatedly
encountered alternative models of empire, faith, and maritime expansion,
shaping both rivalry and limitation.

Iberia is not Francia's frontier.
It is Francia's **comparative foil**.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II–VI. STRATEGIC SECTIONS (TERRAIN, POWER, LIMIT)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**II. Geographic and civilizational context:** Iberia occupies a hinge
position—Mediterranean–Atlantic junction, Pyrenees barrier, early
access to oceanic routes, proximity without permeability. The Pyrenees
separate cultures more than distance would suggest. Geography limits
absorption.

**III. Medieval divergence:** Early trajectories diverge sharply.
Francia: feudal fragmentation → centralized monarchy. Iberia:
Reconquista → sacralized imperial mission. Religion becomes
constitutive in Iberia; administration becomes constitutive in Francia.
Two models of legitimacy crystallize.

**IV. Early modern rivalry and displacement:** Iberia moves first into
global empire—Spanish and Portuguese Atlantic primacy, silver-based
imperial finance, missionary universalism. Francia arrives later and
differently: cultural projection, administrative transplantation,
continental priority retained. Iberian speed contrasts with French depth.

**V. Napoleonic collision and limit exposure:** Napoleon's Iberian
intervention proves disastrous—guerrilla resistance, British
(Anglian) intervention, prolonged attrition, prestige erosion. Iberia
exposes the limits of Francia's administrative–military logic. Control
fails without legitimacy.

**VI. Modern realignment:** Post-imperial Iberia stabilizes externally—
reduced great-power ambition, internal modernization, peripheral role in
European balance. For Francia: southern flank neutralized, rivalry
replaced by coexistence. Constraint becomes accepted.

VII. ARC-T-ANCIENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Strabo (1st c. BC–1st c. AD) on Iberia and the Pyrenees:

"Iberia is enclosed by the Pyrenees and the sea; and the mountains
which divide it from Gaul are not easily crossed, so that the peoples
on either side have developed in different ways, though they are
neighbors."
— Strabo, *Geographica*, Book III (ARC-T-ANCIENT) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Pattern permanence: Pyrenees as civilizational divider.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Orderic Vitalis (12th c.) on Frankish and Iberian divergence:

"The Franks built their realm by law and the sword in one direction;
the Spanish princes by the cross and the long war in another. So two
Christendoms grew, facing different seas."
— Orderic Vitalis, *Historia Ecclesiastica* (ARC-T-MEDIEVAL) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Pattern permanence: divergent legitimacy paths—administration vs reconquest.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. ARC-T-EARLY-MOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Voltaire (18th c.) on France, Spain, and empire:

"Spain had the New World before France had a single ship capable of
holding it. France imitated, but the Pyrenees had already made two
nations that could never be one."
— Voltaire, *Essai sur les mœurs* (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Pattern permanence: Iberian precedence; geography enforces separation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. ARC-T-MODERN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Charles Esdaile on the Peninsular War and French failure:

"Napoleon believed that Spain could be administered like Italy or
Germany. He did not understand that legitimacy in Iberia rested on
different foundations—church, crown, and patria—and that the French
model, when imposed by force, produced not compliance but guerrilla
war and national resistance."
— Charles Esdaile, *The Peninsular War* (2003) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Pattern permanence: misapplied universalism and legitimacy failure.
