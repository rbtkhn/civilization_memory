I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the Somme as **Francia's demonstration that
industrial warfare consumes armies without producing decision**:
the battle that revealed the Western Front's structural stalemate
and the horrific costs of attempting breakthrough.

For Francia, the Somme is **sacrifice absorbed into the national
bloodletting of 1914-1918**—overshadowed by Verdun in French memory,
yet integral to the attritional arithmetic that eventually exhausted
Germany.

Unlike Verdun (France's defining WWI memory), the Somme was primarily
British. But Francia bled there too, and the Somme represents the
shared Allied experience of industrialized mass death.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT: VERDUN AND ALLIED COORDINATION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Somme originates as an Allied coordinated offensive.

Original plan:
• Franco-British attack on 40-mile front
• French providing majority of forces
• Intended for summer 1916

Verdun changes everything:

"The German attack on Verdun, beginning in February 1916, transformed
the Somme offensive. France, bleeding at Verdun, could no longer
provide the forces originally planned. What had been conceived as a
primarily French operation with British support became a primarily
British operation with French support. The British would attack with
forces largely composed of Kitchener's 'New Armies'—volunteers who
had enlisted after August 1914 and had never seen major combat."
— John Keegan, *The First World War* (1998)

Francia's contribution is reduced by Verdun's drain; Britain's
New Armies will pay the price.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE FIRST DAY: INDUSTRIAL SLAUGHTER
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1 July 1916 becomes the bloodiest day in British military history.

The statistics:
• 57,470 British casualties on Day One
• 19,240 killed
• Attack across 18-mile front
• Most gains reversed by nightfall
• Some battalions lose 80-90% of strength

John Keegan describes the soldier's experience:

"The men of the New Armies had been promised that the artillery
bombardment—seven days, 1.5 million shells—would destroy the German
defenses. They were ordered to advance in waves, at walking pace,
carrying sixty pounds of equipment. The bombardment had failed.
The German deep dugouts survived. When the whistles blew at 7:30 AM,
the German machine-gunners emerged from their shelters and found
perfect targets: dense lines of men walking slowly across open ground.
They were killed in rows, in sections, in whole battalions. Some units
were destroyed in the first minutes."
— John Keegan, *The Face of Battle* (1976)

The failure was systematic: artillery that could not destroy deep
dugouts, infantry tactics that assumed destroyed defenses, machine
guns that made open-ground assault suicidal.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. THE FRENCH SECTOR: OVERSHADOWED SUCCESS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
French forces achieved greater success on 1 July.

French tactics differed:
• Advance in small groups, not waves
• Use of cover and fire-and-movement
• Better artillery-infantry coordination
• More experienced troops (veterans of 1914-1915)

The relative success:

"South of the Somme, the French achieved their objectives on 1 July
with relatively light casualties. They captured more ground in a few
hours than the British would take in weeks. The difference was
experience. French infantry had learned—at terrible cost—that walking
into machine-gun fire was suicide. They used different tactics:
infiltration, combined arms, close artillery support. But the French
success was overshadowed by British disaster, and French memory would
focus on Verdun, not the Somme."
— Niall Ferguson, *The Pity of War* (1998)

Francia's tactical learning, purchased at Verdun and earlier battles,
produced better results. But the Somme remains British in memory.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. ATTRITION AS STRATEGY: THE LOGIC OF MASS DEATH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Somme continues for 141 days because attrition *is* the objective.

Haig's reasoning:
• Germany cannot replace losses indefinitely
• Allied reserves are larger
• Even failed attacks kill Germans
• Eventually, attrition will break German army

The grim arithmetic:

"By autumn 1916, the strategic rationale for the Somme had become
explicitly attritional. The breakthrough that the campaign had
originally sought was admitted to be impossible. Instead, success
was measured in Germans killed, Germans captured, German resources
consumed. The logic was mathematical: if Germany lost more than it
could replace, eventually Germany would collapse. The fact that
Britain and France were also losing more than they could easily
replace was set aside."
— John Keegan, *The Face of Battle* (1976)

The Somme institutionalizes the Western Front's fundamental dynamic:
industrial production of death without strategic decision.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. THE TANK: TECHNOLOGICAL EMERGENCE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The Somme sees the first use of tanks in warfare.

15 September 1916: Flers-Courcelette

"The tank was born of desperation. Something was needed to cross
no-man's-land under fire, to crush wire, to suppress machine guns.
The tanks at Flers-Courcelette were primitive, unreliable, and
terrifying to their crews. Of forty-nine deployed, only about thirty
reached the start line; many broke down or ditched. But those that
survived demonstrated that armored vehicles could cross trenches
and create local panic among German defenders."
— John Keegan, *The First World War* (1998)

The RMA that would end trench warfare is visible in embryo. But the
technology is premature; the tank will not be decisive until 1918,
and fully realized only in 1940.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VII. FRENCH MEMORY: VERDUN'S SHADOW
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In Francia's civilizational memory, the Somme is secondary.

Verdun (February-December 1916) absorbs French national attention:
• "Ils ne passeront pas" becomes national motto
• Rotation system (noria) involves entire French army
• 377,000 French casualties
• Sacred soil defense narrative

The Somme, by contrast:
• Primarily British operation
• French role supporting
• ~200,000 French casualties (significant but overshadowed)
• No equivalent national narrative

This displacement matters:

"The Somme entered British memory as the defining experience of the
war: the Pals battalions, the first day, the industrial slaughter.
For France, Verdun served this function. The French Somme experience
was real—200,000 casualties is not trivial—but it was absorbed into
the larger bloodletting rather than standing alone. French memory
of 1916 is Verdun; the Somme belongs to Britain."
— Robert Tombs, *France 1814-1914* (1996), extended analysis

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. THE SOMME IN KEEGAN'S FRAMEWORK
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
John Keegan's *The Face of Battle* uses the Somme as terminal point.

The book's arc:
• Agincourt (1415): Medieval war, face-to-face
• Waterloo (1815): Gunpowder war, formations and volleys
• Somme (1916): Industrial war, impersonal mass death

Keegan's thesis:

"The Somme marked the culmination of a process that had been
transforming warfare for five centuries. At Agincourt, men killed
men they could see; the arrow and the sword required proximity.
At Waterloo, musket volleys and artillery created distance but
formations still clashed. At the Somme, the machine gun and the
artillery barrage made killing industrial: men died without seeing
their killers, killed by shells fired miles away, by gas released
from cylinders, by bullets from machine guns they never located.
Battle had become production: the production of death."
— John Keegan, *The Face of Battle* (1976)

The Somme represents war's dehumanization complete.
