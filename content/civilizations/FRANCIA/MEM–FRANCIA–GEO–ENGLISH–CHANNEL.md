I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves the English Channel as **Francia's most persistent
strategic obstruction**: the narrow maritime barrier that repeatedly
prevents the conversion of continental power, demographic mass, and
military prestige into decisive authority over Anglia.

The Channel is close enough to tempt, yet wide enough to deny.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II–VI. STRATEGIC SECTIONS (TERRAIN, POWER, LIMIT)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
**II. Channel as unfinished continent:** For Francia, the Channel
feels incomplete. Geographic proximity to Anglia, cultural and
dynastic entanglement, recurrent temptation toward unification or
domination. The Channel interrupts continental logic.

**III. Dynastic success and structural failure:** Only one durable
Channel crossing succeeds (Norman Conquest—elite replacement,
administrative fusion). Later attempts fail: armadas, invasion
flotillas, airborne substitution. Structural dominance does not translate.

**IV. Prestige war vs. maritime reality:** Francia repeatedly
misreads the Channel. Emphasis on army mass, underinvestment in
sustained naval control, expectation of decisive confrontation.
The Channel refuses symbolic victory; it demands infrastructure, not glory.

**V. Channel as humiliation multiplier:** Failure at the Channel
magnifies legitimacy cost. Visible inability to subdue smaller rival;
prestige loss disproportionate to distance. The Channel exposes limits
more than losses.

**VI. Ideological & imperial recoding; modern adaptation:** Francia
reframes failure narratively. Moral condemnation of Anglia, universalist
critique replacing conquest. In the modern era the Channel is managed,
not conquered. Civilizational memory: limit and resentment. Continental
power does not guarantee maritime success; the Channel defines Anglia
as unreachable by force.

VII. ARC-T-ANCIENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Caesar (1st c. BC) on the Britons and the sea:

"The interior of Britain is inhabited by those who claim to be
indigenous; the coast by those who crossed from Belgium to raid and
make war. The sea that separates the island from Gaul is narrow,
and the passage has always tempted and often punished those who
attempted it."
— Caesar, *Commentarii de Bello Gallico*, IV (ARC-T-ANCIENT) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Pattern permanence: narrow sea as temptation and punishment.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. ARC-T-MEDIEVAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Jean Froissart (14th c.) on cross-Channel war:

"The French king prepared a great fleet to pass into England, but
the sea and the English did not permit it. So it has ever been:
the water that divides is harder to cross than the land that unites."
— Jean Froissart, *Chroniques* (ARC-T-MEDIEVAL) [ERC-PRIMARY]

Pattern permanence: Channel as persistent barrier to French ambition.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IX. ARC-T-EARLY-MOD
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Voltaire (18th c.) on France, England, and the sea:

"France has always had more territory and more men than England;
but the sea between them has made the difference. The Channel has
preserved England from invasion and has taught France that continental
greatness does not command the waves."
— Voltaire, *Essai sur les mœurs* (ARC-T-EARLY-MOD) [ERC-CONTEXTUAL]

Pattern permanence: maritime limit to continental power.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
X. ARC-T-MODERN
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Robert Tombs on the Channel and Franco-British relations:

"The Channel has never been a bridge for French power; it has been
a barrier. Every attempt to cross it—from the Armada to Napoleon
to the invasion plans of 1940—has failed or been abandoned. The
Channel is Francia's permanent strategic frustration: so close, and
so unreachable."
— Robert Tombs, *France 1814–1914* (1996) (ARC-T-MODERN) [ERC-SECONDARY]

Pattern permanence: limit and frustration.
