I. MEMORY PURPOSE & SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This file preserves Agincourt as **Francia's demonstration that
aristocratic valor cannot overcome tactical discipline**: the battle
that revealed the chivalric ideal's military bankruptcy when faced
with English longbow tactics.

For Francia, Agincourt is **humiliation encoded**—a defeat so
disproportionate that it challenged the foundations of noble military
culture. The flower of French chivalry rode into the mud and died
under arrow storms they could not answer.

Contradictions are preserved without synthesis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT: THE CHEVAUCHÉE AND FRENCH DIVISIONS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Henry V's campaign exploits French weakness.

Conditions:
• France divided: Armagnac-Burgundian civil war ongoing
• King Charles VI mentally incapacitated
• No unified French command structure
• English claim to French throne revived (1328 succession dispute)
• Henry seeks decisive battle to legitimate claim

Juliet Barker contextualizes the political division:

"The France that Henry V invaded in 1415 was a kingdom at war with
itself. The Armagnac and Burgundian factions had been fighting since
1407, and the murder of Louis of Orléans had created a blood feud
that paralyzed central authority. The Burgundians would not march
with the Armagnacs; the Armagnacs suspected Burgundian treachery.
Henry V understood this perfectly. His chevauchée was designed to
force battle against a divided enemy."
— Juliet Barker, *Agincourt: Henry V and the Battle That Made England* (2005)

Francia's internal divisions enable external defeat—a pattern that
recurs across French military history.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. THE BATTLEFIELD: TERRAIN AS WEAPON
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Henry V chooses ground that negates French advantages.

The terrain:
• Narrow front between two woods (Tramecourt and Agincourt)
• Recently plowed, rain-soaked fields
• Mud deep enough to trap armored men
• French unable to use numerical advantage

John Keegan analyzes the terrain effect:

"The battlefield at Agincourt was a natural killing ground for the
English. The woods on either flank compressed the French advance into
a front no wider than the English line, negating their numerical
superiority. The plowed fields, soaked by autumn rains, became a
quagmire that slowed and then trapped the armored French knights.
Men in sixty pounds of steel plate, who had dismounted to advance
on foot, sank to their knees in mud. Some drowned in it. Those who
reached the English line were exhausted before combat began."
— John Keegan, *The Face of Battle* (1976)

The terrain transformed French strength into weakness. Cavalry that
could not charge, armor that became a trap.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. THE LONGBOW: TECHNOLOGICAL ASYMMETRY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
English archery proves decisive.

Capabilities:
• Rate of fire: 10-12 arrows per minute per archer
• Range: ~250 yards effective, ~350 yards maximum
• 5,000 archers = ~50,000 arrows per minute
• Bodkin arrows penetrate plate at close range

John Keegan describes the arrow storm:

"The English archers at Agincourt delivered a volume of fire that
no medieval army could withstand. At the battle's height, fifty
thousand arrows a minute were falling on the French. The noise was
compared to a hailstorm; the darkness as arrows blocked the sun was
compared to eclipse. Horses were struck and panicked, throwing riders
or bolting back into their own lines. Men were hit in the face
through visors, in the armpits through gaps in plate, in the legs
where armor was thinnest. The wounded fell and were trampled; the
dead accumulated in walls of corpses."
— John Keegan, *The Face of Battle* (1976)

The longbow represented an RMA (Revolution in Military Affairs) that
French chivalric culture refused to adopt. England recruited archers
from the yeomanry; France could not abandon the aristocratic monopoly
on military honor.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
V. THE SLAUGHTER: CHIVALRY'S DEATH
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Agincourt becomes a massacre.

The sequence:
• French advance through arrow storm
• Front ranks reach English line, already exhausted
• Close combat favors English (fresher, lighter-armed)
• French second and third waves push forward
• Pressure creates crush; men suffocate in armor
• Henry orders prisoner execution (controversial)

The prisoner killing:

"When Henry ordered the killing of the French prisoners, he violated
the laws of chivalry and shocked his own men. But his reasoning was
military: a rumor of attack on the baggage train, the presence of
thousands of captured French nobles, and only a few hundred English
guards made the prisoners a potential threat. The order was obeyed
reluctantly—the prisoners represented ransom wealth—but it was obeyed.
Perhaps 1,500 captives were killed. It was the final repudiation of
chivalric war."
— John Keegan, *The Face of Battle* (1976)

The slaughter encoded both military necessity and chivalric death.
War as profit (ransom culture) yields to war as annihilation.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VI. NOBLE CASUALTIES: DECAPITATION OF THE ELITE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
French losses concentrate at the top.

Dead nobles include:
• Charles d'Albret, Constable of France
• Duke of Alençon
• Duke of Bar
• Duke of Brabant
• Count of Nevers
• Count of Vaudémont
• 90+ barons and bannerets

The implications:

"Agincourt killed a generation of French military leadership.
The constable, three dukes, eight counts, and nearly a hundred
barons—men who would have commanded French armies for the next
thirty years—died in the mud or under English blades. The
institutional memory of French military command was erased in
an afternoon. The survivors were traumatized; the next generation
had no mentors. France would not produce comparable military
leadership until Joan of Arc, fifteen years later."
— Juliet Barker, *Agincourt* (2005)

The losses are civilizational, not merely military. Francia's
warrior aristocracy is decapitated.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
VIII. THE LONGBOW PROBLEM: WHY FRANCE DIDN'T ADAPT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
France's failure to adopt longbow tactics reveals structural constraints.

The English could recruit archers because:
• Yeoman class existed with property and training
• Archery practice was legally mandated
• Non-noble military service was socially acceptable

France could not because:
• Military honor was aristocratic monopoly
• Peasant soldiers were socially degraded
• No tradition of mass archery training
• Crossbow (slower, less skill-intensive) was preferred

Peter H. Wilson contextualizes the social dimension:

"The longbow was not merely a weapon; it was a social institution.
England's archers were free men who trained from childhood, whose
status derived partly from their military value. France had no
equivalent class. French military culture was aristocratic;
war was the nobleman's business. To arm peasants with longbows
would have been to arm a class against its masters. The French
preferred crossbows, which required less training and could be
issued to urban militias without social disruption."
— Peter H. Wilson, *Heart of Europe* (2016)

Francia's social structure prevented military adaptation.
